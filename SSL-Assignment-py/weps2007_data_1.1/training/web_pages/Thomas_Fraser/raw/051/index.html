01 March 2004
Thomas Fraser's legacy
 
Thomas Fraser was born in Burra in 1927. He was a crofter and fisherman and learned to play fiddle, guitar, mandolin, banjo, and piano. He aquired a gramophone and discovered singers like Jimmy Rodgers, Hank Snow and hank Williams. All his spare time was spent listening to thir recordings. In 1953 he bought a reel-to-reel recorder and would record himself singing and playing the songs of all his favourite singers, just for his own pleasure.

These recordings were all preserved and have now been released on two CDs. The first, Long Gone Lonesome Blues, was released in 2002 and the second, You Me and My Old Guitar, was released in 2003. The man behind these releases is Thomas’s randson, Karl Simpson. Sheila Manson caught up with Karl to ask him more about the Thomas Fraser collection. Part I of II

When did you find out about all these recordings?

Music has always been a central theme in our family so I was always aware of my grandfather’s great interest in music and his recordings. My first inkling that his music was something special came about when Dutch country musicians AG & Kate came to the islands, about 1982 or so. Apparently, they had heard tapes of my grandfather at Eddie Williamson’s house in Yorkshire. They were so taken with it that they contacted my mum and grandmother and eventually visited the islands.

AG & Kate composed a tribute song for Thomas and toured around Shetland, playing halls, schools, etc. I remember they came to Hamnavoe Primary School to do a concert and they played the song to us. I remember feeling very proud that day.

Around the same time, 1983, the Memories of Yesterday cassettes were released and they generated a lot of interest in Shetland at the time. My mum and grandmother were obviously always making references to my grandfather and his music and there was always a bit of hope expressed when we as kids showed any interest in music.

However, I never really had any great interest in music until I was about 15 when I heard Jimi Hendrix. Then I could not get enough of it and guitars in general. I reckon I singlehandedly supported Clives for a while! As this was going on my interest was narrow and Granda’s music was still in the background. I would be reminded of it often at local dances and parties when people like Robbie Cumming would come up and discuss music and they would recall the talents of Thomas Fraser.

Our cousin Bobby Fraser would always be mentioning him as well and playing the tapes to us. At that age though, about 17-18, I didn’t really appreciate it. It wasn’t until about 2000, when I was 26 that I was able to ‘take it in’. Mam and I went to Bobby’s house one night and he played ‘TB Blues’‚ to say I was bowled over was an understatement.

What made you decide to put the recordings onto CD and release them?

I was shocked at what I heard — the quality and authenticity of the music. By then I was listening to blues music by the likes of Robert Johnson and really revelled in these stories of artists who were legends‚ in some particular way. Here was someone in my own immediate family whose music easily fell into the ‘legend’ category. I could not believe it and here they all were on these old reel-to-reel tapes which were becoming precarious to say the least.

I decided there and then that we had to get the music preserved. Therefore, it was a rescue operation in the first instance. The aim primarily, was to preserve the music for myself and the family. What we had in effect, was a priceless family heirloom and all necessary steps had to be taken to secure that. If it was left, soon it would be lost forever. I wanted to have the music readily accessible and in a presentable format i.e. in a CD case with cover.

However, it was clear to me from the start that what we had here was music that could easily be released on a wider scale not only to the Shetlanders who remembered him, but to people further afield who might find the music and story The Thomas Fraser reel to reel collection. behind it interesting. If this was the case, the music would have to be properly restored and packaged appropriately.

The Thomas Fraser reel to reel collection.

It must have been a pain-staking job taking all the old reels, playing them through and getting them onto CD

It was pain-staking and nerve racking to say the least, but it was also a fantastic process of discovery. I felt very privileged to have a unique opportunity to do something like this. I took a few weeks off between jobs in Aberdeen and went through each reel in sequence, writing down and grading all the songs. Often, you would be playing through a great song on very fragile tape and you would be praying that the song would reach its end without the tape breaking. If that happened, the song could be lost forever.

Some of the reels we received later, ones from Scott Christie, John Wm. Ward, etc., had not been played in over 30 years, sometimes up to 50 years! It was a unique experience hearing these tapes - almost like being in a time machine as many of the reels did not just contain music, but conversations, TV and radio music, etc.

After the first release, Long Gone Lonesome Blues, the album was reviewed in Country Music People. Did you get much feedback from that?

We have been lucky enough to have received lots of great press. Everyone who reviewed the CDs has given them great critical acclaim. Funnily enough, Country Music People took a while to get round to doing it. When eventually they did, one of their readers wrote into their letters page and said about time too! He went on to say that he thought that it was “one of the best ever country music  releases to come out of the UK”. Things like that just are amazing and what would Thomas have thought?

At the end of last year you released the second CD, You Me and My Old Guitar. Has this been as popular as the first?

I had a feeling that You, Me and My Old Guitar would find it hard in the wake of Long Gone Lonesome Blues. The first CD was exactly that — the first ever CD and as such it would always generate that extra bit of interest — the first Thomas Fraser recordings to be heard in that format. The second release by any band never has the same impact but I am very pleased with how the second CD has done to date. We are almost at the same sales level as the first CD and the reviews have been great as well.

Long Gone Lonesome Blues is the CD that I would recommend to anyone who has never heard Thomas Fraser before. The whole CD and running order has been geared that way. However, I would recommend that they get You, Me and My Old Guitar to accompany it as many of the tracks are essential listening and present differences and a more complete picture to what is on the first disc. If I were ever to make a ‘Best of’‚ Thomas Fraser CD, I would extract tracks from both CDs in equal amounts. Of course, on the second CD, there are also many tracks which have only recently come to light and this makes it extra special. Louisiana is one of my favourites and it is the only version of that song we have.

Thomas with daughter May circa 1959.

On both CDs there are 25 tracks. Good value for money these days. Why did you decide to put on so many tracks?

I did think long and hard about the decision to put 25 tracks on. Before, we released the first CD we were not sure at all how it would do. We had put a lot of money and time into it at that stage and there was an outside chance that it might be the only CD we released. With that in mind, I took the decision to put as many tracks as was practical onto the disc, simply because we might not have the opportunity to do something like this again.

25 tracks were specifically chosen as that was the length of time since Thomas’s death in 1978. I felt that if that were only the disc available the 25 songs would be a definitive representation of Thomas’s music.

With the second CD it was pretty much the same rationale. I nearly went for 20 then decided that again I should use the opportunity to put as many as possible on. We almost put a bonus track on as well — a great and undiscovered, if very deteriorated, version of My Ship Set Sail from Bluebird Island. I have a bit of an obsessive compulsive disorder though and stuck with 25. That meant there were 50 Thomas Fraser tracks available in a suitable format — exactly 50 years after Thomas began recording in 1953.

We could have done things in a very commercial manner. For example, we could have put three or four CDs out with only 12-15 tracks on. But this was never a commercial, money-making project. There was no expense spared on these two CDs. I made sure and got the best I could in terms of sound, packaging, photos and sleeve notes, etc. Being selfish about it, it was really done for the family and my own motive. If no one had wanted to hear the CDs, I would still have made them anyway, just so I could have them in my own CD collection. Its brilliant that so many people have bought Thomas’s music and all the proceeds from the sales can be ploughed straight back into the ongoing work to restore and promote it further.
