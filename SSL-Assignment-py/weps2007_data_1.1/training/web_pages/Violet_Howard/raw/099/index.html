Tornado Victims Answer Questions To Find Identity

MOORE (AP) — Oklahomans who lost their identification in last week’s tornadoes are being fingerprinted and tested to prove who they are.

Maps are being pulled out for people to point out where they once lived and anyone seeking a driver’s license is asked for a thumbprint.

‘‘There’s information in that tornado that’s been blown all over creation,’’ state Department of Public Safety examiner Sabra Denton said Tuesday while staffing a driver’s license office at the First Baptist Church in Moore, being used as a crisis center for storm victims. ‘‘We’re a compassionate outreach here, but we’re still protecting the public.’’

Meanwhile, the state Medical Examiner’s Office said today the death toll from last week’s storms rose to 43 with the deaths of Thelma Anderson Steele, 89, of Oklahoma City and Vera Lamon, 94, of Oklahoma City. Both died in area hospitals.

For many victims, the twisters carried away nearly all of their vital papers: birth certificates, driver’s licenses, titles to homes and other documents.

In Midwest City, residents who ask for duplicate documents but have no ID are given a pop quiz to prove they were homeowners. Police records specialist Hazel Hamer takes out a map and asks them to point to where their houses once stood.

‘‘We realize some people have lost everything,’’ Hamer said. ‘‘We just go by the map and we check the utility records to make sure the name and address on the bills match the addresses people are giving us.’’

Larry Williamson of the state Department of Public Safety has processed applications for more than 100 driver’s licenses over several days at the Moore church.

People have been offering everything from fishing licenses to American Association of Retired Persons membership cards to prove their identity.

‘‘I took a guy awhile ago who had his entire criminal booking record with his name and address on it,’’ Williamson said. ‘‘He got his license.’’

To guard against fraud, Williamson calls up driving histories and quizzes applicants about speeding tickets, maiden names or how long they have been driving.

Everyone is asked to give a thumbprint, which scares away criminals and gives state investigators some key evidence if the license is used to commit fraud, Williamson said.

‘‘We have had some people come into the exam stations who change their stories or don’t know all the answers,’’ he said. ‘‘They leave pretty quickly.’’

Melissa Hess, 24, hid in a cellar while the twisters destroyed her two-story Moore home. When she emerged, everything was gone, including the file box where she kept all her bank records and her two son’s birth certificates.

‘‘I don’t have my identification, I don’t have my checkbook, I don’t have my credit card,’’ Mrs. Hess said. ‘‘The only thing left at my house is the grill.’’


Blackwell, Peckham OK Bonds

Voters in both the Blackwell and Peckham School Districts approved more money Tuesday to upgrade their school systems.

Blackwell voters overwhelmingly passed general obligation bonds totaling $400,000 to buy school buses and band uniforms, and to provide a cooling tower for the high school and restrooms and a concession stand at the school softball field.

Proposition 1, which sought $100,000 for the school construction, passed by a vote of 580 to 156. Proposition 2, which sought $300,000 for the transportation purchases, passed, 582-155.

In Peckham, voters approved $110,000 for construction and repairs to the school building, passed by a vote of 26 to 4. They also approved by a vote of 27 to 3 another $90,000 to purchase transportation equipment.

Emergency Situations Get Board Review

By Sally Hodges

News Staff Writer

The Ponca City Board of Education recognized all retiring employees of the Ponca City Public Schools, discussed emergency preparedness and approved several bid recommendations at its regular board meeting Monday night

Teachers retiring at the end of the school year were Elizabeth Hinshaw, Marilyn Leming, Pam Lessert, Suzanne Robinson and P.R. (Hap) Youngs IV. Support Staff retiring are Clairene Arkeketa, Larry Graham, Naomi Kahle, Vivian Lewis, Carla McGregor, Barbara Shuart, LaRita Taylor and Janice Wilcoxson.

Also recognized were Cynthia Winterrowd, library media teacher at the Middle School, as May Certified Employee of the Month; Pearly Roland, teacher assistant and HOSYS coordinator at Garfield Academy, as April Support Employee of the Month; Evelyn Huddleston, child nutrition employee at the Senior High School cafeteria, as May Support Employee of the Month; and Betty Lowrey, parent volunteer at Garfield Academy, as May Friend of Education. Barbara Davis, program coordinator of the Teen Pep program received the Oklahoma Child Advocacy Award for the group.

Emergency Preparedness

Bret Smith, coordinator of Risk Management, reported on guidelines and procedures in place for most any type of emergency arising from natural disasters to crisis created by students, employees or people from outside the school.

Barbara Cusick, principal of the Middle School, discusses the importance of a positive climate in the schools and parental involvement in their children’s activities to include communication with teachers and school personnel. She stressed how bullying is hurtful and not tolerated. She reported on the Ambassador Program, a peer mediation program, and that crises teams were used twice this year due to student deaths.

It was reported that classes are selected at random and hand-held metal detector checks every kid and book bag in that class during the search. Lockers are also checked randomly but frequently.

Rick Dale, principal of Mid-High, addressed the board and stressed that in all past “incidences,” someone knew before hand. He pointed out more has to be done to get to know the students and understand characteristics which act as warning signs. Messages have also been sent to the students that “When you know something, you have to tell someone.” It was reported that, due to the recent violence, students are wanting stricter dress codes and other opportunities to provide a safe school environment.

Po-Hi principal, Jerry Winkle, when addressing the issue of emergency preparedness, commended the board and administrators for all the support and safety precautions which have already been implemented in the schools. He pointed out that “we use every source we have available to us to maintain safety and credibility of students.” He did point out more training was needed, and the district will make training available. A recent incident at the high school was reviewed and the board commended the administration for the handling and outcome of the situation.

Standardized dress codes were discussed, and it was pointed out that many students were in favor of the idea as well as principals. The staffing of the high school and possibly the Middle School with an armed officer was discussed as well as the need for surveillance cameras in schools. The board stressed its desire to be informed of any incidents in the schools.

Public Comment

Student Council president for the 1999-2000 year at Ponca City High School, Ashley Van Hoesen, spoke to the board expressing appreciation felt by the student body of Winkle saying it was “awesome to have a principal who cares,” and that Winkle got the “spirit” back into the school. She related how the high school has collected lots of items to send to tornado victims and reported on activities at the high school. She pointed out the student body has learned how city government works and things can be changed if one goes through the proper channels.

J.D. Hanks discussed the upcoming Great Race, pointing out that the $10,000 entry fee had been waived but that without the blessing of endorsements from administrators they would not be about to go. The cost of the three-week trip will be approximately $14,000. Hanks received notice Monday this group is one of only three high school teams in the United States selected for a $4,000 grant by the Model A Ford Club of America.

Bids Approved

Bids for high school lockers were open April 30 with low being received and approved from School Speciality for $86,933 including installation of almost 1600 Lyon lockers. The project will be completed before the start of school in the fall.

Bids for new buses were opened May 3 with two companies submitting quotes for a transit type D activity bus, three 65 passenger buses and one special education bus. Mid-West Bus Sales had the low quotation at $310,666.

Approval of the following contract or agreements were made: renewal of official courier service for bank deposit pick up and delivery with Burnett Security for a cost of $22.50 per school day; renewal of agreement with Walsworth Publishing Company to publish eight hundred 2000 yearbooks at a cost of $19,927; renewal of agreement with YMCA of Ponca City for the use of the community swimming pool for the 1999-2000 school year; renewal of financial accounting, treasurer, activity fund and personnel software service with Municipal Accounting Service, Inc.; renewal of agreement with monthly elevator inspections and maintenance servicing by Montgomery KONE, Inc. for a cost of $221 per month; and agreement with First Assembly of God School to provide student lunches for Ponca City Public Schools for the 1999-2000 school year.

Contracts awarded include a contract for an annual audit of the finances of the Ponca City School District by Jerry Putnam of Yadon and Putnam Auditing Firm; contract with Lewis and Associates for 1999 bond issue construction projects; and contract with Novell, Inc. to provide additional services to the districts networking system for a cost of $3 per student.

The board made approval for limited English student (LEP) to be granted one unit for the ESL class which will be an English class taught by an English instructor with a secondary English certification, with only one unit meeting the English credit requirement for graduation and the other credits counted as electives.

The annual update to the Professional Development portion of the 1997-2001 Comprehensive Education Plan was approved by the board.

Other Business

After executive session, board members approved the following additional positions for the 1999-2000 school year — two elementary gifted education teachers, two more teachers for high school mathematics, two additional teachers for high school English and one for high school social studies, a high school librarian, a part-time high school Spanish position, one teacher for alternative education self contained for 8th and 9th grades, one Mid-High art teacher, one position for special education (ALCH/TSl), a part-time position for teen PEP mathematics, an assistant teacher for technology, and a heat and air maintenance position.


Architects Submit Plans To PTC

By Laura Corff

News Staff Writer

Two local architects presented preliminary ideas for constructing a new health facility at Pioneer Technology Center, during a board meeting Tuesday. In addition to the health center, the board would like to expand the cafeteria dining area, increase the number of parking spaces and obtain more space for new programs.

Troy Lewis and Perry Schauvliege each featured two ideas for consideration; one to construct a separate building and one as an expansion of the current facility. The board will take the suggestions under advisement.

Also, Schauvliege gave a status report of the bid process for the center’s roofing project, including the remodel of the marketing division. The board agreed to contract with the architect last month to put together a bid packet. Bids for the roofing project will be opened on Wednesday.

In other business, the board approved upgrading its fleet of cars and building a carport structure. The board authorized PTC to spend up to $12,500 for a metal structure, which will include poles and a roof. Steve Waldvogel is working on getting another estimate before proceeding. The carport is expected to be about 120 feet wide.

In addition, financial reports were presented and approved by the board. According to Stacey Rush, business manager, the school is ahead by $421,000 from the same time last year.

Additionally, the board was reassured that an emergency crisis plan was in place. Chuck Bayha submitted the plan in written for members.

After meeting in an executive session, the board reconvened to renew contracts for support staff and special programs personnel. Additionally, the board authorized hiring Heather Bennett, as respiratory therapy instructor, and Shawn Lawson, as part time maintenance worker. The board also accepted the resignation of Randy Hoiser, multimedia instructor.

Upcoming events include: an awards assembly Friday, at the Mid High, at 9 a.m. and GED Graduation on May 27 at 7 p.m. The next board meeting is set for June 8.


Pioneer Tech Receives Fifth Gold Star Award

For the fifth consecutive year, Pioneer Technology Center (PTC) received the Gold Star School award, the highest award for vocational schools. The award will be presented during the Oklahoma Vocational Technical Education Council awards ceremony in August.

The award recognizes area vocational school districts that have met a rigid and demanding criteria, demonstrating a high level of excellence, according to the application form.

Out of the 30 vocational schools in Oklahoma, the following five are receiving the Gold Star: Pioneer Technology Center, Canadian Valley Area Vocational Technical School, Central Tech, Autry Technology Center, and West Watkins Vocational Technical School.

The program recognition will be for a period of one year. Schools must reapply each year. To be eligible, the area school district must satisfy requirements in each of seven categories. Applications are evaluated by a committee.

P.C. Still in Running For Future Project

By LOUISE ABERCROMBIE

News Business Editor

Oklahoma and Ponca City in particular remains in the running for a multi-million dollar carbon fiber plant that could be developed in the future by Conoco. This was reported at the Economic Development Advisory Board (EDAB) meeting by Carl Renfro, chairman of the New Industries Committee.

According to Renfro, Oklahoma is among five states competing for the project and Ponca City would be the natural location in the state. According to Jan Jarrett, EDAB executive, Oklahoma’s financial package for the project is the best of the five states. Renfro said he felt positive about the project.

On another matter involving new industry, Renfro asked that action be delayed on the spec building until the Oklahoma Legislature votes on the capital bond improvement legislation. After this vote EDAB will find out if there is some funding available for the building.

Marketing Trips Slated

Renfro told about the effort to market the Conoco research space and told that all major universities had been to visit as well as other entities that have been brought in by the Oklahoma Department of Commerce.

A delegation from Ponca City will be going to Oak Ridge, Tenn., to see the research set-up there and learn if there are any possibilities to locate the same type of operation here. Oak Ridge does a number of projects for the U.S. government. He had been put in touch with this group by Dr. Guven Yaecintas, the director of technology at OSU.

Renfro also mentioned working with personnel from the newly created Oklahoma Technology Commercialization Center. The key role of this agency is to take technology developed in Oklahoma and get it to private enterprise.

Renfro announced plans for a local group to go to Washington, D.C., to call on Sen. Don Nickles, Sen. Jim Inhofe and Rep. Ernest Istook to ask for some assistance on locating industry here and taking advantage of the available space offered to the community for economic development.

Another possibility and probably “pie in the sky” according to Renfro is the location of the state’s training facility for CLEET, which would involve a $25 million capital investment.

He said this would also require passage of a bond issue by the legislature. Renfro said the site location group for the project has visited Ponca City and thought there was some potential. Ken Parr, Lee Evans and Clayton Johnson have been appointed to a committee to work on this project.

U.S. 177

Jarrett announced that it appeared that the right-of-way issue on U.S. 177 South is about to be resolved. A hearing is slated for early June and at that time it is expected that the three miles of right-of-way on the unfinished stretch of 177 near the Ponca Indian Reservation will be approved to be acquired by the ODOT.

Jarrett noted that Neal McCaleb, Secretary of Transportation, said the money has been set aside for the project and as soon as the right-of-way project is resolved the Department of Transportation is ready to finish the Super 2 highway between Ponca City and Stillwater.

Linda Brown, EDAB chairman, called for reports by Larry Buck on housing; George Paczkowski, Local Industry Council; Art Miers, on transportation, and Jarrett, for Workforce Development. Jarrett is working for a marriage of Enid, Stillwater and Ponca City for this project. He also reported that three local industries are planning expansions, which could add 70 to 90 employees.

During Jarrett’s report he told about receiving more and more inquiries about purchasing land in north Ponca City for commercial use. He said these “big box businesses” would enhance Ponca City as a regional shopping area. He said Ponca City needed to have a better mix of retailers. Jarrett noted that businesses such as Lowe’s draw a lot of regional business.

Gerald Hall, Regional representative for the Oklahoma Department of Commerce, announced a joint meeting of the Oklahoma and Kansas Departments of Commerce set for Friday in Blackwell.

Twister Relief Concert Set For Poncan Theatre on Sunday

Twister Relief ’99, a relief benefit concert, will be featured at the Poncan Theater on Sunday, at 4 p.m. Four bands will provide entertainment from 4 p.m. until 10 p.m., with all net proceeds going to the Salvation Army. Admission is $10 per ticket.

Additionally, patrons who bring items to the relief concert will be entered in a drawing for assorted door prizes. The concession stands will be open.

Also in local relief efforts, The Salvation Army of Ponca City continues to accept donations in the atrium area of Ponca Plaza. According to Barry Gabbert, president of Ponca Plaza Merchant Association, there is still a great need for bottled water and non-perishable food. Two trucks have already been loaded and sent from this location. Volunteers are still needed, from 5 p.m. to 8 p.m. each evening, to sort and load items collected during the day.

In addition, Gabbert reported the site received over $12,000 in cash and checks from May 4 through May 8. Teens from the American Legion Childrens Home, and Po-Hi girls softball team in addition to church groups and individuals are among 160 volunteers who have volunteered time at this site.

As of Sunday, the American Red Cross Disaster Operations Headquarters, in Oklahoma City, reported financially assisting 905 families and served more than 36,000 meals. The meals are being served by 37 mobile feeding units at seven service centers.

In a press release, the American Red Cross continues to meet the needs of tornado victims in several ways. First, disaster victims with verified needs can be issued disbursing orders (vouchers) to purchase emergency items they need most. These items include: new clothing, groceries, medical needs, lodging, basic household furnishings and supplies or for minor home repair. The service center is open from 9 a.m. to 6 p.m. Monday through Saturday and Sunday from noon to 6 p.m.

Additionally a toll-free hotline phone number has been established. Through the hotline, callers may be referred to the closest Red Cross Service Center, a feeding site, a shelter or nearby Red Cross chapter. Should a critical emergency exist, a caseworker can be sent directly to the caller for emergency disaster-related needs on the spot. Individuals and families affected by the Oklahoma Tornadoes can call 1-877-753-7324 from 7 a.m. to 11 p.m. every day.

The Red Cross is not a government agency and all disaster assistance is free. Assistance is made possible by voluntary donations of time and money from the American people. To help the victims of this and other disasters, contributions can be made to the Disaster Relief Fund by calling 1800-HELP NOW. Donations can also be sent to local Red Cross chapters.

Furthermore, the Tornado Insurance Information Center is operating out of the disaster assistance center at the First Baptist Church, in Moore. The consortium of insurance industry spokespersons are available for insurance-related information about recent storms and tornadoes. For more information call 405-793-2616 in Oklahoma City. The Insurance Information Institute regional office in Kansas City, Kan., can be reached at 913-768-4700.


Special Ceremony Names Cher Eagle as Princess

By KATHY ZEHR

News Staff Writer

Cher Eagle, Ponca Tribal member, was named Standing Bear Princess at a special ceremony at Native American Standing Bear Park on May 1.

Eagle said during an earlier interview she believes “serving as Standing Bear Princess will help keep me motivated to keep the Native American culture alive and will effect me in numerous other positive ways.”

Prior to the Standing Bear Powwow held at Native American Standing Bear Park in Ponca City in September 1998, Eagle, dressed in appropriate Ponca Tribal regalia, joined five other candidates representing the Osage Nation, Kaw Nation and the Otoe-Missouria, Pawnee, and Tonkawa tribes to vie for the title of Princess.

The young high school and college students, ages 16-21, answered questions, posed by a panel of judges, about current Native American issues, and preserving and observing tribal traditions and culture. Each candidate was also asked why she would like to serve as Standing Bear Princess.

Miss Eagle was selected as a runner-up for the 1998-99 Standing Bear Princess at that time, and was appointed on May 1, to complete the annual reign as Standing Bear Princess when former Princess Faren Revard from the Osage Nation recently resigned as the result of another commitment.

A $500 educational scholarship and some travel expenses are awarded to the Princess by the Native American Foundation of Ponca City.

Eagle will represent the Standing Bear Park and the Native American Foundation of Ponca City at various powwows throughout the United States and represent the park and foundation at area functions through the remainder of the 1998-99 princess reign.

Martha Alice Richter

Martha Alice Richter, longtime Ponca City resident, went to her heavenly home on Monday, May 10, 1999, at the St. Joseph Regional Medical Center. She was 74.

The funeral service will be Thursday, 2 p.m. at the Central Baptist Church with the Rev. John Waterloo, pastor, officiating, assisted by the Rev. Charles Richey, pastor Webb City Assembly of God. Burial will follow in Resthaven Memorial Park Cemetery under the direction of the Trout Funeral Home.

Born April 22, 1925, in Ardmore, she was the daughter of Arch Otto and Mary Alice Brumley Ratliff. She attended Deese rural schools near Ardmore. During World War II, Martha was employed at the defense plant at Anaheim, Calif., and later by Pruett’s Produce in Ardmore.

She moved to Ponca City in 1951 where she attended beauty school. She worked as a beautician at Fay’s Beauty Shop in the Dodge Drug Store for a number of years. She later worked as a waitress at the QuoVadis restaurant. Martha operated The Knit Shop until ill health forced her retirement.

On Aug. 19, 1955 she married Vernon L. Richter in Pawhuska. She was a member of Central Baptist Church. She enjoyed knitting, cooking, camping, and most of all being with her family.

She is survived by her husband, Vernon of Shawn Manor Nursing Home; a daughter and son-in-law, Alice F. And Don Newlin of Shidler; four granddaughters, Renee and her husband Dee Cooper of Grainola, Cindy and her husband Stan Bishop, Tracy Roe and Todd Roe all of Shidler, and Nicole and her husband John Hurd of Pawhuska; great-grandsons H.B. Bishop, Jake Cooper, Corey Hurd, Hayden Cooper, Luke Roe, and Landon Roe; great-granddaughters, Santana Cooper, Jordan Cooper, Brooklyn Bishop, Laney Roe, Bailey Hurd, and Macy Hurd; brother Raymond Ratliff of Ardmore; and numerous nieces, nephews, and cousins. She was preceded in death by her parents and 11 brothers and sisters.

Casket bearers will be Dee Cooper, Stan Bishop, Todd Roe, John Hurd, Dee Johnston and H. B. Bishop. Honorary casket bearers will be great-grandchildren.

Memorials may be made to Oklahoma Medical Research Foundation, 825 N.E. 13th, Oklahoma City, OK 73104.

The family will be at the home of Alice and Don Newlin in Shidler.

Banquet Begins Special Events For Local ALA

Cecelia Easky, past Oklahoma department president of the American Legion Auxiliary, and Helen Landis, past Unit 14 president, registered guests as they arrived for a banquet April 24 at the American Legion Post 14. The event was the beginning of Children and Youth weekend activities for the American Legion Children’s Home.

Donald Schulenberg, department chairman for children and youth, served as Master of Ceremonies. The invocation was given by Betty McElderry, auxiliary department of Oklahoma chaplain, and the Pledge of Allegiance was led by Lloyd Hurt, chairman of the American Legion Children’s Home committee. He also introduced the ALCH committee and staff.

The welcome address was given by Marvin Rider, first vice commander for Ponca City Post 14. Following the banquet Schulenberg introduced Arlene Failes, auxiliary department children and youth chairman, who, in turn, introduced Myrna Ludwig, Oklahoma ALA president. Jim Amerson, department commander for the American Legion, introduced Lawrence Roy, legion national executive committeeman, who introduced the speaker for the evening, John D. Morris of Houston, Texas, national vice commander.

The benediction was given by Charles Richmond, department of Oklahoma chaplain. A dance and fundraiser for the Children and Youth program followed the banquet.


Bingaman, Phillips Family Reunion Plans Are Named

The descendants of Samuel Lewis and Sarah Persilla Bingaman of Adams County, Pa., and James and Sophia (Aunspaugh or Anspaugh) Phillips, will join with the descendants of Eva Ermaline (Herndon) of Illinois and Martin Van Buren Eckley, who settled in Ripley, Okla. on Sunday, May 16 in the fellowship hall of First Church of the Nazarene, 1900 West Grand in Ponca City, beginning directly after the morning worship service. They plan to spend the rest of the day visiting and enjoying a covered dish dinner. The group is invited to attend the 9:30 Sunday School and the 10:45 morning worship service, and stay for the 6 p.m. evening service.

Those attending are being asked to bring pictures and genealogy to share. The event is planned to honor cousins from California — Fran (Birch) Murrey, her daughter, Elaine Parker and grandson, Gibson Parker, who are visiting in Ponca City with their Aunt Daisy (Smotone) Johnson and cousin, Francine Johnson. This is only the third time Mrs. Ron (Fran) Murrey has had to visit her Oklahoma relatives, and the first time for Mrs. Ray (Elaine) Parker and Gibson. They are hoping for the opportunity to meet all their relatives on the 16th. It has been 11 years since her last visit and 41 years prior to that.

For further information, please call Francine Johnson at 1-580-765-5630. For Sunday morning directions to the church, please call the First Church of the Nazarene, 765-6060. The church is located at 1900 West Grand Avenue in Ponca City.

Or you may write to Francine Johnson at 303 South Lake Street, Ponca City, OK 74601-5132, if you are unable to attend, and your letters will be shared.

Cat Golfers Eye Next Year

STILLWATER — Ponca City’s Wildcat golfers finished 10th in the Class 5A State Golf Tournament at Karsten Creek Tuesday, which is just find with coach Dan McGregor.

“We gained a lot of good experience,” McGregor said as the Cats battled a very talented field of high school golfers and a tough course. “I was pleased with our performance.”

The best thing is that most of that experience is coming back. Ponca City had one of the youngest teams at the state tournament Monday and Tuesday and returns all but one member of that team.

“If the kids work hard this summer, we can be a petty good team — maybe top five — next year,” McGregor said.

The Wildcats showed very good balance in the tournament. Freshman Matt Ellis, who shot an 80 in the drizzling rain during Tuesday’s final round, finished with a 54-hole total of 246. Senior Aaron Hill came in at 247, junior Matt Waddell 248 and sophomore Nathan Coats 249. Sophomore Louie Girardi, who struggled through the first two rounds, regained his eye to help Tuesday’s scoring with an 81 to finish at 261.

The Wildcats shot 325 on the final 18 holes to finish at 986.

That was far behind Jenks, who wound up with a 919 total. But no one else was close either. Jenks finished a state-record 39 strokes in front of second place Owasso (958).

The Trojans had four golfers among the top 10 players.

But all four of those players are seniors. Like most of the top teams in the state tournament, Jenks is senior dominated.

“Most of the time Jenks just reloads,” McGregor warned. But this time he may have spotted a weakness in the Trojans.

“Their fifth man didn’t help them all year. That may mean they don’t have the kind of depth they have had in the past. Or it may just mean they have some good young kid they didn’t play.”

But the bottom line is that McGregor feels the Cats have a chance to vie for the state title next year.

“And finishing 10th out of 32 teams is pretty good,” he notes.

Storm Victims Win

OKLAHOMA CITY (AP) — Mission accomplished.

The Oklahoma State and Oklahoma baseball teams came together Tuesday night to raise funds for tornado victims. A crowd of 10,172 showed up and at least $55,000 was raised, including ticket sales and a number of other stadium fund-raisers and concessions.

Oklahoma used five OSU errors to score six unearned runs and win, 10-9.

‘‘That’s one of the best feelings in my life, and I’m a horrible loser,’’ Oklahoma State coach Tom Holliday said. ‘‘We accomplished a lot tonight.’’

Tommy Whiteman hit a grand slam and Oklahoma held on after building a 7-0 lead.

Whiteman’s homer came in the first inning off David Rollandini, who was pulled after giving up a leadoff double in the second. The Sooners (28-25) took advantage of three errors to score three times in the second.

Oklahoma State (37-14) got three runs in the bottom of the second, two of them on a triple by Carlos Gautreaux, and moved within 7-5 with two more in the third.

The Cowboys added single runs in the fourth and sixth to tie the score before Oklahoma took the lead for good with three unearned runs in the seventh.

Lamont Matthews’ 21st homer, a two-run shot in the seventh, got OSU within 10-9 but that finished the scoring.

‘‘We did what we wanted to accomplish, we raised money for the victims of the tornadoes,’’ OU coach Larry Cochell said. ‘‘That’s the big thing.

‘‘The second thing, we won a ball game against a ranked team. Oklahoma State has been playing good baseball lately, and although they didn’t play great defense tonight, we can use a game like this for our confidence.’’

Oklahoma trails eighth-place Kansas State by a half-game in the race for the final berth into the Big 12 Tournament, with three games remaining at home against top-ranked Texas A&M this weekend.

Rocky Cherry (5-2) got the victory, pitching three innings of relief. Evan Greusel worked the final three innings for his first save. The loser was Ryan Herrmann (3-3), the third OSU pitcher.

Bobby Walters had three of Oklahoma’s 12 hits. Whiteman, Casey Bookout and Sean Murphy had two each. Kevin Lucas had three hits for Oklahoma State.

OSU is in fourth place in the league standings heading to Texas Tech, where a three-game sweep would move them into a third-place finish.

‘‘Friday night for us is critical,’’ Holliday said. ‘‘Tonight was critical for thousands of people who need help. Hopefully, the money will hit some pockets and make a difference.’’
