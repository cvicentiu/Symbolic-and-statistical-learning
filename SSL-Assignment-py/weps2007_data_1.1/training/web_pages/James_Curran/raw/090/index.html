Gates: AIDS fight 'falling behind'
25 years later, a pioneer looks back
AIDS experts gather for world's largest summit to combat disease
25 years later, a pioneer looks back
Updated 8/13/2006 11:14 PM ET E-mail | Save | Print |
By Steve Sternberg, USA TODAY
One patient stands out in Jim Curran's mind. He was a New York actor in his late 30s whose face and neck were covered with purplish spots that even stage makeup couldn't mask.

The lesions were caused by Kaposi's sarcoma, a rare skin cancer almost never seen in someone so young. It would soon be epidemic among young men, one of many illnesses that overwhelm people left defenseless by the disease now known as AIDS.

But just three days after the first report of the disease, on June 8, 1981, "We didn't exactly know what we were dealing with," Curran says. Although no one knew it at the time, he says, the New York case and a few others marked a transition "maybe for the history of mankind."

Twenty-five years later, AIDS has advanced the frontiers of science, provoked civil unrest, drained billions from national treasuries and undone decades of progress in the developing world. Still, cases and deaths continue to mount: 65 million people worldwide have been infected with HIV, the virus that causes AIDS; 25 million of them have died.

There from the start

This week, as thousands of scientists and activists gather in Toronto for the International Conference on AIDS, Curran is one of a handful now able to reflect from the beginning on the evolution of a devastating epidemic that has also changed the course of his family and professional life.

From his first trip to New York as the head of the Centers for Disease Control task force on the new disease to this week's conference, Curran, now dean of public health at Emory University and director of the university's Center for AIDS Research, has helped shape the response to the epidemic.

"Jim is one of the crucial visionaries of the HIV field," says Willard Cates of the non-profit public health organization Family Health International, formerly head of CDC's division of sexually transmitted diseases. "Jim has always been on the cutting edge of science and policy."

His efforts weren't always appreciated. In an oral history compiled in 1998 by the National Institutes of Health, Curran describes chaotic scenes at a conference in which protesters spit on him and a mass mailing by AIDS activists, who sent him 20,000 postcards with his face in a bull's-eye because they were impatient with the government response to AIDS.

Curran and other CDC experts made major strides. They helped explain how AIDS spreads, helped make the case that a virus causes the disease and wrote the first recommendations to limit its spread.

Curran says he hardly imagined he would play a leading role when he was a student at the University of Detroit High School or at Notre Dame. After graduating from the University of Michigan Medical School, Curran landed a job at the CDC.

By 1978, he was summoned to CDC headquarters in Atlanta to head STD research. His first decision was to pour $2 million into a study of a new hepatitis vaccine in gay men at STD clinics. That study, he says, put him in contact with gay doctors, who were dealing with sexually transmitted hepatitis in the gay community. It was a fateful decision, giving Curran contacts to call when the first cases of AIDS started popping up. Three days after the CDC issued its first bulletin on the disease, he flew to New York to begin investigating the epidemic, the first of 40 trips to the city he would make that year.

His daughter, Kathryn, 25, was 2 weeks old when the report appeared. She says she realized only later, when she read accounts of her father's activities, how busy he was during the early years of her life. She adds that his commitment hasn't waned with time. "You can kind of hear the passion, how much he cares and how frustrated he is with how politics affects what can be done."

The next generation

In 1995, Curran left the CDC for Emory University, where he helps train the next generation of public health experts. One of them may be his daughter, Kathryn, a student at the Johns Hopkins School of Public Health, who spent her summer working in a drug abuse research program in New Delhi. Although she frets about the pitfalls of going into a field "where everyone knows who my father is," she says she might chart a different path by working on HIV internationally.

Proud as he is of his daughter, Curran says it troubles him to think that he's a member of the last generation to go through life without AIDS. And in the NIH oral history of AIDS, Curran mentioned something else that nags at him. It emerged in his interviews with the actor in New York, who died in his presence.

Curran was struck by how much he and the patient had in common. Both men grew up near Detroit. They were about the same age. They attended Catholic high schools. While Curran went to Notre Dame and medical school, the patient attended Yale and become an actor in New York.

"The differences between us weren't great," Curran said. "He was gay, and I was straight."
