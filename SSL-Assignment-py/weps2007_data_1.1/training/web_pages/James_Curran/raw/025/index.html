Canadian Soldiers Buying Land

Is anyone else disturbed by this image. Last night CTV aired a report showing Canadian soldiers with millions of dollars in Afghani money paying off land owners for the loss of their lands to build a new road in the Kandahar region.

There were scenes of cash in brown envelopes. Most disturbing.

Questions:

1. Was the money supplied by the Canadian government?

2. Don't they have chequing accounts in Afghanistan? Why cash?

3. If it was the Canadian government, why are we paying for the expropriation of lands that roads had to built on to "defend the Afghan people against Taliban insurgents"? Isn't paying for the road to be built enough of a nice gesture?

4. Why wouldn't the Afghan government be responsible for the expropriation of lands in their own country? Who gets "title documents" for the road?

5. Later, can we charge tolls on this road like the 407ETR?

6. How many homeless people can we feed with a million dollars in Canada?

The Conswervative Version of Kyoto

The Conservatives continue to throw the manure around when it comes to issues like the IT scandal and Kyoto. Many of the commercials keep harping on the "13 year" inactivity theme. Canadians should learn some of the facts regarding the implementation of the Kyoto Protocol, so I've thrown a few together via my friends over at Wikipedia.


On December 17, 2002, Canada ratified the treaty that came into force in February 2005, requiring it to reduce emissions to 6% below 1990 levels between 2008-2012. While numerous polls have shown support for the Kyoto protocol at around 70%,[17][18] there is still some opposition, particularly by some business groups,[19] non-governmental climate scientists and energy concerns, using arguments similar to those being used in the US. There is also a fear that since US companies will not be affected by the Kyoto Protocol that Canadian companies will be at a disadvantage in terms of trade. In 2005, the result was limited to an ongoing "war of words", primarily between the government of Alberta (Canada's primary oil and gas producer) and the federal government. There were even fears that Kyoto could threaten national unity, specifically with regard to Alberta [citation needed]. As of 2003, the federal government claimed to have spent or committed 3.7 billion dollars on climate change programmes.[20] By 2004, CO2 emissions had risen to 27 per cent above 1990 levels (which compares unfavorably to the increase in emissions by the United States of 16 per cent by that time).[21]

After January 2006, the Liberal government was replaced by a Conservative minority government under Stephen Harper, who previously has expressed opposition to Kyoto, and in particular to the plan to participate in international emission trading. Rona Ambrose, who replaced Stéphane Dion as the environment minister, has since endorsed some types of emission trading, and indicated interest in international trading.[22]

On April 25, 2006, Ambrose announced that Canada would have no chance of meeting its targets under Kyoto, and would look to participate in U.S. sponsored Asia-Pacific Partnership on Clean Development and Climate. "We've been looking at the Asia-Pacific Partnership for a number of months now because the key principles around [it] are very much in line with where our government wants to go," Ambrose told reporters.[23] On May 2, 2006, it was reported that environmental funding designed to meet the Kyoto standards has been cut, while the Harper government develops a new plan to take its place.[24] As the co-chair of UN Climate Change Conference in Nairobi in November 2006, Canada and its government received embarrassing criticism from environmental groups and from other governments for its climate change positions[25]

Dion, the previous environment minister and current Leader of the Opposition, has stated that were he to form the government subsequent to Spring 2007, Canada would not be likely to be able to meet its Phase I Kyoto targets by 2012, but would try to, and then meet all later targets.[26] However, due to his marked lack of progress during his time as environment minister, his credibility has been heavily criticized by the current Canadian government.

Canada's current government has introduced legislation to set mandatory emissions targets for industry, but it will not take effect until an estimated 2050. The government has since begun working with opposition parties to improve the legislation.

A private member's bill,[27] has been put forth by Pablo Rodriguez, Liberal Member of Parliament for the riding of Honoré—Mercier. The bill aims to force the minority government of Stephen Harper to "ensure that Canada meets its global climate change obligations under the Kyoto Protocol." With the support of the Liberals, the New Democratic Party and the Bloc Québécois, and with the current minority situation, the bill passed on 14 February 2007 with a vote of 161-113,[28] giving the government 60 days to form a detailed plan of action. The government has flatly refused to abide by the bill, which may spark a constitutional crisis, lawsuit, or non-confidence motion once the bill becomes law, as it is expected to do. [29]

Enforcement

If the Enforcement Branch determines that an Annex I country is not in compliance with its emissions targets, then that country is required to make up the difference plus an additional 30 percent. In addition, that country will be suspended from making transfers under an emissions trading program.[9]





The Cons would like to have Canadians thinking and believing that we sat around and didn't do squat since 1993. Thae FACT, the protocol wasn't even ratified until 2002, and did not dome into force until February 2005 - just months prior to an election call.

The moral of the story is that Stephane Dion had 10 months to make sure, as Environment Minister, Canada was meeting its goals. 10 months. Not 13 years.

I would hardly call the Conservative plan to get "active" on the environment by 2050 a good alternative.

Like it or not, we are a signatory to the Kyoto Accord along with 160 other members of the global community. Opting out of Kyoto at this point would be ridiculous, embarrassing and a source of shame for all Canadians.

What would we opt out of next? NATO?
