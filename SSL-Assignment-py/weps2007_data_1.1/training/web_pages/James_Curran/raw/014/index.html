James [DS.8] was a broom maker.1 He worked for Weber, Koenig, the Columbia Box Company, and possibly other employers.1

The people in this photo are (from left to right) John Stein [DS.8.1S], James Joseph Curran [DS.8], and Al Jewson.

 

Circa 1889, James married Martha Froelke [DS.8S] (b. in IL in Feb 1871; d. c. 1947 in Moro IL) in St. Louis. Her father was William Froelke; her mother was Dora Roelich. William died in the early 20's. Dora died in the late 20's.1

William Froelke was born in Ost Friesland, Germany where he was a farmer. He spoke the Friesland dialect. His grandson, George William Curran [DS.8.3] described it as "almost English". George William [DS.8.3] has been to Friesland and understood the dialect "with some effort".1 Dora Roelich was born in Essen, Germany. She, too, grew up on a farm.1

William and Dora moved from Germany first to Bunker Hill, Illinois, and then to St. Louis, Missouri. Bunker Hill is about 45 miles northeast of St. Louis.1

George William Curran [DS.8.3]
Born 6 Apr 1895 in St. Louis, Missouri.1
Died at 4:52 pm, Friday 12 Jun 1981
Cemetery: Sunset Burial Park, Affton, Missouri
Social Security No. 489-09-612319

George [DS.8.3] was the third child of James Joseph Curran [DS.8] and Martha Froelke [DS.8S]. George [DS.8.3] was probably named after his father's oldest brother [DS.1] who was killed in the Civil War in 1864.

George [DS.8.3] attended Yeatman High School. It was located in north St. Louis, Missouri. About five years after graduation from high school, he enlisted in the U.S. Navy Reserve as Apprentice Seaman # 123-53-76. George [DS.8.3] was "enrolled" 22 May 1918 at St. Louis, enlisted 25 May 1918, and honorably discharged at Great Lakes, Illinois on 30 September 1921. At the time of his discharge, George [DS.8.3] was 5 feet 8 inches in height, weighed 151 pounds, and had blue eyes, brown hair, and a ruddy complexion.

On 31 July 1918, just two months after joining the U.S. Navy, George [DS.8.3] married Corinne Margaret Williams [DS.8.3S1] (11 Mar 1895 — 28 Dec 1957). At that time, George [DS.8.3] was a Presbyterian while Corinne [DS.8.3S1] was a Catholic. They were married at Immaculate Conception Church in St. Louis with the Rev. P. D. O'Connor officiating. None of their children, however, is a Catholic. 

[At the right is a photo of  Corinne — date unknown.]

Corinne's father was Henry Williams, a bricklayer. Henry died circa 1916. Corinne's mother was Julia Hogan. Julia died circa 1930.

Corinne had a sister, Irene Julia Williams [Ref 1] (died c. Sep 1958), and a brother, Roy Williams (died Dec 1945). Irene married Sherman Ballentine (9 Dec 1889—Mar 1972), a truck driver. They had one child, Ross.

Shortly after leaving the Navy, George [DS.8.3] founded the Kerry Coal Company in North St. Louis, Missouri. The company was incorporated in 1922. In the 1930's the company was moved to South St. Louis. The name was later changed to Curran Coal and Appliance Co. The coal yard was located at Spring and Itaska and the appliance company was located at Gravois and California. He sold coal, wholesale and retail, and installed heating equipment: furnaces, conversion burners, boilers, steam, and water heaters.

With some income ensured, George [DS.8.3] then continued his education via night school. He studied at City Law and Commerce College (or City Law and Finance College). George [DS.8.3] received a law degree in 1932 or 1933 and passed the Missouri Bar examination in the same year. Following a familiar pattern, George [DS.8.3] would go to his bedroom, close the door, and study his books until late at night.

The photo at the right was taken on 30 Jan 1930 when George was 34 years old.

In 1954, George William Curran (DS.8.3) ran for the U.S. Congress in his St. Louis district. He won the Republican primary, but was defeated by the Democratic incumbent, Lenore Sullivan.

Sometime after 1957, George William Curran [DS.8.3] married secondly Matelden "Mat" Kautzner Schultz [DS.8.3S2] (b. 13 Mar 1905), the widow of Charlie Schultz.

George [DS.8.3] was admitted as an Attorney and Counselor of the Supreme Court of the United States on 30 August 1960.

In 1963, George [DS.8.3] changed the name of his company to Curran Investment Company and concentrated on real estate. Eleven apartment buildings now stand where the coal yard was located, including the Russell Curran and the Cora Curran apartments at 3805 and 3821 Itaska.

George William Curran [DS.8.3] visited Chicago frequently "in the early 20's and 30's". He and his family used the expression "drive up to Chicago to get a cup of coffee". He met his uncle (my great grandfather) Dan Curran [DS.4] several times in Chicago. He also met my grandfather, Tom Curran [DS.4.5], and Tom's brother, Jim Curran [DS.4.7]. George [DS.8.3] said that most of the Currans had red hair. He himself once had red hair and had long been known by the nickname "red". George (DS.8.3) told me that he remembered first meeting my mother, Laura Margaret Curran [DS.4.5.7], when she was still a "little girl".

On Saturday, May 5, 1928, George Curran [DS.8.3] (at the age of 33) pitched a no-hit baseball game in which he struck out 16 opponents. George [DS.8.3] pitched for the Neighborhood Bakers against the American Centrals. Both teams were in the Commercial League. The Bakers won 9-0 while the Centrals got no hits, no runs, and not one ball to the outfield. George [DS.8.3] walked only one batter.13

At the age of 81, George was still pitching for the Sunday Morning Old-Timers. He played every Sunday morning on No 6 Diamond in Fairgrounds Park, St. Louis.

Together with George Ober, George W. Curran [DS.8.3] founded the Apollos (at the Missouri Athletic Association), the Steuben Society, and Liederkrantz. George also belonged to a German Singing Society, the Harmonie Singing Society. Apparently, George [DS.8.3] loved group singing.

When I met George [DS.8.3] circa 1980, he was still a practicing attorney. Previously, he had been a "Provisional Judge" for 12 years. When he first started to practice law, he had many German-speaking immigrants as clients because he spoke German. He had grown up on a farm with his German-speaking, maternal grandparents, William Froelke and Dora Roelich. They spoke the dialect of Friesland, which George described as "almost English". George [DS.8.3] visited Friesland and said that he understood the dialect with some effort.

George [DS.8.3] told me that he preferred to write wills rather than handle other legal work. As executor, he would typically get a fee equal to five percent of the estate. This would be much more than he could charge his clients for other work. Of course, the major part of the fee was collected by him personally only when he outlived his clients.

On his 85th birthday, George [DS.8.3] was pleasantly surprised to receive a birthday card from Rosylyn and Jimmy Carter.

The last home address of George Curran [DS.8.3] was 2738 Utah, St. Louis, Missouri. Private telephone: 314/PR3-7627.

In 1968, his business at Gravois and California was destroyed in an explosion caused by a gas leak. His last business address was 3001 Arsenal, St. Louis, Missouri. Business telephone: 314/664-8600.

In the summer of 1980, George [DS.8.3] wrote to tell me that he had changed his address indefinitely to

    Missouri Baptist Hospital
    3015 N. Ballas Road, St. Louis, Missouri 63131.

At his request, he was allowed to return to his home and died there at 4:52 pm, 12 Jun 1981.

His grandson George Michael "Mick" Curran, Jr. (DS.8.3.1.1) confirms that his grandfather was a good baseball player and a good singer. Sometime in the late 1960's the entire St. Louis Curran family played in the weekly Sunday morning baseball game with George, including his two sons "Mike" (DS.8.3.1) and "Bill" (DS.8 3.2) and his three grandsons "Mick" (DS.8.3.1.1), "Russ" (DS.8.3.1.2) and "Jim" (DS.8.3.1.3). Mick said he went 0 for 3 against his grandfather. The entire Curran family would eat fish at the Bavarian Inn in St. Louis every Friday night. George would teach his grandchildren German and teach them the words to all his favorite songs like "Harrigan", "Good Night Irene", and "The Garbage Gentlemen's Ball - Phew" and always would sing "I had a hat when I came in and I'll have a hat when I go home" when he would leave our home. Mick confirms that George would sing at every opportunity; if he was given a microphone he would do twenty minutes.

Russell Jerome "Russ" Curran
(29 Sep 1926 — 22 Feb 1946).

Russ [DS.8.3.3] served in the U.S. Navy during World War II. Russ [DS.8.3.3] served on a rocket ship at Okinawa and many other landings. He fought fires started by Kamikaze attacks.

Although I never met Russ, I probably passed within a hundred yards of his ship as I was heading toward the beach on Okinawa early on the morning of Easter Sunday, April 1, 1945. The barrage of rockets fired just as I passed was very impressive.

Russ was the last of the Curran brothers to return home from World War II, he returned on leave from San Diego on the afternoon of February 5, 1946. His father George William Curran (DS.8.3) gave a huge party celebrating the successful return of his three sons on February 16, 1946 at the Mountain View Inn in St. Louis. The following week Russ died tragically in an automobile accident in St. Louis.
