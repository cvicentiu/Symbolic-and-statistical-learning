Some in GOP Criticize Bush's Wedding Ban

By Susan Montoya Bryan
The Associated Press

A leader of a GOP group that supports gay rights criticized President Bush's proposal for a constitutional amendment banning gay marriage, calling it a "recipe for disaster." The Log Cabin Republicans worry that Bush's stance on same-sex marriage risks alienating the 1 million gays and lesbians who voted for him in 2000.

"The federal marriage amendment is a big problem for us, and it should be for any person who supports equality. The constitution should not be used as a political football," Mark Mead, the group's political director, told about 25 prospective members late Thursday at the Albuquerque Press Club. Mead said he was "deeply disappointed" that same-sex marriage was the first issue Bush talked about the day after gearing up for his re-election drive. Mead was in Albuquerque to help in the creation of New Mexico's first Log Cabin chapter. The group has offices in Washington, D.C., and 20 other states.

Across town, more than 120 same-sex partners packed the Metropolitan Community Church for a special meeting late Thursday. Many had received marriage certificates from the Sandoval County Clerk's Office last Friday only to be told later that day they were invalid. The crowd talked with members of the Coalition for Equality New Mexico and Lambda Legal, a New York-based gay-rights advocacy group. The discussion centered on the couples' options, New Mexico's political environment and ways people could protect their rights. The coalition plans to hold similar town hall meetings for same-sex couples around the state, said Linda Siegle, a coalition lobbyist.

Patrick Killen, an organizer who has worked for the state GOP and served as political director for John Sanchez gubernatorial campaign in 2002, said it's time to broaden the party's ranks. "There's a lot of hate in the Republican Party and I think people need to try to put emotions aside and recognize there are so many different people in this country and that we need to work together," he said. He added that the party needs a broad base of support to win elections. "The Republican Party will win by inclusion, not subtraction," Killen said.

John Dendahl, a former state party chair, agreed. "The Log Cabin Republicans have done a nice job of providing a place in the party for a particular group of people and if that can be done in New Mexico, I think that would be a constructive step for the party." But state Sen. Rod Adair, R-Roswell, said he had doubts the group would change much in New Mexico, especially when it comes to the debate over gay marriage.
