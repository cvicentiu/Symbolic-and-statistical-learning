Analysis: Economy Tops for Bush, Koizumi
By United Press International, 2/19/2002 2:21:43 AM

TOKYO, Feb. 15 (UPI) -- Foremost among those with fingers crossed for President George W. Bush next week is Tokyo's international and domestic business community, hoping his visit to Japan will produce more results than his father's that history will always mark as "the one when the president got sick on the Japanese prime minister."

The present President Bush is scheduled to arrive Feb. 17 for a two-day visit that is sure to include a stiff dose of "giatsu," the Japanese word for external pressure. Bush is expected to tell Prime Minister Junichiro Koizumi that he needs to get the world's second-largest economy going before a tidal wave of bank and company failures hits country and global finance.

Bush will be armed with a White House report that warns the situation in Japan is worsening with deflation gripping the country, banks faced with a growing mountain of bad debt and unemployment at a record high.

Forbes Global magazine said in its present edition, "You can no longer safely shrug off Japan's economic crisis. It just might drag the world into a depression.

"The world -- and even the previously sanguine Japanese -- is now catching on to the fact that Japan's 12-year slump has deteriorated into a full-blown crisis, threatening a wild global ride. Falloffs in various indicators in the world's second-largest economy resemble the plunge of countries like the U.S. into the Great Depression of the 1930s."

Bush seems certain to echo the words of Treasury Secretary Paul O'Neill, who warned in Tokyo during a January visit that Japan's markets are waiting for decisive action, that the government's policy of a weaker yen to encourage exports "cannot improve productivity or fix performing loans."

According to U.S. Ambassador Howard Baker, the president was also expected to express U.S. thanks to Japan for its forthright support in the war against terrorism, for organizing and hosting the international conference on rebuilding Afghanistan and to discuss regional matters such as U.S.-North Korean relations and the economic and political rise of China.

Baker said in remarks in late January that Bush and Koizumi had become friends as a result of four earlier meetings and "they do indeed have a good rapport."

The meeting will come at a time when Koizumi's once-spectacular popularity rating has fallen to more mortal levels following his firing of politically popular Makiko Tanaka, his sharp-tongued and sometimes undiplomatic foreign minister, and his failure to halt the slide in the economy. Most economic indicators have deteriorated since Koizumi took office in April 2001 and the Tokyo stock market dipped this month to an 18 year low.

One U.S. professor quipped the charismatic Koizumi should be called Mr. NATO -- for "no action, talk only."

While in Japan, Bush is to make a courtesy call on Japan's Emperor Akihito and is expected to address the upper house of the Diet.

It's an ambitious agenda at a critical time for both leaders. With any luck, the U.S. president will avoid the embarrassment suffered 10 years ago by George Sr.

In January 1992, the first President Bush arrived heading a team of traveling salesmen, including the Big Three automaker chiefs, and departed with apologies for his indiscretion.

Hit by intestinal flu but trying to soldier through a state banquet, Bush threw up, pitch forward into the lap of Prime Minister Kiichi Miyazawa, then sank to the floor in the middle of formal evening. To his credit, Bush was reported to have told Miyazawa, "Why don't you roll me under the table and I'll sleep it off while you finish dinner?" First Lady Barbara Bush delivered her husband's remarks while the president recovered.

This article is by Patrick Killen.
