Legalize Marijuana?

Question 7 on the November ballot goes thusly:

AN ACT relating to marijuana; providing that a person who is 21 years of age or older is exempt from arrest, civil or criminal penalty, seizure or forfeiture of assets, discipline by any state or local licensing board and state prosecution for the possession, use and transfer of one ounce or less of marijuana and the use and possession of marijuana paraphernalia under certain circumstances...

In libertarian-minded Nevada, this measure would seem to have a good chance at passage. It certainly would make no sense to send someone to jail for having an ounce of less of marijuana on them - and since we wouldn't rationally send a person to jail on that, why make it illegal at all? Additionally, it is well within the American tradition to not interefere unduly in the life choices of others.

On the other hand, there is this worry: We've strayed so far down the road to permissiveness that we've actually become rather licentious as a nation. Do we really want to add fuel to the hedonistic fire by legalising marijuana? There is also this: what is to prevent a dealer from keeping only an ounce at a time on himself? It would be rather easy to stash a pound close by and just continually re-fill the pocket as ounce after ounce is sold to whomever asks - including minors.

As of right now, I'd have to say that I'm undecided on this issue. I did vote to legalise medicinal marijuana, but I'm not sure that now is the right time to legalise non-medicinal possession.

Thank you for discussing our initiative on your blog! I just wanted to take a moment to address your concerns.

Our initiative recognizes that Nevada's marijuana laws do not work, and it's time to adopt a new strategy. Despite over 10,000 marijuana arrests in Nevada during the last three years alone, marijuana remains widely available and easy to get. The criminal marijuana market is a "free-for-all" controlled by violent gangs and drug dealers, with no oversight or safeguards to speak of.

In contrast, we support the taxation and regulation of marijuana sales. Marijuana will continue to be widely available – whether permitted or not – so a sensible alternative is to place it into a tightly controlled and regulated market. Once we regulate the marijuana market, we create the opportunity to tax it and establish safeguards that are currently non-existent.

You raise concerns about "dealers," but under Question 7, the only entities allowed to sell marijuana will be retailers licensed by the Nevada Department of Taxation. Licensed retailers would have to buy their marijuana from state-licensed manufacturers. Only adults over the age of 21 will be allowed to purchase up to one ounce of marijuana from state-licensed retailers; if an individual possesses over an ounce of marijuana at any given time, they'll be breaking the law and subject to the state's existing penalties in this area.

Regarding sales to minors, Question 7 establishes a “We Card” program for marijuana. If you aren’t 21 or older, or do not have proper ID, you won’t be allowed to enter an establishment that sells marijuana – much less buy it. Marijuana use by anyone younger than 21, driving under the influence of marijuana, and smoking marijuana in public would all remain illegal. In fact, the maximum penalty for any adult who gives or sells marijuana to a minor would DOUBLE, as would the maximum penalty for anyone who kills someone while driving under the influence of alcohol, marijuana, or any drug.

Retailers who sell marijuana couldn’t be located within 500 feet of a school or place of worship. No establishment that sells alcohol or allows gaming will be allowed to sell marijuana – nor would gas stations, convenience stores, grocery stores, or nightclubs. Question 7 doesn’t put marijuana in the local 7-11 – in fact, it specifically forbids it.

Once we regulate marijuana, we can tax it. Half of the tax revenue generated by Question 7 would go toward drug/alcohol education and treatment; the other half would go to the state's general fund, potentially generating millions of dollars for our most pressing needs – like improving education, fixing roads, and dealing with growth.

We believe that after decades of marijuana laws that don’t accomplish what they were meant to – yet cause real societal harm by financing violent gangs and drug dealers, destroying the lives of otherwise law-abiding Nevadans, and wasting precious police resources – it’s time for a new approach.

Voters can find out more about Question 7 at our Web site, http://www.regulatemarijuana.org. We appreciate your consideration of the initiative!

Mark:

The entities supplying retailers will be wholesalers that are licensed by the Nevada Department of Taxation. Licensed wholesalers will be Nevada businesses that cultivate, prepare, package, and sell marijuana to retailers or other wholesalers, but not to the general public.

Regarding your states' rights concerns, the initiative specifically prohibits the transport of marijuana outside of the State of Nevada. As for Uncle Sam, we believe it is within Nevada’s power as a state to pass this law. According to the FBI and the U.S. Sentencing Commission, 99% of marijuana arrests occur at the state and local level -- this is clearly a state issue. Furthermore, back when Nevada’s medical marijuana initiative was on the ballot, federal authorities threatened to shut it down if it passed. It’s been in effect now for over five years, and the federal government's threats never materialized.
