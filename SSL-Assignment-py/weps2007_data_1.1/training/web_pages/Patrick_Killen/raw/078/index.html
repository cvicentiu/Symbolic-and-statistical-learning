VOTE 2002 An Early Look

Three Republican candidates are trying to succeed Gov. Gary Johnson, who leaves office at the end of the year. We caught up with the candidates recently leading up to the June 4 primary election.

Today: state Rep. John Sanchez. Tuesday: Lt. Gov. Walter Bradley. Wednesday: state Rep. Rob Burpo.

During a campaign briefing on taxes, gubernatorial candidate John Sanchez pulls out a yellow legal pad from a desk drawer but only jots down a few notes. Instead, his campaign manager, Jay McCleskey, asks most of the questions of a small-business expert and keeps track of ideas during the meeting at Sanchez's Downtown business office.

Sanchez, a North Valley Republican, chimes in from time to time as he nibbles on popcorn and sips from a can of Coke.

After the one-hour briefing, it is obvious Sanchez absorbed every word that was said. He should. The briefing practically mirrored the campaign speeches he has given for months now.

The point of such briefings, Sanchez said afterward, is to test the waters for his campaign theme. He has held similar briefings on water and health care.

"We want to glean as much information as we can from experts," Sanchez said. "But we also want to make certain whether or not we are on message. Are we going in the right direction with our policies?"

As the front-runner among the three Republican candidates, Sanchez has some buzz around his campaign. Some powerful Republicans like his chances of going on to take on Democrat Bill Richardson in the November election.

But the knock on Sanchez, so far, is his inexperience in politics and state government.

As he travels the state, Sanchez sells himself as having the business know-how to get things done in Santa Fe, a theme outgoing Gov. Gary Johnson rode into the Roundhouse.

During a luncheon at Sadie's Dining Room last week, Sanchez was practically preaching to the choir as he addressed a meeting of the Associated Builders and Contractors Inc.

Sanchez pointed out that general contractors struggle every day to make a profit. He owns a roofing company.

The business community is a key constituency for Sanchez, especially during the primary, when only the most active voters show up at the polls to pick a nominee for governor.

To win over business leaders and voters Sanchez has to persuade them that he can do a better job of cutting taxes, reforming schools and improving the climate than the other two candidates.

Yet he also has to persuade voters he is up to speed on issues he would tackle if elected governor.

During last week's tax briefing Sanchez proved he has a general grasp of the issue, as it affects small business, during an exchange with Garth Simms, state director of the National Federation of Independent Business.

Simms, a lobbyist for small businesses, slid an inch-thick packet of economic studies across Sanchez's desk. He told Sanchez to leave the details of tax reform for his staff to study.

Simms then gave Sanchez a broader perspective of how to overhaul the business climate in the state.

Don't just cut taxes on services and replace them with taxes somewhere else, Simms said.

Phase in tax cuts to give the state time to recruit new businesses, he emphasized.

And use the office of the governor to persuade local governments to cut the red tape that strangles businesses, he said.

"You've got to change attitudes," Simms, a veteran of Roundhouse politics, told Sanchez.

Sanchez sometimes related what he was hearing from Simms to what he has experienced as a businessman.

For example, when Simms complained about how workers compensation reforms have been watered down by unfavorable court decisions, Sanchez scooted to the edge of his leather desk chair.

He said his company, at one time, was paying nearly as much money to provide workers compensation insurance for its roofers, a high risk group of workers, as it devoted to salaries.

While Sanchez's relies primarily on like-minded experts who can help him craft a campaign message that resonates with primary-election voters, he said he also encourages different viewpoints.

"Another perspective is crucial," he said.

For example, he recently met with public school teachers who weren't necessarily on board with his plans for education.

"I hope to be receptive as governor," Sanchez said. "I've learned that sometimes you just have to sit and listen to people."

THE SANCHEZ FILE

Bio: 39, Republican. State legislator since 2001.

Occupation: Owns Right-Way Roofing.

Family: Wife, Debra, and two daughters.

Campaign slogan: Fighting for the American Dream.

Transportation: Ford F-350, Toyota Campaign war chest: Declined to reveal the cash he has raised.

Key supporters: Colin McMillan, former co-chairman of President Bush's New Mexico campaign; Dona Ana District Attorney Susana Martinez; state Rep. Larry Larranaga; George Yates, a Roswell oilman; Col. Allen Weh, held fund-raisers for Bush and U.S. Rep. Heather Wilson.

Number of paid staff: Four (Jay McCleskey, campaign manager; Rebecca Beiler, finance director; Marta Kramer, scheduler; and Patrick Killen, political director.)

Campaign consultants: John Maddox, the Maddox Group (general); Brad Todd, National Media (media); Nicole Fink, Public Opinion Strategies (polling); Dan Hazelwood, Targeted Creative Communications (mail); and Heidi Fuller and Stacey Pawlowski (finance).

Upside: Despite a short political career, Sanchez proved he can run an effective campaign when he beat former state House Speaker Raymond Sanchez to win a seat in the Legislature. He followed that up this year when he got a majority of delegate votes at the Republican pre-primary convention.
