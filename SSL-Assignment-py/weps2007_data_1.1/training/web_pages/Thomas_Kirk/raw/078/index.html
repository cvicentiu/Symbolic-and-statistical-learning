Blind hunter Gilley harvests deer

By Mike Giles / outdoors writer

Seventeen-year-old Kainen Gilley was just a small boy when Kirk Thomas had an accident that changed Kirk’s life. Though they were many years and worlds apart, that accident and fate would later bring them together. Thomas, a mountain of a man, was a star football player and the consummate outdoorsman. He played ball with a fire rarely seen among athletes nowadays. After a freak hunting accident left him paralyzed from the waist down, Thomas was forced to look at life from a different perspective.

While going through all of the emotions that most accident victims experience, Thomas’ friends picked him up one day and carried him to the woods. Instead of looking at what he couldn’t do, they focused on the possibilities. As a result of that accident and experience, Wheelin’ Sportsmen was founded. Thomas became the organization’s CEO. Later the organization became national when it merged with the National Wild Turkey Federation (NWTF).

When Gilley’s dad, Brian, found out that Thomas and the NWTF was hosting a disabled hunt out of Bass Pro Shops in Pearl, he got on the phone and called regional director Steve Thomas, Kirk’s younger brother.

Thomas quickly invited the Gilley’s to the hunt and Kainen and Brian were both excited. Former NFL player Scott Adams had figured out a way for blind hunters to harvest deer and he was scheduled to be at the hunt as a guide. Steve set Adams up to work with Kainen and his dad throughout the event leading up to the hunt.



Early start



Gilley has been going to the woods with his dad since he was six years old and asked to go on a hunt. However, he had never tried to harvest his own deer.

“What really got Kainen hooked was a deer hunt that we made where he called up three deer with a grunt call,” related the proud Dad. “Just figuring out how to do it was the thing that held us up. When we realized that Kainen might be able to shoot his own deer, we wanted to try.” Young Gilley was born blind, with a mild case of cerebral palsy also. If he could see, then you probably would never know he had cerebral palsy. The disease hasn’t stopped him from doing anything.

On Friday before the hunt Kainen and Brian Gilley met Scott Adams at the Bass Pro Shops headquarters and then went to the rifle range. Scott had a special tripod stand that the rifle barrel rested on. Kainen held the rifle to his shoulder and his dad stood behind and told him where to move the crosshairs. When the crosshairs were in the right place, Brian would tap Kainen on the shoulder as a signal to fire. During the trip to the range they dry fired several times, before firing live ammo at the target. Within a few shots they were pumped and primed to try it on a live deer.

When they arrived at the stand Adams asked Gilley what he wanted to shoot. “I want to go for a buck, but I’ll take anything that comes in,” responded Kainen. Kainen advised that they “saw” about thirty deer, but no bucks. After a couple hours it was about time to call it a day so Adams picked out a deer. Brian Gilley told Kainen where to move the cross hairs. Once Brian told Kainen it was about time, Kainen put his finger on the trigger and waited. The tap on the shoulder was the moment he’d been waiting for and he squeezed off the trigger like a seasoned hunter. “When dad tapped me on the shoulder I pulled the trigger and BAM- Sausage!” exclaimed Gilley!



Moment of truth



Gilley had indeed harvested a 160 pound doe. He continued to relate his excitement through his story about the hunt: “Those deer were eating and what she didn’t know was that I was waiting up in the deer stand while she ate her last meal. I’ve been hunting a lot, but this was my first deer and it was really a blast.” Gilley harvested the doe with a 7 mm magnum rifle.

Gilley is wise beyond his years and spoke from personal experience about someone that might have a disability. “I’d just say that if you can’t see, or if you have some sort of disability or anything, don’t let it stop you. I don’t and my friends don’t either.”

Just imagine the possibilities when you have a little help from your friends. How can you change the world - one person at a time. “When you volunteer and help out, you can be their eyes, legs, feet, hands and you take away their disability,” stated Kirk Thomas. “Although I can’t reach every disabled person in the world, my goal is to touch as many people in person as I can.” Yes, just imagine what the world would be like if Thomas had not had his accident. And now imagine the possibilities that he has introduced to the world.

If you don’t believe one person can make a difference, just ask Kainen Gilley. He’ll tell you what a blessing it was to meet Thomas and Adams and to be able to attend a NWTF Wheelin’ Sportsmen hunt. If you know of someone who might want to volunteer, or get involved with the disabled hunts, then contact the National Wild Turkey Federation at their Web site to obtain phone numbers and information.
