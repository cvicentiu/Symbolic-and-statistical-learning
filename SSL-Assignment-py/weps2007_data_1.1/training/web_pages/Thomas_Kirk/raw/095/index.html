Connecticut Department of Mental Health changes course in 2006
(February 2006 Issue)

By Jennifer Chase Esposito

Connecticut's work began in 2005, but 2006 should reap the benefits of its efforts as Connecticut's Department of Mental Health tries to systematically move toward a "recovery-oriented system of care." Three newly implemented programs will help patients navigate Connecticut's DMH system easier, manage their illness better and return to the workforce.

"This is Connecticut's overarching goal. We're not trying to make a slight change, we're trying to overhaul the system," says Thomas Kirk, Ph.D., commissioner of the state's DMH.

Moving toward a recovery-oriented system is a major shift, says Kirk. "I take Lipitor for cholesterol issues" for example, he says, "and these medications and diet help me to manage my condition so I can go on to do normal things. That [this] has not been the case with psychological issues, it has continued the stigma; it's the wrong theme to continue.

"It's more important to help patients move on to a higher quality of life, preferably in the community, rather than think 'I have a disability and will never have a quality of life,'" he says.

At the forefront of Connecticut's programming are its "Centers of Excellence." According to Kirk, the centers reside within hospitals in New Haven and Newark. There, mental health patients are paired with peers - who also have psychiatric disabilities - who have been added to the hospitals' psychological staff and to the patients' "team" of doctors. The peers often are sent to wait with patients as they await their appointments - a seemingly simple act which, according to Kirk, helps keep down the stress levels of people awaiting their appointments.

Peers of patients at the hospitals have gone through special training. When patients see their team, these special trainees are part of the psych team. Kirk says some doctors have an "I'll believe it when I see it" kind of attitude. But it's working.

Connecticut has also implemented a "Recovery Institute" - not a physical space, says Kirk, but a whole new way of creating patients' treatment plans. "Most [treatment plans] are based on problems a person has, not based on the essence of a person," says Kirk. "A Recovery Plan says, 'Yes, we want to help you manage your illness, but what else?'" Connecticut's "Recovery Institute" now helps provide education courses for patients, helps them get training and even the clothing necessary to re-enter the workforce.

The state's "Continuity of Care" approach is helping the DMH assist patients even after their treatment has ended, designed to ensure they won't suffer setbacks and end up back in the hospital. "If a patient shows four episodes of input in six months, needing care each time," Kirk says, "it makes sense for a family member to wonder what's going wrong" if they are constantly going back into a program. "We've put together [an] 'Interactive Care Case Management Approach,' which [follows] cases, linking a person to their care."

Other states have been interested in Connecticut's efforts. Kirk credits Massachusetts with trying to initiate change. He has traveled to the National Resource Institute, the research arm of the National Mental Health Commission Association and has made presentations in New Hampshire and New York.

But recovery-based systems have risks. As programs are implemented, it's both likely and desired that patients will enter the system at the early stages of their diseases. But as people become healthier, Kirk says the state may lose some of its services.

Regardless, it's stories like that of a 30-year-old Yale employee, who had suffered mental illness and said the only conversation people in her community could muster with her and her family was whether she was taking her medications, that make Kirk believe a recovery-oriented system is essential.

"In the throes of her mental illness," Kirk says of the woman, "[she said] 'No one ever asked if I'd read a really good book lately or seen a movie: As if the isolation of the disease wasn't enough, what came after and how people treated me was even worse.'" "Some would view it as soft," says Kirk of the programming. "It's not an easy change. It's a major, major cultural change."
