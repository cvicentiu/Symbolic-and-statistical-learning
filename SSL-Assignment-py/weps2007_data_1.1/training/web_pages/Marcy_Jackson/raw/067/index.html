Institutional Formation

Formation is a term, chosen by Parker Palmer, which describes a special approach to learning that any of us may employ to learn more about ourselves. Formation concepts are based on two fundamental assumptions: (1) that who we are - the way our identity is formed-influences all that we do, and (2) that a more profound understanding, a formative understanding, of who we are strengthens our capacity to act with wisdom and integrity.

Formation asks a fundamental question, "What is going on inside me and how does it influence the work I do, the people who work along side me, and those I seek to serve." To answer this question, we are invited to explore the thoughts, feelings, values, and spiritual understandings that make us unique. Our learning follows a simple path: alone or with colleagues, we take the time to reflect, explore profound questions and issues, and draw on a rich array of resources and traditions for exploring the inner life. The hope is that we will then apply this learning to the way we live our lives.

Institutional formation incorporates two further assumptions: (1) that our inner, formative lives have outward consequences for the organizations in which we work, and (2) that learning at the formation level has enormous potential to enliven and renew our work together.

Institutional Formation in the DCCCD is founded on these guiding concepts as described in our Statement of Purpose. The statement enumerates the unifying themes and skills we hope to stress during this undertaking. The themes are familiar-teaching, learning, leading, serving, and building community-but they take on new meaning and relevance when understood in the context of formation. The specific skills we hope to develop are less familiar in today's fast paced organizations, precisely because they focus on ways to strengthen our inner lives within the context of our work.

Formation efforts were launched nationally in 1994 with a program Parker Palmer conceived called the Courage to Teach (CTT). This series of eight quarterly retreats, spanning two years, is designed to explore the concepts and disciplines associated with formation. Sponsored by the Fetzer Institute and led by Dr. Palmer, the first CTT program exceeded expectations, with the pilot group of 20 experienced Michigan K-12 teachers giving it the highest marks. They believed the two-year retreat process had changed their lives.

In 1996, the Fetzer Institute continued to test teacher interest with CTT programs in South Carolina, Washington State, Southwest Michigan, and Baltimore. When assessing this second cycle of retreats, national evaluators were similarly impressed with the quality of the learning the teachers reported and with the positive changes they brought back to the schools where they taught. CTT offered profound hope and renewal to those involved. The decision was made to continue to expand these initiatives across the country.

In 1997, the national Center for Teacher Formation was established to develop, deepen and expand the work with K-12 teachers. Located on Bainbridge Island, Washington, the Center provides information, consultation and program development, and assistance to schools and individuals that wish to start CTT programs. It also oversees an extensive training program for the facilitators who lead these two-year programs. Rick and Marcy Jackson are the co-directors of the Center, and Parker Palmer continues to be a close advisor. (www.teacherformation.org),

About the time the national center was launched, the DCCCD initiative began. In August 1997, a small group from the Dallas Community Colleges (including the Chancellor and Board Chair), as well as several public school and community leaders, were invited to the Fetzer Institute to learn more about formation and discuss with Dr. Palmer the possibilities of beginning this work in Dallas. The planning retreat was among the first formal attempts to include an institution of higher education in formation work.

DCCCD leaders returned genuinely excited about the prospects. They had Dr. Palmer's promise to work with them, matched with a strong sense that formation concepts fit the needs of the institution. A further catalyst came in February, 1998 when Dr. Palmer visited the District - giving the keynote at the Faculty/ Administrative Conference Day, leading an afternoon workshop, and meeting with the Chancellor's Cabinet and Faculty Leadership. The great appeal of his presentations and the power of his then recently published book, The Courage to Teach: Exploring the Inner Landscape of a Teacher's Life, considerably broadened the organization's support for formation work

During winter, 1998, two more important steps were taken. First, a pilot CTT group of DCCCD faculty (at least one from each college) and teachers from the DeSoto Independent School District began a two-year retreat cycle. At about the same time, six Dallasites started a training and mentoring program sponsored by the Center for Teacher Formation to prepare them to lead formation retreats. Since then, an additional facilitator has been chosen from this area. Both steps were significant. The CTT retreats gave the District its first practical experience with the foundational program of formation, and the facilitator training program increased the number of available staff to lead future CTT retreats.

In partnership with the Fetzer Institute and the League for Innovation, the Center for Formation in the Community College (CFCC) was established in Dallas in spring 2001. The CFCC focuses on preparing facilitators for work in community colleges through building awareness of formation, deepening that learning, and sustaining those involved in the work of formation. Sue Jones and Ann Faulkner serve as Co-Directors of the Center; Earlene Bond is the Executive Assistant: 214-860.2674.
