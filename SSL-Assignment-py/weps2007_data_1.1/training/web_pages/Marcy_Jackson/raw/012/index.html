Touch of the Outdoors Softens Lofts' Urban Chic Style

By Carrie Donovan
Washington Post Staff Writer
Saturday, August 30, 2003; T05

Marcy Jackson, a lawyer in her forties, says her Labrador retriever enjoys the rooftop deck at their apartment in the Hudson, a new loft-style building near Logan Circle. "It's nice to have a little bit of space to be outside, since I don't have a balcony," she said.

But then again, she said, her apartment feels spacious, too. "I like the glass windows that are so large you have the sense that you are outside. It's a really nice way to be inside, yet feel outside," she said

Jackson found her loft via the Internet. She was living out West and had to find a place in Washington quickly, so she made a list of seven apartment buildings to visit in one trip and chose the Hudson. "It was convenient and had modern features, security, a garage and a neighborhood, so I didn't feel like I was in a hotel. And there aren't too many places in D.C. that are dog-friendly."

Danielle Zink, 28, also found the Hudson over the Internet. She was working with her husband, Jim, 37, at the American Embassy in Estonia. After several reassuring phone calls to leasing manager Jennifer Greenquist, she moved into the one-bedroom loft on May 1, followed by Jim in June. The Zinks will be moving out of the Hudson in a few weeks when they close on a house, but they give the building a hearty endorsement.

"It's a small apartment, but the high ceilings and huge windows make it seem bigger," she said. "For being small, it's very well designed."

The building's look is urban chic: stained concrete floors in the living areas, sisal carpet in the bedrooms, exposed ductwork, floor-to-ceiling windows, granite countertops and stainless-steel refrigerators. Aged-birch kitchen cabinets have glass-accent doors. The lobby sports industrial-looking metal arches in the ceiling.

Greenquist says the location is the 84-unit mid-rise building's top draw. The Hudson sits on the 1400 block of P Street, directly across from the Whole Foods grocery store; it is walking distance from the business district as well as restaurants, nightclubs and the Source Theatre.

Proximity to three Metro stops -- Dupont Circle, McPherson Square and U Street -- is a plus, Zink said. "I've been very comfortable walking home alone from the Dupont Metro because there are a lot of people around."

The first tenants moved into the Hudson in April; the building is now half full. A Starbucks has set up shop in the ground-floor retail space and will soon be joined by Blink Optical, Washington Mutual Inc., TCBY and Logan Tavern (from the owners of Grillfish in Georgetown). Beneath the shops, there's a garage where residents can stake out a spot or two for an extra monthly charge.

The Hudson is one of a cluster of loft-inspired buildings built and managed by PN Hoffman, a District-based developer that has been active in constructing and renovating high-end buildings in the city's fastest-gentrifying neighborhoods. The Metro and Saxon Court, both on Church Street NW, are condos, as is the Lofts II at Adams Morgan on 18th Street NW. The DeSoto, under construction next to the Hudson on P Street, will be rental units.

Pets are allowed at the Hudson, although dogs and cats must be interviewed (read: not evil); the nonrefundable pet fees are comparable to a month's rent at cheaper apartments. There is no size restriction. Currently, an Old English sheepdog can be seen roaming the halls. A bonus for dog lovers: The Hudson is just around the corner from Dogs By Day & Nite, a doggy day-care center, and Pet Essentials, a holistic pet-supply store.

Marc Bonara, a 30-year-old lawyer, moved into his one-bedroom unit with balcony in May. On a one-year lease, he pays $1,885 a month.

"It's very nice," he said. "It has a unique interior style. The only bad thing is there is still a lot of construction going on [next door at the DeSoto]. That will end eventually, but who knows when?" According to PN Hoffman's Web site, the DeSoto will be finished by next summer.

Bonara, who moved to Washington from New York, said he liked the building because of the neighborhood and because it was new. He also enjoys the roof deck, which is set up with tables and chairs.

The view from the deck shows the tops of landmarks, including the Washington National Cathedral, the Basilica of the National Shrine of the Immaculate Conception in Northeast and the Washington Monument. And this summer, during the building's first rooftop July Fourth party, tenants could also watch the fireworks display on the Mall.
