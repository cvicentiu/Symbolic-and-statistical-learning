Review/Film; Al Pacino As Detective In Love With His Suspect

  • Print
  • Save
By VINCENT CANBY
Published: September 15, 1989

LEAD: Detective Frank Keller (Al Pacino) of the New York Police Department is hollow-eyed and short-tempered, a physical and emotional wreck. He doesn't eat properly. He drinks too much. He's lonely and on edge. You've met the type before. Detective Frank Keller (Al Pacino) of the New York Police Department is hollow-eyed and short-tempered, a physical and emotional wreck. He doesn't eat properly. He drinks too much. He's lonely and on edge. You've met the type before. It doesn't help that his partner is married to his ex-wife, which means, as Frank points out when drunk, that the two men can't have a comradely beer at the end of the day. The situation is so bizarre that one has to think the assignment was made by Frank's superiors, possibly at the suggestion of some loony police department psychiatrist.

It is, instead, one of the many minor plot contrivances in ''Sea of Love,'' a romantic melodrama that otherwise has nothing to do with Frank's partner.

The film, opening today at the Ziegfeld and other theaters, is supposed to be a star vehicle, but it's more like a leaky radiator from which steam escapes without delivering heat.

The major plot contrivance is a series of ghastly murders of men who have met women through a weekly personals column. Each man has been found on a bed, nude, face down, his life snatched away while, apparently, he was making love to the mattress, the fatal wounds having been delivered from the back.

Frank and a jovial new partner named Sherman (John Goodman), who works the Forest Hills beat in Queens (and is thus less neurotic than the Manhattan-based Frank), get the clever idea of putting their own ads in the personals column and seeing what happens. Their job is made easier by the fact that each of the murdered men wrote in rhyme, which appears to have attracted a murderer with a sentimental streak.

To make an exceptionally long-winded story shorter, the chief suspect turns out to be the beautiful, enigmatic Helen (Ellen Barkin), the manager of a chic Manhattan shoe store. Helen had dated each of the dead men. She responds to poetry not of the highest order. She carries a gun in her purse and somehow avoids leaving fingerprints around.

Even while suspecting her, Frank is swept off his feet by the sheer sexuality of the elegant young woman. She believes in going to bed now, talking later. They make love in a way the movie seems to think is wild, mad and passionate. It's as if each had seen ''9 1/2 Weeks'' once too often on cable and wanted to imitate it, though lacking a natural talent.

''Sea of Love,'' a really quite bad movie, was directed by Harold Becker (''The Boost,'' ''Vision Quest'') and written by Richard Price, a good novelist who has done excellent film work before. He received an Oscar nomination for ''The Color of Money'' and wrote Martin Scorsese's ''Life Lessons'' segment in ''New York Stories.''

His screenplay for ''Sea of Love'' is as sluggish as its title, taken from a song that is heard from beginning to end. It has the manner of a heavily fiddled-with work, something that, after all the suggestions have been incorporated, finds itself in a corner from which it can't plausibly be extricated.

Some of the incidental details are lively, including the ploy used by the police early on to round up wanted felons. Mostly, though, ''Sea of Love'' is a lugubrious imitation of a second-rate television movie, over-produced and over-cast. Mr. Pacino tears into a role made out of rice paper, for messy results, while Miss Barkin does her level best to seem simultaneously sexy, homicidal and innocent, which is not easy.

Mr. Goodman, from television's ''Roseanne,'' treats the rice paper more gently. ''Sea of Love'' is the kind of movie that uses actresses of the stature of Barbara Baxley and Jacqueline Brookes for what are little more than walk-on roles. Their appearances call attention to the waste of talent and money that the entire enterprise represents.
