Scottish executives explain surprising loss to analysts

Shareholder suits claim company failed to properly inform investors

Charlotte Business Journal - August 18, 2006
by David Mildenberg
Staff writer

  • Print this Article
  • Email this Article
  • Reprints
  • RSS Feeds
  • Most Viewed
  • Most Emailed

Scottish Re executives say the company's $124 million second-quarter loss resulted from a change in complex tax planning strategies, rather than any misdeeds by executives.

"No, they did not do anything wrong," Glenn Schafer, the company's board chairman said during an Aug. 4 conference call with analysts.

"There wasn't fraud here, to the best of our beliefs," says Standard & Poor's analyst Rodney Clark. "It was a tax accounting issue that they hadn't anticipated. I think it could be argued that better tax counsel could have helped them avoid or anticipate this."

Law firms that specialize in shareholder litigation, however, contend the company failed to properly inform investors of its financial status.

At least 10 firms have filed lawsuits against Scottish, alleging a failure to disclose vital information about the company's financial status.

The lawsuits allege Scottish lacked adequate internal controls, contending its executives manipulated and inflated financial results to impress investors. Such lawsuits are common when a stock declines sharply.

Since 1999, Scottish has built itself into the nation's second-largest life insurance reinsurer, with 2005 revenue of $2.2 billion and a market value that topped $1.3 billion earlier this year.

Its operations and about 150 employees are based in south Charlotte, though it is officially headquartered in Bermuda for tax purposes.

On July 31, the company said it would lose $126 million, mainly because of a $112 million write-down of deferred tax assets. The loss sent Scottish shares down by 75% and prompted ratings agencies to cut Scottish's credit rating below the A- level typically required for reinsurers to attract new business.

The loss also sparked the resignations of three of Scottish's eight senior executives, including Chief Executive Scott Willkomm and Seth Vance, who had headed the firm's North American operations.

Willkomm and Vance quit to give Scottish "an opportunity to get a fresh start, and it would help significantly with the ratings agencies," Schafer told analysts.

Explaining why Scottish anticipated a $25 million quarterly profit in May, then reported a $124 million loss in July, took more than 90 minutes of discussion and detailed questioning on the conference call. Company officials declined requests for an interview.

Here's a capsule of what Scottish says happened: The company for years produced a deferred income tax benefit because of net operating losses and unrealized losses on its investments. This benefit had averaged $4.5 million per quarter over the past two years and was reported as a $79 million asset on the company's March 31 financial statement.

But accounting rules require companies to write down the value of assets when they realize their value has permanently eroded. Scottish officials say they concluded this summer that the asset was overstated by $121 million because of changes in tax projections, an anticipated loss in the quarter and a large securitization transaction.
