Chris Brockett is a computational linguist in the Natural Language Processing (NLP) Group.

I joined Microsoft Research in August 1996. In past metamorphoses, I have worked as a translator and newspaper-columnist-of-sorts in Japan, and taught Japanese language and Japanese linguistics for a period at the University of Washington. I completed my BA in Asian Languages at Auckland University, New Zealand, in 1973, and subsequently earned an M.A. in Japanese literature from Waseda University, Tokyo in 1979 and a Ph.D. in linguistics at Cornell in 1991.

At Microsoft Research, I have worked on the development of the lexical and morphological components of our group's natural language processing system for Japanese, and on the MSR-MT English-Japanese machine translation system, now deployed to translate Microsoft Knowledge Base articles into Japanese.

My interests include lexical and formal semantics and issues of the representation of knowledge in natural language. One area of especial interest is the extent to which syntactic and semantic structures studied by linguists can be viewed as emergent properties that can be captured by appropriate statistical modeling.

My current work focuses on automated acquisition of paraphrases from monolingual aligned corpora, and supervised and unsupervised machine learning of syntactic and semantic relations. I am also working to extend the present coverage of machine translation to new domains unsupported by extensive parallel corpora and to new language pairs.
