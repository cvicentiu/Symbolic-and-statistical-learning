Abstract

We report the issues encountered in the multi-lingual annotation task for the Interlinguas Workshop.


1               Introduction

The main goal of the annotation exercise, as we perceived it, is to extract and annotate the "story" of the document by identifying objects, events and states in the text, which we refer to as the model of the document. Therefore, we did not take it as an MT-specific task – the same annotation may be useful for summarization, question-answering, etc, so we have these applications in mind as well. We have also understood the task of annotation/mark-up/tagging more broadly as the task of defining a level of representation in which the "story" of the document is captured.  The discussion that follows is not programmatic, but is a more or less impressionistic take on the annotation task from the point of view stated above.

   In Section 2, we discuss some key issues in the annotation we conducted on the original English text, its translation into Japanese and back into English.[1]   In Section 3, we discuss some general problems with the approach taken; Section 4 is a brief conclusion.

2            The Model of the Document

Our basic approach toward the annotation exercise was driven by the following statement found in the description of the workshop task: "Texts will be marked up to identify objects, events, and states. Objects are the concrete (or abstract) entities that the text is about. Events are the actual activities or actions in which these objects take part. States of affairs describe characteristics of objects. Together, objects, events, and states form the story told by the text."

2.1        Identification of objects

Identification of objects constitutes an essential part of the annotation exercise, as well as the description of the story told by the document. We approached this issue through the phenomenon of coreference:  a given object in the model (e.g. the institution BancoSol, indicated by the annotation BANCOSOL1 in the annotation of the original English document) may be referred to by more than one token in the document (e.g., by NPs in sentences 4, 5, 18, etc.).

   Using this approach, we encountered certain problems.  Consider for example the first and third sentences from the original English document:

 

(1) ACCION International is a U.S.-based private non-profit organization that currently provides technical assistance to a network of institutions in thirteen countries in Latin America and six cities in the United States.

 

(2) Three of the most advanced institutions in the ACCION network started their programmes as non-profit organizations...

 

The bold-face NP in (2) refers to three of the institutions in the larger set referred to by the bold-face NP in (1).  The model of this document must somehow indicate this relation.  However, the relation is not one of coreference:  the network of institutions referred to in (1) is not the same object as the three institutions referred to in (2).  Rather, there is a subset relation among them.

   In general, quantified expressions and expressions indicating part-whole relationships posed this kind of problem:  the simple object/event/state ontology provided in the annotation guidelines does not extend in any obvious way to allow us to express these important relationships.

 

2.2        What should we annotate?

Another issue that arose with our approach is the question of which sentences or parts thereof are annotated and which are not.  Put another way:  assume that each clause in the document expresses a proposition; certainly some of these propositions are more central to the “story” of the document than others.  This is directly relevant to the main goal of this exercise, namely how we extract a story behind a document. We have identified two orthogonal, mutually non-exclusive ways of achieving this goal.

   The first is the summarization approach. This approach places particular emphasis on pronominal reference, as entities referred to by pronouns tend to be central to the story being told. In this approach, first the pronominals (or topic-marked NPs in the case of Japanese) and their coreferents (group1 entities) were identified.  Subsequently, the events and states in which group1 entities were an argument were identified, as well the other arguments (yielding group2).  This process was continued until no further events and states could be identified for the entities in the list (only 3 iterations were necessary).  This leaves an interesting set of sentences which appear to be unrelated; this set can be further studied to see if there are hidden arguments or concepts which link these sentences to the main text.

   The second approach for distinguishing important and not-so-important part of the document is based on certain language-specific expressions. For example, in the Japanese translation of the text, the expression equivalent to "X can be enumerated as an example of Y" is often used to encode the relationship X=Y; this can be thought of as a preferred lexical-syntactic device in Japanese, and can be normalized in a more language-neutral representation of document content. Similarly in English, expressions in bold face below also indicate X=Y relationship:

 

   (3) One of the most successful of these institutions is BancoSol in Bolivia.

 

   (4) Many factors contributed to PRODEM's success.

 

   (5) The process included raising the equity capital required by Bolivian law before it would charter a bank; designing the bank's financial structure, as well as its projection for profitability; training staff; and meeting the requirements of the Bolivian Superintendency of Banks.

 

   (6) Other shareholders included ACCION, Calmeadow from Canada, which had been very instrumental in the formation of the bank, Fundes from Switzerland and ICC, the Inter-American Development Bank's private arm.

 

All these expressions correspond to a single Japanese expression "X can be enumerated as an example of Y" in our translation.

   Similarly, "the group lending technique of solidarity groups"  was translated as "the group lending technique that is called solidarity groups" in Japanese. There is an extra predicate to be annotated in the Japanese text, but "called" in this context is simply indicating a relationship between "the group lending technique" and "solidarity groups" and does not constitute an event relevant to the story. The decision as to what words to annotate and what not to annotate may be arbitrary; therefore, whether or not we should normalize these lexical expressions as relations is an open question.

   We believe that these two approaches, the summarization-based and syntax-based approaches, are useful in the annotation exercise, as they both contribute to winnowing the document down to its essential parts, i.e., the “story” of the document (its model). 

3               Issues with the Approach

This section gives a brief summary of the issues to be solved.

3.1        Shortcomings of annotation specification

As described in Section 2.1, we need a more detailed specification as to how to annotate a text to show quantification and part-whole relationships. Similar problems include the annotation of modality, tense and aspect, etc.

3.2        Annotation driven by surface syntax

There is a tension between annotation per se vs. creating an abstract model of document ("story of document"). In general, the more abstract the annotation/representation is, the closer the documents in different languages and translations may be represented with each other, yet the further they are from the surface syntax, so for all practical purposes the annotation becomes extremely difficult, because it is difficult to avoid being influenced by the surface syntax and lexical choices of the document.   We discuss here just one example, though others are easy enough to find.

   Consider the English sentence (7)  and its Japanese translation (8):

 

   (7) Banco Solidario, S.A., or Bancosol, grew out of a non-profit joint venture created in 1986 by prominent members of the Bolivian business community and ACCION International. 

 

   (8) ソリダリオ銀行(南アフリカ)は、もともとボリビアの実業家およびACCIONインターナショナルの著名会員によって1986年に創設された非営利合弁事業である。

   lit ‘Banco Solidario is originally a non-profit joint venture created in 1986 by Bolivian businessmen and prominent members of ACCION International.‘

 

In the original, the bold-face expression seems to express an event of growing-out-of something (i.e., becoming), while the Japanese translation expresses the same concept using a stative expression and an adverbial ("originally").[2]  Clearly, the underlying model of the document should contain both:  there is a becoming event in the past, with the result that a state of original being exists in the present, yet neither text provides a useful hook for the annotation of both concepts.

4               Conclusion

In this report we have briefly discussed our impressions of the annotation task, and have noted some of the difficulties we encountered.  We look forward to discussing possible solutions to these and other problems at the workshop.
