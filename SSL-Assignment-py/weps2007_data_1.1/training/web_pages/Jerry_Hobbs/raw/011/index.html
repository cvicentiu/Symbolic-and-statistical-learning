Artist Statement for Jerry Hobbs
The heavily wooded hills of Van Zandt county in East Texas raised a boy named Jerry Hobbs. He loved nature and especially took notice of trees. And seeings how his ancestors had been in this area since 1850 there was plenty of know-how about trees to pass along. Jerry also did his own discovering as he grew up. In the years since, he and Brenda, his wife of 35 years, explored the woods and forests in many parts of Texas. Jerry is acquainted with about every kind of tree in the state.

His knowledge shows in his work. His sculptures, carvings and turnings show a variety of natural colors. He adds no coloring to his work. One sculpture that illustrates this is called, “Robin Hood in the Forest.” It is made of persimmon which Jerry calls the hardest wood he knows of. The piece shows a variety of colors. Jerry knew how much of the wood to remove to get to each desired color and still get the features of his subject.

But knowledge alone does not produce art. Jerry’s work is not just a craft. Brenda says,”I don’t think many people see what Jerry sees in roots, burl-knots and trees. Everything he sees in wood just has to be brought to life as he sees it.” His most striking works are his sculptures formed from burls or roots of trees. They frequently have faces or other creature features on several sides of the sculpture.

Jerry’s skills are mostly self-taught. He did some metal lathe work many years ago. And he visited with a wood carver from time to time about twenty years ago. But his work shows much originality of method. His works are one-of-a-kind. The kinds of wood he uses include black walnut, cedar, several types of oak, bois d’arc (osage orange), persimmon and his favorite, mesquite.

His visits with the woodcarver were because of his desire for a hobby. When the woodcarver invited Jerry to try the tools himself, Brenda says, “Jerry was hooked like a fish going after a worm.” In the fifteen or so years since, Jerry has shown his work primarily within fifty miles of home at art and fine craft shows. His work was featured on a major network TV station in Dallas. His main exposure to a larger audience came from the months that he regularly showed at the Canton First Monday Trade Days. This is the largest flea market in the world with six thousand vendors. During that time Jerry was building a following of collectors who bought his pieces.
