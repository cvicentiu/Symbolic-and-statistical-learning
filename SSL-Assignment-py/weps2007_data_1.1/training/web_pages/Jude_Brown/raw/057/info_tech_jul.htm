Give PowerPoint Pizzazz: Tips From Academy Meeting Experts

 

By Linda Roach, Contributing Writer

 

A basic slide show created with Microsoft PowerPoint may have felt like “enough” for your last Academy presentation. But if you’re no longer satisfied with plain vanilla PowerPoint, it’s time to move on to some of the more advanced features that this presentation software allows.

Physicians frequently take awhile to reach this level, said computer software trainer Jude Brown-Bellamy, a senior IT trainer for Digital Acumen, the computer and audiovisual consulting firm the Academy uses at its meetings.

“I’ve heard physicians say, ‘I’m not going to put all that stuff in there. That’s more for marketing,’ ” Ms. Brown-Bellamy said. “They worry that some people might think a fancy PowerPoint presentation is a little over the top, or like they’re trying to sell something.” But incorporating OR video clips into your PowerPoint slides, as Academy presenters are starting to do, can be enormously helpful when you’re explaining a surgical technique.

Ms. Brown-Bellamy, David A. Durfee, MD, and Gerald E. Meltzer, MD, are among the PowerPoint experts who will be teaching about the application at the Academy’s 2002 Joint Meeting in Orlando (see “PowerPoint at the Joint Meeting,” next page). There’s an added urgency to the training because this is the last year that presenters will be able to use the traditional slide carousel. Only computer-based slide shows can be used at the 2003 Annual Meeting.

Here are some of the tips these experts have on making the best use of the animation, transitions, standing elements and video.
 

How to Use Animation

The term animation refers to the text effects in which, for instance, lines of text appear on the slide one at a time, or are highlighted sequentially as the speaker addresses them.

Once a slide is animated in this way, a click of the mouse will trigger the animation of the next line or section of text. Or animations can be set to appear every few seconds, thus keeping the speaker moving at the appropriate pace. (Set this up by clicking on Slide Show, then Rehearse Timings.)
 
Work initially in Normal View or Outline View to write the meat of your presentation, Dr. Durfee said. “You need to concentrate on the text first, and dress it up later,” he said.
 
When it’s time to select an animation effect, go to the View menu at the top and select Slide Sorter View, or click on the Slide Sorter icon in the toolbar at the lower left. This gives you a view of thumbnails of all your slides, so you can highlight the ones you want to animate.

Once they’re highlighted, left-click on the Slide Show menu or the Slide Show icon in the Animation Effects toolbar. Using the menu or the toolbar, you can select from several standard effects. A left-click on the effect’s icon will preview the effect.

To highlight lines of text individually on the slide, or to add sounds as each line appears, choose Custom Animation from the Slide Show menu or the Animation Toolbar. The resulting dialog box allows you to preview different effects.

Use animation judiciously, advises Dr. Meltzer. “PowerPoint is so versatile that you can do a lot of things that will mess up your presentation by getting too fancy,” he said. “Your viewers can get distracted from the theme of the talk and seduced by all the things going on in the slides.”

TIP: Preview your animation by clicking on the animation icon just below the slide in Slide Sorter View or clicking on the Slide Show icon in the lower left toolbar, Dr. Durfee advises. Then if you accidentally apply an animation effect you hate, you can get rid of it simply by highlighting the slide or slides again and select no animation. Select the appropriate slides and try again until you’re satisfied with the animation.

How to Use Transitions

Transitions is the term PowerPoint applies to special effects that occur between slides. For instance, one slide will dissolve away as the next one appears. Or slides can scoot into the picture from the side, top or bottom.

Like animation, to add a Transition effect you should go to the View menu and select the Slide Sorter view. Highlight some or all of the slides. Then left-click on the Transitions menu on the Animation Effects toolbar and choose the effect.

Underneath every slide to which you’ve applied a Transition effect or an Animation effect, PowerPoint will insert an icon indicating this. Left-click on the icon to see how the effect looks. All the experts advise choosing one type of transition and sticking to it.

TIP: As with animations, Transition effects can be accompanied by one of a variety of sounds. Have pity on your audience, though. Do they really want to hear a drumroll 20 times in a 10-minute period?

How to Add Video
In Normal or Slide View (go to the View menu and select Normal), create a New Slide using the Insert menu, the New Slide icon on the toolbar, or the Common Tasks list on the toolbar. From the resulting dialog box, choose a slide format that is blank or that contains a large box into which you can insert video. Once the new slide is on your screen, left-click in the box where the video will go, then go to Insert menu and select the submenu Movies and sounds.

Getting your video onto the computer can be done two ways. (Ms. Brown- Bellamy will be teaching a seminar on this at the Joint Meeting’s Technology Pavilion.) You can have a special video card installed in your computer. Then you just plug the video camera directly into the computer to convert video into a digital file with the .AVI extension, said Dr. Meltzer. Or you can make the conversion using an external device such as Snappy or Dazzler.

TIP: Even if you plan to run your PowerPoint presentation from your laptop, it’s best to save a full copy on CD-ROM in case of onsite problems.

Use the Pack and Go feature on the File menu to make the CD, copying the graphics and video files into the final PowerPoint presentation. (Unless you do this, PowerPoint just adds a link, not the file itself, when inserting art, spreadsheets and video.) To be sure it’s all there, test your CD version on another computer before you leave home.

 

How to Change the Template
To add logos or other common elements such as identifying footers to your slides, you have to change the Slide Master.

Go to the View menu and choose Master and Slide Master. You can add the presentation date, a footer identifying the presentation and/or a slide number along the bottom. A logo is added by clicking on the Insert menu, then picking Picture and From File. Once it’s inserted you can move and resize this logo anywhere on the Slide Master.

Once you’re finished making the changes, save them for the future by turning your new master into a Design Template. Go to File, click on Save As and choose Design Template for the type of file to be saved. And be careful to give it a name you’ll remember later.

Keep It Simple

 

Experts on using Microsoft PowerPoint have some basic recommendations for making your presentations work, especially if you aren’t yet comfortable with the program. These include the following:

  • Begin in Outline View or Normal View.
  • Use the templates, color schemes and autolayouts provided.
  • Stick to one or two typefaces (fonts) and avoid busy backgrounds.
  • Use no more than five bullets per slide and no more than two levels of bullets.
  • Use consistent, concise wording in your bullets. If the first bullet is a sentence fragment, the others should be the same type of fragment. (Or they all can be complete sentences, but only if they’re very brief.)
