Madeleine Brown: 1987-2006

Emory Inspired by Fallen Freshman

By: Emily Millen

Issue date: 1/23/07 Section: Sports
  • Print
  • Email
  • Page 1 of 1
When Hilary Campbell and the rest of the swimming and diving freshmen class arrived at a welcoming party for the team on one of the first nights of the school year, they went through the obligatory awkward greetings that accompany first meetings. While many of the freshmen nervously fidgeted and mingled with their new teammates, Madeleine Brown, a bright-eyed freshmen swimmer boldly walked up to everyone in the house and introduced herself.

"You're Hilary from Ohio, right?" Brown asked.

Campbell wasn't the only name that Brown knew. Before coming to campus for freshmen orientation, Brown learned the nearly 60 names of the entire varsity swimming and diving team.

"She had everybody's name and where they were from memorized from Facebook and was able to connect people's faces with their pictures," Campbell said. "I was struggling to remember the two or three names I had learned, and she already had all of them memorized. She loved the team before she even knew the team. That's just who Madeleine was."

As her teammates soon found out over the next four months, this investment in the team was nothing out of the ordinary for Brown. Her commitment to swimming, school and friendships throughout the semester made a marked impact on the team that very few first semester freshmen are able to establish.

But on Dec. 16, Brown was found unconscious in the water at varsity swim practice and passed away three days later.

As an Atlanta area native, Brown transitioned to Emory after swimming both with the club team Swim Atlanta and for four years at Marist High School, a swimming powerhouse. She specialized in the 200- and 400-yard individual medleys and added to the Eagles' loaded lineup in the 100- and 200-yard butterfly. At the Miami (Ohio) University Invitational in early December, Brown posted several lifetime best times and earned National 'B' qualifying standards in the 200-yard butterfly and 400-yard individual medley.

Though her contributions in the water helped position the Eagles for a run at a third consecutive national title later this year, Head Coach Jon Howell and her teammates say that it was her passion for the team and the sport that made Brown exceptional.

"It was hard to be in a lane with Madeleine or be on deck with Madeleine and not be smiling," Howell said. "That's a gift. Not everyone has that. She was not the most talented swimmer in the pool, but what she lacked in talent, she made up for with determination and hard work and that's true with every aspect of her life. It's easy to respect someone like that."

To her teammates, it was her signature curiosity and humor that earned her an immediate role on the team as a freshman. She quickly became distinguished for having a question, or five, about everything.

"She asked questions constantly," freshman Julie Schenker said. "The first time I met her I was like 'Whoa, where are these coming from, kiddo?' But the more I got to know her, it was clear that she genuinely just wanted to know about everyone because she had a genuine interest in other people."

In a sport that requires spending up to five hours a day at practice and sacrificing weekends for meets, not letting the stresses of the commitment become overwhelming is crucial for success. According to her teammates, Brown had a plethora of jokes, pop culture references, and song lyrics that kept the air light during long practices.

During down time at the pool, several swimmers often played a word game that requires a conversation to be held in which each response must be in the form of a question.

"We're all thinking that Madeleine's about to dominate this game, I mean, it's her game, but she was terrible, which made it hilarious," Schenker said. "She thought by emphasizing the last word, she said would make it a question. We'd all be laughing so hard by the end."

Though Brown had a joke for everything, she was also excited to work hard when it was time to be serious, according to her coaches and teammates.

"She was a great competitor and one of those rare personalities that's extremely fierce and competitive in the water but then got out and was really fun and nice to be around," Howell said. "The freshmen this year have done an amazing job, and we've got a great group but there's nobody that has had the same impact that she had."

The work that Brown put in every day silently demanded the same of everyone, according to her teammates. During several practices which Brown felt she could go faster than her interval, she requested to join the men's lane and do their workout with them.

"She really set the standard," sophomore Tess Pasternak said. "She swims the same events as [freshman] Leigh Psaris, who is obviously a great swimmer, but she never, ever got discouraged about it and ended up helping everyone."

Pasternak noted one day when the team had the option of attending either early or late practice. Brown opted to swim in the morning and completed a grueling set of 20x100 yards butterfly. Psaris came to practice in the afternoon, facing the same set.

"Leigh really didn't want to do them all fly, like didn't know if she could do them all fly, but did because she knew that Madeleine had," junior Ellen Flader said. "She made everyone better.

"To be honest, when I was in her lane, I dreaded it," wrote freshman Lillian Ciardelli, who was Brown's roommate. "That was because she was the most motivated swimmer I've ever swum with. I always complained that she was over-achieving but secretly really happy she made me do it."

But her fierce drive wasn't limited within the parameters of the lane lines. Brown planned to pursue a career in medicine, and since the time requirements of swimming require a delicate balance between athletics and academics, Brown applied her same systematic drive to school. Nearly two weeks before final exams last semester, Brown organized a strict study schedule for both herself and her roommate that required long hours spent at Woodruff Library.

Brown's focus on the team over her own individual pursuits didn't begin when she arrived on campus. Her dedication stemmed back to years of swimming at both Swim Atlanta and Marist. As a high school swimmer, Brown was a four-time state participant at Marist and a natural leader of the team.

"She was very in-your-face in the best kind of way," senior Marist swimmer Katherine Locker said. "She had a presence about her. She was so intense about Marist swimming."

During her senior year, Brown printed off rosters of all the potential contending high schools for AAAAA Georgia State Championship and color-coded them according to potential line-ups to determine where Marist could pick up points. Despite having a coach to worry about lineups, Brown and Locker would sacrifice their lunch periods to go over who they thought should swim each event to maximize points.

"We had a book that Madeleine and some of the swimmers made, and we would write all the possible different ways we could finally win it all," Locker said. "Who color codes high school rosters? She was so intense."

After an unfortunate disqualification for the War Eagles at last year's state championship, the Westminster Schools extended its stranglehold on the state title to six consecutive years. Oddly enough, Brown trained with many of Westminster swimmers in the same sect of Swim Atlanta.

Because of her connection to both programs, the two schools renamed their in-season dual meet to the 'Madeleine Jude Brown Invitational' in honor of Brown.

A trophy, nicknamed the 'Maddawg Cup,' presented by the Brown family will be passed back and forth between the winner of the duel each year.

Last Thursday, Marist's girl's team defeated Westminster but lost the combined boys and girls event in first annual Madeleine Jude Brown Invitational by seven points.

Despite the loss, Locker felt that Brown would've been pleased with her team.

"In swimming, some swimmers are individual swimmers but Madeleine was definitely a team swimmer," Locker said.

While Brown's death is devastating to the Emory community, it is especially difficult for the swimming and diving team, which operates more as a family than just a team. News of Brown's death came during the height of last semester's exam period and forced the team to separate just as some felt they needed to be together the most.

"It sucked having to leave right away," Campbell said. "You've got a world at home and a world at Emory, and they don't mix and no one knows what you're going through except for your team."

"The only people that could really help us was us," added Flader.

The team originally planned to meet in Florida for winter training on Jan. 2 but the college administration decided to hold the team in Atlanta at the Emory Inn until Jan. 9 before heading for Florida for the final few days of training. During winter training, many teammates said they found solace in the difficult sets that Brown embraced.

"We were definitely swimming for Madeleine," Schenker said.

They also held a personal memorial service for Brown and rested on the same support that Howell said initially attracted Brown to Emory.

"They're doing a great job dealing with it, but I don't think it's something we'll ever get over," Howell said. "That's just the reality of it. One of the things that attracted Madeleine to our program is that we're like a family, and I think that's really helped people."

As the Eagles head into the second half of the season, they now face an intricate balance of moving on without leaving Brown behind.

"I think it was [senior Andrew] Callam that said that there's a hole in our team that can't be filled, won't be filled, and shouldn't be filled," senior Sarah Nicholson said. "There's a way to move on without losing her or forgetting her."

Though the team has yet to decide on any permanent memorial to honor Brown, they've begun writing "MAD" in black sharpie on their arms at each meet. The women's team also decided to leave Brown's locker up for the rest of the season. The women's team created the Madeleine Brown Award - an honor bestowed to one swimmer that the seniors feel best embodied Brown's spirit and character during the meet.

"During our first meet at Tampa, I knew they were going to do it, but it kind of took me aback to walk out on deck and see 'MAD' everywhere," Howell said. "It was a really nice reminder."

Flader said she's not worried about Brown ever fading from the core of Emory swimming or from the lives of anyone who knew her because she had such a lasting impact.

Brown's phone number still remains in her phone and brings a smile and a favorite 'Madeleine memory' every time she every time she passes over it.

"We're constantly telling stories about her still," Pasternak said. "Something will happen and it will spark a Madeleine story. There are certain things we're not willing to give up yet."

Brown, who was a devout Catholic, had a favorite prayer entitled 'Don't Quit'-an apt maxim for someone who met life with as much vigor as Brown did. With the constant, nagging strains that a long swimming season demands, it's also become an instruction manual for the team as they begin to heal.

"She would never, ever quit on us," Flader said. "We can't quit on each other. Together, we're strong."
