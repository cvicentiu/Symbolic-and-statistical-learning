With the growing interest in and provision of coaching and mentoring attention has also turned to research and evaluation of these forms of development.

Consequently the NHS Leadership Centre evaluated some of its coaching and mentoring provision and carried out a piece of secondary research into the effectiveness of coaching.

We thought it important to summarise and disseminate the key findings and implications from this work to help inform future development activity. Factors that affect the take up of Personal Development Adviser Support
Beers, Helen (2004)
NHS Leadership Centre

Click here to open the full report (PDF, 40KB)

Personal development adviser support is available to all newly appointed chief executives as part of the chief executives development portfolio. It is offered to those taking up their first appointment as a chief executive and also to those who are already chief executives and are taking up a subsequent appointment.

The support aims to help chief executives to link their development needs with the challenges of their new organisation. It consists of four two hour meetings with a personal development adviser (PDA) and the option of undertaking a 360o appraisal. Chief executives are invited to express a preference for a particular type of adviser and are then matched with the one most appropriate to support their needs.

This is the first of two reports relating to the personal development advisor support. This report focuses on those chief executives who did not take up the offer of support. The aim of this evaluation was to investigate and provide explanations for the low uptake of the personal development advisor (PDA) support. The second report contains the findings from chief executives who took up the offer of support.

Personal Development Adviser (PDA) Support: Evaluation Report
Beers, Helen (2004)
NHS Leadership Centre

Click here to open the full report (PDF, 80KB)

Personal development adviser (PDA) support is available to all newly appointed chief executives as part of the chief executives development portfolio. It is offered to those taking up their first appointment as a chief executive and also to those who are already chief executives and are taking up a subsequent appointment.

The support aims to help chief executives to link their development needs with the challenges of their new organisation. It consists of four two hour meetings with a personal development adviser (PDA) and the option of undertaking a 360o appraisal. Chief executives are invited to express a preference for a particular type of adviser and are then matched with the one most appropriate to support their needs.

In 2002 and 2003, a total of 244 chief executives were offered the support of a PDA and 140 of them took up the offer (a take up rate of 57%). The take up varied according to whether it was a new chief executive or subsequent chief executive appointment.

This report focuses on those who took up the offer of support. It covers chief executives experiences of the structure and processes of the PDA support, ways in which the support was useful and helpful to them and how it influenced their work. The findings have implications for the design and implementation of NHS senior management development programmes.

Systematic Review: Evaluation and leadership development
Burgoyne, John and Williams, Sadie (2004)
Henley Management Caollege, Greenlands, Henley-on-Thames, Oxfordshire

Click here to open the summary publication (PDF, 177KB)
Click here to open the full report (PDF, 64KB)

A team from Henley Management College was commissioned by the NHS Leadership Centre to undertake a review of the literature on evaluation in order to support the Centre’s thinking on the design and evaluation of leadership development activities in the NHS.

The review aimed to produce the following outcomes:

  • Summary of research findings identifying key themes, findings and areas of contestation
  • Summary of different evaluation methodologies and models
  • Outline of evaluation methodologies associated with development activities, particularly leadership development, and supporting arguments for their use
  • Key literature associated with each topic area.

The amount of evaluation undertaken both nationally and internationally has increased considerably over past decades in response to the policy agenda of increased management accountability in the public services. Whereas audit and monitoring had a more narrow focus, evaluation takes a wider view, looks at different ranges of variables, uses many methodologies, and often tends to be more qualitative in nature.

This report presents the results of the evaluation of the 'Challenging Perceptions' programme (November 2002 to November 2003). The overall objective of the programme was to provide leadership, career development and mentoring for female nurses in mental health to help them compete for leadership positions and redress the gender imbalance at senior levels of the NHS. Realise Your Potential

Realise Your Potential Programme Summary of the Evaluation.
Cohorts 1 & 2
Beers, Helen (2004)
NHS Leadership Centre

Click here to open the full report (PDF, 89KB)

This evaluation report provides a summary of the findings from all of the evaluation activity carried out with the first two cohorts of the Realise Your Potential Programme. This programme is part of the portfolio of development programmes for directors. Its target audience is executive directors who have the potential to become chief executives within four years. It aims to enable directors to develop capacity in their current role and to explore the chief executive role. Its objectives are to build understanding of the role of a chief executive and to help directors learn and equip themselves for the task of leading and changing a complex organisation. It also aims to challenge directors to question their current attitudes, beliefs and assumptions and to test their commitment to become a chief executive.

The findings provided evidence that the programme had been successful in developing the capacity of directors and equipping them to lead and change a complex organisation. It had enabled them to challenge their current attitudes, beliefs and assumptions and had tested their commitment to become a chief executive.

The evaluation included a variety of stakeholders and methodology in its design. It investigated the impact of the programme at individual, organisation and service levels. This report highlights how participants in a programme find it difficult to identify how they take learning back to work, and how learning has an impact on their organisation or services it provides.

Realise Your Potential Programme Case Studies Evaluation Report. Cohorts 1 & 2
Beers, Helen (2004)
NHS Leadership Centre

Click here to open the full report (PDF, 55KB)

This report presents the findings from case studies with participants on the first two cohorts of the Realise Your Potential programme. This programme aimed to enable directors to develop capacity in their current role and to explore the chief executive role. Case studies provide rich stories about how development processes take place. They enable narratives to be gathered from numerous stakeholders in order to explore the different factors that have an impact on development. They can provide information on the factors that inhibit and facilitate development.

The core research question for the evaluation was “what difference has attending the programme made for the individual, their organisation and the services they deliver?”

The findings have implications for the design and implementation of NHS senior management development programmes. Whilst senior managers have similar challenges, they face these under different pressures in different contexts. Programmes may provide greater benefits at organisational level if they are able to take this into account. The findings show that programmes provide a valuable vehicle for development by supplying relevant material in an environment that enables reflection and the sharing of experiences with others.

Leadership at the Point of Care

Evaluation of the Leadership at the Point of Care programme.
First Evaluation Report
Williams, Sadie (2004)
Henley Management College

Click here to open the full report (PDF, 428KB)

The Leadership at the Point of Care programme is part of the ongoing programme of modernisation and improvement in the NHS. It is a two year programme which started at the end of 2003. It aims to enable front line staff to see themselves as ‘leaders at the point of care’, to become change agents, and to be motivated and energised to improve patient care in a practical and relevant way.

This is the first evaluation report, commissioned by the Leadership Centre and carried out by Henley Management College. It reports the findings from a survey with programme participants. These findings have implications for the design and implementation of NHS development programmes for front line staff.

The evaluation report provides evidence that this is a highly successful programme. The content is practical and relevant, offers learning which is useful in the workplace, and has real impact on improved patient care. The programme is successful at improving interpersonal skills, particularly in the areas of conflict resolution, negotiation, and team working. In addition, it has positive effects on morale, with people feeling better about going to work and feeling that the contribution they make at work ‘makes a difference’ and really does matter. The work based project work is a very important part of the programme and one of the highlights of its success.

Leadership at point of care third evaluation report, Interviews with participants
Burgoyne, John and Williams, Sadie (2005)
Henley Management College

Click here to open the full report (PDF, 295KB)

The Leadership at the Point of Care programme is part of the ongoing programme of modernisation and improvement in the NHS. It is a two year programme which started at the end of 2003. It aims to enable front line staff to see themselves as ‘leaders at the point of care’, to become change agents, and to be motivated and energised to improve patient care in a practical and relevant way.

This is the third evaluation report, commissioned by the Leadership Centre and carried out by Henley Management College. It reports the findings from interviews with a sample of participants. The interviews enabled an exploration of the value of different parts of the programme and the types of impact the programme had resulted in.

The evaluation concluded that the programme had been successful in meeting its aims. It had changed people’s attitudes, motivated them and helped them to make changes in the workplace. In many instances, the project element of the programme had made a real impact on patient care. In addition, the overall experience of taking part in the programme had raised morale, improved motivation and given people the impetus to make changes and to see their role in patient care as valuable and worthwhile. The findings have implications for the design and implementation of NHS development programmes for front line staff.
