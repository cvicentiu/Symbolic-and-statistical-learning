Jude Brown Recognized by ASE for His Efforts Supporting Virginia Class

Synopsis by Mary Zoccola

Jude Brown (5080) recently received the Association of Scientists and Engineers (ASE) Silver Medal Award for his outstanding performance in leading and managing the Virginia Class Propulsor Development Program. The ASE is a professional organization with a membership of more than 400 civilian engineers, scientists and naval officers from the Naval Sea Systems Command and its field activities throughout the United States. The Silver Medal is awarded for continuous outstanding contributions to naval engineering for a period of not less than seven years.

Captain John Preisel presented the award to Brown. "Over the past eight years, Jude Brown has worked on propulsors for the U.S. Navy, and, in particular, for the Virginia class submarines. A propulsor is part of the propulsion system of a modern submarine, but it is also one of the most complex, technologically-advanced pieces of turbo machinery in our Navy today. It converts tens of thousands of horsepower into thrust, and it does so exceptionally well," Preisel said.

"He is the leader of this team effort for the Virginia Class of submarines. It is a broad disciplinary team. From early concepts, he has guided some of the best acousticians, hydrodynamicists, structural, material, and production engineers in pursuit of excellence," Preisel further explained. "The results will frame this key performance parameter for our Navy well into the 21st Century. This is quite an achievement."

Brown's performance over the period of this award, from 1992 to the present, has been truly exceptional in terms of scientific challenge and engineering leadership. His technical and management skills, along with his untiring and dedicated effort have been instrumental in successfully producing the quietest propulsor ever designed for a Navy ship. Through his professional engineering acumen and proactive leadership, Brown has provided visionary leadership and insight that will have a lasting impact on the design of submarine propulsor systems well into the 21st Century. Brown's unparalleled success in forming cohesive and successful headquarters, field organization, and industry teaming arrangements have made him highly deserving of this award.

In successfully planning and implementing the Virginia Class Propulsor Development Program, Brown has directed a $120+-million program involving the most difficult engineering challenges critical to meeting the operational requirements of this most advanced class of nuclear attack submarines. For the Virginia Class Program, the propulsor is considered both a component and a major system of the ship. The reason for this is that the propulsor, while a component of the propulsion system, is also a vastly complex engineering system required to meet an array of highly interactive performance parameters. As a result, Brown has been simultaneously responsible for leading both a systems engineering team and a major area team through the planning, research, development, testing and manufacturing phases of the Propulsor Development Program.

Brown assumed his leadership role beginning in 1992, coincident with the development of numerous alternative propulsor concepts and led the assessment of their technical performance, associated risks, and development costs. His team's efforts led to the down-selection of the most favorable concept for achieving an assured and well-balanced design for optimally meeting the Virginia Class performance requirements. Brown followed up his initial work by directing the detailed planning of a program to identify and address the complex technical, manufacturing, and systems engineering problems involved. In addition, he worked independently with the full ship’s design team to assure that the propulsor met all interface requirements without compromise to either the ship or the propulsor.

Brown was an early leader in the Virginia Class Program's very successful implementation of the integrated process and product design-build philosophy. He did this by incorporating all of the relevant experts, encompassing all technical disciplines, at the beginning of the project with a focus on incorporating design and manufacturing lessons learned from the Seawolf propulsor program. He staffed a multidisciplinary propulsor team with acousticians, hydrodynamicists, structural engineers, materials engineers and production engineers; defined and managed their interfaces; and motivated them to the highest levels of performance. In so doing, Brown engaged the best people available from ten different, sometimes competitive, independent government and private organizations, including shipbuilders, Navy laboratories and private support contractors. He kept the program on schedule, despite evolving technical requirements and parameter changes resulting from the integrated nature of ship design, by applying technical innovations and sound engineering decisions, and by maintaining a focus on detail and program requirements. The program has delivered the design information and manufacturing technology projected to provide a propulsor system that will satisfy technical and programmatic requirements. These requirements include the demanding operational performance needs, shipbuilder's construction schedule and program budgets.

With the drawings finalized and the lead-ship Virginia Class propulsor well under construction, Brown is working with the manufacturing community to deliver the propulsor on time and within budget to General Dynamics, Electric Boat. He has been instrumental in resolving key issues and problems that occur and his continued efforts are essential to the successful completion of this project.
