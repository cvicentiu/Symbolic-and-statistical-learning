Learned in a funk

Bryan Sharick

Issue date: 5/9/03 Section: Accent
  • Print
  • Email
  • Page 1 of 1

Bryan Sharick/The Western Front Lead singer and guitar player Jude Bowerman, left, and bass player Derek Brown jam as part of The Jude Bowerman Band May 3 at The Wild Buffalo House of Music. Brown said the band follows Bowerman's lead.

[Click to enlarge]

Four people on different educational paths met to form the funky sound that characterizes The Jude Bowerman Band.

Members of The Jude Bowerman Band, some with college educations and one with a General Education Degree, played a combination of rock, soul, funk and acid jazz at The Wild Buffalo House of Music on May 3. People at the concert danced all night and the only time fans stopped for a break was between sets.

"The beauty of music is the community creating a relationship between the band and the audience that happens all night long - like having sex," said Jude Bowerman, lead singer, guitar player and Seattle resident.

An example of this relationship occured midway through the concert when Bowerman jumped off stage, still playing his guitar, put his guitar on an unsuspecting female fan and helped her play the strings to one of their songs.

"It's so magical when there's so many people here having a good time," bass player and Seattle resident Derek Brown said.

Bowerman attended Western for three years until he transferred to the University of Washington to pursue a degree in ethnic studies and political science.

"I wanted to go to school to get an education and have an interest in what's going on in the world," Bowerman said. "I live in Seattle, but Western is my home away from home."

Bowerman said the band is like Ben Harper, but it is trying to be original and make music that comes from the heart.

"As human beings, we're all unique one way or another," Bowerman said.

When keyboard player and Seattle resident Nathan Spicer describes the band, he refers to a quote used in Boise's Idaho Statesman.

"The band is like beer-bonging a gallon of Red Bull," Spicer said. "It's the eight-ball of funk."

Spicer said the sound of their music gives patrons energy, which has the same effect as drinking a Red Bull.

People feel the music in funk because of the importance of the bass, Spicer said.

"There's all kinds of dance music, but the only legit one is the funk," Spicer said. "Techno and different music are going to rely on the funk for depth."

Tacoma resident and drummer Darin Watkins said he played drums for money when he dropped out of high school his freshman year. He eventually earned his GED, but he has always played music. He said what makes The Jude Bowerman Band work is that they connect and are comfortable with each other.

"Each night we like to not have it be the same and just push each other musically," Watkins said. "The chemistry is amazing, and we get along well, like a family."

Brown went to the Naropa Institute in Boulder, Colo., to learn creative writing and literature. While at the university, Brown said he met a bass player who interested him in playing the bass and convinced him he wanted to be a musician. The institute has since become the nation's only accredited Buddhist-inspired college.

When Bowerman wants to play a solo, the band will follow his lead, Brown said.

"We all just follow Jude," Brown said. "Each show is totally different, and we never have a set list. We just feel the crowd and play off each other."

Bowerman said he draws inspiration for the songs from the band, crowds and other musicians. He emphasized the fact that he wants to make music that people feel, not just music that people listen to.

"In order to create, you have to feel," Bowerman said. "Feeling is sort of a mythical sense. When you feel music, you feel it in your heart."

The Jude Bowerman Band is currently on tour in Washington, California, Idaho, Oregon and Nevada. The next show, near Bellingham, takes place at the Skagit Valley Casino Resort at 9 p.m. on Sept. 26 and Sept. 27.
