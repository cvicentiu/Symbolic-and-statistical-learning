Morgan Stanley Director likes Dow Jones Full article published: 02/08/2000     DOUGLAS ARTHUR is a Managing Director with Morgan Stanley Dean Witter.

TWST: Doug, will you describe your valuation methodology?

Mr. Arthur: Well, beauty is in the eye of the beholder when it comes to valuation. EBITDA or cash flow is probably the most significant benchmark for how newspaper and publishing companies are compared on both an absolute and relative basis. One of the hybrid oddities of the publishing group, though, is that these stocks are much more responsive to earnings per share surprises in terms of what moves the stocks up or down to different EBITDA valuation ratios. Unlike cable, radio, TV and other media properties, investors who follow and buy these stocks tend to be very earnings per share sensitive regardless of the cash flow characteristics of a particular quarterly result. I also think that DCF value and private market value play into some of these stocks, as Lauren pointed out. The discount to private market value is less relevant because of the restrictive ownership structure of many of these companies.

We've been in a tremendous environment for the stock market for a long time, as is obvious. We've been in an excellent economy for a long time, and we've been in a generally favorable ad environment as well, all of which may continue into 2000. But I do feel from a portfolio management point of view, now is the time to really drill down on names that have outstanding characteristics, and have catalysts beyond just the basic business, and leave everything else aside. Probably the two most interesting names to me right now are Dow Jones (NYSE:DJ) and Times Mirror (NYSE:TMC). I co-cover Dow Jones with Craig Huber, and we believe there is a real multi-year transformation of that company going on, with a revitalized management team, strong national advertising in The Wall Street Journal, and a very powerful electronic publishing story. We believe an unfolding catalyst could be the sale of at least one division, and potentially the spin-off of their Internet business, which is massive. We think that it's a very compelling near-term earnings story, and a story with a number of catalysts that will transcend any cycle in national advertising. Times Mirror is far and away the most attractive newspaper right now by virtue of the fact that 40% of the company's revenues are at The L.A. Times. L.A. is very sensitive to the Asian economic recovery, which I think could be a multi-year trend. We're starting to see accelerating ad revenues at The L.A. Times for Times Mirror, the strongest ad revenue growth in the industry currently, and the stock is far and away the cheapest in the group at well under 8 times cash flow. And it's the only management team that has had the foresight to hedge newsprint. They're over 40% hedged in their newsprint purchases for 2000, which has cost them in 1999, but I think could be a major positive in terms of giving investors a sense of certainty as to what their newsprint costs are going to be. I believe this is far and away the most compelling cyclical buy in the newspaper group right now. I would also agree with Lauren that IDG Books, while a little fuzzy in the near term because of the integration of a recent acquisition, as well as the fact that they just lost their president and CFO to Internet-land, is very compelling. The company has tremendous brands, strong product, new product momentum, and is dirt cheap.
