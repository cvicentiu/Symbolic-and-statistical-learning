Families with sick kids given aid for bills

Geri Koeppel
The Arizona Republic
Dec. 21, 2006 12:00 AM

Courtney Rich got help from the Melonhead Foundation after her son Mason, now 8, was diagnosed with leukemia at age 3.

"It turned out that they were able to pay our car registration and car insurance, which was the first thing I needed so we could keep going back to the hospital," Rich said.

It's tough enough emotionally for families when a child is diagnosed with cancer, but they usually also suffer financially. Often, as in Rich's case, one parent must quit a job - but the bills keep coming.

To continue helping people like Rich, the foundation is asking those interested to pick an ornament off a "wish tree" through Friday at all six Hooters locations in the Valley: Ahwatukee Foothills, Mesa, Peoria, Tempe and both Phoenix stores.

With all the toy drives and other children's charities active at this time of year, it might not be as warm and fuzzy to pay someone's electric bill or buy a gift card for groceries. But it means a lot to the families affected, as Melonhead Foundation founder Deb Leone of Chandler knows.

"Your mortgage company won't pay your house payment if your baby gets sick," she said.

She hopes, however, to get gift card donations for places such as Target so some families can indeed have a little "fun money" this year to buy presents.

The idea for the wish trees for the Melonhead Foundation started with the Ahwatukee Foothills Festival of Lights Association, which put up trees for three charities at its Kick-Off Party on Nov. 25. After that, the trees were moved into local restaurants.

Alvin Cooper, regional manager for Hooters, agreed to house one at the Ahwatukee restaurant. He met Leone at a golf tournament in 2004 and adopted the Melonhead Foundation as a charity to support through carwashes and other fund-raisers.

Hooters marketing director Melanie Gulmetti thought it was such a good idea that she asked Leone to put the wish trees in all of its Valley locations.

"We've had a great partnership with them in the past," Gulmetti said.

"We've been (Cooper's) charity of choice for Hooters," Leone said. "They've been a wonderful blessing for Melonhead."

Leone founded The Melonhead Foundation in 1997 after her youngest daughter, Melanie, nicknamed "Melonhead," was diagnosed with leukemia in 1989 when she was 11.

"I had to make a house payment here in Arizona and go to Seattle for four months (for Melanie's treatment) and pay rent that was twice as much as my house payment in Arizona," she said. "I still don't know how we did it and managed not to lose our house."

Melanie died in 1992 at age 14, and Leone used her legacy to help others by founding the organization. It serves about 265 families right now, mostly in Arizona.

It acts as a "safety net," as Rich called it, after other agencies are tapped out and families have nowhere to turn.

"We play Santa all year long," Leone said.
