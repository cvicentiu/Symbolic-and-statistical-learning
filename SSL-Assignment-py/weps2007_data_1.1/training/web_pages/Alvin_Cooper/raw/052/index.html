Expert Opinions

What do the experts have to say about online relationships?

In their article, Making Friends in Cyberspace, Malcolm Parks and Kory Floyd describe the conflicting impressions of online relationships. One impression contends that online relationships are "shallow, impersonal and often hostile."7 The other impression holds that Internet communication frees people from the confines of geography and offers the possibility of new and genuine, personal relationships.8

Parks and Floyd note that as people receive fewer "cues" in an online relationship, it may merely take longer to exchange personal information and develop a relationship.9 Furthermore, Parks and Floyd note that people have adapted the Internet to their needs more than the 'experts' had envisioned; people "socialize, maintain relationships, play games, and receive emotional support…"10 For those involved in an online relationship, it is a real relationship.11 The authors also note that while people meet on-line, their relationships rarely stay there; people soon begin to communicate via mail, telephone, and in face to face meetings.12

"Although stereotypes of lonely, perhaps dysfunctional people being attracted to cyberspace abound in the popular press, the fact is that we lack even the most basic information about participants in on-line relationships."13

In their article Romance in Cyberspace:Understanding Online Attraction, Alvin Cooper and Leda Sportolari note that "in a culture that emphasizes physical appearance, the Internet affords a different way of developing initial attraction."14 Attraction in an online environment is dependent upon how someone expresses him/her self. Cooper and Sportolari note that because most Internet communication is written, it allows for reflection permitting the author to express his/herself clearly and completely.15 While many contend that online relationships are necessarily shallow due to the lack of cues available in face to face communication, Cooper and Sportolari disagree.
