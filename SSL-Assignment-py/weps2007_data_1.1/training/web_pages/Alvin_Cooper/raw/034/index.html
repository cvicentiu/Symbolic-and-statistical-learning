The Lost Ones (1)

Charles and Albert travel with the Cooper family while on a delivery. A serious accident occurs while the parents are trying to take their wagon down a steep hill, and in the blink of an eye their two young children are orphans. After tireless searching for a proper home, Charles decides that he must take the children to an orphanage, but in the end he doesn't have the heart. After hearing first-hand from Albert how gritty these orphanages can be, Charles decides to take the children home with him, until a permanent placement can be made.

Trivia

add »
One has to wonder why Laura and Almanzo couldn't keep James and Cassandra themselves, at least for the time that Pa was searching for a permanent family. Seeing as Charles went so far as to beg Hester Sue to consider taking in the children, I'm surprised he didn't even try going to his grown, married, stable daughter and son-in-law for help. They had that huge house that included at least one extra bedroom (which the infamous "nephews" used just 7 episodes before this one). (edit)
No explanation is ever given regarding why James and Cassandra were riding in Charles' wagon at the time when their parents were killed. It's obviously a miracle they weren't in that wagon, but why wouldn't they be riding with their parents?
Reply: James and Cassandra were awestruck by Charles and Albert and wanted to ride with them, a request their parents granted. This explains why they were not riding with their Ma and Pa. (edit)
When Mr. and Mrs. Cooper's wagon loses control and tumbles down the hill, you can totally tell there is nobody in it. (edit)
When Charles, James, Cassandra, and Albert are praying at the Coopers' graves, it is implied that Charles pulled them out of the rubble and dug them two graves--yet Charles' hands and clothes are completely spotless. (edit)
There is a nice moment when Charles turns to Hester-Sue for help. Hester-Sue must patiently explain to her open-minded friend the difficulties of what he is asking.

Two points here: Hester-Sue mentions that she would have to legally adopt the children and the state would not allow a black woman to adopt two white children. Why would she have to adopt them? We've seen plenty of examples throughout the series of children being taken in by unrelated families. Albert lived with the family, attended school and used the name Ingalls long before he was adopted by them. In The Wisdom of Solomon, Charles enrolled a black boy in Walnut Grove's school while the boy stayed with him. In part II of this episode, James and Cassandra will attend school while staying with the Ingalls without benefit of adoption.

Secondly, though Charles himself seems devoid of prejudices, I find it hard to believe that he would realistically think that Hester-Sue would have no problem taking in James and Cassandra.

Reply: I think the main point being made with this was that Charles was so desperate to find a place for James and Cassandra, he wasn't thinking straight. He was essentially begging Hester Sue to take the children, and his concern for them impaired his ability to look at the reality of the situation. This is evident when Hester Sue looks at him and says, "I've never seen you like this." (edit)
