The renovated Canton armory, which was destroyed by floods in September 2004, was used for the first time Saturday and dedicated to WWII veterans which were once headquartered in the facility.

Armory dedicated to WWII vets

by PEGGY MANNING

Senior staff writer

CANTON — The Mayor’s and Aldermen’s Labor Day Ball Saturday offered a night to reflect back on the original use of the Canton armory, while it was also an opportunity to share with the public the new look for the building after it was flooded two years ago.

State and local dignitaries were joined by almost 200 people for the event.

“If you folks had seen this building two years ago, you would have wondered how it could ever be repaired,” said Canton Mayor Pat Smathers.

“Who would have thought after two massive floods, we would be able to turn the building into something like this?” Mayor Pro Tem Mike Ray asked. “We wanted to come back better and we have.”

In September 2004, two back-to-back floods destroyed the armory, along with the Senior Citizens Center housed in one portion of the building.

“It’s nice to see what they have done with this facility,” said Mark Clasby, director of the Haywood County Economic Development Commission. “I would hope it gets lots of use.”

Midway through the ball, Smathers called a brief meeting of the town’s governing board. The only action taken was to adopt a resolution renaming the armory the 30th Signal Armory Center. The armory was home to the 30th Signal Company.

Six surviving members of the 30th Signal Company —James Buell, Flossie Roberts, Jack Blyth, Alvin Cooper, Herman Towe and Howard Grogan — were were present for the rededication and renaming of the building. Also present was Brigadier General Timothy Lawing.

“I can’t imagine what these guys went through in World War II. Because of individuals like them I am able to stand here and talk to you,” Lawing said.

“This was quite an honor,” Flossie Roberts said. After the rededication, guests spent the remainder of the evening dancing, something that would not have been possible, Smathers said, without the commitment of Town Manager Bill Stamey and Haywood County’s legislative delegation at the time — Rep. Ray Rapp, Rep. Phil Haire and Sen. Joe Sam Queen.

Renovation of the armory cost almost $1 million, but most of the money was provided from the state. “None came from local taxes,” Smathers said. “We are so blessed with a good town manager and local delegation in Raleigh.”

Smathers said the armory will be able to accommodate up to 600 people for meetings, dances or other functions.

“This is one of the greatest places in Western North Carolina,” Smathers said.

As one of the state representatives, Phil Haire, and his wife joined others on the dance floor, James Titus, the man whose company was responsible for the repairs of the armory, cringed as he watched shoes scuffing the new wood floor.

An ice sculpture gracing a fruit and dessert table was shaped in the form of an 1891 wrought iron bridge from which the town’s name originated. While trying to come up with a suitable name for the town, one of the town leaders at that time was admiring the bridge and saw a bridge plate that contained the words “Wrought Iron Bridge Co. Builders, Canton, Ohio.”

The town leader, Cash Mingus, convinced other members of the town’s governing board that Canton should be the name for the town.

A few years later, in 1906, Peter G. Thomson arrived in Canton to build a new pulp and paper mill. That year, the first Labor Day celebration was held in Canton. Saturday night’s ball was one of numerous events commemorating the town’s 100th year of Labor Day celebrations.
