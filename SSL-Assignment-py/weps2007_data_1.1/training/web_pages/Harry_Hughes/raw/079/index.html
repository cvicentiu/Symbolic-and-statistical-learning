State Politics & Policy | Maryland Stem Cell Advocates Form Not-For-Profit, Push for $25M in States Funds for Research
[Aug 11, 2005]

      Maryland advocates for human embryonic stem cell research on Tuesday announced plans to form the Maryland Families for Stem Cell Research, a not-for-profit group that aims to generate support for such research, and called on Gov. Robert Ehrlich (R) to allocate $25 million for research efforts in the fiscal year 2007 budget, the Baltimore Sun reports. The advocacy group -- led by former Maryland Govs. Harry Hughes (D), William Schaefer (D) and Marvin Mandel (D) and several state legislators -- also promised that a bill authorizing state embryonic stem cell funding will reach the floor during the next legislative session before any other measures. "If it takes everything I have, ...we are going to get this bill past the Senate this year," state Sen. Paula Hollinger (D), who chairs the state Senate Education, Health and Environmental Affairs Committee, said (Skalka, Baltimore Sun, 8/10). A bill that would have authorized about $23 million in state funding for embryonic stem cell research failed to pass under a Senate filibuster threat in the final day of this year's session. Advocates said they plan to raise thousands of dollars for a public awareness campaign in advance of next year's legislative session (Wagner, Washington Post, 8/10).

Next Steps
Hughes said he sent a letter to Ehrlich on Friday requesting that funding for embryonic stem cell research be included in the state budget, but he said he had not yet received a response. Ehrlich spokesperson Henry Fawell said the governor plans to respond to Hughes' letter, but he declined to comment on what the response would be. Fawell added that Ehrlich "has always been very supportive of the concept of stem cell research" and "is currently working with Maryland's medical institutions and research community to ensure they have the resources they need to be at the forefront of medical research." When asked if Ehrlich would be willing to commit public funds to embryonic stem cell research in FY 2007, Fawell said, "It is still very early. The discussions are ongoing" (Stuckey, Washington Times, 8/10). State House Speaker Michael Busch (D) said he thought legislation authorizing research funding would pass the House again next year. However, the legislation's prospects in the Senate are unclear, according to the Post. State Sen. Andrew Harris (R), a leading opponent of the state bill, on Tuesday said, "As far as I know, nothing's changed," adding, "The science hasn't changed. The ethics haven't changed. I think we'll see the same outcome again" (Washington Post, 8/10).
