No Photo Ex-governor's book-signing revives the past
By WENDI WINTERS For The Capital

The crowd of 100 or so at the Maryland State Archives yesterday afternoon wasn't there to research family trees or look at microfiche of old land records.

Many were boldface names from another political era - like former state senator Gerald Weingrad and former delegate Elizabeth Smith.

They were all there to praise the man some called Maryland's "underrated governor," Harry Hughes, and celebrate the publication of his memoir, "My Unexpected Journey: The Autobiography of Gov. Harry Roe Hughes."

"Any successes I've had along the way are attributable to the very good people who worked for me over the years," Mr. Hughes said, gesturing to the crowd.

Mr. Hughes worked with former Baltimore Sun reporter John W. Frece for 18 months on the 256-page book published last month by History Press. The hardback volume brims with detailed, fact-packed observations of the behind-the-scenes machinations on the leading issues of the day.

"John would come down to Denton on Sundays, spend a day on the Eastern Shore, and reminisce," said Pat Hughes, the former governor's wife. "It's collaborative, but it's Harry's book. I'm so glad he wrote it. It's an unusual book. It was an unusual political time."

Politicians - famous and infamous - from Eastern Shore delegates and several U.S. presidents to China's Mao Zedong and Deng Xiaoping are viewed through Mr. Hughes' lens on history.

"Gov. Hughes tells it as he remembers it from the vantage point of today, helping us, in a lively, nicely illustrated and eminently readable narrative, to better understand the times and the decision-making process that shaped his years as a state senator, cabinet member and governor," said Dr. Edward C. Papenfuse, state archivist. "If we are to continue to improve public policy, we need to remind ourselves with memoirs like this."

Mr. Hughes said his legacy was "restoring integrity to Maryland government." His two predecessors, Spiro Agnew and Marvin Mandel, left high office in disgrace. Over a two-hour period yesterday, Mr. Hughes and members of his family mingled with sports jacket- and khaki-clad politicians, former staff members, friends and relatives.

A few dozen lined up to buy copies of the book for $36.99 - some of the profits are being donated to the archives for education and outreach efforts. Still others waited in line for Mr. Hughes and Mr. Frece to sign their copies.

Former governor Parris Glendening strolled in, wearing a thin yellow T-shirt under his sports jacket, leafing through a copy of the book. Sen. Paul S. Sarbanes was close behind, his own copy under his arm.

"I haven't had the pleasure of working with a more dedicated, committed, decent public servant than Harry Hughes, the first secretary of Maryland's Department of Transportation," said Mr. Sarbanes, a former state legislator who's retiring at the end of this year.

"I was struck by Harry's respect for branches of government and his willingness to get them to work through policy matters together and not as an adversary. He's a public servant who sought results, not glory - a team player who resisted the quick fix. He's fair-minded and deliberative."

The autobiography, Mr. Sarbanes said, "opens a window" on a chapter in the state's history.

Connie Lieber, secretary of state planning during the Hughes administration, said the Bay Initiative Laws passed under Mr. Hughes in 1984, which created more than 300 new jobs in various government agencies to plan, clean up and monitor conditions in the bay, were a significant highlight.

"What we did that was so innovative was to marry the health of the bay to land use," Ms. Leiber said.

Mr. Glendening recalled the bitter gubernatorial race in 1967, when Mr. Agnew was the surprise winner.

"There were similar incidents with the next governor," he said. "When people began to doubt the government, Gov. Hughes was elected. He spoke out on issues of racism. He worked to create a just society and took leadership on issues of environmental policy. The work he did all those years ago so decisively affects our lives today."

Another guest was the governor's grandson, Andrew White, who's nearly 19 and a student at a college his grandfather helped to found - Chesapeake Community College. It's been a roller-coaster year for the White family. Andrew's father, C. Douglas White, who married his mother in a ceremony in the Governor's Mansion in January 1985, passed away in April.

Six weeks ago, the younger Mr. White and his wife, Allyson, gave birth to their first child, Andrew Jr., making great-grandparents of Mr. and Mrs. Hughes.

Several guests teased Mr. White about his recent ailment. Hospitalized last month with extreme fatigue, he was diagnosed with a potentially embarrassing condition for a Maryland Democrat: the tick-born rickettsial infectious blood disease Ehrlichiosis.

"It's being treated with antibiotics," Mr. White stated. "It's taken a couple of weeks to get rid of it."

Members of the mostly Democratic crowd were quick to draw the connection between the disease and one of Mr. Hughes' successors, Gov. Robert L. Ehrlich Jr.

Quipped Mr. Weingrad, "We're treating Ehrlichiosis in the state with a new election. It's taken over three years to get rid of it."

Wendi Winters is a freelance writer living on the Broadneck Peninsula.
