Cityscape:
Bay cleanup could be Hughes’ lasting legacy

Commentary by ERIC SMITH, Staff Writer

Former Maryland governor Harry R. Hughes has written a book, and while it’s not exactly a page-turner, it’s still an engrossing look back at the good old days (or bad old days, depending on your viewpoint) of state politics.

Titled “My Unexpected Journey,” Mr. Hughes’ memoir nevertheless covers the expected ground of his childhood on the Eastern Shore and his early path to the Governor’s Mansion. Soon enough, though, he starts to drop some familiar names and ones that aren’t so familiar.

Marvin Mandel, Jeffrey Levitt, Stephen Sachs, W. Dale Hess and Robert Irsay are well known to those of us who were around in the 1970s and 1980s. These elected and unelected characters loomed large in the headlines of the time either for their good deeds or their misdeeds.

Some others who appear in Mr. Hughes’ autobiography, however, are just forgettable old pols whose stars shined briefly and then faded away: George Snyder, Sam Bogley, Victor Crawford, Roy Staten, Casper Taylor, Thomas Hunter Lowe, Harry McGuirk and Mickey Steinberg aren’t household names anymore, if indeed they ever were.

It seems that In Maryland we remember the governors better than other political players, but there is a good reason for that.

Back in the mid-19th century, Maryland defaulted on its state bonds because the General Assembly had unwisely invested too heavily in boat canals. When it came time to write a new state constitution, lawmakers gave the office of the governor extraordinary financial powers, much more than would be conferred on other state executives.

Another financial crisis arose in the early 20th century, and as a result Maryland’s governor was granted additional control over the state budget.

Today the General Assembly isn’t allowed to add to the budget or even move money around within it. The governor still has an unusual amount of power over Maryland’s finances.

So governors are memorable because they are powerful, and Harry Hughes apparently used a lot of that power to combat bay pollution. According to his book, he created or helped to create the Critical Areas Act, the rockfish ban, the Chesapeake Bay Trust and the Chesapeake Bay Program.

These were — and continue to be — among the most successful and effectively managed programs on the bay. Mr. Hughes deserves plenty of credit for them.

The bay is a long way from being healthy, of course, and it will take the efforts of many future governors to clean it up. The most memorable governor of all is likely to be the one who finally achieves that goal.

- No Jumps-

Published December 11, 2006, The Capital, Annapolis, Md.
