Who's that boy?

  • Print

  • Large font

Colin Hughes-Smith (left) with his adopted Ethiopian son Atatafi Harry and David Kavenagh with Noah, also from Ethiopia, represent the new face of inter-country adoption in Australia.
Photo: Paul Rovere

Larissa Dubecki
October 21, 2006

THE parents of Atatafi Harry Hughes-Smith take an uncomplicated approach to the cultural identity of their Ethiopian-born adopted son: to them, he's an "Aussieopian".

Vivienne O'Shannessy and partner Colin Hughes-Smith are the new face of inter-country adoption, encouraging their child to forge meaningful links with his birth culture and becoming mini-experts on the country — its food, culture and peculiarities — in the process.

The three-year-old livewire hurtling around an Ethiopian cafe in Footscray is oblivious to this week's debate over the moral, ethical, social and legal quagmire of inter-country adoption and its impact on the identity of the child.

Ironically, it was sparked by that mother of reinvention, Madonna, who over her 23-year career has invented and discarded various personas as though they were yesterday's fashions.

But her latest role — that of philanthropist and Third World crusader — isn't as anodyne as wearing underwear as outerwear.

Her adoption of an infant boy from the impoverished African nation of Malawi has seen the Material Girl weathering accusations ranging from vanity to neo-colonialism, and with them serious concerns about the fate of young David Banda.

The Madonna imbroglio has incensed Australian groups associated with inter-country adoption, who say it trivialises a serious issue and creates the misconception that Third World children are another commodity.

O'Shannessy, who with her husband waited two years for their longed-for son and face another wait of more than three years for his sibling, bristles at reports Madonna was given photographs of 12 carefully vetted children from which to choose.

"Is that really what it's all about?" she asks. "We weren't given a photo of Harry until just before we met him. It's about creating a family, not choosing a child from a photo. It's meant to be about the best interests of the child."

Any Australians hoping to emulate Madonna's latest foray will quickly find it isn't enough to charter a private jet, pluck the cutest child from an orphanage and get home in time for the gym.

The process under state and federal regulations is so arduous anyone without the determination of an Olympic athlete would call it quits long before confronting the merits of cloth versus disposable nappies.

Between July 2005 and April this year, 60 children born overseas were allocated to Victorians. China and India accounted for more than half, the rest were from Ethiopia, Hong Kong, South Korea, the Philippines, Sri Lanka and Thailand.

Each adoptive couple would have spent thousands of dollars and several years undergoing the exhaustive multi-tiered process administered by the Department of Human Services.

In the hands of the gossip rags, recent adoptions of Third World children by Hollywood celebrities such as Angelina Jolie and Meg Ryan have been characterised as mercy missions. The situation was not helped by Madonna's statement that her aim was to "help one child escape an extreme life of hardship, poverty and in many cases death", the implication being that young David was the lucky winner of a golden ticket to a life of luxury.

Human rights groups in Malawi trying to stop the adoption have raised concerns about the care David will receive; Madonna can certainly satisfy his material requirements but will she be able to satisfy his needs for love, companionship and stability?

David Holmes, chief executive of the British Association for Adoption and Fostering, warned that inter-cultural adoptions were a risky business. "They will need to consider how best to reinforce the child's ethnic and cultural heritage and their sense of identity. Every adoption will have challenges but this is the additional dimension."

Adoption in Australia comes under the rubric of "the best interests of the child" — perhaps a phrase Madonna should look up, given that she abandoned her new son to the care of her personal assistant and caught an earlier flight back to London.

But the definition has been reinvented. From the beginning of Australia's involvement in inter-country adoptions with the 1975 Operation Babylift of Vietnamese war orphans, the assumption was that the child's background would be forgotten as he or she assimilated into Australian society.

Times have changed. The DHS Intercountry Adoption Service now requires parents to attend education sessions about the child's country and the impact of inter-country adoption on their child's sense of identity.

The Victorian Adoptive Families Association recommends prospective adoptive parents consider their willingness to become bi-cultural: "That is, to accept the child's culture as an everyday part of their lives."

In a practical sense, that means Atatafi Harry and his Ethiopian adoptee friend Noah Kavenagh, 3, attend weekly playgroups for children adopted from overseas; each child's social calendar is dotted with myriad celebrations and functions for both adoptees and the wider local Ethiopian community.

"If you didn't want to learn about Ethiopian culture, why would you adopt an Ethiopian child?" his father says.

Hughes-Smith is aware of the political implications of a white couple adopting a black child, but says their role in the Ethiopian community here is largely embraced: "Does the whole community embrace it? No. But the attitude of many Ethiopians we have met has been, 'God has chosen you for this'."

Not that every case of inter-country adoption is reducible to a healthy relationship with two cultures. For Bec Fleet, 26, finding her Sri Lankan birth mother was a bittersweet event that provoked guilt that she had enjoyed a privileged life.

"There was a sense of closure, but it was a difficult time for me," she says. "If anything, it made me feel my adoption experience had been a very positive one and it made me seriously consider adopting one or two children from Sri Lanka."

Violeta Schubert, a Melbourne University social anthropologist specialising in kinship, warns that contact with the birth culture also brings the potential for alienation.

"It's necessary to remember that the child is an individual. There's the very real chance that they somehow become representative of a culture and that's just as miserable as not receiving answers to those 'Who am I?' questions."

Indigo Willing was 14 months old when she was adopted from Vietnam in 1972 and later founded support group Adopted Vietnamese International. She points out that the shift in approaches to identity were driven by the now-adult adoptees.

"People who weren't able to have access to information about their past and weren't able to explore their cultural identity, as a result felt on the margins," she says.

"Transnational adoption is uncharted territory. It's only been happening for 30 years in Australia so what we need to do is talk to the real experts, which are the people who have been through adoption themselves, the adoptees, and from those discussions they have said they would have liked more access to their language and their culture."

Most adoptees eventually search for their birth parents, Willing says. "If they begin with next to no information at all about where they come from, it will be a lot harder to reconnect with a family, say in Ethiopia or India, having no experience with those cultures. It's a matter of getting the balance right so these kids can be like every other Australian kid and they can have a multicultural past if that's what their past is."

In O'Shannessy's understanding, the difference with adoption is that the parents are not creating their own child but inheriting somebody else's, "and with that comes obligations".

"Parents today are totally dedicated. No one gets through the system unless they're really prepared to go the extra mile and integrate that child's background into their own lives."

For those Americans not willing to go the extra mile, adoptee advocates keenly anticipate the tightening of US laws with the ratification of the 1993 Hague Convention on the Protection of Children and Co-operation in Respect of Intercountry Adoption.

The convention sets minimum international standards for adoptions, seeks to ensure that the best interests of the child are paramount and aims to prevent abuses such as abductions, sale or trafficking in children. Australia has observed its standards since 1998; O'Shannessy and others hope its planned ratification by the US next year will prevent more cases of rich Westerners window-shopping for their latest family addition.

"Of course it won't prevent it outright," O'Shannessy says. "But it might help make it a bit harder."

In the meantime, Madonna's latest aquisition will, of course, have an additional weight to bear. As young David Banda becomes David Ritchie — or loses his original name altogether — his identity is already being lost to that of "celebrity".

Larissa Dubecki is an Age reporter and television columnist.
