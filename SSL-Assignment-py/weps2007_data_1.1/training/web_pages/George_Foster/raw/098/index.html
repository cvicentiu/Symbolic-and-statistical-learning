In Search of George Washington Foster Jr: No Longer Omitted from History

by Peter Williams
Introduction

      Unearthing biographical information about this enigmatic architect has been a strenuous task. As a Kean University undergraduate student group, this team had a mission to uncover any and all information relevant to the study of African-American architecture in the beginning of the twentieth century. Thus, we began with George Washington Foster Jr, New Jersey’s first licensed black architect and one of the first architects of African descent in the United States.

      George Foster made vital and enduring contributions. He not only solidified the foundation for future architects of African descent to build upon, but also helped usher in the African-American artistic movement of the 1920s and 1930s known today as the Harlem Renaissance. His legacy to African-American architectural history, moreover, can be viewed as one of the cornerstones for the entire movement.

      In order to understand fully the Harlem Renaissance in its proper chronological framework, one must understand the life and times of this architectural pioneer. George Washington Foster Jr was more than an actual builder of great architectural structures; he aided metaphorically and symbolically in the construction of African-American artistic integrity and culture.
