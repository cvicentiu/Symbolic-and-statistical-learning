George Foster: Confident and Ready

Shanahan Shares Faith That Foster Will Fare Well at RT

By Andrew Mason
DenverBroncos.com

ENGLEWOOD, Colo. -- Even the largest returning player on the team needs friends in high places -- especially when he's being asked to step from relative obscurity into one of the most vital spots on the field.

BRONCOS TV: GEORGE FOSTER IN ACTION

Such is the situation for George Foster as he proceeds through the offseason. In the perpetual transition prevalent in professional football, Foster has as strong a grip on a first-team slot as one can have when possessing only one game played and no starts to his name, lining up at right tackle beginning with the team's mini-camp in May.

The head coach is in Foster's corner, so like Carl Spackler, he's got that going for him, which is nice. But Mike Shanahan offers a caveat to his optimistic assessment of Foster's progress.

"He's going to be an excellent football player for us. We've improved ourselves already (with Foster at right tackle)," Shanahan said. "I've got some high expectations for him. But he's got to stay healthy."

Twin injuries scuttled his rookie campaign -- first, the lingering wrist problem that kept him from full-time work as a senior at the University of Georgia, and then another injury early in training camp that sidelined him for over two weeks -- a time that would have been crucial to his development.

With practice time lost, almost all of the learning Foster did last year was from the sidelines. He didn't play until Week 17 at Green Bay, and even then only came off the bench.

This year, Foster's name is penciled into the first team. But that does not mean the education of the offensive tackle has ceased.

"I've got to keep learning the ins and outs of the system, get in condition and get strong," he said. "It's like a 180-(degree) turnaround. I can just go out there and play basically now instead of thinking so much.

"With the learning sessions in the morning with (the coaches during the May and June camps), you're learning why you do certain things instead of memorizing do this, now you're getting to know why certain things happen with the offense. It's a lot easier now."

There exists understandable pressure on the 2003 first-round pick to deliver and prove his worthiness of that lofty draft status. But such matters bounce harmlessly off the back of the 6-foot-5, 338-pounder.

"I just have to prove it to myself. That's the only motivation I have," he said. "You've got to keep your focus on your job and do it to the best of your ability, and then everybody's happy. You take care of everybody when you do your job right."
