George Foster -- Berkeley anthropologist

David Perlman, Chronicle Science Editor

Monday, May 22, 2006

  • Printable Version
  • Email This Article

George M. Foster Jr., a noted anthropologist at UC Berkeley who for many decades conducted pioneering studies of social change among peasant communities in Mexico and founded the field of medical anthropology, died at his Berkeley home Thursday. He was 92.

Dr. Foster was slowed by Parkinson's disease during his final years. Up until 1990, 11 years after his retirement from UC Berkeley, however, he was still traveling to Mexico to maintain the contact he had developed with the people of the town of Tzintzuntzan, where he had begun studying cultural life in 1944.

One of his long-held interests in that little Michoacan town was comparing the cultural and social institutions of today with those of the 16th century, when Spanish influence throughout Latin America was dominant.

As a medical anthropologist interested in public health, Dr. Foster contributed greatly to an understanding of how and why modern Western medical systems are accepted or rejected by developing countries; how medical beliefs and practices are transmitted among populations, and the impact of medical bureaucracies on people who receive medical care.

He was a consultant to the World Health Organization, the U.S. Agency for International Development, UNICEF and many other medical agencies, traveling widely on their behalf from Afghanistan to Peru, throughout Asia, Africa and Latin America.

At Berkeley, Dr. Foster chaired the university's anthropology department twice and, during his career, developed a campus-wide reputation as a demanding but inspiring teacher. In a ceremony honoring him when he became a professor emeritus upon his retirement in 1979, his colleagues, Professors Eugene Hammel and Laura Nader, noted affectionately: "He does not cozy up to his students. Learning from Foster is a no-nonsense proposition and seldom easy on the student's ego."

But he communicated easily and with affection among the peasants whom he got to know so well during his countless research stays in Tzintzuntzan. His work -- particularly in Mexico -- made him an expert in fields of peasant economics, folklore, linguistics, community development and public health, among many other more recondite areas of anthropology. He was also the author of 21 books and monographs and more than 100 scientific articles and, during the 1960s, served as president of the American Anthropological Association.

Dr. Foster was born in Sioux Falls, S.D., and earned his bachelor's degree at Northwestern University. While studying for his doctorate in anthropology at Berkeley, which he earned in 1941, he completed field research among the Yuki Indians of California and the Sierra Popoluca Indians of southern Veracruz, Mexico.

He was director of the Smithsonian Institution's Institute of Social Anthropology from 1946 to 1952, and a year later joined the faculty at Berkeley, where he remained for the rest of his career.

Dr. Foster's wife, Mary LeCron Foster, whom he met when the two were undergraduates at Northwestern, followed his interests and traveled with him until she died in 2002. He is survived by a son, Jeremy Foster of Basalt, Colo.; a daughter, Melissa Bowerman of the Netherlands; five grandchildren; and four great-grandchildren.

A memorial service is being planned.
