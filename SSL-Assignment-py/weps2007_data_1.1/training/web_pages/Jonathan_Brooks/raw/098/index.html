Senior citizens visit Healthfest

Rhonda Simmons
Staff Writer
Sunday, November 5, 2006

 

Dr. Jonathan Brooks measures Frances Nethken's range of motion with the arthrodial protractor. Brooks is a chiropractic physician at Brooks Chiropractic Clinic in Culpeper. (Staff Photo, Rhonda Simmons) 
 

Steward and Frances Nethken got a healthy start Saturday morning.

Alongside hundreds of Culpeper residents they visited vendors at Healthfest, an annual one-stop health fair sponsored by Healthy Culpeper, at Culpeper Regional Library to gather health-related information and received health assessments.

“This is a really great thing for all of us senior citizens,” said 75-year-old Steward Nethken. “As we develop in age we need to learn about things that we might need.”

Among the 42 vendors were Culpeper Regional Hospital, Miracle Ear, Culpeper Medical Associates and Culpeper Pharmacy.

CCL Director Susan Keller said Saturday’s event turned out extremely well and said the library’s involvement stems from its Healthy Culpeper membership.

“This is also another form of information and another way to get the information out to the public,” Keller said, as she observed patrons interacting with vendors. “And that’s what we are about. We are an information place with all sorts of resources.”

Visiting the vendors
Frances Nethken, 86, received blood pressure and cholesterol evaluations from CRH medical associates.

“Everything checked out good,” said Frances Nethken.
Next, the Nethken’s got their body fat levels checked by Germanna Community College nursing student Lillian Jones.

First, she asked for their weight, height and age. Then had them grab the handles of the body fat counter.

Jones said for women (in a higher age range) the target range is 18-25 percent and for older males (in that same age range) the target range is 12-20 percent.

Frances said she will “try harder to lose weight.”
“If you find yourself over that goal then you need to work on your diet and exercise,” Jones said.

Next, Frances Nethken sat down at Chiropractor Jonathan Brooks’ booth.

She received a range of motion test and a grip strength test.
Dr. Jonathan Brooks placed an arthrodial protractor, a measuring device used for joint flexibility, around her neck and asked her to slowly turn her head in each direction as far as she could.

She measured about 50 degrees on the right and about 55 degrees on the left.

“It’s a little bit limited. It should be about 80 degrees on either side,” he said, explaining the outcome of the test to Frances Nethken. “More than likely arthritis will decrease range of motion in your neck.”

Next, Brooks used a grip strength dynamometer to measure her hand strength.

“It’s not too bad however it is a little limited,” said Brooks. “Grip strength is based on what occupation you are in. If you do a lot of work with your hand then you can do quite a bit more.”
Brooks said the purpose of the grip test is based on each individual.

“It’s more of comparing one hand to the other rather than one person to another person,” he said. “People vary so much in their strength and it’s hard to have a baseline.”

Frances Nethken said Brooks was the most informative.
“I like the way he explained everything to me,” she said. “I was really impressed with him. He was real nice too.”

Further reading
Each vendor had an abundance of brochures and offered a variety of statistics about their organizations.

Culpeper County Resource Specialist Jenny Biche from Aging Together, a partnership of more than 100 groups and individuals, said how important it is to address and support senior issues in this country.

By the 2020, one in four citizens will be considered a senior citizen, said Biche.

“So we are trying to take steps now to get agencies in place so that people who live in this county won’t have to go to other places like Charlottesville,” said Biche. “They can still live in Culpeper and get the services that they need.”

Inside the main part of the library, AARP representative Willis Wood handed out brochures about the program, which provides services for people 50 and older.

Wood said members receive numerous discounts on insurance, meals and travel expenses.

“We are trying to work with seniors to make sure we don’t get left behind,” said Wood.
