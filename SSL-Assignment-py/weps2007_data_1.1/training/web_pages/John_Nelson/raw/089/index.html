History of the lodge

     John Nelson applied for a homestead in 1886 and soon brought his daughters to the split shake cabin he built in the forests of Sequoias and Ponderosa Pines. By the mid-1890's Camp Nelson had become a popular vacation spot, in spite of the fact that vacationers had to ride horseback or walk by trail most of the way from Springville. In the late 1890's Nelson and Carmel Wilson built a pack station about four miles above Springville. There was room at the station for customers to park their buggies or wagons, and later automobiles, while they packed into the mountains, often stopping at Camp Nelson. 

     Gradually John Nelson expanded his facilities. A small store was built to supply visitors, and soon other families began building their own cabins nearby. Some of those cabins are still in active use by the descendents of the original inhabitants.  Soon Nelson installed a sawmill on the creek just below the lodge, and construction of conventional cabins began.

     The rapid development of Camp Nelson began when a road was extended from the Forks in 1922. The original road was narrow, crooked, and dusty. Traffic had to be controlled to ensure that cars coming in opposite directions would not meet. There was no way for them to pass! Later the road was widened and resurfaced. Many more families built cabins in the Sequoias near Nelson's lodge in the years that followed.
The second hotel at Camp Nelson nearing completion around 1907. The boy on the mule is Nelson Smith, grandson of John Nelson. This log cabin hotel burned in 1927.

 

 

  In 1927 a major disaster occurred: Nelson's lodge burned to the ground! Nelson Smith, grandson of John Neslon, immediately began construction of a new lodge. It was to be quite an impressive structure with stone facing on the outside and, a large lobby with two tremendous fireplaces and other facilities to match. Costs were higher than expected and the Smiths ran into financial trouble. The structure remained uncompleted until years later. In 1951 the Southern California Edison Company extended electrical service to Camp Nelson and about the same time the road was extended all the way to the campgrounds at Quaking Aspen Meadow. The Smiths retired soon after.

     The beautiful lodge has been renovated and is now open again, even as improvements continue. Located in one of the most naturally beautiful places on Earth, the lodge has all the modern amenities you would expect. Bed-and-breakfast service will be available soon.
