B.         PRESIDENT’S REPORT

 

Dr. Herbert: Thank you very much Mr. President. My report will not be long this morning. First, I would like to join you in expressing appreciation to Chancellor Patterson-Randles for the wonderful job that they’ve done. It’s always impressive to visit our campuses and see the excellent work that’s being done. I was especially interested during the lunch hour to hear about the incredible work that our students are doing, whether it be West Nile Virus research, technology projects or cultural activities. This is becoming a nexus in the region in terms of cultural activities, and I think all of you on this campus should be proud of those accomplishments.

 

Later in the meeting Mr. President, you and the members of the board will be asked to vote on promotion and tenure cases for several faculty members. This was my first opportunity to take part in that process. I want to observe that we should be very proud of the accomplishments of our faculties on each of the campuses. What this process clearly demonstrated to me is that we have a group of colleagues who truly care about undergraduate education. They are committed to train the next generation of scholars. We are conducting path-breaking research, and there is a culture on the campuses that speaks to the importance of mentoring new faculty members. We are very grateful for the depth of talent that we have and we’re very proud to submit these recommendations to you.

 

I’d also like to acknowledge the success of the IU Foundation. This was mentioned yesterday in one of our meetings. One of the things I would note is that a recent ranking shows that our foundation is among the top 1½% of all colleges and universities in the nation in the amount of support that it receives from the private sector. An article a few days ago indicated that IU ranked seventh in the nation among public universities, and what we should be especially proud of is that we’re second in the Big Ten with regard to contributions to the Foundation. This past year we had a record number of donors, and again, it’s very impressive to see the growth of support over the course of the past decade. As I’ve gone around the state and the country meeting with donors and alumni, one of the things that stands out is the love that all of them have for this institution, and their support for our continuing efforts to move even higher among the ranks of the best universities in America. It also was interesting to note that in looking at some of the support figures that IU has placed in the top 20 in the country in 12 of the last 14 years in terms of donors. So, once again I think that says a great deal about the love of the institution and commitment to helping us achieve the aspirations that we have.

 

Finally, I’d like to mention one other item on the agenda. It relates to the establishment of an enhanced operation in the area of government relations.

 

I think all of us recognize that the partnerships we have with the public sector are extremely important not only at the state level, but at the federal as well. And during these very difficult times the nature of that relationship becomes even more important. And so what I would like to do is to establish in a more formal context an Office of Governmental Relations within Indiana University that will be headed by a vice president. Strategically, units reporting to that vice president will focus on state relations, federal relations, and Hoosiers for Higher Education.

 

The key point here is that as we deal and operate in a context that is becoming increasingly more complex, more demanding of communication, we simply must move to a new strategy that is built upon teamwork and collaboration. This was very evident during this last legislative session, in which several of the vice presidents and I, in addition to our  state legislative affairs staff, worked together, and with Bill Cochran and other members of the legislature, to address some of our major challenges and needs at the federal level. One of the things that stands out is that we simply must have greater collaboration throughout the university. There are a number of strategic decisions that have to be made. We have no choice but to take every step that we can to increase the level of federal support for our operations. Some of that support will occur in the context of the traditional competitive process, but also we can not ignore the reality that Congress continues to appropriate monies to support special initiatives and projects on our campuses. It is the case as well that we must become more aggressive in terms of partnerships with other universities in these activities. In order to accomplish that I think its essential that we have a senior level person that has overarching responsibilities for these activities who also is working with the other vice presidents and me as we deal strategically with how we are going to accomplish some of these activities.

 

So, you will have before you a proposal to establish an office of Vice President for Government Relations, and I am recommending to you the appointment of Dr. Thomas Healy. He has worked in higher education for more than 35 years. He has been the coordinator of Washington lobbying for the state universities of Florida, during a period where he was able to lead efforts that resulted in appropriated funds, not through the competitive process but through appropriated funds of over $125 million. So he understands the federal level, he’s worked for 20-plus years at the state level, and brings a wide array of skills to this position that will make a significant difference for us as we continue this process. In the final analysis, we must be more strategic in all of these activities, must take it to a higher level. My goal is to reach out to many more Hoosiers than we have in the past. I want to establish closer relationships in these efforts with the members of our advisory groups and others so that we can assure that the important messages that we want to convey are, in fact, heard. We are very fortunate to have several members of the legislature who understand the importance of higher education, who understand the special role that our university can play in each of our campuses in the process of enhancing the quality of life for all Hoosiers. In that context, we’re especially appreciative of the kinds of things that Bill Cochran and his colleagues have done to achieve those aspirations, and we want to do all we can to help him help us over the course of the next several years.

 

Thank you very much Mr. President that concludes my report.

2.         Real Estate Matters for Approval

 

INDIANA UNIVERSITY BLOOMINGTON

 

a.         Approval is requested to apply to have the Woodburn House added to the National Register of Historic Places.

 

The original structure of the Woodburn house was built in 1829.  The house came into the Woodburn family with the purchase of the home by Professor James Woodburn in 1855.  Dr. Herman B Wells lived in the house from 1932 until he moved into the President’s House in 1957.  The home was presented as a gift to the University in 1941.  To date, the house has continued its tradition of hospitality for alumni, faculty, staff, students and friends of Indiana University.  In addition to its rich University history, the house has architecture that is of historical significance in the central Indiana area. 

 

Unanimously approved on a motion duly made and seconded.

 

b.         Approval to apply for the addition of Raintree House, located at 112 North Bryan Street in Bloomington, to the National Register of Historic Places.

 

The Raintree House, built in c. 1845, is a Greek Revival style brick house.  The house is in good condition.  It has had few structural alterations and retains much of its original materials, including glass and decorative woodwork. The house is one of the few remaining Georgian plan houses of the 1840s-1860s in Monroe County. 

 

The House was originally part of the Millen farmstead but has had University affiliations since 1923.  During that year, Agnes E. Wells, Dean of the Women at Indiana University from 1919 to 1938 and Professor of Mathematics, purchased the house.  In 1946, the Trustees purchased the house from a subsequent owner, renting the house to faculty and staff and eventually medical students.  The house was later sold to Newton Stallknecht, Professor of Philosophy and Comparative Literature from 1949-1977, and his wife, Ann.  Mrs. Stallknecht sold the house to the Foundation in 1969, and it was once again deeded to the Trustees in 1992.  Thomas Clark, who came to the University as a Sesquicentennial Professor, became Executive Secretary of the Organization of American Historians in 1970 and moved their Headquarters to Indiana University.  That same year, Dr. Herman B Wells arranged to have the Headquarters located at the Raintree House, where it remains today.

 

Unanimously approved on a motion duly made and seconded.

C.         COMMITTEE OF THE WHOLE

 

Trustee Eichhorn: Good morning and welcome. We are here to talk about things financial. Before we start the presentation, Dr. Herbert I think you have some remarks that you want to make?

 

Dr. Herbert: Thank you very much Mr. President. In introducing this presentation, I’d first like to make a comment about the public forum that we held a few weeks ago.

 

Without question, that forum reinforced the commitment of this board to transparency and also to your interest in listening to the citizens of Indiana and making major public policy decisions. I’d like to thank all of those who attended the forum, whether it was in person or via videoconference.  We especially appreciate the Governor’s attendance and his very helpful observations. We want to thank those who have written us, sent email messages, called, and attended that meeting as well as others on our campuses to discuss both tuition and mandatory fees. I want all of you to know that the administration has considered all of this feedback in developing the recommendations that we will make this morning to the board. I’d like to make just a couple of observations about the recommendations.

 

First with regard to tuition, we have attempted to be very sensitive to the desires of this board to limit tuition increases to the lowest level possible for all Hoosiers at both the undergraduate and graduate levels.

 

Second, we have attempted to limit undergraduate out-of-state tuition increases to the lowest level that we possibly can.

 

Finally I will tell you that with regard to tuition, we have given the deans of the graduate and professional schools some flexibility in establishing out-of-state tuition proposals. In doing that we have asked that they demonstrate sensitivity to the impact of potential out-of-state graduate tuition increases on students.  We’ve also asked them to take into account their ongoing unit costs, and finally, we’ve asked them to pay particular attention to market factors for peer programs in the Big Ten and internationally, as well. There have been modifications to some of these proposals as we’ve talked with the deans about these issues. So we are prepared this morning to offer specific proposals for 2004-2005 tuition rates for each of our campuses. 

 

The second issue that we will present to you relates to mandatory fees. In this area, again, we have attempted to be very sensitive to your desire to limit mandatory fee increases to the lowest level possible for all of our students. This morning we are prepared to offer   mandatory fee recommendations for all campuses except Bloomington. With regard to Bloomington, the matter before us relates to a potential student fee for intercollegiate athletics.

 

The rationale for the fee proposal I think, is very clear. There is a deficit in the Athletics Department that continues to grow every year. We understand that this is inconsistent with the policies and desires of the Board of Trustees. This deficit is generated by a number of things, such as rising costs of sponsoring more non-revenue sports over the course of the past few years; the university’s very strong commitment to fulfilling Title IX obligations; and also we recognize that rising tuition costs coupled with the larger number of out-of-state student athletes is creating an added burden for the department and for the support organization, the Varsity Club, which has attempted to provide help in this area, but simply cannot generate enough revenues to cover these costs.

 

So, it is clear that there is a budget crisis that must be addressed. The question that we have attempted to deal with as an administration is how should this matter best be resolved.  It is clear, at least as I look at this situation, that there is a need for greater discussion of this matter among the members of the campus community before we can make an administrative recommendation to you. This in my view is a process issue, and it’s one that we should be sensitive to. So I would ask the board this morning to allow the President to delay the presentation of the mandatory fee plan specifically for the Bloomington campus until the May meeting of the Board. If you will grant this request, it is my intention to take the following action:

 

First, I will ask the staffs in Athletics and the Budget Office to develop a report that contains the following information.  At a minimum, I’ll ask them to document the nature of the Athletics Department’s current debt, so that we have a clear sense of what structural debt means. Two, I’ll ask them to document the projected debt for 2004-05, and third, to identify alternative strategies that the department has considered to address not only the operating budget for the next fiscal year, but also the projected deficit with the attendant revenue implications.

 

I think it is essential that we have this information so that all members of the university community are in a better position to understand specifically what the issues are.
