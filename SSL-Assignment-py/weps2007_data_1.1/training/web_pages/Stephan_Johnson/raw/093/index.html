New EPA Dust Rule Is Just Plain Stupid

October 19th, 2006

I invited the Environmental Protection Agency Administrator Stephan Johnson to Iowa so we could show him their new rule called ‘fugitive dust’ is just plain stupid. The rule implies that a farmer operating in a field needs to keep dust inside his property line or he could be held accountable. Well every farmer knows that you can not combine soybeans without creating dust. The EPA Administrator claims that farmers should not have to worry about the rule, but I want them to add an exemption for agriculture to the rule so that farmers won’t get stuck spending thousands of dollars if someone sues them. I don’t think I changed his mind during his visit, but at least he knows that I have not changed my mind and I will continue to fight this rule.

    • The Hill : The following is the latest installment of Monday Morning Quarterback, a weekly Congress Blog Q&A with lobbyists. This week we interview Lisa Bos of Cassidy & Associates about education.
    • The Hill : How would you characterize Congress's performance in reforming the education system in the last five years? How has it failed? How has it succeeded?
    • Lisa Bos : To a large degree, the last five years have led to increased federal involvement in the education system that while well-intended, has taken away from state and local control. Accountability is a good thing, but it should be accountability to parents and students, not to the federal bureaucracy. Also, there are far too many education programs spread all over the federal government, many of which are obsolete and the money could be better spent. It's a little too soon to measure the successes of No Child Left Behind, but one of the programs with a lot of promise is supplemental services which I hope leads to even more educational options for parents in the future.
    • The Hill : How successful has the Pell Grant system been? Will increases in the maximum value of Pell Grants have significant impact?
    • Lisa Bos : The Pell Grant system has been very successful and has been one of the driving forces that has enabled more students access to higher education. The increase in the maximum grant will help make up for some of the lost value of the Pell Grant over the last few years as college costs have increased, but more needs to be done to address the cost of higher education.
    • The Hill : What are the advantages and disadvantages of school voucher programs? How do you think the program has worked in D.C.?
    • Lisa Bos : School choice is the only way to put parents back in control of their child's education and empower low-income families to get out of schools that are failing their kids. In a way, schools are still segregated - it's just by income, instead of race - and it is inexcusable that we allow students to be literally trapped in a school that is not providing them with the education they are entitled to. The D.C. school choice program is one of the few real education successes of the last five years, but it's going to take some time before its results are measurable.
    • The Hill : What do you think the education system in America needs most?
    • Lisa Bos : More competition and less federal regulation are key, but it also goes far beyond that. We need parents who are able to be home at night helping there kids with their homework and reading to them. We need kids with a love of learning. We need teachers with innovative ideas that they are empowered to implement in the classroom. There are a lot of pieces that need to come together but it starts with decision-making and control being at the local, not the federal, level.
