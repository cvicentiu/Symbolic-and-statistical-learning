Silent Steel : The Mysterious Death of the Nuclear Attack Sub USS Scorpion
by Stephen Johnson
USS Scorpion, a 3500-ton Skipjack class nuclear-powered attack submarine built at Groton, Connecticut, was commissioned in July 1960. Assigned to the Atlantic Fleet, she took part in the development of contemporary submarine warfare tactics and made periodic deployments to the Mediterranean Sea and other areas where the presence of a fast and stealthy submarine would be beneficial. Scorpion began another Mediterranean cruise in February 1968. The following May, while homeward bound from that tour, she was lost with her entire crew some 400 miles southwest of the Azores. In late October 1968, her remains were found on the sea floor over 10,000 feet below the surface by a towed deep-submergence vehicle deployed from USNS Mizar (T-AGOR-11). Photographs taken then and later showed that her hull had suffered fatal damage while she was running submerged and that even more severe damage occurred as she sank. The cause of the initial damage continues to generate controversy more than three decades later. (Excerpt: history.navy.mil) A new book Silent Steel due to release in December 2005 is an investigative journey of over 500 interviews, recently released data, personal archives and never before seen photographs on the USS Scorpion tragedy. Stephen Johnson the author of Silent Steel is a newspaper journalist with over 25 years experience and author of several other books. He has covered a myriad of news stories from homicides to politics and military events. In a correspondence with The Sub Report, Johnson shares the following about his new book: "In 1987 I was telephoned by a former Scorpion electrician's mate named Dan Rogers who told me of how much he hated serving aboard the Scorpion. He was angered to learn that people believed the Scorpion had been sunk by its own torpedo. He ultimately got transferred from the boat by disqualifying himself from submarine duty only to be placed aboard another submarine due to the shortage of nuclear-trained sailors. Rogers drew me into the story of the Scorpion, though I didn't write anything until 1993 when a less classified version of the inquiry transcripts became available." "Even Rogers did not realize how complicated the Scorpion's story was, although I credit him with making me aware of this unresolved mystery." "In 1994 SUBLANT at first claimed it did not have the maintenance records of the Scorpion, but accidentally discovered they had not been destroyed as they believed and they were forced by the CNO to turn them over to me. I wrote an additional story outlining the odd circumstances surrounding the Scorpion's final overhaul which was drastically reduced." "After writing a couple of general interest books with the last one being an engineering history called the Encyclopedia of Bridges and Tunnels, Wiley asked me to write a book on the Scorpion in 2002. This has been a nearly three-year effort to tell the story of this ill-fated boat." "My view of this disaster deviates from the position held by many who are convinced they have "the answer." I don't know what happened to the Scorpion, but the story behind her finals days, her loss, the investigation and the miraculous effort to find her is astonishing." Some reviews from Amazon.com "The magnitude of the tragedy of the USS Scorpion is matched only by the depth of the mystery surrounding her loss. USS Scorpion (SSN-589) This site is dedicated to 99 of my friends and their families. Hopefully all who visit will leave with a better understanding of the dangers and dedication to job and country of submarine sailors. A submarine crew is much more than a group of sailors at a duty station. It is family. Since the Scorpion has been gone it has left a void in my life and in my heart that can never be filled.
