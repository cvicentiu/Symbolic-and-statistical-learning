Ruling a small victory

Friday, December 23, 2005

By KEITH IDEC
HERALD NEWS


While Joe Mesi considered Monday's curious court ruling in Nevada as a convincing victory in his quest to fight despite suffering a brain injury during his last bout, the unbeaten Buffalo heavyweight will still likely encounter difficulty acquiring licenses from the states in which he would prefer to fight.

Although he has been cleared to re-apply there, don't expect to see another Mesi match materialize in Nevada, not in the wake of Leavander Johnson's death there three months ago. And there's a better chance of Rubin "Hurricane" Carter boxing "Butterbean" for the featherweight title outside of Paterson City Hall, with besmirched former Gov. James E. McGreevey serving as guest referee, than there is of Mesi fighting in this state.

The Stephan Johnson tragedy lingers in the minds of everyone involved in New Jersey boxing, so Paterson native Nicholas Lembo, a deputy attorney general and counsel for the State Athletic Control Board, said the state's boxing regulatory agency would be weary of licensing Mesi.

"The ruling is significant in that it frees him to apply in New Jersey or any other state of his choosing," Lembo said. "If he applied in New Jersey, his license would be carefully scrutinized due to his reported prior brain injuries. Like any applicant, he would have to meet our licensing standards. But obviously we'd have grave concerns about licensing him and he would have to be carefully reviewed by our medical experts."

Stephan Johnson, 31, died six years ago in Atlantic City, 15 days after suffering a 10th-round knockout defeat to Paul Vaden (29-3, 16 KOs) in a regional junior middleweight championship match at the Trump Taj Mahal. Queens' Johnson (27-9-1, 18 KOs) was mistakenly removed from boxing's nationally recognized medical suspension list before he faced Vaden. Otherwise, he wouldn't have been permitted to fight here, either.

Following an 11th-round TKO defeat to Fitz Vanderpool in Toronto, seven months before the Vaden fight, a post-fight CT scan indicated that Johnson had possibly suffered a minor hematoma and that further testing should've been taken to determine if there was indeed bleeding on Johnson's brain.

A required magnetic resonance imaging exam was never taken thereafter, but Johnson was still cleared to fight in Greenville, S.C., and Albany, Ga., between the Vanderpool and Vaden fights. The SACB was absolved of any wrongdoing two years ago in an Atlantic County state court.

Assuming Mesi fights again somewhere, he will become the only boxer in American history to have suffered a known subdural hematoma and later participate in a sanctioned fight.

Mesi maintains that his injury has healed and he has been backed by several leading brain specialists. Nevada judge Douglas Herndon helped his cause Monday by determining that the Nevada State Athletic Commission could only keep Mesi on its nationally recognized suspension list until his license expired. All NSAC licenses expire each Dec. 31.

Herndon's peculiar ruling rendered the vital national suspension list meaningless and cleared the way for Mesi to box again, as long as he passes medical exams required by respective states, many of which aren't as medically responsible as those in New Jersey, Nevada, New York and California.

"I'm just so thrilled," Mesi said Monday. "There are some days I woke up thinking, 'How can they stop me?' and that I was never going to get a chance to fight again. Now I have that chance."

It will be most interesting to see what happens when the 32-year-old Mesi (29-0, 25 KOs) applies for a New York license, because he was often touted as Buffalo's third professional sports franchise before suffering a subdural hematoma during a unanimous decision victory over Vassiliy Jirov (35-3-1, 30 KOs) on March 13, 2004, in Las Vegas.

Mesi most likely will be licensed to fight in a state with a weaker commission. But how much revenue would the next Mesi match generate in some random state, especially since he'll probably box at best a C-level heavyweight after a two-year layoff? We raise the money matter only because it appears to be Mesi's main ambition for continuing his career. It makes absolutely no sense otherwise, particularly when you consider the Stephan Johnson evidence. Without casino, Buffalo or HBO/Showtime/ESPN money backing him, Mesi simply won't be able to command the six-figure purses he earned for his last three fights, all of which were broadcast by HBO.

Then again, there shouldn't be an acceptable price tag attached to Mesi's long-term health. Unfortunately, Mesi seemingly disagrees.

Ruiz rematch not guaranteed

His jab-and-grab style has often made John Ruiz difficult to watch. And some might argue that he didn't deserve to defend his World Boxing Association heavyweight title again after a steroid-aided James Toney handled him with such ease on April 30 at Madison Square Garden. But Ruiz, one of boxing's genuine nice guys, deserved better than what happened to him Saturday night in Berlin.

As if dropping a majority decision defeat that drew disapproval from a mostly pro-Nicolay Valuev crowd wasn't bad enough, Ruiz, a two-time WBA belt-holder, might not get another title shot.

WBA rules prevented his handlers from including an immediate rematch clause in the Ruiz-Valuev contract, so the 7-foot, 323-pound Russian champion might not need to face Ruiz again.

Tony Cardinale, Ruiz's attorney and adviser, will try to build a strong case over the next couple months before petitioning the WBA for an immediate rematch.

The Totowa native and Passaic Valley High School graduate will present the findings of an independent judge and CompuBox punch counters at a WBA meeting in March in Hamburg, Germany. If the WBA doesn't grant Ruiz (41-6-1, 28 KOs, 1 NC) an immediate rematch, Cardinale hopes the Venezuelan sanctioning organization will at least make Ruiz its No. 1 contender. That would eventually ensure another title shot, though not necessarily against Valuev (43-0, 31 KOs).

"John definitely won the fight," Cardinale said. "He won at least nine rounds out of the 12. John clearly landed more punches and he controlled the fight. There was really some crazy scoring. John should not have lost his title. It was ridiculous."

Cardinale expects Don King to support their cause, but Ruiz's longtime promoter has a 50 percent stake in Valuev as part of a four-fight option negotiated with Wilfried Sauerland, Valuev's German promoter.

No weight, Jose

Jose Luis Castillo certainly didn't seem interested in making weight before his infamous rematch with Diego Corrales on Oct. 8, but the former lightweight champ promised that he'll hit 135 for their rubber match on Feb. 4 in El Paso, Texas.

"I'm going to work very hard for this," Castillo said. "I'm going to make weight with no problem. And if I don't make weight, I'm willing to give Top Rank half of my salary back (nearly $1 million)."

Castillo's crushing left hook in the fourth round of their second fight was overshadowed by "Weightgate." The Mexican contender officially weighed in at 138 1/2 pounds for the lightweight title fight, a pound-and-a-half more than he was before he headed to a sauna for two hours following his first failed attempt on Oct. 7. Castillo's scale struggles enabled Corrales to retain his World Boxing Council and World Boxing Organization titles despite a knockout defeat.

"I believe that it was never his intention to make weight," said Corrales, who is promoted by Wayne's Gary Shaw. "I never once questioned his performance. The guy did a good job in the fight. He won the fight. The only problem I had was the weight thing."

Regardless of the outcome, Castillo (53-7-1, 47 KOs) said this will be his last fight at lightweight. If he can overcome Corrales (40-3, 33 KOs) again, he will move to 140 pounds and seek showdowns with International Boxing Federation/WBA champ Ricky "Hitman" Hatton (40-0, 30 KOs) or WBC belt-holder Floyd Mayweather Jr. (35-0, 24 KOs), who beat Castillo twice on points in 2002.

Castillo-Corrales III will be broadcast on Showtime from the Don Haskins Center on the campus of the University of Texas-El Paso. Their stunning slugfest on May 7, which Corrales won thanks to an unfathomable comeback in the 10th round, prompted Showtime to televise the rematch via pay-per-view. Surprisingly, however, Corrales-Castillo II drew only 225,000 buys, 180,000 less than the relatively uneventful Roy Jones Jr.-Antonio Tarver rubber match a week earlier in Tampa, Fla.
