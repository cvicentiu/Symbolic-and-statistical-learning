 Stephan Johnson dies at 31
Associated Press

 ATLANTIC CITY, N.J. -- Stephan Johnson wanted to keep boxing, even after a knockout sent him to the hospital last April.

His manager urged him to quit. So did his mother.

Johnson said no.

His death Sunday at 31 from injuries in a Nov. 20 bout underscored the price Johnson paid for ignoring those and other warnings about his health.

Johnson had hoped to make enough money in the ring to move his mother from the projects in Queens, N.Y.

"I always told him that there are other ways, he doesn't have to risk his life," his mother, Ira Johnson, told The New York Times last month. "There are other occupations. I wanted him to get out of boxing."

Placed on medical suspension, he failed to take two of the three neurological examinations needed to be removed from the Association of Boxing Commissions suspension list, Ontario Athletics Commissioner Ken Hayashi said.

Still, he managed to get fights in Georgia and South Carolina before his Nov. 20 junior middleweight bout in Atlantic City with Paul Vaden. He was to make $10,000.

Johnson was knocked out by Vaden and never regained consciousness before he died Sunday.

An autopsy Monday showed he died of a "closed head injury and left subdural hematoma with multiple complications," medical examiner Dr. Hydow Park said.

"If he'd had those tests, it might've made a big difference," Hayashi said Monday. "Fighters think the commissions are out to bear down on them. But it's for their own health and safety. They just throw caution to the wind. It's too bad."

Floored by two punches in the 10th round, Johnson was rushed to Atlantic City Medical Center. A day later, doctors drilled two holes in his skull to siphon fluid in the hope of relieving pressure.

He contracted pneumonia during the Thanksgiving weekend and underwent a tracheotomy to help him breathe. He rallied briefly last week -- a CAT scan showed reduced swelling -- but died about 5:45 p.m. Sunday.

"The Ring" boxing almanac estimates that Johnson was one of four professional boxers worldwide to die from injuries in the ring this year.

"It's just devastating whenever this happens to anybody in the sport," said Craig Hamilton, Vader's co-manager. "It's a terrible thing for him, for his family, for the sport."

Johnson, a journeyman 154-pounder who turned pro in 1987, was hospitalized briefly after being knocked out by Fitz Vanderpool in an April 14 fight in Toronto.

He underwent a CAT scan and was cleared to fight again. But Canadian authorities put him on medical suspension, which is standard for fighters who are stopped or knocked out.

Johnson underwent a variety of tests aimed at measuring memory and motor skills to have his name removed from the suspension list.

He submitted only the CAT scan, however, and its results were "borderline," Hayashi said.

Johnson remained on the suspension list compiled by Fight Fax Inc. until September. But he managed to get licensed for an Aug. 7 fight in Greenville, S.C., which he won by decision.

Georgia's boxing commissioner said Monday that Johnson's name still appeared on the list Sept. 22, but it was taken off three days later. Only then was he cleared for an Oct. 6 fight against Calvin Moody in Albany, Ga., which Johnson won by a knockout, commissioner Tom Mishou said.

He was not on the suspended list for his last fight.

Hayashi said South Carolina boxing authorities told Fight Fax to remove him from the list. James Knight, a spokesman for the State Athletic Commission, denied that. He also said Johnson passed physicals, omitted any reference to the problems after his Toronto fight and was cleared by South Carolina authorities.

"The commission, based on Mr. Johnson's statements and the medical history on his application as well as the results of his exams, felt he was in condition to fight," Knight said.

There was one question on the form Johnson completed Aug. 6 that asked whether there was any other information not covered by the other questions, Knight said. Johnson left it blank. He was examined by a certified physician, who cleared him, and he submitted the results of a CT scan as part of his application.

In New Jersey, Johnson underwent a CAT scan, an electrocardiograph and an eye test before the Vaden fight and passed all three, the Athletic Control Board said.

"He was fine," said Dr. Frank Doggett, a retired physician who gave Johnson his heart exam during the physical. "His accident occurred in the ring itself."

Johnson grew up in Brooklyn but lived in Queens. He billed himself as being from Brooklyn because he thought it sounded tougher, said his manager, Kenneth Woods.

He worked as a boxing instructor at Equinox Fitness Centers in Manhattan, where he was known as a quiet, modest man who loved to teach. The mood there was somber Monday, general manager David Fowler said.

Employees and students had been planning a videotape of greetings to be played in Johnson's hospital room. Instead, they began collecting donations for his family.

Doggett, a former ringside physician, said such fatalities might be inevitable.

"I don't know how you're going to prevent this type of accident," he said. "Some people say helmets, but helmets aren't going to prevent it. In all these years, they've never come up with anything worthwhile."
