Neil Clark (born 1966) is a journalist and blogger from the UK. His work has appeared in newspapers and journals both in the UK and overseas including in The First Post, The Guardian, The Australian, The Times, Observer Sport Monthly, The Fleet Street Letter, The Morning Star, The American Conservative, New Statesman, The Spectator, the Daily Mail, The Daily Telegraph, the Daily Express, Racing and Football Outlook and the Racing Post.

He writes on subjects including politics, literature, travel, television sitcoms and sport. He is a horse racing correspondent for Racing and Football Outlook.[1] In 1992, he wrote the biography of the Scottish point-to-point horse Flying Ace. He opposes British and US policy in the Balkans and the Middle East. Clark is a supporter of the late Slobodan Milošević, found by the Supreme Court of Serbia to be a murderer[2] and who Clark describes as a prisoner of conscience.[3] Clark believes Milošević was "done to death" by poison and that "We are dealing here with murder". [4]

Clark supports the return of capital and corporal punishment and tougher prison sentences. He considers these to be the authentic Labour position which was distorted from the 1950s/60s onwards by people who were "liberals rather than socialists".[5], though in an article in 2003, he cast doubt on whether one of the architects of the "permissive society", Roy Jenkins, could be described as a liberal.[6]

He objects to "same-sex marriages, an open door immigration policy, and free abortion on demand" which puts Clark at odds with most of the contemporary left, while sharing this attitude with the anti-war right. [7] Clark views it as essential for the anti-war left and right to unite against Tony Blair and George W. Bush. Clark also supports renationalization of railways.

Clark has been quoted by South African President Thabo Mbeki for highly contested statistics on living conditions in the former Communist states of Eastern Europe after 1989. [8] In addition to his journalism, Clark teaches International Relations at Oxford Tutorial College, a private school, unconnected with Oxford University, for sixth form students (i.e. usually aged 16 to 18).

In 2006 Clark attempted to sue the writer Oliver Kamm for comments Kamm had made on his blog [9] following a critical review of Oliver Kamm's book, published by the Daily Telegraph in 2005.[10] The case collapsed and the claim was struck out as an abuse of process. Clark has condemned Kamm for "bully-boy tactics".[11]

Zsuzsanna Clark (née Feczko), his Hungarian-born wife is also a freelance journalist and writer who has contributed to the New Statesman and The First Post.
