The Bottom Line
This book will greatly aid the single person's thinking process, but as a how-to manual, it is frighteningly ritualistic at times.
Full Review
Dr. Neil Clark Warren, founder of eharmony.com, wrote this book, which is subtitled How to Know if Someone is Worth Pursuing in Two Dates or Less. Sound good? I thought it might be useful too. That's why I grabbed this book.

The target audience is wide, from college on up. Unlike recent relationship bestsellers like I Kissed Dating Goodbye, aimed more at teens, or Men are from Mars..., aimed more at adults, this book is written for anyone who might be considering marriage anytime soon. In fact, it could realistically be used as a book in marriage counseling for most any age.

Warren writes (I think) as a Christian, but not from an explicitly Christian perspective. He uses Christianity as an example at times and discusses spiritual compatibility in a few spots. If that's not your thing, those references can be easily ignored. Warren motivation for the website and for writing this is to make people happy in their marriages. He says there are two basic necessities to reach that state of bliss. Know yourself and know person you're dating. Simple enough. But Warren proceeds to explain what knowing a person is and how to get there.

In order to win at the dating game, Warren advocates first knowing yourself, particularly your relational self. In chapter three, he provides a list of twenty questions and also interaction suggestions that will help you accomplish that goal.

Chapter four is the axis of the book because it kick-starts the thinking process. Warren promotes developing a top ten list of "must-haves" and "can't-stands." By these terms he means personality traits or quirks to look out for in potential spouses. He also lists 50 of each to aid in the compiling of your own list. Again, it sounds simple enough. But if you really set out to do it (as I did), you'll run into trouble. Finding a balance between specificity and necessity is tricky, as is narrowing your list down to a mere ten. But once you have the list, you'll find it remarkably useful but also dangerous.

The title of the book promotes that danger, which is that the list itself and the book as a whole can be viewed as a checklist or a how-to manual that seems to suck the fun and joy out of what should be an enjoyable experience. It reminds me of The Bachelor, minus the cameras. I suppose you can treat dating/courtship/whatever as just a process to reaching the end of marriage, but I don't want it to be that methodical. I want dating to be exhilarating, not like shopping for a computer that simply meets your needs. Warren doesn't advocate that; in fact he cautions against just that several times. But his advice still often borders on the ritualistic. If you take his words to heart too much, I think you'll find yourself peering over that ledge too.

Warren goes on to discuss how to read a person. More specifically, he writes about the kind of questions and mannerisms that truly let you get to know someone. This chapter will no doubt open wide the eyes of some people who never studied interpersonal relations before. Those people will find these parts extremely helpful. As a Communications major who took classes of that ilk, I found it informative but redundant.

Expanding upon the list concept, the last few chapters deal with matching similarities, differences, and emotional health. These chapters decently go beyond the checklist concept, as Warren explains various character traits, relationships, etc., to explore when dating someone. Going beyond the list concept, Warren dictates specific compatibility issues to look out for. Things like relationship to parents, relationship of parents, other relationships, life goals, etc. These chapters are the most applicable of all, as Warren's suggestions are all very necessary to truly know someone. They are also all issues that are often overlooked by young people in their rush to get hitched. I'm quite glad he pointed them all out.

The only other theme I disliked was Warren's continual warning against making a mistake. While I agree that the marriage decision is hugely important and often misunderstood, his negative waves of caution are sometimes overwhelming, emoting a fear of the institution. Balancing that cautious tone with positivity and encouragement would have been a better idea.

The writing of Date...or Soul Mate is surprisingly enjoyable. Warren writes in the first person, a technique that makes the book more personal. More importantly he also writes well, utilizing good sentence variety. Although the advice sometimes resembles a how-to manual, the writing thankfully never does. He also doesn't go to the other extreme, over the heads of the reader, as some psychologists are prone to do. To illustrate his points, Warren uses precise language and clear examples, providing accurate and usually negative instances of how not to go about things.

Despite all my issues with this book, I found it very useful, better than most of its genre. This is because I was able to read it critically, with a grain of salt. I did write out my own answers, lists, and questions. That was very helpful, but more so as general guidelines to massage my thinking rather than a series of steps to eliminate marriage candidates. If you interpret the book that way too, you'll be quite satisfied with what you take away from Warren's work.

Recommended:
Yes
