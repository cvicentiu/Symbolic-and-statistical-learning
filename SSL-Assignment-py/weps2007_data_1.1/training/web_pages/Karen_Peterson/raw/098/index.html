Thursday, January 18, 2007
 Karen Freesia Peterson, 60

Sherry Remez

“It wasn’t about money. It was freedom about the inside self.”

When Karen Peterson was 55, she lost all of her money. It would have been understandable for a single woman, at this age, to panic. There were surely moments when she felt that emotion, but Karen remained balanced and focused enough to create something that she both deeply believes in and provides an income.

Giving Back Mentoring, a nonprofit corporation, is now in its sixth year. Its flagship program, “Kupuna and Keiki Together,” builds on the Hawaiian model of child rearing: elders (kupuna) with the primary responsibility of raising keiki. Karen trains seniors to mentor youngsters and lead them through easy exercises that increase their ability for focus and attention.

The exercises are derived from Brain Gym (BG) techniques, originally developed to help children and adults with learning challenges. BG effects balance in the whole brain. It is successfully employed in 100 countries worldwide in the areas of education, the arts, sports, business, healthcare, etc.

Karen was licensed to teach BG 10 years ago, and used it in private practice along with Natural Vision Improvement and Touch For Health in Denver before moving to Maui in 2000, after a difficult second divorce in which she protectively gave up custody of her son. This is the time-period in which she invested, and lost, her finances.

“It dawned on me that I now had all this freedom,” she told me. “It wasn’t about money. It was freedom about the inside self.”

Recognizing her own approaching elderhood, Karen began teaching BG to elders through Kaunoa Senior Center, working on balance to help prevent falling. She loved the work.

“I had been going to this woman’s group,” she said. “We would each just say how we felt. By listening to other people talk, it gave me the permission to be creative. All of a sudden, I am thinking of a program to bring elders together with kids.”

Her intergenerational inspiration, Giving Back Mentoring (givingbackmentoring.org.), was awarded a county grant. Karen developed a training manual, and now brings 20 seniors into three school sites to work a half-hour each week with 6- and 7-year-olds with learning disabilities. BG, plus the guidance and support, has proved extremely valuable—both for the seniors and their students.

This success has led to a second program centered on elders helping elders. It is being initiated at the Hale Mahaolu elderly housing project. Senior mentors work with the residents there. Karen’s vision is to recruit the active residents to assist with those who are frail, and plans to extend the program to people in long-term care.

A senior-to-senior mentoring program could be of tremendous service on many levels. Studies show active participation in something that is spiritually rewarding and enjoyable, mentally stimulating, and socially engaging greatly enhances the quality of life in our precious years of elderhood. (This is true at any age!)

Karen derives happiness from “the work, grandchildren, swimming, studying astrology, life.” Whenever she feels out of balance, she listens to a CD by Tibetan Buddhist teacher Pema Cho¨d-ron, does BG, or meditates in her “own way.” In 1988, she studied with John Ray, who taught: Embrace “what is,” don’t avoid anything. “That is unconditional love”, said Karen.

Our conversation turned to the subject of death and dying. As we age, it is normal to think about physical death, not as a morbid preoccupation, but a natural component of living. Tibetan Buddhists, psychologists and others maintain that the way in which we view death can bring peace of mind long before, as well as during, this inevitable process.

“I don’t think I am afraid of death,” Karen stated. “I’ve had a near death-experience surfing Kanaha, and several severe losses—which I consider death—and rebirth. That has prepared me. I don’t want a long illness. I’ve a greater fear of that than of death.” She has prepared the Five Wishes, a living will published by Aging with Dignity, in which you express exactly how you want to be treated if you are seriously ill and unable to speak for yourself. This unique living will encompass all medical, personal, emotional and spiritual needs.

This 60-year-old Baby Boomer, who didn’t go bust along with her assets, currently rents a room in a house shared with three other women. “I don’t have that much stuff,” she said, “so I’m not very attached. I’m not going to do the stereotype, and am going as long as I can. Right now I am going with the flow.”

Be Well & Enjoy Life to the Fullest!

Sherry
