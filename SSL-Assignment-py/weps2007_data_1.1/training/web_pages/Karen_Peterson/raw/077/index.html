TNT wins color-printing honors

Posted by David Zeeck @ 10:08:30 am

The News Tribune won 2nd place among all newspapers over 100,000 circulation in the 2007 Inland Print Quality Competition. The competition is co-sponsored by the Inland Press Association (a newspaper organization dedicated to improving newspaper practices and standards) and the Kodak Graphic Communications Group.

The contest pitted newspaper production departments, which printed the same photo on live press runs on the same date.

Here's what the judges said about the TNT

"Good color reproduction as compared to the color proof. Good color saturation."

Norwegian newspaper finds the secret to profiting online

Posted by David Zeeck @ 10:02:43 am

From the International Herald-Tribune:

At a time when other newspaper companies lament a loss of readers and advertisers, the Norwegian newspaper Schibsted is thriving. No profit warnings here: Earnings rose 28 percent in the fourth quarter. Online operations will generate about 20 percent of the company's revenue this year, according to analysts at Kaupthing, a bank based in Reykjavik, even as many other big newspaper publishers struggle to reach the 10 percent mark.

Perhaps more important, at least for investors, online businesses will provide nearly 60 percent of the company's operating earnings by next year, the Kaupthing analysts predict. Schibsted has become so emblematic of online success that Bharat Anand, a professor at Harvard Business School, is writing one of the institution's well-known case studies on the company.

Dear Kathleen letter

Posted by John Henrikson @ 11:52:27 am

Kathleen Merryman - or "Kits" as we know her in the newsroom - gets no shortage of feedback on her column. She shared a great e-mail this morning from reader Megan Lindholm, who riffed on today's column about baristas being terminated from a Spanaway espresso stand because they didn't fit with the joint's new bikini theme.

Here's an excerpt from Lindholm's tongue-in-cheek letter.

Subject: So Sorry, Kathleen

But we, the readers of your column in the News Tribune, are going to have to let you go. It's not that you don't write a great column. Really, it's all there. Humor, the personal touch, the warm anecdotes, all combined with great syntax, respect for grammar and good spelling. But today's column made me realize that we all have to get with the times.

Unfortunately, you don't look like Lois Lane. Having a little picture of someone who looked more like Lois Lane, jaunty girl reporter, would probably sell more newspapers. A picture of someone who looked more like Lois Lane would probably inspire really pretty girls to get into journalism. And maybe the guys in the newsroom would be inspired to buff up like Clark Kent.

Please don't feel alone. I'm also going to fire all those UPS delivery guys who don't fill out their brown coveralls the way they should. Women delivering packages – oh, please! Forget that! That would be like, well, like an average looking guy working as a barista. And plumbers showing 'crack' – fellows, kiss your jobs goodbye. We don't care if you can fix our pipes. If you can't look good in a Speedo while doing it, well, expertise with copper tubing is no substitute for a cute little tush. For the same reason, I'm letting all Tacoma nurses over the age of 28 go, and we're going to mandate sexier hospital uniforms for all of those who stay. All Pink Elephant employees will wear swimwear to wash your car. And your mechanic – well, unzip those coveralls, my friends. Let's see a little skin to make up for telling me that my car has blown a head gasket.

It's not discrimination, Kathleen. It's not sexism. It's not a huge backward step in civil rights. It's just smart business.

And we think WE invented dynamic newspaper design

Posted by David Zeeck @ 11:32:48 am

Modern journalists are proud of the direction of newspaper design, and are inclined to think no one before us really, really, knew how to attract readers visually.

Fred Matamoros, one of our graphic artists, sent this link to Jack Shafer, media critic for Slate magazine, on newspaper design/art as produced by Joseph Pulitzer's papers in the 1800s.

We could learn a few things from these folks.

Says Shafer of Pulitzer's New York World:

. . . what made this vivid copy sing was its graphic and typographical presentation. Pulitzer's people bulldozed the dreary, gray newspaper design template. The World ran headlines across a couple of columns, not just one, or completely across the page if it really wanted to provoke readers.

You don't have to be Hemingway to blog for us

Posted by Matt Misterek @ 08:25:10 pm

If you didn't join us here at the newspaper for our "bloggers class" Thursday evening, you missed out on chocolate chip cookies and a primer on how to start posting your neighborhood news to our website.

But you still are welcome to sign up to be one of our bloggers. The blog will "go live" in the next week, and we are striving to get people signed up early so they can start writing and posting right away.

Twenty South Sounders came to our interest meeting Thursday, representing a broad range of geography, race, personal interests and computer literacy. They all signed up for log-in credentials, and we started distributing them Friday.

Editors read the paper

Posted by John Henrikson @ 12:41:29 pm

Highlight's from discussion of today's paper by editors and reporters.

A1
Business editor Marce Edwards kicked off the discussion by praising Brent Champaco's story and photo package on changes at your local library, saying it was a good explainer.

Conversation then shifted to our coverage of the gunfire near East Side schools. Consensus was the story turned out more thorough than some people anticipated and seemed rightly played on A1, despite concerns yesterday. Much of the credit went to breaking news editor Randy McCarthy, who assembled the final prose from the work of several reporters and added the necessary context. Sports reporter Darrin Beene said he wished the accompanying map had run on Page 1, so readers had a better idea of what we were talking about.

Local news editor Hunter George pointed out that both the shooting story and our Java Jive story were in the Top 5 on the Web site. He also liked the teasers highlighting what's inside the paper, specifically for Go.

South Sound

Karen Hucks' story on the Peace Corps drew praise. Managing editor Karen Peterson pointed to stepped up coverage of the higher ed beat from reporter Hucks, who's new to the beat.

Photo editor Jeremy Harrison liked today's edition of The Nose. Suburban/Nose editor Matt Misterek credited Kris Sherman with chasing down the item on Charlie "BC" Milligan. "More people should do real reporting for The Nose," Matt said. We couldn't tell if he was joking.

Sports

Matt said we made a good choice in highlighting coverage from those no-longer-upstart Coug cagers.

SoundLife
General praise for reporter Bill Hutchens' piece on the World of Warcraft expansion. People loved Bill's obvious expertise and the nice details in the package, including the min-profiles of the other players who offered their opinions. Page designer Carmen Dybdahl gets credit for adding Bill's character info to his column icon.

One nit: Could we have gotten at least on more story start on the cover for the non-video game players out there?

GO

Another great cover and a really good package by Ed Murrieta on breakfast in the South Sound. Managing editor Karen Peterson liked it so much she wanted even more. Insider secret: We got the "GO" on the toast with automotive spray paint and a stencil. Don't try this at home.
