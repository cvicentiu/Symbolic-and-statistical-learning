Digest of the Presbytery Meeting Minutes - May 25, 2004

The Presbytery of Philadelphia held a stated meeting at the Doylestown Presbyterian Church, Doylestown, Pennsylvania on Tuesday, May 25, 2004 at 4:00 p.m. The Moderator, the Rev. Ms. Ethelyn Taylor, noting a quorum present at the appointed hour, called the Presbytery to order. The meeting began with a Procession of the Symbols of Our Faith and was constituted with prayer by the Moderator. The meeting was organized around the theme of “The maintenance of divine worship,” one of the great ends of the church, as stated in the Book of Order: “All power in heaven and earth is given to Jesus Christ by Almighty God,...Christ calls the Church into being, giving it all that is necessary for its mission to the world,...The great ends of the church are the proclamation of the gospel for the salvation of humankind; the shelter, nurture, and spiritual fellowship of the children of God; the maintenance of divine worship; the preservation of the truth; the promotion of social righteousness; and the exhibition of the Kingdom of Heaven to the world.”

Speaking for the resource team, the Rev. Ms. Julia Sheetz-Willard and the Rev. Ms. Bernice Warren presented the following resolution, which the Presbytery approved:

Resolution on Haiti

Whereas: Escalating violence and political unrest in Haiti in recent months has led to an increasing number of persons fleeing in search of refuge, often to the United States in crowded and unsafe vessels;

Whereas: In February alone more than 100 people were killed and 867 Haitians were repatriated by the U.S. Coast Guard without having an opportunity to adequately state their claims for political asylum;

Whereas: The Presbyterian Church (USA) stands in prayer with and for our Haitian brothers and sisters who secured their freedom from slavery 200 years ago and thus encouraged others held captive to follow their example;

Whereas: During an earlier similar Haitian humanitarian and political crisis, the 204th General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church (USA) in 1992 issued a statement calling on the president and United States government to “suspend immediately the high seas interdiction and forced return of Haitian refugees”;

Whereas: The 1951 UN Convention on Refugees and the 1967 Protocol Relating to the Status of Refugees, to which the United States is a signatory, provide for the protection of persons seeking refuge and political asylum;

Whereas: President Bush on February 25, 2004, stated that “we will turn back any refugee that attempts to reach our shore,” a policy in clear violation of the UN Convention on Refugees and the 1967 Protocol Relating to the Status of Refugees;

Whereas: The Temporary Protected Status program was established by Section 244 of the Immigration and Nationality Act to provide protection to people who are temporarily unable to return to their homelands because of dangerous and ongoing conflict; and

Whereas: An international consensus that this is the current situation in Haiti is made clear by the decision of the UN Security Council to provide peacekeeping forces to the region, forces that include elements from the United States;
Therefore Be It Resolved That: The Presbytery of Philadelphia go on record as thanking the President of the United States for contributing troops to the international peacekeeping force in Haiti so that the country can be stabilized, and urging him to provide protection for all Haitians intercepted at sea and to offer to Haitians currently residing in the United States Temporary Protected Status for a period of 18 months.

Be It Resolved Further That: Secretary of Homeland Security Tom Ridge be requested to immediately halt all deportations and repatriations to Haiti because of the instability of that country and the almost assured threat to the lives of all Haitians returned to the island at this time.

The Presbytery Instructs the Stated Clerk to forward a copy of this resolution to President Bush, Secretary of State Colin Powell, Secretary of Homeland Security Tom Ridge, members of Congress representing the four-county area covered by the Presbytery of Philadelphia, any relevant media, and to the churches of the Presbytery, encouraging them to share it with their members.

GUIDELINES FOR ALTERNATE EXAMINATION
OF CANDIDATES


The Book of Order states: (G-4.0403; Full Participation) “The Presbyterian Church (U.S.A.) Shall give full expression to the rich diversity within its membership, and shall provide means which will assure a greater inclusiveness leading to wholeness in its emerging life. Persons of all racial ethnic groups, different ages, both sexes, various disabilities,...shall be guaranteed full participation and access to representation in the decision making of the church.”

In light of these obligations our goal is to present a procedure for reviewing and assessing those inquirers and candidates under our care who have disabilities.

Philadelphia Presbytery encourages all its candidates to complete all ordination requirements set out in the Book of Order. Where disabilities make it difficult or impossible to do so the Committee on Preparation for Ministry will seek means to enable them to do so. For example, it will provide access to extra tutoring, extra course work, mentoring opportunities, advocacy, etc.

The following may ensue, after a second failure to pass any of the examinations, if these conditions are met:

  1. The difficulty has nothing to do with any inability to understand the content of the subject matter.
  2. The candidate is otherwise ready to receive a call.

The Committee on Preparation for Ministry (“CPM”) may consider an exception to the requirements of the Book of Order for the successful completion of the ordination examinations. In the event such a motion is voted on in the affirmative, the following procedure will take place:

  1. CPM will establish a resource team which will include as a minimum a seminary professor, member of Presbytery experienced in grading Standard Ordination Examinations, and the advocate.
  2. The resource team will devise an adequate test, which may be a written examination, an oral examination, or a paper to be prepared.
  3. The advocate will meet with the student to discuss the type and nature of the examination, as well as the date, time and place at which it will be administered, or the date on which the paper is to be submitted. The candidate will also be required to provide written permission for all parties involved, including all members of the CPM, to evaluate the examination and/or paper.
  4. A member of the resource team will proctor the examination, or receive the written paper, convene the resource team to evaluate the result, and then report to CPM.
  5. CPM will decide by three-fourths vote, in the absence of the candidate, as to whether to petition the Presbytery to make the exception (which will require a three-fourths vote of the Presbytery). It is appropriate for CPM to interview the candidate on the subject if deemed necessary.
  6. If the Presbytery approves the recommendation of CPM, the minutes of Presbytery will reflect the reasons for making the exception, and the alternative method of examination used.

For the Bible content examination, CPM will develop a testing procedure in the following manner:

  1. Ask the Examination Office of the PC (USA) to provide a proctored examination at the inquirer’s or candidate’s seminary, with special provisions as approved by CPM such as oral responses, unlimited time with breaks, circle the answers in the exam rather than the answer sheet
    or
    Ask the Examination Office to furnish CPM with an examination which will be proctored and graded by the advocate with the same provisions as above.
  2. CPM will report the test score to the Examination Office and the test will become a permanent part of the inquirer’s or candidate’s file.
  3. Proceed to steps 5 and 6 of the above procedure to ascertain the Presbytery’s willingness to make an exception.
