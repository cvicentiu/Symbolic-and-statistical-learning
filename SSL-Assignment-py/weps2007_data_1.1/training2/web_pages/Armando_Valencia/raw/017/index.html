The week in Mexico

June 6, 2004

Cigarette contributions:  Mexico's tobacco companies have agreed to contribute nearly $350 million to Mexico's health system through November 2006, President Vicente Fox announced Monday, an arrangement that apparently avoids creation of new formal taxes.

Ciudad Juarez report:  Federal officials issued a report Thursday that threatened to bring criminal charges against 81 current and former Mexican law enforcement officers for incompetence in their failure to solve a decade-long string of women's slayings in the border city of Ciudad Juarez.

Ciudad Juarez drama:  The slayings of more than 300 women in Ciudad Juarez have inspired a soap opera-style drama to be shown on Mexican television this summer, broadcaster TV Azteca said Tuesday.

Ex-governor sentenced:  A Mexican court has ratified former Baja California Sur Gov. Guillermo Mercado Romero's 10-year prison sentence on corruption charges, the Mexico City daily El Universal  reported Thursday. The paper said several hundred thousand dollars worth of airplane tickets were purchased at a travel agency owned by his wife and daughter. Mercado Romero, of the Institutional Revolutionary Party, served from 1993 to 1999.

Energy Ministry:  President Fox tapped his point man on tax reform, Fernando Elizondo Barragán, as energy minister Tuesday. Elizondo Barragán, who represents Fox on a national tax reform council, replaces National Action Party stalwart Felipe Calderón. Calderón resigned Monday after angering Fox by openly expressing his presidential aspirations.

Drug kingpins:  Two Mexican drug gangs and eight people the United States considers drug kingpins were placed on a U.S. sanctions list Tuesday in an attempt to cut down on the illicit drug trade. The Mexican groups and individuals were the Arellano Félix Organization of Tijuana; Eduardo Ramón Arellano Félix; Francisco Javier Arellano Félix; the Carrillo Fuentes Organization; and Armando Valencia Cornelio.

Suspects arrested:  Jorge Aureliano Félix and Efraín Pérez Pazuengo, believed to be prominent members of the Arellano Félix drug cartel, were captured in Tijuana last week and sent to Mexico City, officials said.

Border shakeup:  A regional head of the U.S. agency responsible for border protection has been removed as authorities investigate an agency informant's apparent participation in the killing of a Mexican drug dealer, officials said. Giovanni Gaudioso was replaced as special agent-in-charge of the El Paso Immigration and Customs Enforcement office by Frank Figueroa, a veteran administrator who arrived this week in El Paso, The Dallas Morning News  reported.

Peace talk offer:  Mexico on Friday officially offered to host peace talks between the Colombian government and the country's second-largest leftist guerrilla group, the National Liberation Army.

$600,000 settlement:  Ten Mexican immigrants who alleged they faced daily ethnic insults on the job in Denver won a $600,000 settlement of their discrimination lawsuit. The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission disclosed the settlement Tuesday for the former workers at Phase 2 Co., a drywall and steel framing contractor.
