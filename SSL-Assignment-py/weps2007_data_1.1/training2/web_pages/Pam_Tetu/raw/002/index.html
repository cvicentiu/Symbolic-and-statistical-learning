Binational Conference on Environmental Research and Policy to be Held at UC San Diego

UNIVERSITY OF CALIFORNIA, SAN DIEGO — A binational conference and workshop on U.S.-Mexico related environmental problems will be held on June 12-13, 2000 at the Institute of the Americas Complex at UC San Diego. Organized by Keith Pezzoli of the UCSD Urban Studies and Planning department, Palmer Taylor of the UCSD Department of Pharmacology, and Robert Tukey of the UCSD Departments of Chemistry-Biochemistry and Pharmacology (Taylor is also Director of the UCSD Superfund Program), the event will bring together an invited group of U.S. and Mexican academics, scientists, industry representatives, and community and national leaders to examine binational issues with an emphasis on the California/Baja California border region.

Organizers hope to show how a broad coalition of academicians, scientists, community-based organizations and policy-makers in federal and local agencies, and industry with binational and border interests, can be organized to pursue common problems. The principal objective of the conference and workshops is to provide the leadership of the National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences (NIEHS), a cosponsor of the event, with a set of research and funding priorities geared to informing the agenda of a prospective university-based Binational Center. Another major objective, in which SDSC will play a principal role, is to chart broadly applicable methods and models for enabling an innovative integration of information technology and communications issues with environmental science and policy issues in a binational context.

Although the cost of the event is being covered by the cosponsors, registration is required to ensure space. To register or for more information, including directions, hotel accommodations, the agenda, and a list of participants, see the conference Web site at http://www.sdsc.edu/niehsconference. To answer any other questions, contact Pam Tetu, 858-534-4028 or ptetu@ucsd.edu.

The conference will open with a morning plenary session and a keynote luncheon with speakers involved in the development of environmental policy at the local, state, and national levels. The remainder of the day-and-a-half event will be organized around a series of participatory breakout workshops in which, among other things, participants will be encouraged to focus on how to integrate information technology and communications issues with domain science and policy issues. To help in this effort, research scientists from the San Diego Supercomputer Center will provide an overview of cutting-edge enabling technologies available for data collection, integration, and sharing, as well as for enhanced modeling and prediction. Day two of the conference will allow a more in-depth and forward-looking discussion based on the collective results of the breakout sessions.

Breakout workshops will focus on:

1. Hazardous waste management (binational priorities and challenges for data collection, integration, and sharing).

2. Industrial ecology and environmental management systems (environmental engineering, regional planning and pollution prevention).

3. Biomedical research (linking exposure with insult at the genomic level); bioremediation.

4. Water quality in regional bays, estuaries, and coastal zones (pesticides, heavy metals, and ecotoxicology).

5. Prevention/intervention research with a public health approach geared to the U.S.-Mexico cross-border and transnational issues (emphasizing occupational health, exposure assessment, infectious disease vectors and environmental epidemiology).

6. Air pollution and global climate change (integrated risk assessment).

7. Enabling multidisciplinary approach to environmental research, education and training in the U.S.-Mexico transborder context (the culture of binational networking and communication).

Generous support from the National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences (NIEHS), the UC Toxic Substances Research & Teaching Program, and other organizations is making this event possible.

Along with the San Diego Supercomputer Center, cosponsors include NIEHS; UCSD Toxic Substances Research and Teaching Program; Dirección de Planeación de Desarrollo Urbano y Ecologiía, Municipio de Tijuana; and UC San Diego, including Health-Sciences—School of Medicine, Center for U.S.-Mexican Studies, Urban Studies and Planning, Center for Environmental Research and Training, and the Southwest Center for Environmental Research and Policy (SCERP). —PT
