Interview: Tony Abbott
August 8, 2004
Reporter : Laurie Oakes

It's been a dramatic week in Federal Parliament, with the Opposition and the Government slugging it out over the Free Trade Agreement with the US. A Labor amendment to the FTA legislation to keep down the costs of prescription drugs was the major sticking point, amid esoteric arguments over patent laws and the Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme. The Federal Health Minister, Tony Abbott, will be talking about those issues, and a possible imminent election, with Sunday's political editor, Laurie Oakes ...


Transcript
JANA WENDT: This week's political stand-off over the free trade agreement with the United States has caught the Government on the wrong foot. Labor has stolen John Howard's mantle of the battler's champion when it comes to cheap medicine. How's the Government going to fight back? One of its chief strategists, Health Minister Tony Abbott, is in our Sydney studio now, to talk with Sunday's political editor Laurie Oakes.

Good morning, Laurie.

LAURIE OAKES: Morning, Jana.

Mr Abbott, welcome to the programme.

HEALTH MINISTER TONY ABBOTT: Morning, Laurie.

LAURIE OAKES: Before we talk about the free trade agreement issue, I wanted to ask you about a story that's broken today. More than 40 former intelligence chiefs, military top brass, departmental heads, and senior diplomats, have signed a statement scathingly criticising the Government over the Iraq war. How embarrassing is that?

TONY ABBOTT: Oh, well, we live in a robust democracy, Laurie, and retired officials are entitled to their viewpoint. But the point is that there were intelligence failings, but those - those failings don't alter the fact that the Government didn't misuse the intelligence that it had.

LAURIE OAKES: Well, these people, many of them quite illustrious names, say we're concerned that Australia was committed to join the invasion of Iraq on the basis of false assumptions, and the deception of the Australian people. That's pretty tough.

TONY ABBOTT: But - but there was no advertent deception. The Australian Government believed what the British Government, the American Government, the French Government, the German Government, the Russian Government, the Israeli Government all believed that Iraq did have these weapons of mass destruction.

Now it turns out that that intelligence was flawed, but there was no - no misuse of it, and there was no intention to mislead.

LAURIE OAKES: Well, these people include the last three Australian ambassadors to Iraq, the former director-general of intelligence, former heads of ONA and the National Intelligence Committee, two former chiefs of the Defence Force. They're people who should know what they're talking about, and they say we were deceived.

TONY ABBOTT: Well, obviously the intelligence that everyone was getting turned out not to be absolutely accurate, but even Andrew Wilkie thought before the Iraq war that Iraq had weapons of mass destruction, and sure with the benefit of hindsight we now know that it wasn't absolutely accurate. But let's not get too wise after the event.

LAURIE OAKES: All right. The free trade agreement, and Labor's proposed amendment, aimed at ensuring that cheap generic drugs are not blocked by big companies using patent laws. Are we going to see a compromise in the next few days?

TONY ABBOTT: Certainly the free trade agreement will get through. It's too important to waste this once in a century opportunity. But I think it's high time that Labor actually produced its amendment, Laurie. They've been talking about it for a week. They still haven't produced it.

At the beginning of last week Latham said that it could be passed this week. Well, the week's gone. We haven't even seen Labor's amendment, let alone got the thing passed.

LAURIE OAKES: Well, Mr Latham said this morning that the amendment will be available tomorrow. Has the Prime Minister been out manoeuvred on this?

TONY ABBOTT: Whether someone has been manoeuvred one way or the other way is really beside the point. The point is this free trade agreement is in Australia's national interest. It's a once in a century opportunity for Australia to lock into the world's largest and strongest economy.

It is John Howard and Mark Vaile and Alexander Downer's work. It's probably the greatest achievement of this term of the Howard Government, and it ought to go ahead, and it ought to go ahead as soon as possible.

LAURIE OAKES: But we're living in a period of intense politicking, an election coming up very soon. And according to the Government's script for last week, Mark Latham was supposed to be - to undergo a humiliating back down, and then to be hammered by the Government, and the - it turned out to be exactly opposite, didn't it?

TONY ABBOTT: Well, I'm not sure about that. I think Mark Latham was caught out in a stunt. He said that the free trade had to be amended to eliminate the possibility of bodgy patent applications. He still hasn't put forward his amendment. And every patent lawyer in the country is saying that Latham's mooted amendment is just unworkable.

Now, if he does produce an amendment tomorrow, it'll turn out to be a different amendment to the one which he said he wanted earlier in the week.

LAURIE OAKES: Well, Mark Latham says that under his amendments, there'll be multi-million dollar penalties to ensure the big drug companies can't delay the introduction of generic drugs. Will the Government go along with that?

TONY ABBOTT: Well, they - they can't delay the introduction of generic drugs now. And we don't have this ever-greening problem, which the United States has. Australian patent law is quite different to American patent law, and the mere application for a patent does not found any legal rights, and this idea that drug companies can stop generics coming on simply by lodging bodgy patents, it's just nonsensical.

LAURIE OAKES: But they can threaten legal action. There are all sorts of ways ...

TONY ABBOTT: And ...

LAURIE OAKES: ... that big companies can use the courts to delay such things.

TONY ABBOTT: Yes. They can. And nothing that Labor has proposed will detract from existing rights. But because Australian patent law runs on a much sounder basis than American patent law, we don't have this problem. We do not have the problem that the Americans have.

Generics play a very important part in our system, and that will continue to be the case under the PBS.

LAURIE OAKES: Well, Steve Conroy ...

TONY ABBOTT: And the free trade agreement.

LAURIE OAKES: Steve Conroy, Labor's Trade Spokesman, says that under the proposal they have, big companies could lose profits they make from the date they file legal action for patent protection. That improper profits could then go to the generic companies. Won't that work?

TONY ABBOTT: Well, already if someone does something wrong, a company can bring an action for damages. And damages usually involve profit which has been improperly creamed off by the aggrieved company. So this is already there under our - under our patent law.

LAURIE OAKES: Is the Government preparing its own amendments, Mr Abbott, or are you waiting to see the colour of Labor's?

TONY ABBOTT: Well, the interesting thing, Laurie, was this week in the Parliament Labor talked about two amendments. One of them was about dodgy patents, the other was about protecting local content.

And indeed the Government provided them with their amendment on local content. Now, certainly Labor ...

LAURIE OAKES: Are you going to do it again?

TONY ABBOTT: Labor can't do what it - what it says it wants to do. Laurie, our commitment is to get the free trade agreement through. We think this is too important an opportunity to waste. We think it's too important to grandstand on, and look, if it's necessary to humour the Labor Party, we'll humour them.

But we won't humour them with an amendment which is not only unnecessary but dangerous.

LAURIE OAKES: Is it fair enough to say that Labor has the easier case to make - they stand up and say we're the party that's standing up for the PBS, and people's ability to get cheaper generic drugs. You're arguing arcane aspects of the patent law.

TONY ABBOTT: Yeah, look, you're right, Laurie. It is - it is - it's always easy to raise a hue and cry with people, saying 'Oh, drugs will be more expensive'. But if that's what Labor really thinks they're against the whole FTA, they're not just against this particular aspect of it.

Mark Latham said some years ago oppositions can afford to be irresponsible, and certainly he's been living up to that - that motto ever since he became Leader of the Opposition, but particularly in this last week.

LAURIE OAKES: But can the Government afford to go into the election campaign with this unsettled, and with Labor posing as a champion of the PBS and cheap drugs?

TONY ABBOTT: We - we would be happy to fight an election campaign on the free trade agreement. We are not going to hold the free trade agreement up. We - we want this free trade agreement. It is absolutely in Australia's national interest, and the Government won't be playing politics over it.

LAURIE OAKES: What Mark Latham's achieved, I think, with his tactics, is to shift this debate away from the US alliance issue, onto health, which he thinks is a Labor issue. Do you agree that health is a Labor issue?

TONY ABBOTT: Traditionally people say it is, but the latest Newspoll suggests that the Government has fairly dramatically improved its ratings in health, Laurie. And certainly for all the talk this week from the Labor Party about protecting the PBS, Julia Gillard is on the record on numerous occasions wanting to collapse the PBS and the Medicare benefits schedule, and the aged care funding, into one giant health pot that will be spent in accordance with the whim of - of the bureaucrats.

So I'm not sure that health these days is quite the happy political hunting ground that the Labor Party assumes. What is this national health reform commission going to do? Is this just writing a blank cheque for the bureaucrats? Will anything be off limits?

I don't think the Australian people really know where Labor stands on health. Once upon a time Labor was the party that said they wanted to defend Medicare, but the only party in this Parliament at the moment which is proposing to cut health spending is the Labor Party, which wants to destroy the brand new Medicare Plus Safety Net.

LAURIE OAKES: What sort of a cut would that involve?

TONY ABBOTT: Well, it would involve taking $150 million a year off some one million Australians who face high health costs, and there are already half-a-million Australians at this point who are entitled to higher Medicare rebates under the Medicare Plus Safety Net. People who face high out-of-hospital, out-of-pocket expenses - and those people are going to be left in the lurch by the Labor Party.

LAURIE OAKES: Will the - will the Coalition go into the election with new health initiatives, or have you done your dash now?

TONY ABBOTT: Certainly we've got a very strong record on health. I - I looked at the figures the other day, Laurie, and since October last year we have added an additional $8.5 billion to the forward estimates in health and ageing.

Since 1996 the percentage of the Federal Government's spent on - the Federal Government's budget spent on health has gone from under 15 per cent to over 20 per cent. So this Government has delivered on health. This Government has protected and strengthened Medicare.

Yes, we will have a few more ideas between now and the election. But I'm afraid people will just have to wait and see.

LAURIE OAKES: Okay. Two quick issues - we're running out of time. But you've set out recently to again make abortion a political issue in Australia. Why are you stirring that up?

TONY ABBOTT: I'm not stirring it up, Laurie, but obviously I have some views, and it would be wrong of me not to - not to be frank with people about it. I think the hundred thousand abortions we have in Australia every year are a national tragedy, and the interesting thing is that a lot of people who were the great advocates of choice two decades ago are themselves having second thoughts.

So a debate has begun and let's see where it goes.

LAURIE OAKES: A final issue. You're a leading monarchist, as everybody knows. What do you think of the controversy surrounding the Tasmanian Governor Richard Butler.

TONY ABBOTT: Oh, well, I don't know that he was necessarily the greatest choice, but look, I'm not into the business of criticising viceroys, Laurie. He's not ...

LAURIE OAKES: What ...

TONY ABBOTT: ... the Federal Government's appointment, and that's really a matter for the Tasmanian people and the Tasmanian Government.

LAURIE OAKES: Is it possible that this republican Governor has become an advertisement for a republican system - an embarrassment for the monarchy system?

TONY ABBOTT: Well, look, if you're suggesting, Laurie, that he has set out to deliberately undermine the system he swore to serve, that's a pretty - pretty serious suggestion, and that's something that I couldn't possibly comment on.

LAURIE OAKES: No, I was suggesting it might have happened by accident, though.

TONY ABBOTT: Well, it would be a pity for someone of that standing and that kind of record as a senior public servant for many years, it would be a great pity if someone like that had stumbled into this kind of thing.

LAURIE OAKES: Mr Abbott, we thank you.

TONY ABBOTT: Thanks, Laurie.

LAURIE OAKES: Back to you, Jana.
