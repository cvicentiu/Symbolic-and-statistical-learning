Parents of Obese Kids Should Switch Off TV, says Abbott

By Melissa Polimeni
AAP
 Oct 18, 2005

Health Minister Tony Abbott. (Torsten Blackwood/AFP)
High-resolution image (558 x 649 px, 150 dpi)

CANBERRA - Health Minister Tony Abbott is refusing to cave in to demands for a ban on junk food ads during children's programs, saying parents should simply switch off the television.

New research by the NSW Health Department shows there are 1.5 million overweight children in Australia and they are beginning to suffer major health problems.

The study, to be detailed on the ABC's Four Corners program tonight, found that between the mid-1980s and mid-1990s the number of overweight children doubled, and of even more concern, the childhood obesity rate tripled.

Amid mounting calls for an ad ban, Mr Abbott says the solution to the childhood obesity crisis lies with parents.

"If the parents are foolish enough to feed their kids on a diet of Coca-Cola and lollies, well they should lift their game and lift it urgently," he told the program, to be aired tonight.

"The federal government doesn't say to someone in the supermarket `please take that bottle of Coca-Cola'.

"No one is in charge of what goes into kids' mouths except their parents."

Mr Abbott said ad bans would not solve the problem.

"If you don't like the advertising on television, switch it off, as simple as that, watch the ABC," he said.

Kaye Mehta of the Coalition on Food Advertising to Children (CFAC) said Mr Abbott's attitude showed a lack of understanding of the true pressures facing working families.

"I'm sure Tony Abbott hasn't experienced whinging children around his feet at five o'clock in the evening, or when he's trying to get a meal together," Ms Mehta said.

She said most parents backed a push by the CFAC for a ban on all food ads on TV during periods when children aged under 12 years were watching.

"All our work shows that parents want some help here, they want tighter regulations and if possible, they want ads off the TV," Ms Mehta said.

Dr Elspeth McInnes, a lecturer in early childhood education at the University of South Australia, said Mr Abbott's advice that parents should switch off the TV was unrealistic.

"That's if they own the TV set," said Dr McInnes, who is also convenor of the National Council of Single Mothers.

"If we are as a society keen to make sure we eat better then we should be regulating advertising."

Family First Senator Steve Fielding agreed with Mr Abbott that parents must shoulder the responsibility.

"The focus has to be on the parent, they have to put boundaries around their kids' eating habits and parents should lead by example," he said.

In 2003, former minister for children Larry Anthony warned a kids' marketing conference that if the industry was not responsible, the government would intervene.

Mr Anthony never raised the matter again.

The former National MP has told the ABC that at the time, he came under pressure from within government ranks to ease off - a claim disputed by Mr Abbott.

"I have absolutely no reason to think that he would've been (pressured)," Mr Abbott said.
