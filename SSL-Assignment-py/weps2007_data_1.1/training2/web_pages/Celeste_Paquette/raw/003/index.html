Meningitis Info Column 11/18/03

Dear Director:

I’ve heard a lot of different things about meningitis since coming to college. Can you tell me how serious it really is and whether or not I should get the vaccine?

A concerned FSU student

Dear concerned student,

In the past year, a Tallahassee high school senior died from meningococcal meningitis, and several months after this incident occurred, an FSU student also contracted this same type of meningitis.

Outbreaks of bacterial meningitis are rare but they do happen. The illness is passed on by mucus or saliva in direct contact, so casual contact is not something to worry about.

In recent months, meningitis has become a hot topic for discussion between clinicians at Thagard Student Health Center and the students here at FSU. New laws enacted by the State Legislature that went into effect in January mandating that students enrolled in Florida’s universities must be notified of the risks associated with meningococcal meningitis and the benefits of becoming vaccinated against this disease. Remember how angry some of you were when you discovered there was a medical block on your registration for spring semester classes? Remember how you panicked? Well, Thagard Health Center did that to make sure that you had the most up to date, accurate information about this disease and the opportunity to either get the vaccine or the ability to sign a waver stating that you did not want the vaccine AFTER reading the information. FSU has taken measures to ensure that students are in compliance with these new requirements, but many of you still have questions about the risks associated with meningitis. This week’s column is being devoted to answering some of the most common questions we receive regarding meningitis and the Menomune vaccine that is administered to protect against the infection.

What is meningitis?
Meningitis is an infection of the tissues surrounding the brain. It is most commonly caused by bacteria or viruses that are spread through direct contact with secretions from the nose or throat of an infected individual, and while viral meningitis is generally well tolerated, bacterial meningitis is extremely severe. According to Dr. Celeste Paquette, MD, Thagard’s Medical Director, “Bacterial meningitis is a very serious illness, and if left untreated, it can lead to permanent neurological complications or even death.”

Actions such as coughing, sneezing, kissing, or sharing items like drinking glasses or cigarettes can transmit meningitis from person to person. However, meningitis can’t live outside the body for a prolonged period of time, so it can’t be spread through casual contact or by breathing the air where an infected individual has been.

Who is at risk for contracting meningitis and what are the symptoms?
Young adults between the ages of 17 and 24 share an increased risk of acquiring meningococcal meningitis, and because it is spread in the same manner as a cold or flu, there is an increased incidence of this type of meningitis among individuals living in large groups, such as residence halls. Furthermore, there is also a higher risk of contracting infection with bar patronage and alcohol and cigarette usage.

Symptoms of meningitis are similar to those that accompany influenza and include high fever, stiff neck, headache, severe body aches, nausea and vomiting, sensitivity to light, altered mental status or confusion, and sleepiness. In order to make the correct diagnosis for meningitis, your physician must obtain a sample of your spinal fluid to identify the agent responsible for the infection. “Knowing whether meningitis is caused by a virus or by bacteria is important because of the differences in the seriousness of the illness and the treatment required,” said Paquette. An early, accurate diagnosis of meningitis is critical for administering proper treatment in the form of antibiotics. But, in order for treatment to be effective it must be started quickly, and therefore it is important that patients see their healthcare provider as soon as they begin experiencing symptoms.

What is Menomune?
Menomune is the vaccination against one type of bacterial meningitis, which is most often seen in the college age group. It is not a live vaccine, and it is exceptionally safe with a low incidence of side effects. The most common side effect is soreness and redness at the site of the injection, and in rare instances, individuals may experience a low grade fever. Severe allergic reactions to the vaccine are extremely uncommon. Only a single injection is required to provide protection for 3 to 5 years, and the vaccine is available at the health center.

The Centers for Disease Control strongly recommend that, because of the factors involved, all college students should consider receiving the vaccination for meningitis, and Thagard’s clinicians agree. “86% of college students diagnosed with meningitis since 1997 would have been protected if they had received the vaccine,” said Paquette.

Students wishing to receive this vaccination should call Thagard Student Health Center’s General Medical clinic at 644-TIME to make a nursing appointment. The cost of the meningitis vaccine is approximately $70, and if you have the campus-sponsored student health insurance plan, this cost may be covered.

More information about meningitis can be obtained at Thagard Student Health Center. If you have questions, please call us at 644-6230, visit us on the web at www.tshc.fsu.edu or email me at lsacher@admin.fsu.edu.
