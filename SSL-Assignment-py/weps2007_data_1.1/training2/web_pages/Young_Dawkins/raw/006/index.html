Littlejohn: 'Bigfoot' no stranger to island

By Jim Littlejohn
Lowcountry Characters

Jim Littlejohn
For those whose time in grade on Hilton Head Island is more than a few years, the memories of Young Dawkins help make the passing years easier to take, somehow.

Young, or Y.P. as some of his friends once called him, was an integral part of Hilton Head for almost a decade from the mid-70s on up to the 1980s. Some of his accomplishments prior to coming to Hilton Head include graduating from Dartmouth College in 1972, working as a cowboy on a ranch on the Red River between Texas and Arkansas, and serving as a reporter for the Sand Point News Bulletin, a weekly newspaper in Sand Point, Idaho.

Like a number of islanders, Young's association with the island was due to his father's decision to purchase property here in 1963. That was when Young got his first look at Hilton Head during a family vacation. It wasn't until Christmas 1975, however, that he actually decided to move here. His early teen-age memories of the island had much to do with his decision.

But 1975 was a sort of watershed year for Young. The Sand Point News Bulletin was named the best weekly publication in Idaho, Young won an award for the best news story in the state, his brief first marriage ended, and the Bulletin was sold to a paper that brought in its own management team. He was out of a job.

"When I came down to Hilton Head for the Christmas holidays, I really didn't have any plan on staying here any length of time," Young once told me.

But he eventually started writing for The Island Bulletin. It was edited by Bill Wright and it needed a good bit of editorial copy to fill up the blank holes. Bill, Young and George Cathcart began to supply the paper with stories about Bigfoot in the Lowcountry.

Each would write a chapter, in turn, and they had a great deal of fun with it. By the time the summer season was over, they had a number of stories completed and in June 1977 they brought out a book of them, titled "Moonshadows: The Search for a Legend."

According to Young, the book led local book sales for three months, but added, "Things fell off a bit when I decided that fall to go back to Idaho."

Actually, Young had changed jobs by that time. He first was made editor of a new magazine called The Hilton Head Quarterly and later took on the additional editorship of a weekly publication called The Island Financial Journal and Low Country Tattler.

The latter assignment gave Young a chance to write a good bit of humor. Not long ago I ran across an old clipping from the Tattler when Young, writing as Baron von Nosegay, compiled lists of "The Island's Ten Funniest Men," "The Island's Ten Most Powerful Women" and "The Island's Ten Sexiest Real Estate Salespersons."

Young stayed on in Idaho for a time, but when the only job he could find was helping a Mormon stonemason from Salt Lake City search the Idaho rivers for suitable tombstones, he elected to return to Hilton Head.

Home once more, he started working for a new magazine, Southern World. He helped with the first several issues of that publication and then became a copywriter for the Smelkinson/Cerrati Advertising Agency.

It was during this time that Young's humorous stories about the fictional private eye "Bull Tarragon" began to appear in the pages of the then-weekly Island Events magazine. These stories, written as a parody on the tough guy detectives of Mickey Spillane and others, were quite popular.

In 1983 Young returned to his alma mater, Dartmouth College, to work for the school's fund-raising department. His stay lasted for 10 years, during which time he was responsible for securing the largest single gift in the college's history -- $30 million to be used for construction of a new library. He was then vice president of Oberlin College in Ohio, where he again set records in fund-raising. The last time I talked to him, he was president of the University of New Hampshire Foundation, where, again, he was setting records for donations to that school.

While at Dartmouth he earned his master's degree in poetry and has been published in numerous journals throughout the nation.

Young now lives on the New Hampshire coast where, he says, "the country is prime for sightings of Bigfoot."

Whether or not the Lowcountry has seen the last of Young would be difficult to say, but one of his friends, a Dixieland jazz singer named Sweet Jelly Fontaine, feels that Young and his blues harmonica may indeed return to the warmer climate of South Carolina. "That cat's got too much marsh mud between his toes to stay up there forever," is the way he puts it.

Whether he does or not, there is one thing that's sure. Young Dawkins is, indeed, a Lowcountry Character.
