"Spiv," a handy English pejorative

Billy Clark quotes Richard Dawkins as calling George Bush an "unelected and deeply stupid little oil spiv" which sounds nastily apt even though I have no idea what a spiv is.

Thanks to Google, though, here's an explanation:

A spiv was typically a flashily dressed man (velvet collars and lurid kipper ties) who made a living by various disreputable dealings, existing by his wits rather than holding down any job. (Another name was wide boy, with wide having the old slang sense of sharp-witted, or skilled in sharp practice.) He was a small-time crook, living on the fringes of real criminality. He is most strongly associated with the period during and immediately after the Second World War in Britain; he always seemed able to get those coveted luxury items that were unobtainable in that period of austerity except on the black market, such as nylons.
The piece goes on to discuss possibly etymologies. Let us raise a pint to Shrub Bush, our first (and hopefully last) unelected oil spiv President!

The explanation matches up with what I was taught, with the added idea that US servicemen brought that acronym 'spiv = suspicious person itinerant vagrant' to Britain in the 1940s from, supposedly New York where [I read somewhere] SPIV was a police term. However on the face of it, backslang for VIPS - a suggestion I'd never heard - sounds just as, if not more, likely.

Funny Richard Dawkins said that about Bush. I've thought for a while Dawkins has a lot of anger inside him. Apparently as a young teenager Dawkins [amazingly] was a devout Bible-reader and found evolution unbelievable, contributing to a lot of the angry convert style he seems to have had since.

Perhaps it was just the first idea I came into contact with, but I can recall at five years of age, when first hearing of the deep past, at once finding evolution deeply logical and satisfying as an explanation, just ringing beautifully true as an aspect of the vastness of the world. I have never been able to imagine what it must be like to find evolution hard to grasp. Even if it wasn't true, finding it simply _unbelievable_ [like finding large numbers 'impossible to accept'] seems to me synonymous with shallow-mindedness and lack of imagination [my apologies if you're a creationist!]. So I think Dawkins is a bit of an intellectual snob, on account of having a rather narrow youth of his own to live down. Why should a British biologist comment on what kind of president you Americans elect anyway?

Ah, you're quite right....

I was just trying to be sensitive about remarks on other countries' leaderships. We all should take a mutual interest, I agree.

As far as bombing Iraq goes, I think people dislike hypocrisy or arrogance more than they should, but I can understand them getting upset about, for example, the West helping a character like Hussein to seize power, and then condemning him as not democratically elected a few years later. We have to sort this imperialist thing out one way or the other - either no intervention in other countries' politics, or a kind of full and open account - as in "Yes, we did put Pineapple Face in power in Panama, and now we accept this was a serious mistake". It is embarrassing that Britain helped someone like the Shah of Iran to take power and torture his own people, and no-one needed to explain their behaviour, because it was "post-empire".

I know this may sound off to an American, but if a major power is openly imperialist, at least there are lines of accountability. People are being tortured in the Shah of Iran's regime and it's still a British protectorate? Who in the Foreign Office or the Cabinet should answer questions in the House of Commons about this?

The official pretence that all its allies are independent and autonomous can make a certain kind of corrupting manipulation very seductive to officials in powerful countries like, currently, the US.

You're also right about the dangers of being too easily swayed by the sheer elegance of evolution. The philosopher of science Paul Feyerabend wrote an interesting essay in I think the 1980s called 'Defending Society against Science' where he makes the case that high-school science teaching doesn't actually teach children to think for themselves as it should, but in practice teaches young people to uncritically accept arguments from authority and not to question prevailing wisdom. I just can't quite see the young Dawkins as _too independent-minded_ to take evolution at face value. I suspect it's more likely that if he found Darwin's ideas hard to believe as a youngster, it was because he simply lacked imagination and vision.

Isn't real free thinking about being able to sense the appeal and breadth of an idea and still not be seduced without questioning it? Not believing things we don't really grasp is pretty easy by comparison.
