"I did not believe the information /

I spent more time in silence this morning, doing my best to simply listen to the world around me. This has had some noticeable effects, and all positive:

• The chatter in my head is greatly reduced.
• I am calmer, less agitated, and my breathing is more natural.
• I perceive all my senses are heightened, especially sight, touch and hearing.
• Certain insights into nagging problems appear possible.
To this last point, a new and significant further understanding re: forgiveness is coming into view. (More on this, perhaps, if it is appropriate.) And so for now, I am going to try to speak as little as possible. Try it for just a day at first. Just to see what happens.

Thursday, September 23 2004

[Il Manicomio]

"We know all our lines so well /

Last night I fell asleep on the couch for 10 minutes or so, only to wake up feeling as if I had been run over by a truck. Headache, body ache, nausea, the whole bit. Fearing the onset of the flu — just about everyone at Il Manicomio has been hacking or sneezing of late — I took some meds, chugged some juice and went to bed. What followed was a night-long parade of police and fire sirens. You'd have thought there was a Blitz reenactment going on...I half expected to see morning reveal a bombed-out hamlet that was once Dover. But all the sun lit up were the bags under my eyes...I look like Bela Freaking Lugosi. I was so out of it, so completely unable to focus, it took me five minutes to put on my socks. ("Open end on the foot first, right?") And the sirens continued...I had to dodge more fire trucks on the way into work. And yet: no accidents, not one whiff of smoke, nothing.

It feels like I'm in a Jean Cocteau film...and dammit, I can't remember my French.

Tuesday, September 21 2004

[Il Manicomio]

"I like the way the music goes;
there's a few good guys who can play it right..."

A2MD's performance last night was Theatre of the Weird, from beginning to end. Upon arriving at the venue, I noticed the marquee and posters had spelled out name "Atoomd." What the hell? "E tu, MD" I can see, perhaps even "Aytoo Emdee," but this? Astonishing creativity at work here.

The lad behind the bar was none too happy when a member of the band asked for a marker to correct the error. He was obviously having a bad day, which I can most certainly understand. Still, when one is in the "people business" and demonstrates a less than hospitable face before a even a single person has arrived for drink and general merry-making...this perhaps is an indicator of the need to consider a change in profession. Oh well, I thought, let it go. There's work to be done. (I did, at that moment, instruct all band members that forever more, the group was to be referred to by its full name only.)

I parked outside at 5:54 and brought my first load if gear in. Upon returning to the car to get my last load, I noticed a parking ticket for an expired meter, time-stamped at 5:58. Parking after 6:00, of course, is free. Ohferchrissakes. But I let that go too.

We were scheduled to begin playing at 8:00, although it is common practice to wait until the room fills, say 8:30. Looking around the room, the band, readers and those guest-listed outnumbered the other patrons 2-to-1, so we elected to wait a bit. This did not prevent the bartender from chewing one musician a new asshole. Okay, fine, let that go as well. 1-2-3-Play. The first set struggled through the initial getting-the-sound right, but quickly settled into the groove. The playing was right snappy all around, and the readers, especially young KT, were on fire. The still-small-but-slowly-growing crowd was most enthusiastic, and before we knew it more than an hour of words and music had passed.

Immediately after the set, KT informed us that she was being ushered out of the club. Two weeks shy of her 21st birthday, the bartender was laying down the law. I politely asked him to explain what was going on, and received a seriously gruff and defensive response. I explained that I had no intention of asking him to break the law or otherwise place the establishment in jeopardy; running the club is his responsibility, and deciding who stays and who goes is his right. I was merely inquiring about actions which were about to significantly affect my ability to carry out my responsibilities, which is my right. Further, I caught him in two, shall we say, "Ratherisms." One, that the state liquor commissioner was "in the club"; I personally know this man through my business, and he was nowhere in sight all evening. The second was that there were "no exceptions" to the under-21 rule; however, the establishment itself employs at least one such individual on its wait staff, and an exception for KT — who could technically have been considered in the employ of the venue for the night — could have been made.

But reason was not prevailing, and it was time to let go of this situation as well. KT went outside to sit in the car for the rest of the night, and we scrambled with the other readers to fill the void with partially remembered poetry and on-the-spot musical compositions. The remaining two sets went extremely well, the band playing better and better as the night went on. By midnight, the club was full of (mostly) enthusiastic listeners.

Following, some tense moments with Mr. Bartender regarding payment, and efforts to calm an understandably agitated KT. This was not her idea of a debut performance with the band.

Back at The Keep, I was bit agitated myself, and so decided to unload and stow equipment to work off the stress. By 2:00 am I was in bed, slept like a stone, and woke surprisingly refreshed this morning.

Oddly — or perhaps not — I've been getting emails today from all concerned, each politely acknowledging a fine time. I'm not sure if my hesitation to accept this at face value is a reflection of acute perceptive powers or deep-seeded neurosis. What is clear to me is that the challenges of playing out are both significant and surprising. The rewards of playing, then, must be equally so.
