Philosophical Generosity (AKA Why do I hate Richard Dawkins)

I’ve been wondering why it is that every time I see Dawkins name my blood just boils, especially when I see a quote. I figured out last night that I get irritated not so much by Dawkins ideas as by his attitude. Simply put Dawkins lacks philosophical generosity. Perhaps it is the case that this was not a concept that anyone ever taught young Dawkins at Oxford. Perhaps it might stem from the fact that Dawkins studied zoology and not philosophy. Whatever the case you would think that Dawkins would have stumbled across the concept by now.

I think the concept is really rather simple. When discussing philosophical matters try and give the most positive reading to your opponent, remember that you may think your opponent wrong but that doesn’t necessitate them being stupid, and try to act magnanimously .

I’m wondering if anyone else ever learned of such a concept, or maybe it’s just my little neck of the woods? Perhaps such a notion is well spirited but wrong-headed?

After months of review I think I've still hit on the reason why I don't care for Richard Dawkins. I'm of the opinion that there will always be a plurality of ideas and philosophical positions. Now I certainly don't think there all true or that some are even mostly true. But I try to avoid denegrating people I don't agree with. Just because you don't agree with me doesn't necessitate you being an idiot or an evil person. My friend Paul often talks of relying on the Ram Rod Principle. You can say what you want and disagree as long as your not a ram rod about it.

The real question is whether or not philosophical generosity is a desired trait. My talk of Dawkins was just a way to get around to it.

I agree with this assessment, and I'm gratified to hear others taking a similar perspective. The more of Dawkins I read, the more impressed I am by his intellect and his knowledge of certain aspects of biology, and ability to reason through difficult biological theories. However I'm also taken aback by exactly what Matthew calls philosophical ungenerosity.

Dawkins seems very much to assume that anyone reasoning from a background of any theistic tradition is arguing from revelation or "blind faith," and that this is the kind of reasoning that characterizes anything associated with religion. While he's not entirely wrong, it certainly seems to lead him to sometimes to cross that translucently veiled line we all (should) fear into assaulting strawmen.

This is indeed one of the things that for me best distinguishes Dawkins from other people who often take very similar positions on current issues. For example, Dawkins is often aligned in the media with Dennett, for example for their nearly simultaneous and similar assaults on Stephen Jay Gould, and their nearly simultaneous and similar support of the Brights movement. However, to me Dennett is a model of philosophical generosity, and takes great and admirable pains to argue against the strongest form of his opponents argument he can stomach. Some of his dialogs with Rorty are a great example of this.

I agree that philosophical generosity is a worthwhile value, perhaps a core value of what I think of as the modern liberal intellectual tradition, and that we can and should support it both through reasoned argument and by directly expressing our gut feelings about it.

To put the matter succinctly, as it was in a negotiation course I once took, I take as a useful and important guideline to be "hard on principle, but soft on people." To me that translates to giving some benefit of the doubt to opposing viewpoints, to explore them actively rather than simply taking our gut impression of them as accurate and final, but to do this exploration without capitulating on important principles. It's a challenge, but sometimes it isn't hard to identify people who seemingly haven't even accepted the challenge.

Hi guys,

Perhaps Dawkins view of a gene serves as a departure point.

Like you I had always been a little suspicious of Richard Dawkins' view, which seems to derive directly from Darwin's notion of survival of the fittest. But Darwin was honest enough to recognise the limits of that view as with his acknowledgment that the functioning of an eye was unlikely to have developed from random mutations and selection alone, certainly not from the selfishness of the cells that make up the eye.

Dawkins does seem to have toned down his view to the extent that he wrote a kind of apology for the 'selfish gene' notion with his 'Unweaving the Rainbow'.

I suppose the attribution of 'selfish' is an interesting point of departure in itself. Only human brains can predicate and speak in terms of selfishness. Dawkins is a human being, so it is obviously possible for a human being to call a gene selfish.

Then again the development of language faculties in our evolution must have come out of more than selfishness - there must have been something like cooperation. At the other end, the gene itself forms part of a cell, and cells must have a communicative mechanism since they exist in societies of cells that form things like human organs such as the Dawkins brain. Other cells specialise, like white blood cells, whose role is to sacrifice themselves for the good of the whole organism. It seems that when Richard Dawkins catches a cold, his recovery is due to the action of unselfish cells in his body. This seems to indicate that cells have the capacity to be 'aware' of something like a concept of the 'whole' (the whole Dawkins and nothing but the whole). This begins to get very complex and so there must be feedback mechanisms.

The least we can say is that Dawkins view of a selfish gene is insufficient, or inadequate, since the gene gives rise to unselfishness, cooperation, communication, organisation, and feedback at higher levels. Sure there must be a trace of selfishness, and this might translate to an element in say Schopenhauer's Will. Hmmm, now we're getting towards Nietzsche with his 'will to power'...did Nietzsche read Dawkins?...of course he didn't, but he read Darwin!!

There are microtubulins within genetic structures, and there are microtubulins in brain cells, which some theorists suggest employ quantum mechanics. Perhaps the feedback goes all the way down to the genetic level, but not Nietzschean feedback...Nietzsche is dead, and so is the selfish gene, and God is safe and well in Her heaven, planning the next evolutionary step.

I wonder whether, if Dawkins had done his work a decade earlier, in the 1960s, he would have ascribed 'loving' to the gene, since the 60s was the love era, as opposed to the late 70s which was the beginning of the selfish greedy 80s era.

I think Dawkins is quite fun to work with...I wouldn't say I love him though, even though I am a child of the 60s.

Quote:
Like you I had always been a little suspicious of Richard Dawkins' view, which seems to derive directly from Darwin's notion of survival of the fittest. But Darwin was honest enough to recognise the limits of that view as with his acknowledgment that the functioning of an eye was unlikely to have developed from random mutations and selection alone, certainly not from the selfishness of the cells that make up the eye.

Dawkins does seem to have toned down his view to the extent that he wrote a kind of apology for the 'selfish gene' notion with his 'Unweaving the Rainbow'.


I discovered something similar when I read Dawkins' blurb on the back of the jacket for Matt Ridley's book "The Origins of Virtue." Ridley's book is essentially about the role of cooperation in evolution. Rather than taking what might seem the predictably opposing stance, Dawkins said that Ridley's book would make a good second volume to his "Selfish Gene."

That surpised me until I went back to Selfish Gene and gave it a little more sympathetic reading. It seems to me in retrospect that in so strongly emphasizing the role of the gene in reproductive fitness, Dawkins was mostly putting a stake in the ground regarding the biological controversy of the level at which natural selection acts: on the gene, the individual organisms, or on groups of organisms (or species), the so-called unit-of-selection problem.

He was also pointing out the politicized nature of the concept of group selection, which seemed to often be used to try to escape the nasty implications of "survival of the fittest." The establishment of the modern synthesis in the 1930's and 40's coincided with a backlash against "social Darwinism" and racial theory (in which Darwin's ideas were applied to human culture in ways that made its potential for abuse particularly dramatic).

The postwar consensus equated social Darwinism and racial theory with free markets and individualism, and made the idea of selection for the benefit of a group particularly attractive to many. Against this consensus, pioneering biologists George Williams, William Hamilton, Robert Trivers, and John Maynard Smith emphasized selection at the level of the gene, in order to make biology respectably mathematical and strip it of some of its seemingly politically motivated conceptualizations.

When you read the debates between Dawkins, Gould, and others, the political element quickly becomes obvious, with each side accusing the other of extremes of politically motivated theorizing, and in the case of Gould, making the political element extremely dramatic and explicit.

Supposedly, one argument has it, if selection acted on groups rather than individuals (or genes), then we can be more comfortable considering ourselves cooperative social species, and this gives a better biological grounding for our ethics. However, not only is group selection unlikely under most conditions, but the argument from group selection to cooperative ethics is questionable. Selection for in-group-supportive behavior would likely mean selection for out-group-hostile behavior as well.

So whether group selection is part of human heritage or not, it does not really help us escape the presumably nasty implications of survival of the fittest. And, I think, it simply isn't true that Darwinian psychology implies psychological selfishness, but that's an argument for another place and time.

I think we can read the descriptions given by Dawkins in "The Selfish Gene" as a clever presentation of the general argument that evolutionary biology should be treated as a precise, quantifiable science by establishing natural selection as the central mechanism of evolutionary change, and the gene as the unit of selection for analysis.

To me, we can then often read his further books as qualifications of that idea, attempting to explain the various limitations of that view, as well as expanding on it. Dawkins' "The Extended Phenotype" for example makes the point that "selfish gene" calculations would under some conditions be virtually indistinguishable from group selection mathematically, making the same predictions.

Dawkins does, it seems to me, carry Darwin's legitimate program forward in the spirit of the modern synthesis, although it is not exactly clear that he grasps the philosophical and ethical subtleties of applying that program to human beings in a balanced way. His direct assaults on religion in general seem to me to bring that into question.
