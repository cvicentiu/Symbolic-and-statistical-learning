News and Information: Where Next?

May 04, 2005

Digital news paper

Jamie Cowling, formerly the media research fellow at ippr, has authored a paper, 'Digital News: Genie's Lamp or Pandora's Box?' which outlines the range of ways in which digitisation alters our understanding of what constitutes 'news', and the various critiques and counter-arguments about the performance of news agencies in public life today. This will set the context for a seminar we're holding next month, to explore the convergence of broadcasting and new media, and its implications for news values.

April 22, 2005

Consultation week 3: How can we empower better?

For the next week, we are seeking ideas, opinions and specific recommendations, in response to the following questions on how people can be reassured in the face of fast technological change:

  1. Is there any way that online civic networks can be constitutionally recognised or formally channelled into the democratic process?
  2. Which agencies are best placed (or should be created) to develop 'wired communities' or develop locally-based e-democracy innovations?
  3. How should regulators behave towards the grey area between publishing and socialising, such as weblogs? Is there a middle ground between regulation and laissez-faire (such as media literacy training)?
  4. The government is committed to encouraging the development of 'innovative broadband content'. How might this be encouraged at a range of regional and local tiers (as with community radio)?

Please post comments below, and number your response appropriately. If we would like to cite or use your comment in our publication, we will contact you personally first.

April 14, 2005

Consultation week 2: How can we reassure better?

For the next week, we are seeking ideas, opinions and specific recommendations, in response to the following questions on how people can be reassured in the face of fast technological change:

  1. In general, how should the government respond to the fears of that section of the public that do not want more personalised, more joined-up public services, in health in particular?
  2. Given how difficult it is to police data protection law, what more can the government do to uphold privacy rights in the digital age?
  3. How can Government better protect society from cyber-crime?
  4. What process should be implemented in circumstances where digitisation becomes an obligation, and not a choice (e.g. digital switch-over of broadcasting; closing down face-to-face public service channels)?
  5. Are there examples or recommendations for how Government can support education surrounding the risks of the digital age, as ISPs and others do for consumers?

Please post comments below, and number your response appropriately. If we would like to cite or use your comment in our publication, we will contact you personally first.

April 07, 2005

Consultation week 1: How can we innovate better?

For the next week, we are seeking ideas, opinions and specific recommendations in response to the following key questions on Britain's innovative capacity:

  1. Does Britain's competitiveness over the next five years depend on further infrastructural improvements, or should skills, content and media literacy issues become the Government's over-riding concern?
  2. What single measure (for instance, new governance structures) could support better relationships between public and private sectors, in the delivery of eGovernment projects
  3. What is the most significant obstacle to productivity growth in UK organisations, and are there any further interventions that Government could make to remedy this?
  4. How could ICT be channelled to better forms of workplace flexibility, such as tele-working, and how might public policy support this?
  5. Can Britain realistically expect to replicate the US 'New Economy'? If so, what is the single biggest factor in making it happen?

Please post comments below, and number your response appropriately. If we would like to cite or use your comment in our publication, we will contact you personally first.
