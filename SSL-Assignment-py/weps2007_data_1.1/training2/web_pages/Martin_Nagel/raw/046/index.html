Price takes Million Dollar Golf

SUN CITY -- Zimbabwe's Nick Price won his second Nedbank Million Dollar Challenge title at the casino resort yesterday in a nailbiting finish.

Price edged home at the Gary Player Country Club course by a single shot from local hero Ernie Els and USPGA champion Davis Love, with Californian left-hander Phil Mickelson, who led for most of the tournament, faltering in the closing stages to end up two back in fourth place.

Price closed with the sort of round he wanted, an almost flawless 68 that included five birdies and only one dropped shot.

That gave him a 72-hole total of 13-under-par 275, one better than Els and Love who closed with 67s, and two better than Mickelson who slipped to a 73.

The tension reached a peak on the 18th green. Price, just over the green with his second, watched in horror as the ball skidded off his wedge and ran more than 3m past the hole.

Els then thrilled the huge gallery by sinking a 20m putt which snaked across the green and into the cup. That put him level with Love, one behind Price, and a four-way play-off looked possible with Price facing a tricky putt and Mickelson still playing the 16th.

Price said he had felt before teeing off he would need to get to 14-under to have a chance of winning, but Mickelson had let the others in after his double-bogey at the eighth.

Mallett: We were majestic

EDINBURGH -- Springbok rugby coach Nick Mallett could not hide his delight after completing a European clean sweep with a record-breaking 68-10 defeat of Scotland at Murrayfield on Saturday.

"Overall, this was a fantastic performance, following on from the 52 points we scored against France in Paris. It shows that Springbok rugby is being allowed to express itself," he said.

"I felt we underperformed in the first half, our backs hadn't really had an opportunity to have a go. But in the second half we were absolutely outstanding when we managed to start getting the ball out wide. We were majestic."

Skipper Gary Teichmann had plenty of praise for the Scots, who fought bravely throughout, but always looked a second-rate outfit.

"They've had a lot of injuries, but they came on really firing. It's always difficult to take on a side which has played badly the week before.

"They did, however, make a lot of critical mistakes. When you're playing against a top side you cannot afford to to that."

The broadest smile of the night belonged to Bok wing James Small, whose two second half scores saw him post a new South African try-scoring record of 20 in 46 Tests.

"It was a very emotional moment for me," admitted a jubilant Small, who joined in singing 'Flower of Scotland' when the Scottish anthem was played before kick-off. It means a lot to me to break Danie Gerber's record. To do it here at Murrayfield was very special."

Scotland coach Richie Dixon said the Boks handed out a lesson on how to play modern rugby. "I was disappointed that we failed to capitalise on our first half chances, but the writing appeared to be on the wall going into the last quarter.

"The Boks simply had too much pace and power. In terms of our own development it's obvious that we're falling further behind the southern hemisphere nations."

Scotland flyhalf Gregor Townsend, who tasted victory against the Springboks with the Lions just four months ago, said: "I'm totally shell-shocked. We knew they had been improving , but we didn't expect that sort of hiding. South Africa have really established themselves as a force."

* The tour ended in disaster for Bok flyhalf Jannie de Beer, who damaged knee ligaments late in the firrst half and may miss the start of the 1998 Super 12 season.

* Mallett has called on provinces to give their leading Springboks a complete break from rugby until February.

Mallett is anxious to reduce the attrition rate among the country's top players in 1998, which has climbed alarmingly since the World Cup.

"I've given Sarfu a list of 25 players, which will only be announced in January. I've asked that they not be considered for any rugby such as sevens tournaments, pre-season tours and training camps before February 1.

"That will give them at least six weeks total break."

Mallett plans to have the group of 25, which includes several players who missed the tour through injury, assessed at the Sports Sciences Institute next month to see whether such stars as Natal lock Mark Andrews have fully recovered from niggling injuries.

"At the start of this season we had a lot of guys still battling with injuries. That's clearly unacceptable. You've got to give them time to recover so they can at least be fresh for the start of the season," he said.

Green machine buries Scotland

EDINBURGH -- South Africa rounded off their European rugby tour in fine style, burying Scotland with a second half try avalanche to record a record 68-10 victory at Murrayfield on Saturday.

The Springboks, who led 14-3 at the break, were simply light years ahead of their hosts in technique and ability.

Ironically, the injury-hit Scots, who crashed to their worst ever Test defeat, showed far greater commitment than they had against Australia. But they had no answer to the power and pace of the well-oiled green machine.

It was a momentous occasion with James Small scoring two of the Boks' 10 tries to break Danie Gerber's all-time South African try-scoring record of 19.

South Africa's highest score against any of the major Test playing nations was founded on a dominant tight five, backed by superb support play from the loose forwards and the rapier-like thrusts of a rapidly-improving back division.

In fullback Percy Montgomery -- who contributed 26 points via two tries and eight conversions -- and wings Small and Pieter Rossouw, South Africa now have a back three to match anything in world rugby.

They terrorised the Scottish defence with their breathtaking counter-attacking runs as the Boks showed no mercy to run their hapless opponents ragged.

As at Twickenham the week before, the tourists started slowly and at one point actually trailed 3-0. But their ability to step up a gear in the second half left their northern hemishere opponents far behind in their turbo-charged slipstream.

In the first half the Scots' aggressive defending succeeded in disrupting the South African momentum, Dick Muir and Jannie de Beer in particular being singled out for attention from captain Rob Wainwright and his back row colleagues.

But they never looked like going the distance against a side looking like genuine world champions.

The Boks softened them up with first half tries by Montgomery and Rassie Erasmus before closing in for the kill.

Within the space of four minutes the Scots had been floored by a three-try blast from Rossouw, skipper Gary Teichmann and Andre Venter.

It it had been a boxing match, the referee would have called a halt. But there were still some 20 painful minutes left for the home side to negotiate. If they had planned on conducting an exercise in damage limitation, they failed miserably.

Small duly passed Gerber's landmark with his 20th try in 46 Tests after Montgomery had done the spadework.

Montgomery then capped a splendid afternoon's work with a searing burst through the middle to raise the South African points total to 54.

As the home supporters made for the exits, Andre Snyman and replacement flyhalf Franco Smith added further tries to rub salt into the Scottish wounds.

Left wing Derek Stark, who had hardly seen the ball going forward, scored a deserved consolation try for the Scots.

Kiwis tame SA

ADELAIDE -- South Africa, fresh from a 67-run victory over Australia and rated as the top one-day cricket outfit in the world, were made to look very ordinary by the 10-1 tournament outsiders, New Zealand, here on Saturday.

Watched by close to 10 000 spectators, the Kiwis, who had gone winless throughout their 'Down Under' tour this summer, turned the form book inside out by coasting to a 47-run victory.

Asked to bat, the New Zealanders, propped up by a sixth-wicket partnership of 124 between man-of-the match Craig McMillan and Chris Harris, scored 224 for six and then nailed the South Africans down to 177 in 47,5 overs.

It was a clear-cut triumph, which left no room for excuses and, to his credit, South African captain Hansie Cronje didn't try looking for any.

"The pitch was perfect, the lighting was good, we just played poorly," he said. "Most of our batsmen needed two balls to score one run," he added, "and that just isn't good enough. Fifty-six after 15 overs isn't good enough. It was a poor effort."

The two countries have now met nine times and the Kiwis lead 5-4.

"We know how they play. We know what we've got to do against them," said Cronje. "They haven't deviated much from the approach they adopted in the '92 World Cup, but we failed to get 200 against them then and failed to get 200 again today.

"We have got to be more positive and back ourselves against their attack, which is basically just based on good line and length. We've either got to go over the top or create the gaps."

Having had the Kiwis struggling on 93 for five, however, the South African bowlers also let things slide a bit. Notable exceptions were Allan Donald, who turned in an exceptional two for 17 10-over spell, and Shaun Pollock, three for 36 from 10.

The rest, Cronje included, bowled too full. "In retrospect we probably should have dropped the ball just short of a length, like they did," he admitted, "but frankly it was our batting that let us down."

Delighted that his men had finally broken the ice -- and against a team like South Africa at that -- New Zealand captain Stephen Fleming said: "We are a lot more confident at the one-day stuff than we are at Test level and that makes a big difference." -- Sapa

Aussies win thriller

ADELAIDE -- Mark Waugh's 11th century in limited overs cricket internationals gave Australia a thrilling three-wicket victory over New Zealand at the Adelaide Oval yesterday.

Waugh, opening the innings with Michael di Venuto (77), scored a superb 104 off 113 balls as Australia scrambled past New Zealand's 260 for seven with two balls to spare.

Waugh and Di Venuto put on 156 for the first wicket in 26 overs, but Australia made hard work of wrapping up the win.

Australia, New Zealand and South Africa have one win apiece from two games each in the tri-series competition.

The Kiwis made a flying start to the game with openers Matt Horne (31) and Nathan Astle (66) bringing up the first 50 in the seventh over and making 77 before the first wicket fell.

Captain Stephen Fleming added 69 with Astle before the opener departed. Fleming went on to make 61.

Australia pegged back the batsmen late in the innings before Roger Twose slogged 26 off 20 balls.

Waugh and Di Venuto, who was dropped twice, gave Australia a perfect platform in reply before Di Venuto was stumped.

Waugh was second down with the total 202 in the 37th over and his dismissal was the first in a fall of four for 19.

Greg Blewett was out for a duck and Ian Harvey was out for three before Steve Waugh, batting six, and Michael Bevan at seven, guided Australia from 221 to 249.

Steve Waugh went lbw for seven, bringing Shane Warne in to join Bevan with 12 needed from 16 balls.

Warne missed three balls in the penultimate over to leave six runs needed from the final six balls, bowled by Gavin Larsen.

Warne took a single from the first, Bevan lofted a three over cover from the second and Warne was bowled for five off the third. Andy Bichel guided his first ball down to the vacant third man boundary for the winning four runs.

England regain
some rugby pride

LONDON -- New Zealand captain Justin Marshall admitted his side were fortunate to escape defeat after being held to a 26-26 draw by a fiery England side, who restored some pride to European rugby in a thrilling encounter at Twickenham on Saturday.

The All Blacks, widely regarded as the one of the greatest teams of all time, were stunned by Clive Woodward's new-look England, who bounced back from last Saturday's 29-11 defeat by South Africa in dramatic fashion.

It was a disappointing end to a gruelling year for the All Blacks, who had won all 11 of their matches before Saturday's encounter and were determined to make up for their poor display in the 25-8 win over England at Old Trafford two weeks ago.

"It was a tough game and we were very lucky to manage a draw," said Marshall. "You can't give international teams like England 23 points start.

"At half-time we knew we had to cut down on our errors. England played well, they were retaining the ball where, when we got it, we gave it away easily and they scored a couple of tries off mistakes of ours.

"Our ball retention was shocking for an All Black side."

England captain Lawrence Dallaglio suggested that New Zealand paid for failing to give his side enough respect.

"It was a fantastic game, all credit to the players and the coaching staff," said Dallaglio. "Their players didn't respect us, but we certainly went out there in the first half and earned a lot of respect."

England scored three tries -- by winger David Rees, eighthman Richard Hill and flanker Lawrence Dallaglio, while flyhalf Paul Grayson struck one conversion and three penalties.

In reply, the All Blacks scored tries through flyhalf Andrew Mehrtens and centre Walter Little. Mehrtens also kicked two conversions and four penalties.

England, written off after their defeat by the Springboks, got off to a sensational start with three tries in the first 18 minutes.

Dallaglio insisted that England were not celebrating their draw and that his team were disappointed not to win. "There's nobody celebrating in the England dressing room," he said. "We definitely should have won the match."

Josia wins in Japan

FUKUOKA (Japan) -- Atlanta Olympic champion Josia Thugwane spurted after passing the 30km to clock his personal best for an easy victory in the Fukuoka marathon yesterday.

The South African broke away from a bunch of a dozen runners and never faced a serious challenge afterwards, crossing the finishing line in 2hrs 7min 28sec.

Thugwane, who also set a new meeting record, broke his personal best of 2:08:06 he set in the London marathon, where he finished third in April this year.

"My target was to try to break the course record and so I tried to run first throughout the race," Thugwane, 26, said. "The pace maker ran very good. That allowed me to break the record.

"Last year I wasn't prepared for the race very well and I had to retire. This year I had planned to run well to win the race because if I couldn't do it . . . well, I lose the chance to be invited next year."

The top group of 13 runners included Thugwane, world champion Abel Anton of Spain, Paris marathon winner John Kemboi of Kenya and Tokyo City marathon winner Darren Wilson of Australia when they passed the 25km point.

Thugwane made his breakaway after the 30km mark and stayed in front for the rest of the race. Second place went to Toshiyuki Hayata in 2:08:07, followed by fellow Japanese Nozomi Saho in 2:08:47.

Huber defeats Hingis

FRANKFURT -- Anke Huber of Germany battled five sets for an upset of top favorite and World No. 1 Martina Hingis in the final of the first Masters of Champions tennis tournament yesterday.

The match, Huber's only tournament win this season, started off as expected with the 17-year-old Hingis rushing off to a 6-2 first set win.

Huber pulled even taking the second set 6-3, with Hingis bouncing back with a 6-2 third set, and Huber winning the fourth with the identical result as Hingis appeared to be tiring.

Then the battle started.

Hingis held her serve in the first game of the fifth set, then broke Huber's serve in the second game at zero, with the help of one of many net rollers in the match, and a double-fault by Huber.

But the 23-year-old Huber, who has dropped from No. 6 in world rankings to 14 this season, got a rebreak against Hingis in the 3rd game then held her own serve to tie the last set at 2-2.

Both women held their services to go to 5-5, with the volleys getting longer and the crowd loudly applauding as the two chased down shots that seemed impossible to return.

But the young Swiss star tired and in the 11th game and with the help of yet another net roller, Huber broke Hingis' service to pull ahead 6-5.

Hingis fought to come back in the last game going ahead 30-0, but Huber recovered to win the set 7-5, the match, a purse of 675000 marks ($381,000), and her only tournament of the year.

Hingis, who ends the 1997 season with 79 match wins against only six losses, got $324,858 as the runner-up.
