If Edward Fox's cousin hadn't been interested in photography, this would have been a very different story. When Fox arrived in the United States in the late 1800s from Russia, he needed a job. He found one, apprenticing with his cousin, who was a photographer. Fox enjoyed the field; so, in July 1902, he founded Edward Fox Photography (www.edwardfox.com) in Chicago, Ill. He ran the business with his wife and two daughters for many years.

"My grandmother [one of Fox's two daughters] worked at the studio from the time she was 13 or 14 until she was 86," says Ken Nopar, vice president and fourth-generation owner of the business.

During the studio's early years, people would come in to have their portraits taken, Nopar says.

"Our area of Chicago was largely populated by Eastern European immigrants. They would get their pictures taken and send them back to Europe. During 1942, when everyone was being drafted, my great-grandfather and his assistant, David Dribin, who worked here for 60 years, photographed 39 weddings in one day. All the servicemen were getting married before they shipped off," he says.

When Nopar's father, Richard Nopar, president, took the studio over from his parents in 1960, however, he made changes that brought about major growth.

"My father started doing candid photography. He went out and photographed weddings on location," Nopar says. Today, wedding photography makes up about 75 percent of the studio's sales. Other business comes from in-studio portraits and corporate photography, a market developed by the younger Nopar and Charlene Martin, director of Sales and Marketing for the studio.

Today, there are four Edward Fox Photography locations: the downtown Chicago studio, as well as one in the Chicago suburb of Schaumburg, Ill.; one in Milwaukee, Wis.; and one in the Detroit, Mich., suburb of Southfield. The studio also has sales offices in a number of bridal salons throughout the Chicago area. Edward Fox has 50 full-time employees, and about 75 independent photographers.

No company could succeed and grow for 100 years without the ability to adapt. When the lab the studio had been using for 35 years, LaSalle Photo Service, closed after its owner died, Edward Fox Photography acquired the equipment and opened its own lab, called Best Image, with Bob Wada, who had been LaSalle's general manager.

"It's been very successful," Nopar says. "We do almost all our own processing, except for portraits that need retouching. We send those out. But a number of other photographers and studios send their work to Best Image. The lab has a very good reputation."

A more recent change for Edward Fox Photography was the addition of Collages.net, a service by which wedding clients can view and order pictures online.

"The advantage is, if the bride and groom's families and friends live out of town, they can all view the pictures without the bride and groom having to ship them," Nopar says.

The company also recently acquired a digital imaging system, primarily for use in corporate photography.

"If a company publishes a newsletter and wants pictures from their event on a CD immediately after the event ends, we can provide that," Nopar says. "Or if a company invites clients to a black tie event, they often want to produce photographs and give them out that night as the clients leave. We would do that also."

But Nopar has no plans to start shooting weddings digitally any time soon.

"I don't think the quality of digital photography is as good," he says. "We have found the bride who shares the actual hardcopy proofs with families and friends comes back with a lot more reorders than if people are just looking at the proofs on the Internet."

Another reason to avoid shooting weddings digitally is the cost of equipment.

"Our photographers are predominantly independent contractors. They supply all their own equipment, and it would cost them quite a bit of money to transition into professional digital equipment," Nopar says. "That's not to say it won't happen at some point, maybe five years from now. It may eventually become a necessity. If film and paper costs become too high, we will look more seriously at the digital option."

In addition to technology, customers themselves have changed in recent years, Nopar says.

"Our customers are looking for different things than they did 10 years ago. They read every article that's ever been published about wedding photography and come in with longer lists of questions," he says. "We get more requests to meet their photographer beforehand, and more requests for black-and-white photography and photojournalistic photography."

As for the next 100 years--or at least the foreseeable future--Nopar says he hopes business continues to grow. "We have excellent, very loyal photographers and staff. We have always had as successful business. Whether we end up shooting digital or not, we want to continue to produce a fine product. If we do that, the customers will continue to come," he says.
