The 'Fire Friar'
After nine years as a New York Fire Department chaplain,
Fr. Mychal F. Judge, OFM, met ‘Sister Death’ while on duty
The following interview with Fr. Mychal was conducted last year for a publication of the liturgical ministers at New York's St. Francis of Assisi Church. Fr. Mychal became the first officially listed casualty of the Sept. 11 terrorist attack on the World Trade Center. He was killed when struck by falling debris from a tower, while administering the last rites to victims. A news photo of him being carried from the scene by rescue workers has become an international symbol of the tragedy. Nearly 3,000 participated in his funeral Mass Sept. 15. He is buried in the friars' plot of Holy Sepulchre Cemetery, Totowa, N.J.

Most liturgical ministers wouldn’t know Fr. Mychal Judge if they bumped into him. He is not on the Mass or confession schedule and is rarely seen around church or the friary. Why? Because since1992 he has been serving as the chaplain for the Fire Department of New York (FDNY) for the boroughs of Manhattan, Staten Island and the Bronx. He ministers to the firefighters and their families. In this job he is present at many serious fires and has ministered to injured and dying burn victims and their families and conducted many funerals. We may hear about him but rarely see him. Let me introduce you to him now.

 

I felt a tremendous urge to be a priest
 
Fr. Mychal (left) and Fr. justin Eccher, OFM, (right) welcome General Minister Fr. Constantine Koser, OFM. in 1971


Fr. Mychal Judge was born in Brooklyn to Irish immigrant parents. His father died when he was 6 years old after having been hospitalized for more than three years. “I never called anyone ‘dad.’ I’ve always been very much on my own.” His parents ran a boardinghouse and after his father’s death, his mother, his two sisters (one of them is his twin) and Fr. Mychal lived there. He handled much of the handyman tasks for their house. “Although we had nothing, my mother made us feel like we were upper middle class.” His sisters are both widows now and he has “a tremendous relationship with them.” He lived in Brooklyn but traveled frequently to Manhattan to shine shoes at Grand Central Station and Penn Station. "I was always hustling." On these excursions, he stopped in at St. Francis Church.

Why would he come all the way from Brooklyn to this church? “Where else in the world would you go? I remember the lower church and the fountain and a friar. I loved him and his brown robe. I used to follow him around everywhere. I knew then that I wanted to be a friar.” He entered the seminary at 15 and has been a priest for 40 years. He was called. “I felt a tremendous urge to be a priest and answered “yes.” “The life I have is not what I planned on, but I had no idea what life I’d have. You just don’t know, so you just go and take one day at a time. I don’t get into tomorrow because God hasn’t created it yet. So I live in today as best I can for the glory of God and His people.”

Being a FDNY chaplain is “marvelous.” “I’ve done all kinds of work, parish work, ministering to the poor and to the homeless and to people with AIDS. I’ve been assistant to the president of Siena College and took a sabbatical in England studying. When the FDNY chaplain, friar Fr. Julian Deeken, was dying, I filled in for him. A week later I was offered the job. I said ‘yes,’ and so here I am.”
 
A 1999 meeting at Siena College of the province's fire chaplains: (left to right) Bro. David Schlatter, Br. Christopher Coccia, Fr. John Piccione, Fr. Gregory Brennan, Fr. Mychal and Fr. John O'connor.
The first thing I do each day is get down on my knees and pray

 

How does he prepare for this job? “The first thing I do each day is get down on my knees and pray, ‘Lord, take me where you want me to go, let me meet who You want me to meet, tell me what to say and keep me out of Your way.’ He’ll show me how. But I learn. I make mistakes. Life is a learning process. I learn from people. I watch and listen. When I was a pastor, a newspaper article referred to me as ‘the listening priest.’ ”
 
I’m only the means. It’s the Lord
 
CREATE's Fr. Benedict Taylor, OFM, chats with board member Fr. Mychal while in procession with Fr. Severin Brady, OFM.(left) and Fr. Vincent McCann, OFM, during the 150th Anniversery of St. Francis Church, May 7, 1994.

 

How to minister to those suffering with serious illness? “I don’t plan anything. I come into their presence and silently pray, ‘Lord, be with us, let Your will be done in our lives, show us, have mercy on us.’ I greet them and then start out with a simple verbal prayer, ‘Lord, thank you for bringing us together.’ You see, I’m only the means, the instrument, it’s not me, it’s the Lord. It’s His world. I respond with empathy and ask how I can help them.
 
Fr. Mychal accompanied Detective Steven McDonald and his son Conor to Northern Ireland's Long Kesh Prison to promote reconciliation.

A weekend vacation to a California prison

Fr. Mychal knows “Tim,” a man who is incarcerated in California for a drug offense. Tim wrote to Fr. Mychal and asked him to write back and, if possible, to send a few dollars since he cannot earn money in prison. Fr. Mychal began to write to him regularly. Tim’s family abandoned him and he has been incarcerated for three years without any visitors or letters from home. Now he faces an additional six years in a federal prison. A priest friend of Fr. Mychal also lives in California and extended an offer for Fr. Mychal to visit but he never did.

Recently, Fr. Mychal was given a free airline ticket to travel anywhere in the U.S. “I thought I could use that ticket to visit Tim.” He made arrangements to fly to California and stay with his friend and visit Tim. He flew to the West Coast and stayed overnight and drove the next morning to the prison. He arranged in advance for permission to meet Tim but when he arrived, it was pouring rain and he couldn’t find a parking place. When he finally parked, he was 15 minutes past the scheduled time and was denied admission.

Fr. Mychal pleaded to be admitted and the official finally consented. Tim was not told that a guest was scheduled. The room where prisoners and guests meet is divided by a glass partition and they are not allowed to touch each other. “While I waited, I prayed, ‘Lord, let this visit go well, take care of him. Let Your will be done in our lives,’ When Tim entered and saw me, the most beautiful smile came over his face. He said, ‘What are you doing here? I’ve never had a visitor.’ So we sat and I listened for an hour and a half, then I blessed him and left. I was so grateful. You can’t prepare for a visit like this. You just stand there and let it happen and the words will come.”


I always say ‘yes’ to life
 
Fr. Mychal and Conor McDonald

How many of us if given a free ticket to travel anywhere would use it to make a cross-country return trip in less than four days to visit a prisoner who we barely knew?

Why did Fr. Mychal do it? “The idea just came to me. I said ‘yes.’ I always just go. I always say ‘yes’ to life. Saying ‘yes’ often means responding affirmatively to those who need help. It means giving support. I don’t know how I keep going, I just do it. I don’t plan to tell someone who is suffering anything in particular. I just empathize with them and encourage them to find a solution in dealing with their situation. I pray ‘Lord, show me, show them, what to do. I’m asking for Your will to be done in our lives. Please, help. I love You, I trust You, I believe in You, I hope in You. Stay with us. Help us.’ Something has to come. And maybe a few days later the person will become a little stronger and take a few steps to improve their situation and someone somewhere says something that they hear and it stays with them. That’s how God works with you. God’s grace can touch a person and change their life. It takes courage and faith. We are all filled with fears but the Lord is there to relieve you of your fears.”

As FDNY chaplain, he sees a unique form of suffering. He is alerted to all serious fires in his boroughs and is called to the bedside of injured firefighters. “You could work at this 24 hours a day ministering to the firefighters and their families. It’s endless. There is so much work to do. They have very stressful jobs. As much as I can I talk with them. That way I learn to hear them, and listen to their anxieties and fears, and how they deal or don’t deal with those fears. And how I can help them. For example, one day while I was filling in for Fr. Julian, I received a phone call that a lieutenant was killed by a falling tree on an upstate highway. I was accompanied by his fellow officers and went to break the news to his wife. She sat in a chair and I knelt next to her. She asked me if there was any chance if he was killed by a tree because ‘I passed a car accident this afternoon on the highway but I didn’t stop to look.’ What could I say? It was very hard that time and it’s hard each time.”
 
I, Mychal Judge, am not capable of doing these things on my own
 

I wondered whether God gives him a special grace to face these situations? “There is no question about it at all. None. I, Mychal Judge, am not capable of doing these things on my own.” When I asked him how ordinary men do extraordinary things, I mentioned Fr. Maximilian Kolbe. He said, “He is running through my mind at this very moment. I pray to him every day. His story is absolutely extraordinary. It is definitely God’s grace. The wonderful thing about that is saying ‘yes’ and accepting that grace. You could say ‘no’ and walk away. But when we accept it and say ‘yes’ and go forward, great and wonderful things will happen. It takes courage in the midst of fear and you do it with the grace of God.”

I love to walk...
I love the Brooklyn Bridge
 

“I love to walk because it releases all the stuff pent-up inside. There is something for me about the Brooklyn Bridge—I must walk it at least once a week. I walk from the friary downtown then across the bridge and maybe keep going out to Brighton Beach. I get an ice cream cone there and then come home. I get ideas on the Brooklyn Bridge even when I’m not looking for one. The stone on the buttresses of the bridge is from West Milford, N.J., the place where I was last pastor. I love to look at the Statue of Liberty, the lights of the city, the Verrazano Bridge, the Manhattan Bridge carrying the subway cars. The city is just the most extraordinary place to live.”

 

I’ve had the best life of any friar

 

“Making decisions at age 67 is not the same as making them at 37. I pray all the time for guidance on how to make decisions. I don’t know what’s next for me. I’ve had the best life of any friar in this house or of any priest I know. It’s great to be a Franciscan. I take the ups and downs, the joys and sorrows, but that’s life and I try to keep them balanced. I get down on my knees and pray, ‘Lord, help me.’ I have to get God in there because it’s His world and He loves us and wants to hear from us.”

 


TWA Flight 800

 

“I was home and received a phone call from officer Steven McDonald asking me whether I heard about the crash of TWA Flight 800 on Long Island. He told me that the families were meeting at a hotel at the airport. ‘Are you going? I think you should.’ So I left immediately and drove out to the hotel. I went as a chaplain and a priest. People were gathering and Mayor Rudolph Giuliani was trying to help them with a manifest to inquire whether their loved ones were on the plane. I stood next to him and began to move with him from table to table. Then I went home and returned the next day and said Mass. We sat and waited for 16 days for the bodies to be found. During that time I got to know these people and went to the funerals for their loved ones.” Who among us would want to wait 16 days for washed-up bodies? Who would want to comfort the grieving? Fr. Mychal goes where most people wouldn’t go for a million dollars. And he does it again and again and again.
Fr. Mychal often walks right into the middle of a catastrophe - the kind of chaos that would cause most people to run the other way. He goes into devastation and comforts the injured and grieving people without knowing anyone there. He walks into chaos and begins to minister without an invitation. “I don’t think about it, I just do it. I walk right in, hold a hand, wipe a tear and say a prayer. That’s not me, Mychal Judge. That’s the grace of God. I don’t worry about the details or arrangements. It’s a mystery. It’s God.”
 
Fr. Mychal offers himself to God in the true spirit of the prayer of St. Francis
 
“Lord, make me an instrument of Your peace.” Fr. Mychal’s humility makes it possible for him to let God work through him to minister to His people. Being open to the workings of the Holy Spirit, saying “yes” to God’s call, “yes” to the calls of the injured, the suffering and the dying, “yes” to whatever God asks even when Fr. Mychal has no idea of where he will go or what he will do or say. He simply places his trust in God and let’s God’s will be done. The next time you hear the sirens of a fire truck, think of Fr. Mychal as he accompanies the firefighters into the next tragedy, heading straight for the flames and confusion, offering himself as God’s instrument to comfort the injured and the dying.
Six members of Engine Co. 1 and Ladder Co. 24 firehouse on West 31st Street, Manhattan (above), were among more than 350 firefighters who lot their livesat the World Trade Center site.
 
I have a wonderful life - but I work at it and pray continuously
 
“There are so many great things in my life. I have a wonderful, wonderful, wonderful, fruitful life - but I work at it and I pray continuously. I have to work at it because life is a mystery. I pray ‘Lord, show me, take me, mold me, fashion me, show me what You want.’ ” How does he know what God wants? “I watch and listen and it will come. The phone will ring or someone will ask for something. It just happens like it has for the past 67 years and it’s just beginning.”
By Patricia Ballner
