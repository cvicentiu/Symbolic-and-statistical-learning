If There Were a Real Alternative in '04… by Gregory J. Brennan
Posted on Tuesday, May 20 @ 13:32:48 EDT by eHezi

If there were a genuine Democratic alternative to George W. Bush’s current conservative government in the 2004 election I wonder what it would look like. If one wanted to answer that question and went to South Carolina to observe the Democratic debate and get an idea they probably would have left disappointed and with no better an understanding of what ideas distinguish Democrats from Republicans than they had before they arrived. With the exception of Richard Gephardt’s health care plan, there was not a single idea expressed that substantively distinguished Democrats from Republicans. The debate could not have been an encouraging sign to Democratic voters who are hoping their party can somehow emerge from the 2004 election with at least a foot hold in one branch of federal government.

Beginning with the mid-term elections and extending up to the recent presidential primary debate in South Carolina, it is clear that Democratic leaders still do not grasp the kinds of substantive philosophical and policy changes that are necessary for Democrats to rebuild a governing majority. A good place for Democrats to start is by declaring to the American public a clear philosophy of governance. Republicans have done this quite successfully over the last 20 years. Most Americans know how Republicans view government-less is always better. Ask a person to define today’s Democratic view of government just as succinctly, and the person would be hard pressed. If anything they would be most likely to repeat the Republican mantra of how Democrats view government-the bigger the better. As long as voters remain uncertain as to how Democrats view government, democratic leaders and presidential hopefuls when they present policy ideas in the coming year will continue to come across to the voting public as reactionaries espousing knee jerk responses to a Republican governing majority that is certain of itself and the direction it wants to take the country.

The fact is if Democratic leaders are uncertain or unwilling to declare their view of government and the role it should play in the life of the country rank in file Democrats are not. They want their leaders to return the party to its New Deal roots, and that means embracing the view that government can help citizens build a better life and a better country. Ever since President Reagan announced to the American public in the 1980s that “government was the problem” and that once government was removed from peoples backs America’s social ills would improve Democrats have distanced themselves from their New Deal heritage. President Reagan was wrong about government. Government will always play a role in our lives. The real question for the country to decide is what kind of role it will play.

Conservative Republicans adhere to a philosophy that says cut government where ever and when ever possible. What you cut doesn’t matter. What is of singular importance is that you cut and thereby achieve smaller government. Conservatives pursue this policy by attempting to starve the government of revenue through tax policy. The most recent and best example of this is President Bush’s tax cut proposals and his administrations unwillingness to fully fund key government programs like education programming – “Leave No Child Behind” and “Home Land Security”. The fact is history does not support the current Republican view of government. Government has played an important role in this country’s history. It has helped fight and win wars, provide citizens with a measure of security in old age, protect the environment, combat disease and educate children through publicly funded schools. None of the advances in these areas of American life could have occurred without government playing an important role. Government has not always been perfect, but over all Americans have and continue to benefit from government action.

If Democrats want to offer a real alternative to voters in the 2004 election they should begin by declaring what many in the Democratic Party believe to be true: Government has a role to play in peoples lives and citizens should not be afraid to put government to work for people where and when it can be done so effectively. What Democratic leaders need to do is to present to Americans where and how government can play a role. For too long, Democratic leaders have been afraid to make such a declaration. They have run away from a proud record of putting government to work for people through Medicare, Medicaid and Social Security. Democrats lost in the last election because they tried to blur the line between Democrats and Republicans. If Democrats are willing to take the risk of declaring that government has a role to play in building a better country, they may not win the 2004 election, but they will at least have offered American voters a real alternative.

Note: Mr. Brennan researches and writes progressive political commentary. He has worked on political campaigns and managed foreign assistance projects for the Federal Government. He has a Masters Degree in Public Administration and is working on a Masters in Education. Please send inquires or comments by e-mail to Gregory Brennan
