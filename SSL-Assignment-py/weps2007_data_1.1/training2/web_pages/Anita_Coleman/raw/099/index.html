I'd like to comment on the relevancy of S.R. Ranganathan's The Five Laws of Library Science to Open Access.

Anita Coleman, editor of dLIST, has suggested that the origins of contemporary OA appear in SRR's Five Laws, originally published in India in 1931. See Anita's posts on the OA Librarian blog:

http://oalibrarian.blogspot.com/2005/11/open-access-is-not-new.html

http://oalibrarian.blogspot.com/2005/11/ranganathan-open-access.html

Likewise, Judith Turner, Editor of Journal of Electronic Publishing, recently argued at a colloquium in Bangalore that the "roots" of OA can be traced to SRR. See:

http://www.deccanherald.com/deccanherald/sep182005/state1942332005917.asp

http://www.hti.umich.edu/j/jep/index.html

When SRR used the phrase "open access," he was referring to open-stack shelving, a then new arrangement by which library patrons could physically browse and pull books off shelves without staff intervention. Interestingly, use of open-stack shelving raised concerns about theft and preservation, much like current OA is concerned with intellectual property rights and preservation of rapidly changing/obsolete technology.

In 1996, Finnish scholars Timo Kuronen and Paivi Pekkarinen demonstrated a closer link between SRR's Five Laws and OA when they argued that Five Laws could be applied to virtual or digital libraries with the addition of two supplementary laws, "Every reader his library" and "Every writer his contribution to the library" (Herald of Library Science, v. 35 n. 1-2, pp. 3-17). Kuronen and Pekkarinen envisioned a "citizen-centered civil society" in which "the virtual library may make available and accessible information which contradicts with the prevailing societal order."

While current self-archiving OA repositories enable users to organize virtual libraries and contribute their own works to civil discourse, the "idea of emancipation" is indeed deeply rooted in the original Five Laws, written by a relatively young and inexperienced librarian within the context of the Indian struggle for independence, extreme poverty and illiteracy, and harsh discrimination based on religion and caste, ethnicity, gender, etc.

As with OA, SRR's Five Laws are frequently presented simply as a means to improve user convenience or customer service. Taken out of context, the significance of SRR's exclamations "Books are for use" and "Every reader his book," published at a time when books promoting Indian nationalism were censored and banned, is difficult to understand. At the very least, SRR's Five Laws should encourage us to think about the meaning of OA within the context of our times.

For more info on SRR's Five Laws see:

Ranganathan Yogeshwar. S.R. Ranganathan, Pragmatic Philosopher of Information Science: A Personal Biography, 2001.

Mohamed Taher, Libraries in India's National Developmental Perspective, 2001.

N. Ravi, ed. The Hindu Speaks on Libraries, 1992.

Anthony Read and David Fisher, The Proudest Day: India's Long Road to Independence, 1997.
