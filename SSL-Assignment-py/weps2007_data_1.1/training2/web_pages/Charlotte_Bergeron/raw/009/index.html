PAR is on track with call for ethics reform
October 9, 2003
 
Issue:
Louisiana’s negative image is damaging to economic development.
We Suggest:
PAR recommends changes that could have a positive effect.

Louisiana has a national reputation for poor ethics practices, and much of it has been earned by the actions of elected officials and gaping loopholes in state lobbying and campaign finance laws. The Public Affairs Research Council, a Baton Rouge independent public policy group, has offered some strong measures for improving the state's reputation.

The group’s recommendations for sweeping reforms are prompted largely by the undeniable fact that the shabby reputation hampers industrial inducement efforts. As PAR research analyst and lawyer Charlotte Bergeron points out, “a state’s image is one of the many factors businesses consider in deciding where to locate or expand operations.”

The 23 recommendations in the report focus primarily on the role of money in politics. The report includes a recommendation that legislators not be allowed to accept campaign contributions during legislative sessions and that businesses should not be able to make contributions to the elected officials of regulatory agencies that oversee them.

A revolutionary recommendation is that the lobbying law that applies to the Legislature, also be applied to the governor’s office. PAR urges that lobbyists in all cases be required to provide detailed reports on their spending, and that those who hire lobbyists be required to report their lobbying expenses.

These and other recommendations in the PAR report deserve strong support. The chances that such support will be forthcoming, however, are slim. They must be voted on by people who have a vested interest in maintaining the status quo.

We doubt seriously that a majority of elected officials will come forward in support of the recommendations. It is even more doubtful that enough votes can be garnered in the Legislature to convert the suggestions into statutes.

The bottom line is that only public recognition of the problem and strong pressure from the voters will compel adoption of PAR’s badly needed program of change.
