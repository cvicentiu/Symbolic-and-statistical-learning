Gold rush: U.S. age groupers claim 12 titles at world's
...Continued

Lynda Hickam of Canada took a 1:24 margin of victory over Susan Bathgate of Great Britain with a 2:29:17 clocking in women's 50-54. Canada's Irene Becker took 2:30:01 to whip Tiare Lund by 6:17 in women's 55-59. Canadian Graeme Martindale took the men's 25-29 by a razor thin 11 seconds with 1:58:44 time.

Great Britain's Helen Cawthorne took the women's 40-44 crown with a 2:13:17, which left her 1:10 up on Edie Fisher of Canada. Catriona Morrison of Great Britain took women's 20-24 with a 2:06:31, best overall women's amateur time, 3:07 faster than countrywoman Tanja Allen. And Jack Maitland of Great Britain took men's 35-39 with a 1:58:07, just nine seconds in front of runner-up Bruce Gennari of the U.S. Ferrous MacLean of Great Britain won 30-34 with a 1:59:09, just 43 seconds up on Chris Peeters of Boulder, Colorado, pushing Great Britain's gold total to four.

Australia was next with three golds. Oz star of the future Paul Matthews took men's 16-19 with a 1:57:31, a 30-second margin over Leonardo Chacon of Costa Rica. Kevin Ferguson of Adelaide, Australia, took revenge on the USA's Tony Schiller for his 40-44 win last year at Perth, scoring a 15-second win from John Brockenbrough of the U.S. in 1:59:38. Schiller was a close third in 1:59:59. Joan Coates of Australia took a 1:09 win over Corinna Goodman of Canada to win women's 60-64 in 2:44:23.

Dr. Guenter Beilstein of Germany won the men's 65-69 title with a 2:21:31, 7:46 ahead of runner-up Robert Goddard of New Zealand. Junior elite Sebastian Dehmer raced to an eight-second win in 1:51:22 to increase Germany's total to two.

Orjan Sandler of Sweden, winner of a bronze medal at the 1968 Winter Olympics in Grenoble in 10,000-meter speed skating, won his eighth ITU World title with a speedy 2:10:00 to take 60-64 by 5:50 over Kevin Taylor of Australia.

Jean Claude Cestideau of St. Denis, France, won men's 55-59 with a 2:13:19, topping David McNedy of the USA by 4:45.

Megan Hall of Ginsborn, South Africa, won the women's 25-29 title with a 2:08:09.

The men's 20-24 category was under review after Rodrigo Quiroga of Bolivia was timed in an impossibly fast 1:42:11, with a too-fast 49:04 bike split, temporarily topping the list of men's 20-24 winners by 16 minutes over Francisco Serrano of Mexico.

Hoping one day to follow in the golden footsteps of countrywoman and Olympic champion Brigitte McMahon, Nicola Spirig of Switzerland topped the women's junior elite with a 15-second victory over Italian Beatrice Lanza in 2:04:11 for her country's lone gold at the end of a 12-hour day of competition.

Sunday at 11 a.m, the elite women will tee off for the 13th ITU World Olympic distance title, with Canada's Carol Montgomery the narrow favorite over Michellie Jones of Australia and Siri Lindley, Barb Lindquist and Joanna Zeiger of the United States. Canada's Olympic gold medal winner Simon Whitfield is favored to win the crown over Chris McCormack of Australia when the men's race begins at 2 p.m.
