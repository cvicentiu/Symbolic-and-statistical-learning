September 19, 2000

-an adventure in learning-

 

September 11 - 19

Tourist symbolism has not caught on in the far reaches of Western Australia. A simple roadsign, the only break in an endless horizon, indicated we had crossed the Tropic of Capricorn for the second time. The coastal region directly below the tropics is unique in that it doesn't experience the dual seasons of the wet and the dry. The climate is close to ideal and the city of Carnarvon makes the most of a year-round growing season with luxuriant plantations that provide all the ingredients for bowls of fruit salad. The wide streets were lined with dazzling displays of poincettas, bougainvilleas and hibiscus and we saw green lawns again thanks to the plentiful supply of water provided by the Gascoyne River. We made the most of the opportunity to sample the fresh fish and prawns that provide another thriving industry to the region.

Boundary Fence

 I reached the Overlander Roadhouse hot, tired and dry after 100 km of crosswinds to be met by a sign that warned, "Do not ask for water as refusal may offend". We avoided the prospect of being offended by having an ample supply of Carnarvon water and enough time in the day to ride another 35 kms before finding a place to pull off the highway to camp for the night. We had surprise visitors in the morning when two emus walked into camp, at first oblivious to our presence, but then dancing off in their strange swaying, bobbing, wingless gait as they became aware of us. It was a busy morning as their vacated spots were filled by a herd of wild goats. We were to see large numbers of feral goats and sheep in the next few days until reaching the Murchison Boundary fence that stretches for 1700 km across Western Australia providing a barrier to stop them munching on the greening vegetation that met us as we headed south.
Wildflowers are a major reason people travel to Western Australia in the spring. The more we followed the compass south, the more colourful and prolific the roadside displays of wildflowers became. The isolation of Western Australia is a factor in the uniqueness and variety of the species to be found here, numbering up to 12,000. Approaching Kalbarri National Park the road was lined with the silvertipped branches of the smokebush and yellow flowering wattle with clusters of red banksias, grevilleas, kangaroo paws and patches of everlastings in carpets of brilliant pink, yellow and purple. It was a spectacular showcase that slowed our progress as June insisted on identifying each new species she saw. At a roadside reststop I caught up to June who introduced me to a fellow wanderer, Mary Ebbott. Mary's daughter, Abby Watkins lives in Golden and along with Rick Marshall spends much of her time in the mountains climbing and exploring. We look forward to making a connection with them upon our return.
Kalbarri is one of those jewels on a traveller's roadmap that makes you want to hang around for a while longer. Surrounded on one side by heathlands of exquisite wildflowers and on another by the deep red-rock gorges of the Murchison River and yet on another side by an ocean that has carved a jagged coastline of red-banded cliffs, Kalbarri has managed to retain a low profile village atmosphere that provides a sheltered harbour from the storms of the world. It may not have appeared so idyllic for the first settlers in the area. Back in 1629 two mutinous crew members from the Dutch East India trading ship, the Batavia were put ashore here, giving them the distinction of being the first European settlers, albeit unwillingly, in this country yet to be discovered by Captain Cook.

Kalbari Cliffs

The Batavia shortly thereafter hit a reef and sank as did numerous other early visiting ships to the region. The constant pounding of the Indian Ocean surf has claimed massive chunks of red sandstone and white limestone from the towering cliffs, continuously moulding the landscape into caves, natural bridges and rugged coastal gorges. One of the trips most memorable meals was eaten at Finlay's B.B.Q. in Kalbarri, Aussie style, cooked in a tin shed and eaten outside under the stars on a sand floor sitting around a campfire. The pile of seafood and salad was staggering and entertainment was provided by anyone who wanted to get up and play an instrument or sing. Need a napkin?, grab a toilet roll and drinking water came in wine bottles. You couldn't complain about the service in this restaurant, there wasn't any. If you didn't serve yourself you went without.
We thought we had taken a wrong turn out of Kalbarri and had somehow ended up in England. The countryside quite suddenly became rolling green farmland in total contrast to the scenery of the past 3 months. Northampton was a stopover where we visited the local school. Set in rolling hills dotted with sheep and smothered in purple wildflowers, it is an agricultural village with a population of 800 and quaint sandstone block buildings. Teacher Ann Williams arranged for us to give a presentation to a large group of students ranging in age from grades 1 to 7. They were a very attentive and well-behaved group of students and it was most rewarding to visit a school that my former students had been emailing from Kimberley.

Following the coast road down to Perth we camped overnight in a shanty town of lobster fisherman shacks. Lobster fishing is big business which is encouraging the haphazard growth of squatter's shacks along the remote beaches. Government bulldozers were busy at work on a not-very-popular clean-up campaign.

Pinnacles Desert

The southern beaches have the whitest sand we had ever seen, due to a high concentration of lime in the region. It is also one of the richest areas for plant species in the world with the heathlands boasting more than 900 recorded species, more than all the native plants recorded for Great Britain. It is difficult to fathom since the sandy soils are extremely low in nutrients and don't hold moisture well but the plant life has adapted to this adversity and evolved in amazing numbers. We discovered an interesting desert landscape when we visited the Pinnacles Desert near Cervantes. Huge limestone pillars rise out of the surrounding desert sands resembling ancient tombstones. The early Dutch explorers seeing them from offshore thought were seeing the remains of an ancient city.

It is time for a break and we went in search of a T.V. to watch the opening ceremonies of the Sydney Olympic Games. It certainly was a spectacular opening and the first few days of competition indicate that this Olympics will be a very successful event.

Questions for students to ponder:

1. On a map locate the geographical position that we crossed for the second time at 23 degrees 26.42 minutes South and 113 degrees 57.22 minutes East.

2. How soon before Captain Cook's discovery of Australia did the earliest European visitors, (the two mutinous crew members from the Batavia) land on the west coast?

3. What is the only other city in the southern hemisphere to host an Olympic Games? In what year?

4. How many sports will be featured in Olympic competition in Sydney?

5. How many countries are competing in this Olympic games?

6. One of the sports that Australian students play is cricket. What is the name for the three sticks that the batsmen defend?

7. Australia has six states and two territories. Name them. Which state is the biggest in area?
