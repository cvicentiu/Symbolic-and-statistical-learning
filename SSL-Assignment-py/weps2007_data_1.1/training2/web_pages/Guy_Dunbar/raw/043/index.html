'Trotters dazzle crowd

By Mike Manston
Staff Writer
Friday, January 12, 2001

Kevin Cederstrom/Staff Photographer

Clyde "The Glide" Sinclair, of the Harlem Globetrotters, goes up for an early score against the New York Nationals at the Minot Municipal Auditorium. The Globetrotters made a stop in the Magic City as part of the team's 75th anniversary tour.

   When 'Sweet' Lou Dunbar joined the Harlem Globetrotters 27 years ago, he was the youngster on the team with Globetrotters legends like Meadowlark Lemon and Curly Neal.

   Now the 6-foot-10 former University of Houston product is the veteran of the 75th Anniversary edition of the Harlem Globetrotters as they performed their basketball wizardry in front of 2,000-plus fans Thursday at the Minot Municipal Auditorium.

   "When I first started I had an opportunity to play with guys like Meadowlark and Curly and they were the old guys on the team, now I'm the old guy," Dunbar said with a laugh. "But on a more serious note it is the smiles on the faces of the crowd and especially the kids that makes this great."

   Dunbar said that the clowning is a lot of fun and just part of the entertainment package that makes the Harlem Globetrotters one of the best shows on earth.

   "The Harlem Globetrotters is a brand name that people associate with certain things and we want to leave everyone in the crowd with a memorable experience once they've seen one of our shows," Dunbar said.

   Some of the patented Globetrotters clowining includes the purse gag, the water/confetti toss, the ball under the jersey trick and of course the dribbling exhibition, the Globetrotters shuffle and the halfcourt hookshots.

   "When people who have seen our show before come out to see us, they always want to see the purse gag and water tricks and the hard time we give to the referee," said Dunbar. "But at the same time it's all for the kids and with the new players coming in all of the time there is something new that goes on everyday."

   Although Dunbar refused to speculate on how long he would continue to perform with the Globetrotters, he indicated that he will always be a part of the world-famous traveling squad in one way or another.

   "I don't really know how long I'll play, but once a Globetrotter always a Globetrotter," Dunbar commented. "I will continue to work as an ambassador and be a part of the organization as long as I can. It's a great thing to be able to see the crowds leave the game with a lasting impression and a big smile."

   And with Dunbar leading the charge, the Globetrotters are well on their way to another 75 years of basketball entertainment.
