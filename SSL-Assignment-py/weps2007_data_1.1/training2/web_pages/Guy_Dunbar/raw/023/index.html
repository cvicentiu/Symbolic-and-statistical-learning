Eddie looked troubled. Eddie always looks troubled, but this time he looked perplexed as well. It was a rainy night in San Francisco, I had made my usual trip to my favorite drinking emporium and found Eddie on his usual stool.

    "What's up, Eddie? You look puzzled."

    "Ah, it's this Unabomber guy Lewinsky. I think he pulled a fast one on everybody."

    "You mean Kaczynski."

    "Who?"

    "Kaczynski. Ted Kaczynski. Lewinsky's the lady with the Presidential knee pads."

    "Oh. OK, so this guy Kryshevski (Eddie is incorrigible) really pulled a fast one, you know? The whole thing reminds me of Catch-22. You ever read Catch-22?"

    "Of course. It was Heller's triumph."

    "Who?" Eddie isn't exactly a literary genius, but he comes up with some interesting thoughts.

    "Never mind. Tell me about Ted."

    "Well, you know how in Catch-22 this guy Dunbar is absolutely nuts, except every time he tries to get out on a Section Eight they don't let him?"

    "Right. They figure if he's sane enough to want to get out of the Army, he's not crazy enough to be given a Section Eight."

    "You got it. He can have a Section Eight any time he wants. All he has to do is ask. But as soon as he asks, he's not eligible."

    "Sounds like the Army all right."

    "Right." says Eddie. "Anyway, I figured at first this Ted guy was really nuts, 'cause he kept telling his attorneys he didn't want them pleading an insanity defense. Which of course would have been the only logical thing for his attorneys to do."

    "Indeed. If his attorneys try him without the insanity plea, he fries."

    "Like an oyster. Of course, if he's smart enough to plead that he's nuts, then he's not really nuts. And if he ain't really nuts. . ."

    "He's dead meat."

    "Right!" repeated Eddie. "If he's smart enough to say he's nuts, he's sane. And if he claims to be sane, he must really be nuts."

    "But he claimed to be nuts. I mean sane. I'm getting confused."

    "YOU'RE confused. How do ya think the jury musta felt? Hey, this is where Ted pulled a genius move. If he had pled insanity, the jury might nota bought it. And then, like you said, he'd fry. But he couldn't take the stand as a sane man, which he was pretending he wanted to do, 'cause then he would fried for sure. So he cops a plea."

    "It worked."

    "Like a charm. They were so afraid of what Crazy Ted might do, they agreed to give him life. He escaped the chair, and they escaped what could have been a very bizarre trial."

    "But now he's stuck in jail the rest of his life."

    "At least he has life. Dying is a big number. And believe me, with what he's done he wouldn't enjoy it. The cosmos would slam his soul in a little black box for about 40,000 years to teach him a lesson."

    "Where did you come up with that one?" Eddie amazes me at times.

    "I listen to talk radio. Hey, at least the jury can breathe easy. How'd you like to be on the panel that sentences Ted to death and ten years from now, while he's still on Death Row, you get this package in the mail?"

    "You got a point there. It's a strange world, isn't it?"

    "It certainly is. Hey, I gotta get home before my wife fries ME. Thanks for the beer."

    "My pleasure, Eddie." And it was.
