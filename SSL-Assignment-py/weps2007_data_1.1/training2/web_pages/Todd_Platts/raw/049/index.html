Thursday, May 6, 2004

 

Meeting with White House Officials

 

Members met with Chairman James Connaughton of the President’s Council on Environmental Quality and Acting Assistant Secretary Benjamin Grumbles of the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, Office of Water, and Maggie Grant, Special Assistant to the President on Intergovernmental Affairs in the Eisenhower Executive Office Building .  The Commission presented to Chairman Connaughton a letter to President Bush signed by all members seeking issuance of an Executive Order that would reaffirm the federal government’s commitment to the Bay restoration effort and identify the Bay as a “national treasure”.  An outline of the content of such an Order was also suggested.  Following the meeting, the Commission requested the Executive Director to call upon the partnering governors, mayors and administrators of the Federal Agencies to communicate to the President their support for such an Order.

 

Congressional Delegation Meetings

 

Members convened by Delegation with the members of Congress for one-hour briefing sessions.  The meetings were chaired by Congressman Todd Platts (PA), Senator George Allen (VA), and Senator Paul Sarbanes (MD).  The importance of enhanced Federal support and issuance of a Presidential Order were emphasized.    

 

 

Friday, May 7, 2004

 

Chairman’s Welcome

 

Chairman Stoltzfus welcomed the group to the University of Maryland with its’ 25,000 undergraduates and 9,000 graduate students.  It is also the largest on line higher education institution in the world. 

 

Senator Stoltzfus announced that due to an emergency session of the Virginia General Assembly, several members had to leave.  The minutes of the January Meeting were adopted.

 

Review of Congressional Delegations Meetings with the White House

 

Pennsylvania – Senator Waugh - Congressman Todd Platts convened the meeting, which was well attended by staff of  Pennsylvania Congressional members. Pat Stuntz , Maryland Director, provided an overview of the Bay restoration challenges, the financial needs and the Commission’s suggestions for Congressional action. Congressman Platts offered to send a letter of support for the Presidential Executive Order declaring Chesapeake Bay “a national treasure.” Several Delegation members were able to hold one-on-one meetings with members following Congressman Platt’s meeting which further contributed to the success.  Senator Waugh believes it was successful, and he is hopeful that the Delegation will be able to continue their relationship.

 

Virginia – Melanie Davenport – Senator George Allen hosted the Virginia meeting.  The Senator was very supportive of the Commission’s efforts to persuade President Bush to adopt an Executive Order and offered to send a letter of support to the President.   Melanie Davenport and Secretary Murphy provided an overview of the challenges and cost of the restoration, along with recommendations for Congressional action.  The Virginia Delegation concluded by recognizing that such meetings are productive, that every effort should be made to encourage member turnout and that such meetings be continued in the future.

 

 

 

Maryland – Senator Stoltzfus- The Maryland Delegation briefing was convened by Senator Paul Sarbanes.  It was by far the most formal of the sessions, well attended by Senators Paul Sarbanes and Barbara Mikulski, and Congressmen Wayne Gilchrest, Chris Van Hollen, Jr. and Benjamin Cardin, along with their staff.  Staff of nearly all of the remaining Congressional members was also in attendance. Ann Swanson, Executive Director and Will Baker , President of the Chesapeake Bay Foundation provided briefings similar to those provided in the other sessions.  The members demonstrated a keen interest in the Bay and a willingness to help.

 

Following these briefings, Pennsylvania Commission member Representative Russ Fairchild questioned the possible worth of the Bay, commenting that this information could be compelling to the Congress and, importantly, to people living far from its shores. Swanson said that, in 1989 dollars, a University of Maryland study estimated the combined worth of the Bay to Virginia and Maryland at $678 billion.  Adjusted in today’s dollars, and assuming that the condition of the Bay has remained somewhat stable (with some conditions/species decreasing while others increased in respective worth), that worth translates to $1.1 trillion. At Fairchild’s request, the Chairman then asked staff to examine whether a similar estimate would be possible for the watershed.  

 

 

Breakfast Delegation Meetings – Overview

 

Pat Stuntz – On the Maryland Delegation member’s behalf, Stuntz further concluded that the Congressional meetings should continue each year as they help to reinforce important relationships.  The members preferred the Delegation briefing format to the individual meetings with the Congressmen and Senators used the previous year.  It was also suggested that we may get better participation if we get assistance up front in the planning from the Governor’s Federal Liaisons.  There may also be a benefit to plug into the Congressional Bay Caucus (Senate) and the Congressional Bay Task Force.  Swanson had presented to both within the past year.

 

Melanie Davenport – For the Virginia Delegation members, Davenport briefed the members on the Ad Hoc Panel of the Virginia Seafood Council Aquaculture Project.  The three big issues are: the inability to quantify the risks; lack of monitoring capabilities to determine adverse effects; and unknown environmental impacts.  The Bay restoration partners are  working with federal and state agencies and the Seafood Council to determine ways to manage this project while establishing the risk at an acceptable level.  The Corps of Engineers has extended the public comment period to May 17, 2004. 

 

Senator Stoltzfus asked that Secretary Murphy and Deputy Secretary Jensen share their state’s perspective.  Secretary Murphy said that it is very important to continue this project and bring it to a conclusion to determine if triploids in the water will remain sterile.  Since negotiations are ongoing, a project extension was given.  Deputy Secretary Jensen noted that Maryland is in strong support of the extension, and believes that all interested parties subscribe to finding the best available science.

 

Both states are going together on a proposal to study the introduction of a diploid C.ariakensis to repopulate the Bay.  Jensen added that they have chosen to do an Environmental Impact Study following federal guidelines.  However, DNR doesn’t believe that there is any federal jurisdiction. 

 

Tributary Strategies Updates

 

Mark Bundy - Reported that Maryland has been actively involved with its ten tributary teams and members of the general public in the development of their strategies.  They held over 25 public meetings throughout this year-and-a-half period, working with stakeholders to develop recommendations on the best management practices needed to reduce nutrient and sediment loads.  In order to achieve MD’s commitment to restore water quality sufficient to meet the Chesapeake 2000 Agreement goals, the state will have to do far more than what was originally thought.  Bundy reported on the state’s intensive effort to get to this point in the process and reiterated Maryland ’s commitment to the effort.

 

Bundy said they recognize that in order to really be effective in getting the job done they are going to be extremely dependent on the federal government’s participation in this process, as well as those in the private sector and local governments. And lastly, as they put these strategies together they feel somewhat confined in terms of the best management practices that are recognized and credited by the Bay Program model.

 

Russ Baxter - Reported that on April 5th of this year, Virginia released for public comment its tributary strategies for all Virginia river basins, coastal bays and creeks of the Eastern shore .  They held a series of public information meetings throughout the watershed and had a 30-day public comment period which ended on Wednesday, May 5th.

 

Virginia ’s obligation in terms of total basin allocation is about 51.8 million pounds of nitrogen and about 6 million pounds of phosphorus.  Russ said that all of their strategies summed together slightly exceed those overall targets.    In terms of the point sources, they are revising water quality standards, but have also embarked on a regulatory process to establish nutrient limits and discharge permits.

 

Virginia projects a total cost for full implementation of the drafts as they are currently proposed at about $3.2 million.  Virginia will achieve its reductions principally through point sources and agriculture, the two biggest nutrient and sediment contributors in the basin. 

 

Senator Stoltzfus asked if they had ”bottom lined” a cost?  Russ said that Virginia had determined a generalized cost, based upon the Bay Program’s Use Attainability Analysis (UAA) modifying the numbers somewhat based on their own information.  The largest reason for the difference in cost between Virginia and Maryland is septic issues.  In Virginia , although septic issues are addressed, they do not go to the extent that Maryland does.  Maryland also has stormwater issues that Virginia doesn’t have.

 

 

Cathy Myers -  Reported that Pennsylvania has many of the same issues and has taken a similar approach, wanting to make their tributary strategies a very “bottom-up” process.  The 13 tributary watersheds have each been given a nonpoint source and a point source goal.  She further informed the members that this is a priority for the Rendell administration, noting that  his 2004 budget included $80 million for streams and rivers in the Growing Greener II Initiative.  Specific earmarking of those dollars is for cost sharing for nitrogen reduction, point sources and enhancement of their CREP program.  Pennsylvania will now concentrate on its nonpoint source effort in order to achieve the goals that are set forth. 

 

Legislative Reports

 

Pennsylvania – Senator Mike Waugh – Gave the report of the Pennsylvania legislature.  Voters overwhelmingly approved The Water and Water Treatment Bond Act, a $250 million grant/loan program that must now be followed through legislatively to be put in place.  The second item is the Infrastructure and Facilities Improvement Program which deals with wastewater infrastructure projects, grants and brownfields remediation grants.  He also noted the Governor’s Growing Greener II Program, which includes significant increases in funding to include forest and open space, farmland protection, cleanup of hazardous sites, and abandoned mines.  Senator Waugh also noted his bill, the Alternative Fuels Incentive Program, and lastly a bill to create a Chesapeake Bay License Plate.  This would establish a $20.00 designated fee toward stream restoration. 

 

 

Virginia – Melanie Davenport - Reported on behalf of the Virginia Delegation.  Water Column Leasing for Aquaculture Purposes, which the Commission sponsored, authorizes the VMRC to ease certain bottomlands for aquaculture activity, thereby encouraging investments in that facility.  A bill was passed that entitled environmental permitting which will generate approximately $6 million for the funding of air, water and waste permitting programs at DEQ.  A bill also passed that establishes a Secretary of Agriculture and Forestry. It is hoped that by adding that cabinet level focus to Agriculture and Forests, it might give the Commission another opportunity to work with those folks in the context implementing tributary strategies.  And lastly, a significant piece of legislation consolidates all of the stormwater management programs into one agency.  Since these were started they ended up with at least three agencies with a local government component.  There have been a lot of complaints from both the regulators and the applicants that this was very confusing to understand what the requirements were and what agency was responsible. 

 

Maryland – Senator Stoltzfus – Gave a brief report on the Chesapeake Bay Restoration Fund, which assesses every user of a wastewater treatment plants $2.50 a month, or $30.00 a year.  The bill, introduced by the Governor, was amended to include users of septic systems and holding tanks.  The fund is expected to generate about $62 million per year from user fees; bond proceeds are estimated at $738 million from FY07-FY10 and will be used to upgrade the 66 largest sewage treatment plants in the state with enhanced nutrient removal technology.  Septic/holding tank proceeds will be split 60:40 between septic upgrades and the establishment of agricultural cover crops.

 

 

 

Chesapeake Bay Commission FY 2004-2005 Budget

 

Senator Stoltzfus opened the discussion by telling the members that he and Ann Swanson thoroughly examined the details of the CBC budget.  The Executive Committee, via conference call, further reviewed the budget.  Ann explained to the Commission that we had performed a very thorough analysis of the budget, projecting three years out while also cutting nearly $50k from FY2005 expenses.  The budget was voted on and approved by the members.

 

Swanson then reviewed the Executive Committee’s cost projections to Fiscal Year 2007.  She explained to the members that, based on these projections, even with the money saving measures taken, an increase in annual appropriation of $30,000 from each state was necessary in order to remain solvent. She then reminded the members an increase to $190,000 would require full legislative and administration support.

 

Senator Stoltzfus reiterated that we had been operating on a surplus of money that was now nearly expended and that it is now necessary to request from each state an increased appropriation of $30,000 a year.  He then reminded members that each state has to contribute equal amounts, by law; each state must act in unison in FY2006   The motion was made and seconded to request such increases. 

 

Cathy Myers noted that she did not yet have a clear read from the Governor’s office and the budget office since increases for any of the seven interstate commissions had not been planned.   Senator Stoltzfus asked if this should be presented as a request and was told yes.  Senator Stoltzfus asked for a vote.  The vote passed.

 

 

Air Quality Presentations              

 

Reducing Airborne Nitrogen Deposition

           

Maggie Kerchner, from the Chesapeake Bay Program’s NOAA Air Resources Laboratory, presented an overview of the contribution of air emissions to the Bay’s water quality problems.  Her focus was on nitrogen deposition, which includes both nitrogen oxides emissions from power plants and motor vehicles, as well as ammonia emissions from agriculture and urban sources.  Nitrogen oxides are regulated under the Clean Air Act, and the water quality impacts of these emissions are only beginning to be understood.  Both the 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments and the Chesapeake Bay Agreements of 1992 and 2000 commit the EPA and the states to monitor the effects of airborne pollutants on water quality and to develop reduction strategies. 

 

 

 

 

The contribution of air deposition is significant:  of the total 305 million pounds of nitrogen delivered to the Bay from all sources, about 98 million pounds, or 32 percent is attributable to atmospheric deposition.  The Bay Program estimates that Clean Air Act regulations will result in an 11 million pound nitrogen reduction by 2010, increasing to 18 million pounds by 2020.  Improved modeling tools are needed in order to assess the effects of additional air pollutant controls on the nitrogen load to the Bay.

 

Ms. Kerchner showed a time series map of ammonium wet deposition over the period 1985-2001.  High concentrations of ammonia in the North Carolina area were shown to coincide with increased swine production.  Several counties in the Bay watershed that have high animal population concentrations were also shown to have elevated ammonia emissions.  One of the challenges before us is to improve scientific understanding by focusing on these “hot spots” with enhanced monitoring and modeling. 

 

In conclusion, Ms. Kerchner stated that policy options for reducing nitrogen pollution due to air emissions will only be successful if they involve good public communication and stakeholder engagement.  The size of the airshed makes it particularly difficult to garner support for local or statewide initiatives, and regional solutions are likely to be most effective.

 

 

North Carolina ’s “Clean Smokestack Act”

 

Brock Nicholson, Deputy Director of the North Carolina Department of Environment and Natural Resources, summarized the successful effort to engage a broad base of support for more stringent air pollution controls on coal-fired electric plants in the state. 

 

The Clean Smokestack Act caps nitrogen oxide and sulfur dioxide emissions, under a phased compliance schedule starting in 2007, to achieve about 75 percent reductions by 2013.  In addition, the legislation calls for recommendations to be made to the legislature to further control mercury and carbon dioxide emissions by a future date certain.  A unique cost recovery feature enabled the utilities to finance the cost of the additional pollution controls without a significant increase to ratepayers.  This was the culmination of a two-year effort to bring together environmental groups, the legislature, the governor’s office and the utilities commission and companies to forge consensus and support.

 

In an effort to encourage emission reductions in other states, whose air pollution travels into the state of North Carolina , the North Carolina attorney general filed a Section 126 petition with the EPA in 2004.  The result of this action is not yet known.
