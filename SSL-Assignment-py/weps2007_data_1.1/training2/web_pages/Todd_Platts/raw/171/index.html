Republicans Appoint 6 New Members to House Committee

The House Education and the Workforce Committee had seven Republican slots open at the beginning of the 107th Congress; slots that were vacated by members who either retired, ran for other office, or who left to serve in other committees. Six of those positions have been filled by: Reps. Todd Platts of Pennsylvania; Patrick Tiberi of Ohio; Ric Keller of Florida; Tom Osborne of Nebraska, John Culberson of Texas (all freshmen members), and Judy Biggert of Illinois (a second-term member). One slot is still open, but it is not known if or when it will be filled.

The Democrats have two positions to fill, but have not announced those appointments as of yet.

If you live in one of the new Committee members' states, it's a good idea to get to know them as soon as possible, before the real work of this Congress begins. Contact them either in person, by phone, or by e-mail through CEC's Legislative Action Center at http://congress.nw.dc.us/cek/. Introduce yourself to your member of Congress, and offer yourself as a resource on special education or gifted issues.

Clinton, Edwards Added to Senate HELP Committee

After the November elections, the Senate was split evenly between Democrats and Republicans, with the Vice-President issuing a deciding vote should a tie occur. The Senate committee and subcommittee structures have been adjusted to reflect this new equality, and two new Democratic positions opened up on the Senate Health, Education, Labor, and Pensions (HELP) Committee. Freshmen Senators Hillary Clinton (D-NY), and John Edwards (D-NC) have been appointed to the Committee.

President Clinton Releases His "FY 2002 Economic Outlook"

Although President-elect Bush is required by law to issue a proposed federal budget by the first Monday in February, outgoing President Clinton has issued a document that provides a current services budget projection - essentially what is the cost of maintaining current programs and policies after factoring in an inflation adjustment for discretionary programs and mandatory programs increases. According to information from the Committee for Education Funding (CEF), of which CEC is a member, for FY 2002, the current services surplus is projected to be $277 billion, with an on-budget surplus (not counting Social Security and Medicare) of $69 billion. The 10-year total (FY 2002-2011) unified budget surplus is projected to be $4.996 trillion, with an on-budget non-Social Security, non-Medicare surplus of $1.914 trillion.

According to CEF, one interesting situation is that starting in 2009, the amount of the surplus that is available to repay debt held by the public will exceed the amount of debt available to be paid, which will cause "excess balances" to build up - essentially there will be a "surplus surplus." In reality this is unlikely to occur, because most of the surplus will be used up for tax cuts and spending increases.

The document also provides information on budget baseline alternatives. If the baseline is adjusted to assume that expiring tax provisions will continue, and adjusted for other anomalies, then the 10-year on-budget surplus would decline from $1.914 trillion to $1.643 trillion. If discretionary programs grow at the same rate as the economy, as opposed to just an inflation adjustment, the surplus would be reduced by another $979 billion. If population increases were factored into discretionary programs, that would add another $362 billion in spending over ten years.

In reviewing budget actions during President Clinton's term, the document notes that for Function 500 (under which education programs fall), total discretionary spending has increased from $38.192 billion in FY 93 to $61.121 billion in FY 2001 (+60%), while tax expenditures for education and related programs rose from $21.855 billion to $59.450 billion (+172%).

Following is the portion of the Clinton document that discusses special education:

Special Education: Under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), the Education Department works with States to ensure that more than six million children with disabilities receive a "free appropriate public education." During this Administration, Federal support for special education programs authorized under the IDEA increased by more than 150 percent, from nearly $3.0 billion in 1993 to $7.4 billion in 2001, and the Federal share of special education costs increased from eight percent to 15 percent.

In 1997, the IDEA reauthorization focused attention not only on guaranteeing access to public schools, but also on ensuring that students with disabilities receive a high-quality education based on the same challenging standards as their nondisabled peers. As of July 1, 1998, all States were required to have performance goals and strategies in place for students with disabilities aged three to 21, and to report their progress toward meeting those goals on a biennial basis. By July 1, 2000, all States were required to include special education children in State and district-wide regular assessments or provide alternate assessments to measure educational performance. Based on the latest data available, during this Administration:

* The number and percentage of students with disabilities who leave high school with a regular diploma has increased from 114,000, or 62 percent, of all students with disabilities in 1994, the earliest year comparable data are available, to 149,000, or 57 percent, in 1999.

* The percentage of students with disabilities who dropped out of school decreased from 35 percent in 1994 to 29 percent in 1999.

The entire document, "FY 2002 Economic Outlook, Highlights From FY 1994 to FY 2001, FY 2002 Baseline Projections," is available at: http://w3.access.gpo.gov/usbudget/fy2002/pdf/economic.pdf

Early Head Start Shows Significant Results for Low Income Children and Parents

HHS Secretary Donna E. Shalala today released a preliminary evaluation of Early Head Start showing that children in the new child development program performed significantly better in cognitive, language and social-emotional development compared to children not participating in the program. Parents in Early Head Start also showed more positive parenting behavior, reported less physical punishment of their children and provided more help for their children to learn at home.

Early Head Start provides high quality child and family development services to pregnant women and infants and toddlers, from birth to age

3, a time considered one of the most important in a child's cognitive and social development. It was started in 1995 as a new component of the Head Start program that serves children age 3 to 4.

"Early Head Start is about giving our youngest and most disadvantaged children a chance to grow up healthy, to learn and to prepare for school," said Secretary Shalala. "Everyone who helped create the program, the providers who teach the children, and the parents who are taking part can be very proud of what we've accomplished in a very short time."

Major findings of the preliminary evaluation of 2-year-old children in the program include higher scores in standardized tests of infant and toddler development and reports of larger vocabularies and the ability to speak in more complex sentences. One-third of Early Head Start children were found to be at risk of slower developmental learning compared to 40 percent of the non-Early Head Start children. This indicates that Early Head Start children may also be less at risk of needing special learning intervention services in later years.

The results pertaining to parents present further evidence of Early Head Start's ability to promote learning in the home, to better prepare children for school, and to reduce family stress. The preliminary study reports that Early Head Start parents were more supportive of promoting learning, language and literacy at home by reading more to their children and engaging in more structured play activity than parents not in the program. Early Head Start parents also set more regular bedtimes and read to children before sleep. They spanked their children less and were more likely to resolve problems by using distraction, explanation or mild responses rather than physical punishment. Early Head Start parents also reported lower levels of family conflict and stress related to parenting. These findings indicate that the program is equipping parents with strategies to cope with conflict and to help break a cycle of parental stress and potential harm to children.

"This study is enormously important for the future of Early Head Start and all early childhood programs by showing that a large national program that is well-implemented, maintains quality, and focuses on specific child development goals for children and parents can succeed," said Olivia A. Golden, HHS assistant secretary for children and families. "There will be more to learn, but we now know that the Early Head Start blueprint of an early, intensive program can yield significant results and brighter outcomes for children."

Early Head Start provides its full range of services in home-based, center-based and mixed settings. The evaluation found that the child development services provided directly by Early Head Start programs were generally of high quality. Though there were differences in results for children and parents among the three program formats, overall findings regarding improvements in children's cognitive development and parental behavior were broad and substantial. Findings were strongest in programs that fully implemented the Head Start performance standards. The preliminary report covered children up to the age of 2. The study will continue to assess their progress for another year.

Early Head Start was launched in 1995 based on recommendations from the bipartisan Advisory Committee on Head Start appointed by Secretary Shalala. There are currently 635 Early Head Start programs serving 45,000 low-income children and their families. Mathematica Policy Research, Inc. and Columbia University's Center for Children and Families, in collaboration with the Early Head Start Research Consortium, prepared the national evaluation, which was begun in 1995. The evaluation studied approximately 3,000 children and families in 17 sites across the country during the first two years of the children's lives.

The final report following children through age three will be out next year. A copy of the preliminary study is available on the ACF web site at http://www.acf.dhhs.gov/news.

National Council on Disability to Conduct Meetings in Myrtle Beach

The National Council on Disability (NCD) will conduct its next quarterly meeting February 5-6, 2001, from 8:30 a.m. to 6:00 p.m., at the Landmark Resort Hotel, 1501 South Ocean Boulevard, Myrtle Beach, South Carolina.

During the meeting, NCD will also conduct a community briefing for people with disabilities on February 6, 2001, from 3:00 p.m. to 6:00 p.m. The briefing will focus on NCD's latest findings and recommendations on federal disability civil rights laws.

NCD is an independent federal agency making recommendations to the President and Congress on disability policy. It is currently coordinating a multi-year study on the implementation and enforcement of several disability civil rights laws. NCD's recently issued reports on the Air Carrier Access Act, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, and the Americans with Disabilities Act are available on its Web site (www.ncd.gov).

For more information, contact Mark S. Quigley at 202-272-2004.

U.S. DOE Office of Elementary and Secondary Education Selected FY 2001 Initiatives and Funding Opportunities January 2001

Now that the Department of Education's FY2001 budget has been passed, with the largest ever increase in education funding and new funding for school renovation, teacher quality, arts in education and other key priorities, following are updates on Department of Education initiatives and funding opportunities in FY2001.

In addition to regular initiatives and funding opportunities documents, the Office of Elementary and Secondary Education has prepared a special document highlighting new programs in that office.

Both documents provide basic information on Department of Education initiatives and funding opportunities, as well as application timelines and the name and contact information for a person in the Department who will be available to answer questions and provide information for the program.

We've included only those initiatives that we feel may be of interest to the special education field.

NEW INITIATIVES

Urgent School Renovation Grant Program - $1.1 billion Provides grants to local educational agencies to help them make urgently needed repairs and to pay for special education and technology-related technology expenses. Funds are distributed to States based on Title I LEA grant shares. States distribute 75% of their awards on a competitive basis to local educational agencies to make urgently needed school repairs, such as repair of roofs, plumbing and heating systems or meeting fire and safety codes. The remaining 25% of a State's award is distributed competitively to local educational agencies to use for technology activities related to school renovation or for activities authorized under Part B of the IDEA. School districts apply to the State to receive funding for either purpose. For additional information, contact Sylvia Wright at (202) 260-3778 or e-mail sylvia_wright@ed.gov.

Charter School Facilities Financing Demonstration Program - $25 million Will support a competitive demonstration program that will award one-time grants to three or more applicants. These projects will demonstrate innovative credit enhancement techniques that help charter schools meet the cost of acquiring, constructing and renovating facilities by improving access to private capital. Nonprofit agencies, public entities and consortia of the two are eligible to apply for awards to guarantee or insure debt or leases so that charter schools may obtain or improve school facilities. Award recipients will also use these funds to help charter schools identify and obtain loans and to provide assistance in issuing debt, such as recruiting bond counsel and underwriters. If possible, the program will fund at least one project from each of the following three eligible entities: a public entity, such as a State or local governmental entity; a private entity; or a consortium of public and private entities. For additional information, contact John Fiegel at (202) 260-2671 or Sylvia Wright at (202) 260-3778.

Transition to Teaching - $31 million Helps local educational agencies address their teacher shortages. The program provides grants to encourage and assist talented mid-career professionals working in other fields and recent college graduates with outstanding academic records and baccalaureate degrees in fields other than education to make successful career changes and become licensed or certified teachers. Competitive grants will be awarded to local educational agencies, state educational agencies, educational service agencies, and nonprofit agencies and organizations for recruitment, preparation, placement and follow-up support activities conducted for this purpose. Some 72 awards will be made, including approximately 13 projects that involve LEAs located in more than one state, 15 projects that involve LEAs scattered across a particular state, and 44 projects that involve a single LEA or a group of LEAs in a state in close proximity to one another. Application packages should be available in mid-January with an application deadline of March 20, 2001. For more information, contact Frances Yvonne Hicks at (202) 260-0964, or e-mail Frances Yvonne_Hicks@ed.gov.

Early Childhood Educator Professional Development Program - $10 million Will support high-quality professional development to improve the knowledge and skills of early childhood educators who work in early childhood programs located in urban or rural high-poverty communities and serve primarily children from low-income families. This training will be designed to improve early childhood pedagogy and further children's language and literacy skills to prevent them from encountering reading difficulties when they enter school. These funds will be used for competitive grants to consortia of one or more institutions of higher education, State agencies for higher education, local educational agencies, educational service agencies, State educational agencies, and other public and private agencies, organizations, and institutions such as child care consortiums, and Head Start programs. The Department will award approximately twenty grants, and the estimated range of the awards (per year) is $300,000 - $700,000. The estimated average size of awards (per year) is $500,000. Grants are available for up to four years (dependent upon the availability of funding after 2001). Applications should be available January 10th with a closing date of March12, 2001. For more information regarding this program, contact Doris Sligh at 202-260-0999 or Patricia McKee at (202) 260-0991, or visit the Web site at: http://www.ed.gov/news.html.

Project SERV - $10 million These funds will be used by the Education Department for Project School Emergency Response to Violence [Project Serv]. Local Education Agencies [LEAs] which have had their learning environment disrupted due to a violent or traumatic crisis, such as a school shooting, will be able to receive funds to assist in restoring the environment to normalcy. The Department will provide these funds on a case-by-case basis. Contact Bill Modzleski at 202-260-1856 or Bill_Modzeleski@ed.gov for further information.

CONTINUING PROGRAMS

Class-Size Reduction - $1.63 billion (a $323 million increase) Helps school districts hire 100,000 teachers over 7 years to reduce class sizes in grades K-3. Districts may also spend a percentage of funds on professional development activities for new and current teachers. In 1999 and 2000, 29,000 new teachers were hired. This year, $1.623 billion will allow an additional 8,000 new teachers to be hired in 50 states and territories. State funding is distributed to school districts based 80 percent on poverty and 20 percent on school enrollment. School districts apply to states for funds as part of their ESEA Title VI applications. More detailed information is available on the program Web site at http://www.ed.gov/offices/OESE/ClassSize/; additional information about class-size reduction programs around the country are available from WestEd at http://www.WestEd.org/policy, or you may contact Christine Miller at 202-260-8766, or e-mail class_size@ed.gov.

21st Century Community Learning Centers - $845.6 million (a $392 million increase) Provides three-year grants to school-community partnerships to keep inner-city and rural public schools open after school, weekends and summers as safe havens for enhanced learning. In 2000, a new competition awarded $186 million to 310 new grantees to fund 1,500 schools and community centers. The 21st Century Community Learning Centers program supports 903 grants in local school districts around the country serving more than 3,600 centers spanning every state, the District of Columbia, Puerto Rico, the Virgin Islands, and the Federated States of Micronesia. In FY2001, and new competitions for $205 million will support approximately 400 new awards, and 385 highly qualified applicants who could not be funded in FY2000 will receive grants totaling $212 million. Including continuation grants for centers funded in the 1999 and 2000 competitions, the program would support over 6,600 centers and provide extended learning opportunities for 1.3 million children. Application packets are currently available on the 21st Century Community Learning Centers website (http://www.ed.gov/21stcclc/), and hard copies will be available by the end of January. The application deadline is March 30. Announcement of awards is expected by May 31. For more information, contact Amanda Clyburn at (202) 260-3804, e-mail 21stCCLC@ed.gov, or visit the Web site at http://www.ed.gov/21stcclc/.

Safe Schools/Healthy Students Initiative - $117 million (a $35 million increase) A collaboration among the U.S. Departments of Education, Justice, Health and Human Services, and Labor to support urban, rural, suburban, and tribal school district efforts to link prevention activities and community-based services, as well as to provide community-wide approaches to violence prevention and healthy child development. The Safe Schools/Healthy Students Initiative provides three-year grants to help communities design and implement comprehensive educational, mental health, social service, law enforcement and juvenile justice services for youth. In FY2000, $41 million in new grants was awarded to 23 school districts, and 54 districts received continuation grants. This year, a new competition is expected to award 40 new grants. In addition, continuation funding will support continuation grants for 1999 and 2000 grantees. Applications will be available in early March with a closing date in early May. Announcement of FY2001 awards is expected in mid-September. For more information, contact Bryan Williams at (202) 260-2391 or Karen Dorsey at (202) 708-4674, e-mail safeschl@ed.gov, or visit the Web site at http://www.ed.gov/offices/OESE/SDFS.

Special Education State Improvement Grant Program— - 35.2 million in FY2000, $49.2 million in FY2001 ($14 million increase) Provides competitive grants to help State Educational Agencies reform and improve their systems for providing educational, early intervention and transitional services to improve results for children with disabilities. At least 75 percent of the funds must be reserved for professional development. The remaining funds are used to carry out State strategies for improving educational results, including efforts to hold school districts and schools accountable for the educational progress of children with disabilities, providing high-quality technical assistance to school districts and schools, and changing state policies and procedures to address systemic barriers to improving results for students with disabilities. FY2000 competition closes February 13, 2001, with awards to be made by the end of June. FY 2001 competition details will be available later in the summer, with awards planned for spring, 2002. For additional information contact Larry Wexler at (202) 205-5390.

More information on these initiatives is available on the U.S. Department of Education Web site at http://www.ed.gov/inits/FY2000/index.html. Or Call 1-800-USA-LEARN
