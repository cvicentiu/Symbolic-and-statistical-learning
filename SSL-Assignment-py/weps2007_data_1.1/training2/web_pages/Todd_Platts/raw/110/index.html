1. House Republican ESEA Bill Introduced This Week

H.R. 1, the "No Child Left Behind" education bill, was introduced by House Republicans on March 22. The legislation is a comprehensive reauthorization of the federal Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965.

The bill is sponsored by Chairman of the House Education and the Workforce Committee John Boehner (R-Ohio), and has 70 cosponsors. It is a Republican bill that is the embodiment of President Bush's education reform proposal.

According to Boehner, the bill reflects President Bush's efforts to close the achievement gap between disadvantaged students and their peers and to work with states to improve America's schools.

"Despite a decade of uninterrupted prosperity, nearly 70 percent of inner city and rural fourth-graders cannot read at a basic level, and low-income students lag behind their counterparts by an average of 20 percentile points on national assessment test," Boehner said at the press conference introducing the bill.

Boehner stressed that money is not the answer for change in schools; accountability for results is the vehicle for change in schools.

Summary of the No Child Left Behind Act of 2001:

Testing and Accountability

  • Requires annual testing in math and reading in grades 3-8.

  • Provides federal funding to assist states in developing their tests.

  • Allows states the option of using the National Assessment of Educational Progress (NAEP) or another test as a means of confirming their state assessments.

Flexibility

  • Provides flexibility for states and local school districts to confirm their state assessments.

  • Gives states the option to implement reforms that work according to what they need.

Reading and Literacy

  • Encourages states to use scientifically based research to make sure students read by the third grade.

Closing the Achievement Gap

  • Rewards states and schools for making progress in closing the achievement gap between disadvantaged students and their peers.

  • Provides assistance to Title I schools identified as failing after one year.

  • Gives parents the option of using their share of federal Title I money to transfer to a higher performing public or private school.

Safe Schools

  • Requires states to allow for the transfer of students enrolled in dangerous public schools to another public school or a private school.

  • Combines the Safe and Drug-Free Schools program and the 21st-Century Community Learning Centers Act, and reauthorizes the Gun-Free Schools Act.

  • Allows local education agencies to allocate funds for drug and violence prevention and before and after school activities.

Teacher Quality and Class Size Reduction

  • Provides grants to states and schools to assist in efforts to increase student achievement through improving teacher quality.

  • Consolidates the Eisenhower Professional Development program and the Class Size Reduction program.

  • Prohibits mandatory nation teacher tests or certification.

Limited-English Proficient Students

  • Changes current law to focus programs on teaching English to limited English proficient children and expediting their transition to other classes.

Other Provisions

  • Eliminates Goals 2000.

  • Repeals 18 programs.

3. House Budget Committee Passes FY 2002 Budget Resolution

On March 21, the House Budget committee passed the Fiscal Year (FY) 2002 budget resolution on a party line vote, 23-19. The 2002 budget resolution allocates $661 billion in discretionary budget authority; appropriates $44.5 billion for the President's education proposal.

Summary of the budget resolution:

  • Assumes $900 million for the President's Reading First initiative, which increases the current program funding level of $286 million by $614 million.

  • Creates a $1.25 billion reserve fund for IDEA Part B grants to states.

  • Eliminates the class-size reduction program by consolidating it with teacher-quality programs.

  • Assumes $2.6 billion for states to improve teacher quality through professional development, recruitment and retention activities.

  • Eliminates the new $1.2 billion urgent school repair program.

  • Does not invest in Title I and low-performing schools.

  • Includes the President's voucher proposal to allow Title I money to be used by parents of children in failing schools.

  • Provides a $100 increase in the Pell grant maximum award.

  • Freezes funding for Head Start.

Representatives Mike Honda (D-Calif.), David Price (D-N.C.), Ben Clement (D-Tenn.), Darlene Hooley (D-Oreg., Rush Holt (D-N.J.), and Carolyn McCarthy (D-N.Y.) offered an amendment that would increase education funding to $79.1 billion over the next 10 years. The amendment was defeated on a party line vote, 23-17.

A bill to provide a $118.1 billion increase for special education funding over ten years was defeated as well; it was sponsored by Representatives Hooley, Ken Bentsen (D-Tex.), Clement, Holt, McCarthy, Honda, and Dennis Moore (D-Kans.).

According to the Committee for Education Funding (CEF), the budget resolution could be debated on the House floor as early as March 27. CEF has also reported that President Bush will release his FY 2002 budget on April 9.

4. House Democrats Unveil New Higher Education Initiative

Earlier today, House Democrats introduced a new higher education initiative that is designed to make college more affordable and to strengthen minority-serving institutions. The plan was introduced by Rep. George Miller (D-CA), House Democratic Leader Richard Gephardt (D-Mo.), and more than 90 other House Democrats. The initiative, dubbed "The 21st Century Higher Education Initiative," would provide new resources to low-income students and minority-serving colleges in many ways. The bill would:

  • Restore the purchasing power of Pell Grants by increasing the maximum Pell Grant from $3,750 to $7,000 over three years.

  • Double funding for minority-serving institutions over three years.

  • Expand opportunities for community college students to transfer to 4-year colleges and universities, providing $40 million to support partnerships of minority-serving 2-year colleges and 4-year colleges and universities.

  • Increase the Supplemental Equal Opportunity Grants by over $300 million over 3 years, to provide grant assistance to low-income students whose need is not fully met by Pell Grants.

  • Increase Federal Work-Study by $300 million over 3 years.

  • Implement sustainable dropout prevention strategies at high schools, based on similar legislation introduced by Senator Jeff Bingaman (D-N.M.), at a cost of $250 million.

  • Double funding for the TRIO and GEAR UP programs over 3 years to $1.5 billion and $690 million, respectively, because these programs successfully encourage academic success and college attendance by disadvantaged kids.

  • Encourage universal access to advanced placement classes.

  • Strengthen college academic enrichment programs.

  • Create new opportunities for minority-college students to earn advanced degrees, by providing $40 million for dual degrees initiative to increase opportunities for students to earn advanced degrees, including M.A.s and Ph.D.s, in fields in which they are underrepresented.

  • Preserve most endangered historic properties owned by historically black colleges and universities.

  • Recruit minority teachers for America's public schools.

  • Provide new incentives to wipe out the "digital divide."

5. Bush Proclaims Education and Sharing Day on April 4

President Bush today proclaimed April 4 as Education and Sharing Day USA 2001. "By teaching children the highest standards of ethical behavior, Americans prepare our next generation of leaders to pursue meaningful lives as members of a decent and caring society," the president explained. In his proclamation, Bush added:
"As teachers, parents, and citizens, we have a responsibility to pass on more than just academic knowledge to our children. We also need to provide them with the moral strength to see them through turbulent and challenging times. An education that nurtures goodness and kindness gives direction and dignity to the lives of our young people and strengthens our communities. Humanity has long recognized such core and never-changing ethical values as vital to the well-being of a society and its citizenry."

6. Bush Taps Neuman to Head Elementary and Secondary Office

The White House has announced that President George W. Bush plans to nominate Susan Neuman to be Assistant Secretary of Education for Elementary and Secondary Education. Neuman is currently a professor and Director of the Center for the Improvement of Early Reading Achievement at the University of Michigan. She received her undergraduate degree from American University, a Master's degree from California State University, and a Doctorate from the University of the Pacific.

7. House ED Committee Democrats Make Subcommittee Assignments

After weeks of wrangling with House Education and the Workforce Committee Chairman John Boehner (R-Ohio) over subcommittee jurisdictions, Committee Democrats have now made their subcommittee assignments. Boehner and Committee Ranking Member George Miller (D-Calif.) reached agreement last week to resolve the dispute over which subcommittee would have jurisdiction over Historically Black Colleges and Universities (HBCUs), Hispanic-Serving Institutions (HSIs), and Tribally Controlled Colleges. Democrats were boycotting subcommittee activity because Boehner originally placed jurisdiction over these institutions in the Select Education Subcommittee instead of the 21st Century Competitiveness Subcommittee, which had jurisdiction over other higher education institutions. These minority-serving colleges and universities now are under the jurisdiction of the 21st Century Competitiveness Subcommittee.

8. New Bill Introduced to Increase Voting Accessibility

Representative Jim Langevin (R-R.I.) introduced a bill this week to increase voting accessibility for elderly and disabled Americans. The Voting Opportunity through Technology and Education (VOTE) Act requires the FEC to develop voluntary standards to promote the accessibility and effective use of voting systems and polling places. The bill would also provide $800 million in grants to states to improve the accessibility of their voting technology and to educate poll workers.
