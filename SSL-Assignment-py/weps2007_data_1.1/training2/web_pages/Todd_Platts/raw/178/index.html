House Committee Approves Legislation for Vouchers in DC

On July 10, the House Government Reform Committee approved H.R. 2556, the DC Parental Choice Incentive Act of 2003, which would create a private school voucher program for the District of Columbia. The vote, 22 to 21, split almost completely along party lines, with only two Republicans opposing the measure, Reps. John McHugh (NY) and Todd Platts (PA). Rep. Major Owens (D-NY) was absent.

The bill would authorize the appropriation of $15 million annually for five years to fund vouchers of up to $7,500 for fewer than 2,000 low-income children to attend private schools. DC public schools enroll almost 80,000 students, meaning most will be left behind by this measure, which does nothing to improve public schools.

Rep. Eleanor Holmes Norton (D-DC) offered a substitute amendment that would have channeled the authorized funds to public schools instead, directing it to charter schools and to "transformation schools," those that have been identified for improvement, and would have provided additional resources for wraparound services for both students and their families. That amendment was defeated along strict party lines.

Rep. William Clay, Jr. (D-MO) also offered an amendment, which would require private schools receiving voucher money to abide by some of the same federal accountability rules required of public schools. That, too, was defeated along party lines. Speaking at a charter school in the district earlier this month, however, President George W. Bush said, "Of course, if a school receives a scholarship, then the school needs to be held accountable, as well. The same accountability system applies to the recipient school as it does to the public schools in Washington [DC]. We want there to be accountability throughout the system.... After all, it's taxpayers' money. We want to know. We want to know whether ... in a public school or a private school, whether or not the children are learning."

It is unclear yet whether House leadership will attempt to move the bill to the floor on its own, or attach it to the appropriations bill for the District of Columbia. That measure was approved by the District of Columbia subcommittee of the House Appropriations Committee on July 9, and includes $10 million for a DC voucher program. In either case, National PTA will continue to oppose this voucher measure, as National PTA opposes all voucher proposals. Vouchers divert funds from public schools while doing nothing to improve them or to expand parents' true educational options. Vouchers also undermine accountability and threaten civil rights by permitting discrimination in publicly funded programs.

Education Funding Not a Congressional Priority

Providing the lowest federal increase for education in eight years, the U.S. House of Representatives adopted the Labor, Health, Human Services (HHS), and Education Appropriation bill for fiscal year 2004. National PTA opposed the measure, H.R, 2660, which funds education at $2.3 billion above FY 03 levels, an increase of 4.3 percent. The bill also freezes 67 programs, resulting in cuts to current services once inflation and enrollment growth are calculated. Voting on July 10th, the measure passed by a seven-vote margin of 215 to 208. Twelve members did not vote.

Education numbers in the Senate's fiscal year 05 Labor, HHS, Education appropriations bill are lower than the House level. National PTA opposes the Senate bill as currently proposed and will urge senators to defeat the measure that may go to the floor for a vote as early as next week. The Senate version would increase education funding by only $1.5 billion, or a mere 2.8 percent increase above the current level. While the bill restores funding for the 21st Century After School Centers program and increased funds for Title I and the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, the bill cuts funding for programs in the Elementary and Secondary Education Act, also known as the No Child Left Behind Act, by $486 million. The bill also freezes many vital programs such as the Pell grant maximum award and all campus-based programs.

Amendments to increase funding for various education programs will likely be offered on the Senate floor, but at press time no language has been provided.

The FY 03 investment for Department of Education Programs is about $60 billion, or 2.8 cents of every federal budget dollar. National PTA is calling on Congress to double its education investment to five cents of every dollar. PTA believes the federal government can afford a nickel of every dollar to help ensure that every child has the opportunity to receive a quality public education.

Five Cents Makes Sense for Education

National PTA is promoting a "Five Cents Makes Sense for Education" campaign to bring attention to the fact that the federal investment in education does not meet the demonstrated needs in our states and communities. This campaign was launched at the National PTA Convention in June, where PTA advocates sent electronic letters to their representatives in Congress and signed postcards to be delivered to President Bush expressing their support of increased funding for public education.

The letters depict a nickel as a reminder of our goal: that at least five cents of every federal budget dollar be dedicated to U.S. Department of Education programs. This level of commitment to funding education is critical to our children's academic success and to the future economic and national security of our country.

Update on Head Start Reauthorization

Although no date is scheduled, the U.S. House of Representatives may vote on the School Readiness Act of 2003, H.R. 2210, as early as next week. National PTA opposes H.R. 2210 because it would transfer control of Head Start funding to the states, and allow states to lower program standards and reduce the range of comprehensive services provided.

Supporters of the change claim the transfer would allow for greater coordination and collaboration among Head Start and state child care and other early childhood programs. However, collaboration and coordination between Head Start and other early childhood programs are already required.

Furthermore, even if control of Head Start funds is given to states, current fiscal realities will make it difficult for children to be appropriately served. The bill would only increase the authorization of funds for 2004 by $17 million, which is not sufficient to expand services to reach all eligible children. States cannot make up the difference and are cutting state-funded early childhood programs to help close budget deficits.

National PTA supports believes federal legislation should support
  • Quality standards that are linked to K-12 standards.

  • Professional development for early childhood educators

  • Effective collaboration between all levels of government agencies and community agencies with authority for education, health and social services.

  • Services and training to increase parent and family involvement.

Department Announces Grant Competition

The grant competition for Parental Information and Resource Centers is now open. Applications for this program are due July 18. The competition is open to nonprofit agencies, including faith-based and community organizations, or consortia of nonprofit agencies and school districts (though not school districts alone). These centers will, among other things, inform the parents of children who attend schools needing improvement about their options. More information can be found at the Department of Education website.

New Report Documents Impact of E-rate on Communities
The Education and Libraries Networks Coalition (EdLiNC) has released a new report that documents the impact of e-rate connecting communities across the country. "E-rate-A Vision of Opportunity and Innovation" was released in partnership with Verizon and includes success stories from schools and libraries around the country.

EdLiNC was formed in 1995 to represent the viewpoint of public and private schools and libraries in the FCC proceeding dealing with the implementation of the Telecommunications Act of 1996. The Coalition seeks to expand the use of educational technologies in schools and libraries by making sure that these entities receive affordable rates guaranteed to them in the Universal Service Provisions of the Telecommunications Act.

National PTA believes that telecommunications and technology resources are vital for educating America's children and youth, and that all students should have equal access to education technology. National PTA supported the creation of the e-rate and efforts to fully fund the program, and would oppose any effort to reduce funding or terminate the program entirely.
