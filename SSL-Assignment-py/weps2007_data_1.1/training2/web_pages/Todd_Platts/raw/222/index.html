Fitzgerald hails House passage of bill to strengthen financial accountability at the Department of Homeland Security
Senate approved similar Fitzgerald bill last year
 
July 21st, 2004 - - WASHINGTON DC...U.S. Senator Peter G. Fitzgerald (R-IL) hailed House passage yesterday of reform legislation proposed by Congressman Todd Platts (R-PA) that will strengthen financial accountability at the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) by bringing it under the Chief Financial Officers (CFO) Act of 1990. Fitzgerald, whose own version of the bill was approved last year by the Senate, said the measure would apply the highest financial standards to one of the federal government’s largest agencies.

“Citizens deserve nothing less than the highest level of vigilance when it comes to management of their hard-earned tax dollars,” said Fitzgerald, who chairs the Governmental Affairs Subcommittee on Financial Management, the Budget, and International Security. “One of the best ways we can achieve that is to subject government agencies to a vigorous auditing process to root out waste, fraud, and abuse. I applaud Congressman Platts for his hard work in pushing this important reform through the House.”

At a Governmental Affairs subcommittee hearing earlier this month, Fitzgerald noted that DHS is the only cabinet-level department not subject to the CFO Act. According to the senator, the sheer size and scope of DHS — with 180,000 employees and a $33 billion budget — would likely magnify the impact of any financial irresponsibility and make it all the more necessary to ensure the department is held financially accountable through the CFO Act.

Specifically, the CFO Act requires agencies to submit audited financial statements to the Office of Management and Budget (OMB) and Congress, thereby improving accountability to taxpayers. The act also requires that the chief financial officer of an agency be confirmed by the Senate and report directly to the agency head. Currently, the DHS chief financial officer is not subject to Senate confirmation.

The original CFO Act of 1990 applied to 24 federal agencies. Fitzgerald helped expand the auditing requirements of the CFO Act to most other executive agencies when he sponsored the Accountability of Tax Dollars Act (ATDA). President Bush signed the legislation into law on November 7, 2002. Last week, Fitzgerald proposed a follow-up bill to the ATDA that would impose the CFO Act’s auditing requirements on the remaining executive agencies.

Throughout his career, Fitzgerald has been recognized for his efforts to strengthen financial practices of federal agencies and make government more accountable to taxpayers. In June, the Council Against Government Waste, a national taxpayer watchdog group, called Fitzgerald a “Taxpayer Hero” for his efforts to curb government waste, fraud, and abuse. Late last year, Taxpayers for Common Sense, another non-partisan watchdog group, presented the senator with its “Mr. Smith Goes to Washington” award given to officials and activists who have “consistently fought the influence of special interests to reduce wasteful government spending.”
