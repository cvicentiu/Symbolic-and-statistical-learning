HEADLINE

Bush Signs FCTC Tobacco Treaty – More Work To Be Done
On Monday, May 10th, President Bush signed the Framework Convention on Tobacco Control (FCTC) treaty – an international treaty to reduce tobacco consumption worldwide. The FCTC treaty would:

  • Ban tobacco advertising and promotion unless constitutional barriers exist,
  • Limit public exposure to secondhand smoke,
  • Require health-warning labels on cigarette packages to cover at least 30 percent of the display area, and
  • Prohibits false, misleading and deceptive language – which may include “low tar,” “light,” or “mild” – that imply that a tobacco product is less harmful.

While the President signing the FCTC treaty is welcome news, there are still significant steps remaining both in the U.S. and internationally before the treaty takes effect.

Internationally, the FCTC treaty must be ratified by 40 nations. To date, 108 countries have signed the treaty but only 12 have ratified it.

In the U.S. context – after signing the treaty, the President still needs to transmit the treaty to the Senate. It is not uncommon for treaties to be signed by the President, but never transmitted to the Senate. After the treaty is transmitted, the Senate must then ratify the treaty. The tobacco control community expects it will take significant political pressure to get the Bush Administration to transmit the treaty. Getting the Senate to ratify the treaty will take additional political pressure.

TOBACCO

Task Force on Tobacco and Health Seeks New Members
On May 7, Reps. Marty Meehan (D-MA) and Todd Platts (R-PA) sent a letter asking their colleagues to join the Task Force on Tobacco and Health. Reps. Meehan and Platts are co-chairs of the task force and took the occasion of Mother’s Day to highlight the toll tobacco takes on families, especially mothers and children.

The letter stated the following statistics as evidence of why smoking increases the risks of serious health problems for mothers and their children.

  • 12,100 children lost their mothers to a smoking-related illness every year.
  • 1 in 5 adult women currently smoke, putting them at risk for lung cancer, emphysema and other life-threatening illnesses.
  • 11.4 percent, or 483,000, women smoke while they are pregnant thus increasing the risk of sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS), respiratory disorders, learning and developmental problems.
  • Exposure to secondhand smoke after birth also causes bronchitis, asthma, pneumonia, and lower respiratory tract infections.

The task force on tobacco and health recommends that Congress needs to do more to protect mothers and children from the harm caused by tobacco. Such legislative actions include providing the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) with authority over tobacco and providing funding for proven tobacco cessation treatments through Medicare, Medicaid and other federally financed health care programs.

The ATS agrees with the legislative remedies put forth by the task force on tobacco and health and will work to increase the membership and help to achieve the legislative goals.

Senate Committee Holds Hearing about Smoking in the Movies
On May 11, the Senate Commerce, Science and Transportation Committee held a hearing regarding the use of tobacco in motion pictures, and whether such portrayals lead to smoking among children and adolescents. Dartmouth Associate Professor of Pediatrics, Dr. Madeline Dalton testified that in 2003, Dartmouth conducted a study that concluded viewing smoking in movies promotes smoking initiation among adolescents. The study analyzed the smoking content of 600 top box-office hits released over the past decade. Eighty-five percent of those movies portrayed smoking. Movies were more likely to have smoking as the rating increased.

Jack Valenti, representing the Motion Picture Association of America and LeVar Burton, of the Directors Guild of America (DGA) testified their opposition to possible government influence in the movie business because of the first amendment right of free speech.

In particular, Mr. Burton testified that the DGA adopted a resolution and is firm in its belief that allowing a character to smoke is a creative decision to be made by individual directors and that our members’ First Amendment rights to free expression must be upheld. The resolution goes on to state that:

  • Gratuitous on-screen smoking in films and television should be discouraged.
  • That directors should recognize the social responsibility they hold in making creative decisions, including how they depict characters that smoke.

Dr. Stan Glantz, Professor of Medicine, University of California at San Francisco and Maryland Attorney General, J. Joseph Curran, Jr. gave recommendations that could be implemented by the motion picture industry within its current practices and without requiring government action. The recommendations to reduce the portrayal of smoking in movies are as follows:

  • Give smoking movies an R-rating,
  • Eliminate brand identification,
  • Certify that no consideration was received for smoking in the movie, and
  • Run anti-smoking messages before any movie that depicts smoking.

The four smoking in movies recommendations have been endorsed by the World Health Organization; American Medical Association; American Academy of Pediatrics; American Legacy Foundation; Society for Adolescent Medicine; American Academy of Allergy Asthma & Immunology; American Heart Association; American Lung Association; and Campaign for Tobacco Free Kids.

The ATS will continue working with Congress and the anti-smoking community on the issue.

CLEAN AIR ACT
During the week of May 10, the Environmental Protection Agency released its new rules on emission from off road diesel engines – which covers small diesel engine construction equipment to bulldozers, trains and boat engines. The EPA rule closed a gaping hole in the Clean Air Act that allowed off road diesel engine emission to be controlled by EPA regulations.

The environmental community uniquely heralded the rule as being a positive environmental step by the Bush Administration to improve the quality the nation’s air. When fully implemented in 2015, EPA estimates that 738,000 tons of nitrogen oxides and 129,000 tons of particulate matter will be reduced by this rule each year. EPA estimates the rule, which will be phased in over 11 years, will save more than 12,000 lives per year by 2030, and prevent 6,000 asthma-related pediatric emergency room visits each year.

HEALTH INSURANCE
Senate Task Force Releases Policy Recommendations for Uninsured Americans
A Senate Republican task force released policy recommendations for providing coverage to the uninsured. The task force was lead by Senator Judd Gregg (R-NH) and recommends limiting medical liability awards, making it easier for small businesses to band together to purchase coverage, and creating tax credits for individuals who purchase insurance.

In related news, on May 10, the Kaiser Commission on Medicaid and the Uninsured released a study stating that healthcare spending would rise by $48.2 billion if the 44 million Americans without health insurance had coverage. The report, released as part of national "Cover the Uninsured Week," also estimated that the uninsured would receive about $40.7 billion in uncompensated care in 2004, with the federal government picking up two-thirds of the tab. The ATS is a sponsor of the Cover the Uninsured Week.
