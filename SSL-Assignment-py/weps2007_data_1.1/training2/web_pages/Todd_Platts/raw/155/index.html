Residents strive for elite title

By David Blymire, August 23, 2004

Carlisle is hoping to be named a "Preserve America" community, a label expected to bring the community some added prestige.

The White House initiative supports community efforts to foster cultural heritage and economic development.

Merri Lou Schaumann, a member of the board of Historic Carlisle Inc., recently approached Carlisle Borough Council about adopting a resolution in support. The council voted unanimously to approve the resolution.

Could bring in tourists

"It gives us prestige and helps tourism," Schaumann says.

Carlisle has to apply for acceptance into the program. Historic Carlisle Inc.'s wayside marker program that has posted signs in front of historic sites in the borough appears to fulfill one of the requirements.

Historic Carlisle Inc. has organized historical re-enactments and school presentations designed to bring local history alive. Each year the group dedicates historical markers that eventually will be part of an extensive self-guided walking tour.

Schaumann says Chris Houston, another Historic Carlisle board member, discovered the Preserve America program and suggested seeking the designation.

Immediate benefits include roadside signs, a listing on an Internet directory and inclusion in national and regional press releases.

President George W. Bush's proposed 2005 budget calls for $10 million in grants for "Preserve America" communities.

A government Website says the funds, if approved, would not fund "bricks and mortar" projects.

Instead, the funds could help communities develop resource management strategies and sound business practices aimed at the preservation of historic assets.

Schaumann says no one has thought much about how such funds could be used in Carlisle. The first priority is being accepted into the program.

A first

If accepted, Carlisle would be the first community in Pennsylvania to receive the designation, she says.

Numerous area officials have endorsed the idea, including Cumberland County commissioners, Carlisle Borough Council, U.S. Rep. Todd Platts, R-19, state Rep. Hal Mowery, R-31, Cumberland County Redevelopment Authority and Dickinson College.

To be accepted, the community must meet the following criteria:

  • the community must have recently supported a historic or cultural preservation project that promotes heritage tourism or otherwise fosters economic vitality, and involves a public-private partnership;


  • the governing body of the community must adopt a resolution indicating its commitment to the preservation of its heritage assets;


  • the community meets at least five criteria specified in three broad categories: discovering heritage through historic places, protecting historic resources and promoting historic assets.
Schaumann recently was named to a Hershey-Capital Region Visitor's Bureau committee formed to study how to capitalize on the growing interest in "heritage cultural tourism."

For more information on the Preserve America program, go to the website at www.preserveamerica.gov.

‘Certified' status sought

Earlier this year Carlisle sought to become part of another, unrelated state program.

Borough Manager Fred Bean says the borough is nearing completion of the paperwork necessary to seek "certified local government" status under the Pennsylvania Historical and Museum Commission.

The program helps communities in Pennsylvania manage and promote their historic districts.

It also makes available matching grants of up to $25,000 per year for local efforts to educate property owners of their responsibilities in the historic district, develop brochures promoting the district, revise design guidelines or even hire historic preservation consultants.
