Republicans buck leadership
Demand domestic programs
be spared funding cuts

By BRANDON MACSATA
Contributor

On April 30, Congress left Washington, D.C., with the sun ready to set on yet another month without a deal brokered on the Budget Resolution for fiscal year 2005. Disagreements over the "PAYGO" provisions continue to stall the mundane budgetary process. Meanwhile, House and Senate Republicans are increasingly pressuring their leadership to fund vital domestic programs.
PAYGO provisions refer to budget reconciliation language directing congressional appropriation committees to "pay" for any proposed tax cuts or spending increases with monies already in the budget (for example, cutting funding somewhere else in the budget). The language is an attempt to regain control over the annual federal budget deficit, which has ballooned to over $450 billion.
"It’s now or never for budget conferees. Scant information is being released on their progress since it may thwart sensitive negotiations on issues such as PAYGO," summarized Dustin May, Legislative Director for Powers, Pyles, Sutter and Verville, a Washington-based health care practice. "The PAYGO issue is the 800-pound gorilla preventing a final budget resolution being completed. At this point in negotiations, it is likely that multiple PAYGO proposals are being floated in order to secure the necessary 51 votes for final passage on the Senate floor."
The delays, however, have not stopped lawmakers from actively trying to influence the process. It is becomingly increasingly clear that congressional Republicans are pressuring their leadership to forego cuts to domestic spending. Many of these efforts would be extremely beneficial to programs vital to providing the necessary services and supports to the HIV/AIDS communities.
House Republican leaders – specifically House Budget Chairman Jim Nussle, R-Iowa – have increasingly been under pressure to allocate more funding for HIV-related programs, both foreign and domestic. Roll Call, which is a Washington, D.C., newspaper circulated only on Capitol Hill, first reported that religious leaders and AIDS advocates have been criticizing the Nussle-authored budget for failing to meet the needs of the international HIV/AIDS communities. According to the April 24 edition of Roll Call, Lutheran, Catholic, Presbyterian and ecumenical leaders from Nussle’s Iowa congressional district are "gently warning" him to approve more funding for AIDS initiatives.
Last month, Reps. Richard Burr, R-N.C., and Sherwood Boehlert, R-N.Y., circulated a "Dear Colleague" letter that pleaded with Chairman Nussle to remove language from the conference report that would effectively cut $2.2 billion in Medicaid spending over five years. The effort being spearheaded by Burr and Boehlert is also being supported by 26 House Republicans.
"I am very pleased that 26 of my colleagues joined me in sending this letter asking for the removal of the House budget resolution Medicaid language," Representative Burr said of his Dear Colleague letter. "Given our country’s current situation with high numbers of unemployed and uninsured individuals who depend on their states’ Medicaid programs for health care, the federal government should not reduce its commitment to those programs."
House Republicans who also signed the Burr-Boehlert letter are Rob Simmons (Conn.), Robin Hayes (N.C.), Randy Cunningham (Calif.), John McHugh (N.Y.), Jim Leach (Iowa), Shelley Moore Capito (W.Va.), Peter King (N.Y.), Jim Gerlach (Pa.), Johnny Isakson (Ga.), Donald Manzullo (Ill.), Greg Walden (Ore.), Jim Ramstad (Minn.), George Radanovich (Calif.), Mary Bono (Calif.), Ralph Hall (Texas), Todd Platts (Pa.), George Nethercutt (Wash.), Christopher Smith (N.J.), Ron Paul (Texas), Tim Johnson (Ill.), Christopher Cox (Calif.), Max Burns (Ga.), Jon Porter (Nev.), Jeff Miller (Fla.) and Jo Ann Emerson (Mo.).
The proposed $2.2 billion reduction in Medicaid spending included in the House-passed Budget Resolution has been identified as a serious concern for the HIV/AIDS community because nearly 55 percent of all persons with AIDS receive their medical care through Medicaid. While the Republican leadership in Congress attempts to control domestic discretionary spending, other more-moderate GOPers are fighting to protect this vital HIV/AIDS funding.
The American Health Care Association and the National Center for Assisted Living praised the Burr-Boehlert letter, calling it growing evidence that the GOP wants to forego cutting vital services to seniors and individuals with disabilities.
"We are encouraged that 27 GOP House members are voicing their support for the many seniors and persons with disabilities who have already been subjected to cuts in the benefits and services that make a real difference in their lives," stated Charles H. Roadman II, President and CEO of AHCA/NCAL. "We are hopeful the budget conferees will recognize $2.2 billion in Medicaid cuts will jeopardize care access, and threaten the progress we are making to improve quality in long-term care."
Sen. Gordon Smith, R-Ore., initiated a similar move in the Senate by writing a letter to Senate Budget Chairman Don Nickles, R-Okla. Smith stressed the need to "protect" Medicaid from future cuts in any conference agreement.
The letter reads: "Recognizing the importance of the Medicaid program, the Senate-passed Budget Resolution does not contain reconciliation instructions to the Finance Committee on Medicaid. We are concerned to see, however, that the House directed the Committee on Energy and Commerce to make cuts to the Medicaid program. Such cuts will leave more people without health insurance and further tax the providers’ ability to serve this vulnerable population."
As states have been forced to cut their Medicaid budgets over the last several years, lawmakers are increasingly finding it hard to sit idle as they hear more and more stories from constituents. In Florida for example, recent news reports mentioned in The Weekly News documented that Palm Beach and Broward counties are receiving fewer federal dollars (See April 1 issue, twnonline.org).
Meanwhile, other senators have also expressed concern over the lack of funding being allocated for the AIDS Drug Assistance Programs authorized under the Ryan White Care Act. Ryan White provides medical care assistance to those with low incomes and little or no insurance. Approximately $1 billion annually goes toward comprehensive care programs, which include health care, support services and drug assistance programs. Most of the funding is made available for ADAPs to provide anti-retroviral therapy, as well as to purchase health insurance for eligible clients.
Smith was accompanied by Sens. Olympia Snowe, R-Maine, Dick Durbin, D-Ill., and Jeff Bingaman, D-N.M., in writing to Sens. Arlen Specter, R-Pa., and Joe Lieberman, D-Conn., who serve respectively as Chairman and Ranking Member on the Senate Subcommittee on Labor-Health and Human Services-Education appropriations. They asked for an additional $217 million increase for the cash-strapped ADAPs.
"Some states are fortunate enough to have additional funding through their own commitments as well as the support of local communities," reads their letter. "Yet, there are dozens of states that find themselves unable to keep up with the demand for coverage under ADAP. As documented in the annual National ADAP Monitoring Report, some states are being forced to take drastic measures to offset the federal funding shortfall, including establishing waiting lists for AIDS medications, reducing drug coverage and restricting eligibility."
Additionally, SAVE ADAP sent an alert to its grassroots network, encouraging them to contact their congressional delegation. "There are close to 1,300 people on waiting lists for the AIDS Drug Assistance Program across the United States," read the action alert. "Help put an end to this crisis today by urging your representative to sign an important ‘Dear Colleague’ letter!"
While the fate of the Budget Resolution remains unclear, it is increasingly likely in this election year that many programs originally slated to be cut will be spared. Advocates are certainly doing their part to make sure that message is heard loud and clear in Washington.
Note: Dear Colleague letters are circulated by Members of Congress amongst their colleagues in their respective chambers asking for their endorsement or sign-on regarding pending legislation.
