Samori Coles shares a moment with George Clinton

 

A DISCUSSION WITH GEORGE CLINTON
by Samori Coles

I could not believe my good fortune. I was scheduled to meet with George Clinton for a press discussion in less than an hour. The morning studio sessions had gone well, but I could hardly focus with the interview less than 60 minutes away. After my last recording session, I gathered my notebook and headed up to the 4th -floor boardroom to find out what was really going on with “The Atomic One”. I did not have a chance to prepare any questions, so I was looking forward to just chatting with Mr. Clinton.

I approached the boardroom and realized right away that there weren’t a whole lot of people in the room. I could see the multi-colored hair adornments and knew that I was definitely in the right place. I was invited to have a seat on the opposite side of the table facing George.

George Clinton talked a lot about his pressing legal entanglements involving over $100 million in publishing royalties owed to him. Apparently, the individual who he hired years ago to administer his publishing had found a way to wrestle ownership away from Mr. Clinton right under his nose. That means that every time one of George Clinton’s songs comes on the radio, is used in a commercial, is sampled, or otherwise reproduced, he does not get a penny. The other guy, who was supposed to have a fiduciary responsibility to his client’s needs, now controls all of the publishing ownership and rights. The wildest part about it is that the Florida judge who ruled in favor of the other guy in the George Clinton dispute is the same judge that ruled in favor of George W. Bush in the 2000 Florida presidential election scandal.

In terms of sampling of his music by urban music producers, George Clinton has a fondness for today’s music pioneers. “It’s not the Hip-Hop producers who are getting over. In most cases, they pay what they are supposed to pay for the use of my music. The problem is that ownership of my publishing has been stolen from me, and when anyone pays for the right to sample my music, someone else gets the money.”

I asked Mr. Clinton if he felt today’s music has progressed or regressed from music of the past. “The music always progresses”, said George. “We (Black People) don’t have a lot of real assets (real estate, cash, stocks, etc), but we are rich in metaphysical assets. We have the ability to create music and other art forms that benefit the entire world. Initially, our contributions are deemed unworthy and unsophisticated, but once we decide to discard them, others come and claim them as their own,” he said. “We no longer have a lot of music programs in our communities. Therefore, Black youth pick up turntables, samplers and drum machines to create new art forms of self-expression. And, youth in every other culture try to emulate it”.

After spending an hour and a half with the P-Funk pioneer, I knew it was time to count my blessings and let him get on with his business. He was in the process of taping a pilot for a new Philly-based television show entitled “Up Close And Personal”, which will be hosted by award-winning Black Music personality Gary Shepherd. The program is being produced by BEMA (Black Entertainment & Music Association) and Bob Lott of Philadelphia-based Teamwork International Productions.

Wrapping up, I asked George Clinton about all of the profane and negative language and images perpetuated by a lot of today’s top Hip-Hop artists. Mr. Clinton had an interesting take on the subject. He said, “Sometimes we get so caught up in language, and “this” word and “that” word, that we lose site of the bigger problems in the world. At one time, the word “Bitch” simply meant female dog. Now, it has a negative meaning. Many negative words in one time and/or culture are meaningless in another. We’ve got to stop wasting time fighting over nonsense. I’m still waiting for aliens to come”, he finished. Enough said.

It was an interesting and mind opening experience. George Clinton has wisdom for the ages. He has a lot of information that can keep artists from falling into the same pitfalls that he fell into. I am forever grateful for the opportunity.
