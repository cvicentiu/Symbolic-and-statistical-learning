George Clinton (musician)

Encyclopedia Article

George Clinton (musician), born in 1941, American singer and songwriter, bandleader, and seminal music producer. In the 1970s Clinton helped pioneer and popularize funk, a style of rhythm-and-blues music (R&B) that emphasizes rhythm over melody (see Rhythm-and-Blues Music: Funk and Disco). Along with American singer James Brown, Clinton was influential in creating black popular music that appealed to a wide audience regardless of age, gender, race, or geography. Spanning three decades, Clinton’s groups and his music pervaded American popular music, established the careers of dozens of musicians, and developed an international following.

Born in Kannapolis, North Carolina, Clinton was the oldest of nine children. He eventually settled in Newark, New Jersey, and worked as a barber in nearby Plainfield. Barbershops have been popular places for blacks to socialize and sing since the 19th century, and it was at the barbershop that Clinton developed his vocals. In 1956 he formed a harmony group called the Parliaments, with Calvin Simon, Grady Thomas, and later Ray Davis and Clarence “Fuzzy” Haskins. The group worked in the Newark area before heading to Detroit, Michigan, in 1963, where they hoped to record for the Motown record company. Instead they recorded for the small, independent Revilot label. In 1967 the group released the song “(I Wanna) Testify,” which reached the top five on Billboard magazine’s R&B charts and number 20 on Billboard’s pop charts. Clinton also had some commercial success writing songs for others, including singer Diana Ross and the soul group Jackson 5.

After a contract dispute with Revilot, Clinton lost the rights to the name the Parliaments and consequently formed a new group, Funkadelic, in 1968 with his old backup band. With Funkadelic, Clinton established an innovative mix of old and new by drawing from the layered sounds and syncopated rhythms of James Brown, the hard-rock style of American guitarist Jimi Hendrix, and the mixture of sounds of Sly and the Family Stone. Funkadelic combined sophisticated funk rhythms with electronic arrangements, outrageous lyrics, and lavish stage productions. Indeed, P-Funk (for Parliament-Funkadelic), as Clinton’s work later became known, grew to be a philosophy and state of mind as much as a kind of dance music. Funkadelic’s debut album (Funkadelic, 1970) was followed by a series of provocatively titled albums for Westbound Records: Free Your Mind and Your Ass Will Follow (1970); Maggott Brain (1971); America Eats Its Young (1972); Cosmic Slop (1973); and One Nation Under a Groove (1978).

In 1970, after regaining the right to use the name the Parliaments, Clinton formed a new group called Parliament with the same members as Funkadelic. Clinton hoped to reach a broader audience by performing more accessible songs that emphasized the voice with Parliament while continuing to produce wilder instrumental music with Funkadelic. After signing with Casablanca, a small label that supported Clinton’s creative endeavors, Parliament recorded several well-received funk albums, including Up For The Downstroke (1974), Mothership Connection (1975), and ParliamentLive/P-Funk Earth Tour (1977). Many well-known instrumentalists have performed with Parliament and Funkadelic over the years, including bassist Bootsy Collins and saxophonist Maceo Parker. In addition, dozens of spinoff groups have emerged from the P-Funk experience.

Clinton’s visibility diminished somewhat during the 1980s, yet his influence did not. By 1989 his P-Funk was a keen source of samples (electronic snippets of prerecorded sound) for rap and hip-hop artists, particularly rap group De La Soul. Rapper Dr. Dre later sampled Clinton and P-Funk in his 1993 album The Chronic. In 1990 Clinton appeared in the motion picture House Party and also as himself in the film Graffiti Bridge, starring the artist formerly known as Prince. Clinton released a number of recordings in the 1990s, both with various members of the P-Funk groups and under his own name, including Paint the White House Black (1993), Police Doggy (1995), and T.A.P.O.A.F.O.M. (1996). He has also assisted in the production of albums for several artists and groups, including the rock group Red Hot Chili Peppers. Parliament/Funkadelic was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1997.
