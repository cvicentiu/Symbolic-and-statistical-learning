Interview: Andrew Powell, Kapsules creator

Interview: Andrew Powell, creator of Kapsules

Recently Andrew Powell of Carbon Lokai fame released his latest work, Kapsules, a .NET-based engine for desktop widgets, which you can build with the language of your choice - Python, Ruby, Javascript.. We caught up with the harried Michigander between Playboy Playmate trysts:

shell-shocked:
So how did Kapsules get started? Is there a strategy behind Kapsules? Where is it going?

Andrew: [dragging a hand through a mop of mad scientist hair]
Honestly I was bored last summer with the shell I was working on, and was looking for an app to display some weather info one day. I was already messing around with Layered Windows in VC++ and had picked up a copy of vs.net through work so it really came out of tinkering in many different areas. I had already written a shell in visual basic (not recommended, never released because it stunk) as a proof of concept for scriptable plugins. The concept was based upon an old shell called Graphite (circa 1998) which made use of the Windows Scripting Host. It wasn't until I had run across Konfabulator that I got the idea for Kapsules.

I had played around with Samurize, but it wasn't for my tastes. There were several things with the plugin/script interface that I didn't care for. I had gotten a copy of DesktopX for evaluation as well, with the same result. Two great apps, but a guy cant help personal preference. So when I stumbled upon Konfabulator my eyes lit up. I could put together the areas of programming that I had the most fun with. That and it was nearly what I thought the other apps were lacking. There wasn't much I didn't like about it. That and it's a bloody gorgeous eye-candy app. I make no qualms about saying that it was my initial inspiration for the project. Mad props to Arlo Rose and Perry Clarke.

It started out shooting for a way to display the weather. Honestly I wasn't planning a poopton of widgets. I started out using the actual weather images from Konfabulator, that is until I ran across the flame-ridden posts on Konfab's and Samurize's websites over the use of the Konfabulator images. So my search for new weather images ensued and I ran across gDesklets. That gave me the direction for the drive widget, which I use every day. KapWeather also uses the weather images from one of gDesklet's widgets, with permission of course. So from there I started adding functionality. The KapVWM widget came about because of a use of Citrix at work, and a funky deal where I couldn't minimize my mail window and restore it, so I adapted Coreshell's vwm plugin (with permission) and walla, multiple desktops with Kapsules.

Coding up to this point was off and on for about a year. It wasn't really a priority, just something to play with between beer, sleep, beer, food and occasionally classes. Pistos came along and volunteered to help me test Kapsules and pushed RubyScript. So we set that up. Then Python. I tried perl, but the only active scripting engine is WAY inconsistent, and I know nothing of perl, so it was kinda left by the wayside. I eventually sent it out to a few people for testing, and they requested the Slideshow and Sysinfo widgets, so I put those together. A few more people got their hands on it and the whole idea got really hyped.

So most of what is Kapsules at the moment took place over 2 or so months. I've been working on the next version for about a month now, and it should be ready soon. I'm a big fan of testing the slop out of software before allowing the public to download it. It's kind of a 'your product reflects on you' type of a pride thing. That and it really irks me to download buggy software. There are always, of course, those bugs that you just don't see. 0.7.1.19 was tested by 20 or so people for nearly a month and we still didn't catch them all. Go figure.

There really is no more strategy to Kapsules, other than to get the name and software out there to more people. It's like the other apps of the same nature, its survival is completely reliant on the people who write for it and can provide the occasional support. I hate to use the term; but you could call it community (shudder) reliant. I know I'll always be using it, but I'm speaking more about Joe Sixpack user.

Kapsules is headed in a forward direction, and that's all I can really say about the future. We continue to get requests from widget developers for more (and improved) features and I continue to add them. I've brought on a very active and very knowledgable person who goes by '_dk_' to handle a new feature for widget developers, which is something I hadn't planned on. I always envisioned this as a one-man-show. Then again I didn't think it would be come nearly as popular as it has already, and it's still little known.

shell-shocked:
What's the state of Carbon Lokai development? Is your current focus exclusively on Kapsules? Will Carbon Lokai get less attention in the future? We asked this in your previous interview but: Do you (still) expect Lokai to reach a "1.0"?

Andrew: [looking wistfully over his shoulder into the eyes of a blushing brunette selling Salvation Army flags]
Ah, good old Carbon Lokai. It's still my shell on my PC. That's about all I can say for it. The state of development is basically nil. I doubt I'll ever release another version of it, but I'll continue to use it. It serves me well, quirks, bugs and all. And yes, Carbon is getting less attention; it's basically getting no attention. It's still my personal favorite shell.

The current focus of my free time development is exclusively Kapsules atm.

shell-shocked:
I assume Kapsules doesn't work with desktops provided by shells other than Explorer; but then perhaps it would depend on whether the other shell implements the desktop through some native Windows library or not. So can Kapsules work with other shells? And if it can't, could it? And if it could, how much work would it take?

Andrew: [deftly disarming an enemy hacker combatant of his semi-automatic anti-tank uzi with the flip-top from his soda can - soda water, that is]
Kapsules works with every known replacement shell. A few of them (which aren't in heavy development) still implement a fake desktop window, which is an ugly hack, but nonetheless. Kapsules plays fine with these fake desktop windows, that is, until you tell a Widget to attach itself to the desktop (Send To Back).

There were issues with geoShell, but Matrice64 and I worked that out and the fake desktop window hasn't been in a release since two releases ago. I'm also aware that some plugins/modules for various shells also create a fake window. And while I would love to make Kapsules play nice with every app that created one of these windows, it would be a never ending job. Lesson of the day 'Use something standard!' - All the Firefox nuts should like that one ;)

shell-shocked:
You state on the Carbon 6 site that Kapsules was inspired by Konfabulator but that beyond the general purpose the similarities stop. Care to go into more detail?

Andrew: [catching an explosive lobbed from a passing van and coolly popping open the back and crossing the yellow and chartreuse wires, causing the van to explode]
Yup, that's also in about 6 different places on the Kapsules site (plug: http://kapsules.shellscape.org) as well.

To take a snip from the front page:

Kapsules makes use of the Microsoft Active Scripting Engine, which is a part of Windows. Using this built-in scripting engine, Kapsules can allow widgets to be written in a multitude of scripting languages, provided that a user has a language's add-on engine installed. That being said, widgets can make use of a certain languages strengths, weaknesses, and special features. Kapsules does not limit a developer to what language they can use!

This means that Kapsules doesn't use a scripting engine or format anything remotely close to Konfabulator. I liked the idea, and that was about it. All settings are stored via XML. A widget is accompanied by two dialogs; one for widget specific settings, and the other for individual widget window settings (transparency, etc.), meaning the user should never have a reason to edit the raw file.

shell-shocked:
I'm intrigued by the fact that Kapsules relies on native Windows functionality. .NET and Avalon are going to greatly broaden and improve the interface and customizability of Windows. Do you follow these technologies closely? What do you foresee as their impact on Windows users in general and our own shell-hacker community in particular? With Kapsules are you consciously jumping on a bandwagon?

Andrew: [whipping out a PDA with satellite uplink, logging onto MS' encrypted network via an NSA back door and downloading the latest .NET bugfixes and easter eggs]
I try to follow the .NET stuff closely. MS is always coming out with gimmicky stuff, so I try not to get too excited too fast. I'm usually at least a year behind to update to a latest version of windows and it's basically the same with new technologies like the .NET framework. When I first read about it I wasn't generally impressed.

Anyone who had followed Carbon Lokai in the past knows that I got the bare bones app from Brandon Sneed (nivenh). The guy pretty much walked me through everything that is GDI+ (graphical library for windows, lets you do neat things) with respect to Carbon. When I embarked to take on the Kapsules project I was going to write it via standard C++. The GDI+ part was what moved my attention to the .NET framework. Any .NET app uses GDI+ drawing natively. Due to the RAD nature of VB.NET and my already extensive knowledge of the language, I made the move, got a copy of VS.NET and went to town breaking myself of the old VB6 habits.

I love .NET now. I can switch back and forth between managed C++, C# and VB.NET like I'm looking at the same language. Not to mention I recently discovered (told ya I was a little slow) that you can compile vb.net and c# files in the same project.

Ok, so I'm ranting like I'm on IRC now. But I'm sure you get the idea.

I'm really not sure what impact .NET will have on the shell community as a whole. I really feel that the alt. shell scene is on the decline. That could be the arguably tainted opinion of a fairly old school alt. shell users though. I do know, and I will be happy to start this rumor, that there is a shell in development in C# right now, and contrary to common belief, it screams. Very fast application.

I haven't just jumped on the bandwagon, I'm the band, and own the horses pulling it. :)

shell-shocked:
The use of the .NET framework is interesting and even provocative. Some people call .NET bloat, or think it makes the developer's job easier at the user's expense; and others talk of it's greatly improved object interface. Obviously you think enough of .NET to develop in it, and your posts to the Kapsules support forum hint that you think .NET is pretty awesome. What specifically turns you on about .NET? Do you recognize any disadvantages to using it for Kapsules? For projects bigger than Kapsules or development in general? Do you plan to use it for your future projects? What would you say to someone to convince them to try out the .NET framework?

Andrew: [narrowing his eyes ever-so-007-like, causing nearby females to fumble for their smelling-salts]
That gives me a smile every time. Anyone calling .NET bloat is absolutely, without a doubt naive. It's got to be fun living in fantasy land. ;)

What turns me on : Long walks on the beach, sunsets... Oh you were serious? ;) I love the power of the .NET languages. Basically everything that was a pill to do in VB* has been made simple. I'm a VB fanatic though. It was my first language and it's got a special place in my head. .NET apps are generally easier for me to write as well. The interface development time has been cut in half (IMHO) as opposed to a straight C++ app. Although those who use such tools as ATLX (Sneed's beast) and WTL might argue differently. I love the graphics capabilities as well. Minimal coding for maximum effect.

Disadvantages? COM Interoperability. LORD I LOATHE IT. This week has been a shining example of how the difficulty of COM Interop. COM, for those not up with the standard, is a means by which a library of code can be referenced system wide (I'm talking essentially here, don't disembowel me for leaving out details). COM is peachy keen in every programming language which can support it, except .NET. You've got a 50/50 chance of a COM Library or Control working 100% in .NET. The latest 2.0 Beta 1 Runtime is supposed to solve a lot of those issues, but it's beta.

Yeah, I'll probably use .NET now for every app I write, unless it's just overkill, or I'm writing a website. I'm still not convinced that ASP.NET is 'so much better' than ASP 2.0, but that's another deal altogether. The Kapsules website is a mix of the two, a testament to my thoughts on that.

To convince someone to get the .NET runtime? 'Hey bub. You're missing out on a lot of very cool apps. You're probably gonna have to get it at one point or another, so why not now.' I don't think I would try much harder than that. It's not important to me that Kapsules take over your desktop ;) It's there for those who want to enjoy it.

shell-shocked:
I've personally become very interested lately in the use of scripting and XML to create applications - all areas of the industry are pushing these technologies. Have you considered creating an entire shell using this scripting technology, using what you've learned from Carbon Lokai perhaps? Would you say it's capable or ready for such an application - flexible and powerful enough? It seems to me at the least that these technologies are naturally modular, and it would not only be natural to create a basic framework for a shell that could be used for all kinds of different styles, themes, and interfaces, but it would be easy to make the framework and the interface modules/plug-ins really easy to understand and modify by users, just like the Kapsules widgets.

Andrew: [with a faraway "I could tell you, but then I'd have to kill you" look in his eyes]
Yeah I considered it. So much so that I wrote one. Back in 2002 though, when I was still a VB6 junkie and was just messing around with proof of concepts. That's all that shell really was. VB6 really isn't suited for a language by which to write a shell. Basically, all the work that I did on that shell was the basis of my knowledge for work with Kapsules.

I would say you could write a shell to run on scripts. Graphite (for those who remember it) did this QUITE WELL. Very fast and very stable shell. I've been getting requests for Taskbar and System Tray widgets for Kapsules, to which I've declined to write. It could be done, but Kapsules isn't intended to be a shell.

"It seems to me at the least that these technologies are naturally modular, and it would not only be natural to create a basic framework for a shell that could be used for all kinds of different styles, themes, and interfaces, but it would be easy to make the framework and the interface modules/plug-ins really easy to understand and modify by users, just like the Kapsules widgets."

Two words : Raptor Platform. The 3-year attempt at just that (well, nearly that), which never was really completed.

shell-shocked:
One of the shell-shocked list subscribers has asked how far the interaction via scripting languages goes. How broad are the uses of Kapsules? Can one for example create a script-driven popup menu with Kapsules? Could one create a widget interface for one's favorite application? To Foobar for example? I'm going to guess.. Yes?

Andrew: [leaping onto the rope ladder of a passing super-secret miltary helicopter piloted by his nemesis-slash-love-interest, the beautiful and deadly Eastern European Off-Gray Widow, usually played by Halle Berry]
I'm not quite sure how to answer that. There hasn't been anything asked of me that I couldn't write via a script for Kapsules yet. If his/her question pertains to how much the script actually controls; the answer is everything. Kapsules handles the window for you, shows it and whatnot, but all graphical, visual and user interaction are handled by the script. Uber-Powerful.

You can use Kapsules for whatever you would like. There really aren't any limits.

You can't create a popup, YET. You could sure create an interface for your favorite app. We've an EMule interface and iTunes at the moment, with more in development (3rd party widgets). A general media player interface is also planned for development, and will ship with the installer when it's complete.

shell-shocked:
Thanks for your time Andy! (And by the way, save some of those Playmates for the fish.. okay?)

And by the way, people.. in case it isn't obvious.. the editor made up all that spy stuff. Even if Andy were a spy, which he isn't, and all that stuff really happened, which it never did, the editor would still have made it up. Well, maybe the bit about the chick selling Salvation Army Flags really happened. But that's it! The rest is all lies!
