David Lodge (1935-)

(David John Lodge)

Merritt Moseley, University of North Carolina

(2004 words) Print

Add to Bookshelves

Novelist, Literary Critic, Essayist, Dramatist.
Active 1958- in England, Britain, Europe

David Lodge is one of England's pre-eminent novelist-critics. His work in either sphere – fiction and literary criticism – would be sufficient to earn him recognition and esteem. His combination of the two over a long, prolific and impressive career has both demonstrated an admirable versatility and, somewhat more subtly, permitted the cross-fertilisation of fiction by literary theory and criticism. With his long-time friend and sometime collaborator, the late Malcolm Bradbury, Lodge helped to create the category of novelist-don (it is much more uncommon in Britain than in the US for creative writers to be university professors as well) and the contemporary genre of campus fiction. It is for his academic novels – perhaps most notably Changing Places, Small World and Nice Work, the so-called Rummidge trilogy – that Lodge is best known, but his body of fictional work includes much more and may be most valuable for the vision it embodies of the life of the modern English Roman Catholic in an increasingly secular age.

Lodge was born on 28 January 1935 in Dulwich, South London. The only son of William Frederick and Rosalie Murphy Lodge, he grew up in South East London. His father was a professional musician. Mrs. Lodge was Catholic and David was raised as a Catholic. In 1939 the outbreak of World War II produced some dislocation in his life; he had vivid experience of the Blitz (his home was not far from the Docks), and spent some of the war years in Cornwall or Surrey after being evacuated from London. In 1945 he entered St. Joseph's Academy, a Catholic grammar school in London; upon graduation, in 1952, he entered University College London (UCL) to read English. He received his B.A. (Honours) in 1955 and then spent two years satisfying his National Service requirement in the Royal Armoured Corps. In 1957 he returned to UCL for postgraduate study, receiving an M.A. in 1959, the same year he married Mary Frances Jacob. In 1958 he had published a short book called About Catholic Authors. He spent 1959-60 teaching English in London, then was appointed Lecturer at the University of Birmingham, his academic home until his retirement in 1987.

That same year his first novel, The Picturegoers, appeared. It was an account of life in South East London, featuring a large Catholic family based on his wife's family, and had obvious affinities with the work of the “Angry Young Men” of the 1950s. His second novel, Ginger, You're Barmy, set in Catterick, North Yorkshire (where he had trained) and focussed on the stultifying life encountered by a sensitive Londoner in his army service, was published in 1962. In 1963 he collaborated with Malcolm Bradbury and James Duckett on a revue, “Between These Four Walls”, which was produced in Birmingham. Lodge attributes his decision to write comedy to this experience and Bradbury's influence. In 1964-65 he benefitted from a Harkness Commonwealth Fellowship, travelling in the United States. Another revue, “Slap in the Middle”, was produced in Birmingham in 1965, the same year his first comic novel and first academic novel, The British Museum is Falling Down, was published. Focussed on a day in the life of a harassed post-graduate student named Adam Appleby, it included pastiches of a number of prose styles and was a modest hommage to Joyce's Ulysses.

In 1966 Lodge published both a short book on Graham Greene and his very influential book of novel criticism, The Language of Fiction. In 1969, a very exciting time in American higher education, he taught at the University of California, Berkeley, on an exchange scheme. In 1970 appeared Out of the Shelter, a sort of Bildungsroman about a young boy's experiences during the War and in the liberating aftermath, particularly in Germany.
