Muriel Spark - an appreciation

'She dealt with solemn subjects in a bright and sparkling style'



David Lodge
Monday April 17, 2006
The Guardian

Muriel Spark was a truly original writer, one of those rare figures who change the possibilities of an art form for other practitioners. She started out as a poet and biographer, and published her first novel, The Comforters, in 1957, when she was 39. It was as if she had been secretly building up a word-hoard of fictional ideas, techniques and tropes and suddenly released them in a remarkable explosion of creative energy.

Several more novels followed in quick succession, two of them (The Bachelors and The Ballad of Peckham Rye) in the same year, 1960. The appearance of The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie in 1961 confirmed the arrival of a major talent.

She appeared on the British literary scene as the neo-realist novel of the 50s was beginning to run out of steam, and she demonstrated a different style of storytelling we would learn to call postmodernist.

Instead of hiding behind a first-person character-narrator or cultivating a Flaubertian "impersonality" Spark's authorial voice was up front, briskly summarising the characters and their actions, and shifting the temporal focus of the story - not with the deliberation of Conrad or Ford, but at dizzying speed, from present to past to future and back again, sometimes in a single paragraph.

Solemn subjects, such as guilt, religious faith, and death, were dealt with in a sparkling epigrammatic style. The supernatural, in the form of angels and devils, was apt to intrude disconcertingly into the modern secular world, as in her fictional debut, a short story called The Seraph and the Zambesi which won an Observer short story prize and so impressed Graham Greene that he generously gave Muriel Spark enough money to allow her to write full time.

She wrote her first novel, about a young woman who is a convert to Catholicism, shortly after being converted herself. Catholicism is an important element in many of her novels, but it is far from the agonized, sin-obsessed faith of Greene's characters at the same period. The observation of the heroine of The Comforters that "the True Church was awful, though unfortunately, one couldn't deny, true", encapsulates the difference. What especially fascinated Muriel Spark were the parallels between the novelist and God - both being "omniscient", both knowing the beginning and the end of the worlds they create. But she insists on the difference between the two orders. The providence of fiction is benign because it is a kind of superior game between writer and reader; but when people like Miss Brodie begin to play God, they do damage.

In the mid-60s Muriel Spark began to live mainly abroad, in New York and then in Italy, and the settings of her novels reflected this change.

One might almost say that she began to write a kind of Eurofiction in works such as The Driver's Seat (1970) and Not To Disturb (1971) but the preoccupation with faith and moral choice, explored through ever more daring metafictional devices, persists. In the former, a woman pursues across Rome the man who must murder her; in the latter a butler presides over a crime of passion in a Swiss villa in a manner which is both God-like and Jeeves-like. Both these novellas are quite brilliant, and nobody else could have written them.

In my opinion they were the high-water mark of her genius, but she went on producing fiction of very high quality, returning in later years to a nostalgic engagement with her British experience.

She dwelt apart from the frenetic prize-and-publicity-obsessed world of modern publishing, writing her novels by hand, pleasing herself with what she wrote, and never ceasing to surprise and challenge her readers.

· David Lodge is an author, commentator and essayist
