Middle School/Junior High Book List

The following books tie in with many of the skills and themes taught in the Second Step curriculum. They are appropriate for students in Grades 6–9 but vary in maturity, explicitness, and reading level. Brief summaries of the books and a list of the skills they cover are included.

Teachers may want to give the book list to students or to the school librarian, who may wish to pull out the books and put them in a special place.

Bridge to Terabithia by Katherine Paterson. New York, NY: HarperCollins. 1987. The powerful story of an unusual friendship cut short. Jess grapples with his feelings about Leslie’s death. (Empathy, anger management, peer pressure, friendship, stereotyping)

Children of the River by Linda Crew. New York, NY: Dell. 1989. After immigrating from Cambodia to the United States, Sundara finds herself caught between two cultures. (Empathy, problem solving, change of feelings, perspective taking, assertiveness, friendship, stereotyping, peer pressure)

Crash by Jerry Spinelli. New York, NY: Alfred A. Knopf. 1996. Crash has bullied his neighbors and classmates his whole life. When his grandfather suffers a stroke, Crash suddenly finds that friends and family have a new meaning for him. (Empathy, friendship, bullying, change of feelings)

Cry the Beloved Country by Alan Paton. New York, NY: Simon and Schuster. 1995. A poignant and passionate story about the tragic affects of apartheid on both black and white South Africans. (Empathy, anger management, problem solving, friendship, stereotyping, change of feelings)

Deliver Us from Evie by M. E. Kerr. New York, NY: HarperCollins. 1995. When Evie reveals to her family that her relationship with Patsy is romantic, her family must deal with both their own and their community’s anger and stereotypes. (Empathy, anger management, change of feelings, stereotyping)

Diary of a Young Girl by Anne Frank. New York, NY: Bantam. 1995. This diary documents Anne’s experiences as a young Jewish girl in hiding during World War II. (Empathy, anger management, impulse control, assertiveness, change of feelings, friendship)

Dicey’s Song by Cynthia Voigt. New York, NY: Simon and Schuster. 1982. After holding her family together for a summer, 13-year-old Dicey has difficulty adjusting to a new home where she is not the primary caretaker of her siblings. A sequel to Homecoming. (Empathy, problem solving, anger management, friendship, perspective taking, peer pressure)

Dragon’s Gate by Lawrence Yep. New York, NY: HarperCollins. 1995. When a political incident forces Otter to flee from China and join his father in the United States, Otter is confronted with the racism and unsafe working conditions that he and Chinese workers must face as they help build the transcontinental railroad. (Empathy, impulse control, anger management, problem solving, stereotyping, friendship, feelings change, assertiveness)

Earthshine by Teresa Nelson. New York, NY: Dell. 1996. Slim and her family struggle to cope with her father’s slow death from complications from AIDS. (Empathy, anger management, impulse control, friendship)

Farewell to Manzanar by Jeanne Wakatsuki Houston and James D. Houston. New York, NY: Bantam. 1983. A true story based on Jeanne Wakatsuki Houston’s experiences as a child growing up in a Japanese internment camp and her efforts after the war to comprehend her experience. (Empathy, anger management, problem solving, stereotyping, change of feelings)

The Giver by Lois Lowry. New York, NY: Doubleday Dell. 1994. Set in a futuristic society that has eliminated feelings, the book tells of the difficult decisions Jonas must make that will affect his whole community. (Empathy, change of feelings, problem solving, assertiveness, peer pressure)

Gypsy Davey by Chris Lynch. New York, NY: HarperCollins. 1995. A rare insight into the troubled life of a boy whom others call “retarded.” The narrative alternates perspectives, allowing the reader to view Davey as he views himself and as others view him. (Empathy, anger management, stereotyping, perspective taking)

Homecoming by Cynthia Voigt. New York, NY: Simon and Schuster. 1981. Abandoned by their mother, four children vow to stay together and find a new family for themselves. A prequel to Dicey's Song. (Problem solving, impulse control, empathy, assertiveness, perspective taking)

Iceman by Chris Lynch. New York, NY: HarperCollins. 1994. Brought up in an emotionally distant family, Eric prioritizes his parents’ wishes over his own until he realizes the harm he is causing himself. (Anger management, problem solving, change of feelings)

I Heard the Owl Call My Name by Margaret Craven. New York, NY: Dell. 1980. A dying priest is sent to spend his last years with a tribe of Kwakiutl Indians. (Empathy, stereotyping, perspective taking, feelings change, friendship)

Island of the Blue Dolphins by Scott O'Dell. New York, NY: Dell. 1987. Left behind on an island when her tribe relocates to the mainland, Karana not only survives, but learns much about herself. (Empathy, problem solving, change of feelings, assertiveness)

Jacob Have I Loved by Katherine Paterson. New York, NY: HarperCollins. 1990. Brought up in the shadow of her twin sister, Louise struggles to define herself as an individual. (Empathy, anger management, stereotyping, change of feelings)

Julie of the Wolves by Jean Craighead George. New York, NY: HarperCollins. 1974. Orphaned at 11 and married at 13, Julie escapes an unhappy home situation and struggles to survive out in the Alaskan wilderness. (Impulse control, empathy, feelings change, assertiveness)

Letters from Rifka by Karen Hesse. New York, NY: Puffin. 1993. When Rifka and her family flee Russia during the 1917 Revolution, Rifka is denied passage on the boat to America for medical reasons and must spend the year alone. (Empathy, problem solving, friendship, assertiveness, change of feelings)

The Lilith Summer by Hadley Irwin. New York, NY: Feminist Press. 1979. Ellen reluctantly agrees to spend the summer “ladysitting” her elderly neighbor in exchange for a bicycle. Through the course of the summer, she overcomes her preconceptions of the elderly and forms a deep bond with her neighbor. (Empathy, problem solving, perspective taking, stereotyping, friendship)

Make Lemonade by Virginia Euwer Wolff. Thorndike, ME: Thorndike Press. 1993. 14-year-old LeVonne takes a babysitting job working for a teenage mother in order to save money for college. (Empathy, problem solving, friendship, perspective taking, assertiveness)

Maniac Magee by Jerry Spinelli. New York, NY: HarperCollins. 1992. In his search for a home, Maniac helps a town break down racial barriers. (Empathy, problem solving, perspective taking, stereotyping, assertiveness)

My Brother Sam Is Dead by James Lincoln Collier and Christopher Collier. New York, NY: Scholastic Books. 1985. Tim recounts the experiences of his family during the American revolution when his brother Sam joins rebel forces and the rest of the family remains neutral. (Empathy, anger management, impulse control, problem solving, perspective taking, feelings change)

Remembering Mog by Colby Rodowsky. New York, NY: Farrar, Straus and Giroux. 1996. Two years after her sister’s death, Annie decides finally to confront what happened and begins a slow process of healing. (Empathy, anger management, impulse control, friendship, change of feelings)

Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry by Mildred Taylor. New York, NY: Puffin Books. 1991. Cassie and her brothers struggle to understand and fight against the racism in their community. (Impulse control, anger management, problem solving, stereotyping, assertiveness)

Scorpions by Walter Dean Myers. New York, NY: HarperCollins. 1988. With his brother Randy in jail, potentially facing a life sentence, Jamal must determine whether or not he will take Randy’s place in a gang. (Empathy, anger management, impulse control, friendship, peer pressure, bullying)

Shabanu by Suzanne Fisher Staples. New York, NY: Alfred A. Knopf. 1991. When her family arranges for her to marry an older man, 11-year-old Shabanu finds herself torn between her feelings and Pakistani traditions. (Empathy, impulse control, anger management, assertiveness, stereotyping, change of feelings)

A Solitary Blue by Cynthia Voigt. New York, NY: Ballantine Books. 1983. Jeff’s mother left his family when he was very young. When she reenters his life several years later, he is confronted with a flood of new emotions. Overlap in characters from Homecoming and Dicey’s Song. (Problem solving, anger management, empathy, perspective taking, friendship)

Somewhere in the Darkness by Walter Dean Myers. New York, NY: Scholastic Books. 1993. Jimmy’s father arrives unexpectedly, after an absence of 14 years, to reclaim his role as a father. (Empathy, anger management, change of feelings)

Taking Sides by Gary Soto. San Diego, CA: Harcourt Brace. 1992. When Lincoln and his mother move from San Francisco to a suburban town, he experiences many unfamiliar emotions that cause him to redefine himself. (Friendship, stereotyping, anger management, change of feelings)

A Tree Grows in Brooklyn by Betty Smith. New York, NY: HarperCollins. 1968. An episodic novel documenting the experiences of Francie and her family during the early 1900s. (Empathy, problem solving, assertiveness, change of feelings)

The True Confessions of Charlotte Doyle by Avi. New York, NY: Avon. 1992. Set in 1833, this “diary” tells of Charlotte’s experiences sailing from England to America and how they cause her to question herself and her role in society. (Empathy, problem solving, change of feelings, friendship, assertiveness, stereotyping)

Twelve Days in August by Liza Ketchum Murrow. New York, NY: Avon Books. 1995. Alex’s superior soccer skills and loner personality make him the target of a team’s harassment. Todd must decide if he has the strength to stand up for Alex. (Empathy, anger management, problem solving, stereotyping, peer pressure, bullying, change of feelings)

What Hearts by Bruce Brooks. New York, NY: Harper Collins. 1992. These four short stories provide glimpses into different points of Asa’s life as he copes with family problems and continually strives to define himself. (Empathy, anger management, impulse control, change of feelings, friendship)

When Heroes Die by Penny Raife Durant. New York, NY: Simon and Schuster. 1995. Gary has always wished his Uncle Rob was his father. When he finds out Rob has AIDS and is dying, he must confront his anger, confusion, and stereotypes. (Empathy, anger management, stereotyping, friendship, feelings change)

Where the Red Fern Grows by Wilson Rawls. New York, NY: Batam Books. 1984. Billy’s love for his dogs helps him develop a deep appreciation for friendship and loyalty. (Empathy, problem solving, feelings change, assertiveness)

The Witch of Blackbird Pond by Elizabeth George Speare. New York, NY: Dell. 1972. When Kit moves from Barbados to the Connecticut Colony, her nontraditional upbringing and personality arouse suspicions and anger in the community. (Empathy, impulse control, problem solving, stereotyping, change of feelings, friendship, assertiveness)
