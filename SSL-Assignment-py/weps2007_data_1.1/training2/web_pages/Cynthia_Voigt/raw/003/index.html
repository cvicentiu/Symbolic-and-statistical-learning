Cynthia Voigt: A Personal Glimpse

 Especially since the Newbery, people ask me if I am going to stop teaching now. At first I was surprised at the question, but then I realized the most of the people who asked me that were not teachers. Some of them were present or future students, and it crossed my mind that this might be a hopeful question, but I dismissed that thought quickly.

My answer to the question is always the same, "Oh, I hope not."

But I can understand why they ask. Writing is the glamour job. Teaching is...not glamorous. Certainly, when I left school, teaching was the one job I knew for sure and certain I never wanted to have to do. Teachers' lives smell faintly of chalk dust, and failure. Writers appear on TV. I don't know if people are surprised at my answer to that question. I usually justify it by pointing out that I have a cupcake schedule (which most teachers don't have), teaching one class a day. I try to explain that, having gotten myself into a position where I can have my cake and eat it too, I feel no compulsion to get up from the table.

What is it about teaching that is so interesting? The very things that make writing interesting, I think. Like writing, it keeps the mind alive. I go into a classroom and am faced with any number of different characters, each of whom will work well for basically egocentric reasons, each of whom I will need to understand individually in order to be useful to him, all of whom must assume some collective identity in order for the classroom to function well. Moreover, we spend our time together looking at material which is worth thinking deeply about, looking at first class literature. So that, in preparation, in correction, in classroom management, my entire mind is occupied with a task as complex and rich as any I know. There are not, I think, many occupations which offer the variety of rewards teaching does. Writing happens to be one of the others.

And it is not just mental rewards teaching has to offer. Again, like writing it offers also the prospect of continual good fun. Once I retired for two years. I did not mind having my time to myself and I certainly did not miss the hours of work, not really. I was having a good time: my first book came out, my second went to press, I wrote Dicey's Song. However, as I stepped back into the classroom that third fall, I found myself smiling in anticipation of the dialogue that was about to begin, and the prospect of immediate feedback about whatever I was up to. Books come out months, sometimes years, after the time when you write them, so you never get the chance to wallow in the present vitality. Teaching is mired in that present vitality, of the students, of the teacher, of the material. It is stimulating and invigoration work, and I am still pleased that people will pay me to have such a good time. I smell faintly of chalk dust, and of failures from which I hope I have learned; and I have come to grips with being out-of-step with what most people consider a successful life. I know how much hard work teaching is, and there is much I have to discipline myself to get done. The same can be said of writing. It can also be said of writing that it is a task which I approach with an eager heart, just as I always come eagerly through the classroom door: because you never quite know what will happen even with material you have been working with for years.

Students, and this is fair enough, do not see teaching school in exactly the same way that I do. Most people, I suspect, also do not see it that way. I am not ignorant of this; neither am I unaware that school is, for students, probably the only place where they can learn how their own minds work, learn to discipline that working so that they can do more with their own abilities. Thinking clearly, thinking thoroughly, thinking honestly--none get much attention or encouragement outside of school. However, this is a long-range objective and seems entirely too conceptual when you are squirming in your seat, chewing on a pencil, bored to distraction by the topic of nouns, or Hamlet, while the sun shines bright out there in the free world. My most recent metaphor for teaching has inherent in it, I think, this ambiguity: "Here's what I've been thinking teaching is like," I say to anyone who asks, "It's like going out for dinner and everybody at the table has to talk about what I'm  really interested in."

Will I give up teaching? Oh, I hope not.
