This is the seventh and final book in the Tillerman Series by Cynthia Voigt. You don't need to have read the first six (I haven't) to understand it, however, you might get more out of it if you have.

The book starts off pretty well but doesn't seem to go anywhere, at all. The main character Dicey has just started her own boat-building business at 21 years of age, a strange career choice if you ask me. She is determined to make a success of it and at first I quite liked this resolve of hers. Then things begin to go wrong and you start to see that it isn't so much resolve as stubbornness. It gets really, really annoying. A few mistakes are understandable, she is just starting out after all, but after a while her behaviour gets to the point of stupidity.
I don't know, maybe I have too much commonsense to sympathise with her naivety. Who am I kidding, seriously, she's an idiot.

What's worse, there is no plot. It is just this woman, Dicey, making a fool of herself trying to build boats. Then again, I haven't read the first few books and this does seem like the ideal ending to a series. Very boring, perhaps slightly disappointing. I won't give anything away but so much for feminism and a woman making her own way in the world and all that. I just looked at the date this book was first published, which was 1989, that may explain some of it. It might also explain why I don't like this book very much, the eighties isn't my favourite era.

Also, the book is very American. There is this whole culture underlying the books that anyone who has never lived in America couldn't possibly understand. I certainly didn't understand it, my knowledge of American culture comes from Sweet Valley High and the Sopranos.

All in all, my advice, if you've read the other books and enjoyed them or just need closure then go ahead and buy a copy. If you're just looking for some light reading, don't, you'll end up throwing the book against a wall.

I give "Seventeen Against the Dealer" (very appropriate title) a 2.5/5.
