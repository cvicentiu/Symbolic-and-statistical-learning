Summer Reading List

2004

Sixth Grade

 

Students in the sixth grade are required to read a total of four (4) books: the one required book and three books from the suggested reading list that have not been previously read. Students will be asked to pledge their summer reading assignment. Students who wish to receive extra credit may read up to two (2) additional books from the suggested reading list for a total six (6) books.  One point extra credit for each book will be added to the first quarter grade in either English or history.

 

As you begin your reading, remember that the choice of book is yours. Should you begin a book that you are uncomfortable reading, please exchange it for another. Interests and appropriateness vary from reader to reader.

 

Required Reading:

 

Esperanza Rising, Pam Munoz Ryan.  Esperanza believed her life would be wonderful forever.  She would always live on the family’s ranch in Mexico and would always have fancy dresses and a beautiful home filled with servants.  Her parents would always be with her.  But when a sudden tragedy shatters her world and forces Esperanza and her mother to flee to California, they settle in a camp for Mexican migrant workers.  How can she survive in America?

 

This book will be discussed during the first weeks of school in an interdisciplinary study of Mexico.  As you read the novel, you might find it helpful to do a chapter summary.  The summary should be two to three sentences in length and tell the important events in the chapter.

 

Readers' Guide for Sixth Grade Reading:

 

Summer reading is intended to be pleasurable as well as thought provoking.    The other books will be discussed through book talks, project presentations, and informal assessments.  You might find short summaries helpful in preparing to discuss these books. Detailed notes will not be necessary.

 

Suggested Reading for Sixth Grade:

 

At Her Majesty’s Request, An African Princess in Victorian England, Walter Dean Myers.  A young West African princess watches as warriors murder her parents and destroy her village.  The girl is captured and held to be killed, but in 1850, on the very day set for her death, a British naval officer rescues her.  She is taken to England and presented to Queen Victoria, who decides to provide for the upbringing of the young, orphaned princess.  The writer researched with great detail this true story of a royal friendship.

 

Anne of Green Gables,  L.M. Montgomery. Expecting the orphanage to send a sturdy boy to help with the farm chores, the Cuthberts of Green Gables are dismayed when Anne, a mischievous, talkative girl shows up instead. Expecting her to be no help at all, the Cuthberts are surprised that the longer Anne stays, the more endearing she becomes. You may read any of the “Anne” series.

 

Because of Winn-Dixie,  Kate DiCamillo.  Because of Winn-Dixie, a big, ugly, happy dog, 10-year-old Opal learns 10 things about her long-gone mother from her preacher father. Because of Winn-Dixie, Opal makes new friends among the somewhat unusual residents of her new hometown, Naomi, Florida.  Because of Winn-Dixie, Opal begins to find her place in the world and let go of some of the sadness left by her mother’s abandonment seven years earlier.

 

Black Beauty, Anna Sewell.  Black Beauty tells the story of the horse's own long and varied life, from a well-born colt in a pleasant meadow to an elegant carriage horse for a gentleman to a painfully overworked cab horse. Throughout, Sewell rails--in a gentle, 19th-century way--against animal maltreatment.

 

Bridge to Terabithia, Katherine Paterson.  This is the story of a very special friendship between a boy and girl. It doesn't matter to Jess that Leslie dresses funny or that her family has a lot of money but no TV. Leslie has imagination. Then one morning a terrible tragedy occurs. Will Jess be able to put into action the strength and courage Leslie has given him?

 

The Cat Ate My Gymsuit, Paula Danziger.   When the unconventional English teacher who helped her conquer many of her feelings of insecurity is fired, a junior high student uses her newly found courage to campaign for the teacher's reinstatement.

 

The Cay, Theodore Taylor. Shipwrecked on a tiny Caribbean island, Philip must overcome his prejudice towards Timothy, the old black sailor who becomes the key to his survival.

 

Cheaper by the Dozen, Frank B. Gilbreth, Jr., & Ernestine Gilbreth Carey.  This is the story of the 12 lively Gilbreth children and their remarkable parents.

 

Children of the River, Linda Crew.  Sundara flees Cambodia for the safety of Oregon, where she struggles to be a “good Cambodian girl.”  Although she is forbidden to speak to any white boys, Sundara falls in love with Jonathan.  Is her new life disloyal to her past?

 

Chronicles of Narnia Series, C.S. Lewis.  Four children travel repeatedly to a world in which they are far more than mere children and everything is far more than it seems.  This is a series to be read again and again.

 

Crash, Jerry Spinelli.  Seventh grader John "Crash" Coogan has always been comfortable with his tough, aggressive behavior until his relationship with an unusual Quaker boy and John's grandfather's stroke make him consider the meaning of friendship and the importance of family.  Other titles such as There's a Girl in My Hammerlock would be suitable.

 

The Cuckoo's Child, Suzanne Freeman.  Living in Beirut with her nonconformist parents, Mia pines for all things American and longs for a "normal" childhood.  When her parents disappear, she must live with her aunt in the U.S. This story is written by the niece of former GPS science teacher Charlotte Freeman.

 

Daddy Longlegs, Jean Webster. Jerusha is an orphan girl who expects to be turned out into the world when she leaves the John Grier Home. Since only poor boys qualify for college assistance, Jerusha is astonished when an anonymous man, Daddy-Long-Legs, grants her funding for school. All she must do in return is write letters to him reporting her progress. Her feisty letters provide an excellent view of a girl's life in the early twentieth century.  

 

Dicey's Song, Cynthia Voigt.  With her grandmother's help, Dicey learns to cope with life's problems.  This is the sequel to Homecoming.

 

Dragonwings, Laurence Yep.  This story portrays the rich traditions of the Chinese community as it made its way in a hostile, new world.

 

The Ear, The Eye, and the Arm, Nancy Farmer.  In 2194 in Zimbabwe, General Matsika's three children are kidnapped and put to work in a plastic mine, while three mutant detectives use their special powers to search for them.

 

The Endless Steppe, Esther Hautzig.  This autobiographical work describes the five years Esther Rudomin and her family spent in a Soviet forced labor camp.

 

The Face on the Milk Carton, Caroline B. Cooney.  A favorite of the class this year, the girls were intrigued with the mystery of just who is Janie Johnson.  The other books in the series are also possible choices for you.

 

Flip-Flop, Katerine Paterson.  Uprooted following the death of their father, nine-year-old Vinnie and her five-year-old brother, Mason, cope in different ways--one in silence--but both with the help of Lupe, the flip-flop girl.

 

From the Mixed-Up Files of Mrs. Basil E. Frankweiler, Konigsburg. This is just one of Konigsburg’s delightful tales noted for their inventive plots and memorable characters. You may read any of her works.

 

Gathering Blue, Lois Lowry.  This eagerly awaited companion volume to The Giver takes place in a village with only the most rudimentary technology, where anger, greed, envy, and casual cruelty make ordinary people’s lives short and brutish.  Merciless authorities with their own complex agendas and secrets control this society, like the one portrayed in The Giver, and at the center is a young girl given the responsibility of preserving the memory of the culture.  Lowry has again created a vivid and unforgettable setting for this thrilling story that raises profound questions about the mystery of art, the importance of memory, and the centrality of love.

 

A Girls Named Disaster,  Nancy Farmer.  While fleeing from Mozambique to Zimbabwe to escape an unwanted marriage, Nhamo, an eleven-year-old Shona girls, struggles to escape drowning and starvation and in so doing comes close to the luminous world of the African spirits.

 

The Giver, Lois Lowery.  Jonas lives is a perfect society, or is it really that perfect?  A must read for students who enjoy thinking about what life could be like if the rules were different.

 

Habibi by Naomi Nye.  When Liyana Abboud is fourteen, her father decides the time is right to move the family from St. Louis to his native Jerusalem.  All she and her younger brother Rafik know of their Palestinian father’s culture comes from his reminiscences of growing up and the fighting they see on television.  A strong female character searches for her identity in this novel about starting over and making new friends.

 

Harry Potter and the Order of the Phonenix, J.K. Rowling.  The much-anticipated fifth book in the continuing adventures of Harry Potter will make great reading during the summer.  You may count any other book in the series that you have not previously read.

 

The Hiding Place, Corrie Ten Boom. This is the remarkable story of Corrie Ten Boom and her family who, at risk of their lives, followed the mandate of their Christian understanding by providing refuge to Jews marked for death by Nazi forces occupying the Netherlands during World War II.

 

The Hobbitt, J.R.R. Tolkien.  The world of fantasy called Middle-earth and those charming Lilliputian creatures, the Hobbits, have become a favorite of middle school readers.  Additional books in the series are suitable.

 

Homecoming, Cynthia Voigt.  Four abandoned children embark on a remarkable journey from Connecticut to Maryland in search of love.

 

How Do You Go to the Bathroom in Space? Pogue. Serious and not so serious, here is everything you always wanted to know about living in space but did not know whom to ask.  Colonel William R. Pogue served in the Support Crew of the Apollo 7 and Apollo 11 missions and was the pilot for Skylab 4, the final Skylab mission.

 

If It Hadn't Been for Yoon Jun,  Marie G. Lee.  Alice, who is Korean but who was adopted as a baby, considers herself 100 percent American. So, when her father insists that she be a friend with Yoon Jun, a geeky-looking Korean kid who's just come to America, Alice is very upset. How can she risk her friends and popularity by befriending Yoon Jun?

 

In the Year of the Boar and Jackie Robinson,  Lord.  Shirley Temple Wong sails from China to America with a heart full of dreams.  Her new home is Brooklyn, New York.  Having no friends and knowing no English, Shirley finds a hero in Jackie Robinson, star of the Brooklyn Dodgers.  

 

I, Juan de Pareja, Elizabeth Borton de Trevino.  This is the story of Juan, a slave, and the great Spanish painter Velaquez, his master, and how their relationship changed from master/slave to equal/friend.

 

Inkheart,  Cornelia Caroline Funke.  Twelve-year-old Meggie learns that her father No, a bookbinder, can “read” fictional characters to life when an evil ruler named Capricorn, freed from the novel “Inkheart” years earlier, tries to force Mo to release an immortal monster from the story.

 

Just Ella, Margaret Peterson Haddix.  It is a familiar story: Ella goes to the ball, sweeps Prince Charming off his feet, and is chosen to be his bride.  It is happily ever after time, right?  Wrong! Read how Ella tries to break her engagement in this modern version of the old fairy tale.

 

Julie of the Wolves, Jean Craighead George.  Protected by a wolf pack while lost on the tundra, a 13-year-old Eskimo girl begins to appreciate her heritage--and the oneness with nature that modern man is destroying.  Julie is also appropriate.

 

King of the Winds, Marguerite Henry.   This is the classic story of Sham and his friend, the stable boy Agba. Their adventures take them from the sands of the Sahara to the royal courts of France and, finally, to the green pastures and stately homes of England. Sham was the renowned Godolphin Arabian, whose blood flows through the veins of almost every superior thoroughbred. Sham's speed--like his story--has become legendary. Other titles by this author are also appropriate.

 

Kiss the Dust, Elizabeth Laird.  Because the Iraqi secret police are looking for her father, Tara Hawrami and her family must flee the only home they have ever known and live in a brutal refugee camp.  While in the internment camp, Tara is forced to face interrogations, her mother’s pneumonia, and general culture shock

 

Kon-Tiki, Thor Heyerdahl. This is the story of the Pacific journey by six men on a raft in search of the path taken by Kon-Tiki, a white voyager, 1500 years before.

 

The Lost Years of Merlin,T.A. Barron.  A brilliant epic about  Merlin and his childhood, Barron has created a story of adventure that is filled with ordeals, terrors, and struggles while Merlin-to-be discovers who he is and where he came from.  Wonderful life lessons are shared through Merlin’s adventures.  There are four other books in the series that are also appropriate.

 

Lyddie, Katherine Paterson. Impoverished farm girl Lyddie Worthen is determined to gain her independence by becoming a factory worker.  When working conditions threaten the health of co-workers, she has to make a choice--speak up and risk her job, or be silent.

 

Land of Hope, Joan Lowery Nixon.  Rebecca and her Jewish family flee from Russia in the early 1900's to seek a better life in America.  This book is the first in the Ellis Island series that also include Land of Promise and Land of Dreams.

 

My Friend Flicka, O’Hara.  This is a wonderful story for those who love horses.  The sequel, Thunderhead, may also be read.

 

My Name is Not Angelica,  Scott O'Dell. In 1733 when a slave revolt occurs in the Virgin Islands, Raisha, a young slave girl, is swept into the midst of the rebel war. The sole survivor, she lives to tell the tale of the ill-fated revolt and bear the child of the rebel leader.

 

Nothing But the Truth, Avi.  A 9th grader's suspension for singing the National Anthem during homeroom becomes a national news story.  Other titles by Avi include:  Something Upstairs, The True Confessions of Charlotte Doyle, and The Man Who Was Poe.

 

One Bird, Kyoko Mori.   Mourning her parent's divorce and resenting having to stay with a cranky grandmother, fifteen-year-old Megumi takes a part-time job with a wise veterinarian and finds peace while helping to care for sick birds.

 

The Pearl, John Steinbeck.  A Mexican fisherman finds a pearl of great value--far more than what he needs to pay the doctor to save his child's life. But the pearl brings only evil and tragedy.

 

Prydain Cycle Series, Lloyd Alexander.  Any of the books in this popular series:  The Black Cauldron, The Book of Three, the Castle of Llyr, The High King, would capture the reader who enjoys science fiction.  Other titles by Lloyd Alexander are also suitable.

 

The Red Wall Series, Brian Jacques.  This is a  highly recommended series for the fantasy reader by the present sixth grade class.  Many have found the story of a world of mice quite entertaining.  Any book in the series is acceptable.

 

The Secret Garden,  Hodgson. An enduring British classic, this story about two children struggling with loss and pain is a tribute to the healing powers of friendship and nature.

 

The Serpent's Children,  Laurence Yep.  Set in 19th-century China, this is a compelling story of a young girl's struggle to survive the upheaval of her homeland and keep her family together.

 

Toughboy and Sister,  Kirkpatrick Hill.  A boy and his sister leave their Athabascan Indian village to be with their father at their family’s summer fishing camp in the Yukon.  The death of their father leaves the children marooned with little hope for immediate rescue.  This quiet, simply told survival story coveys a quality of stoic courage and dignity.

 

So Far from the Bamboo Grove,  Yoko Kawashima Watkins. Though she is Japanese, 11-year-old Yoko has lived with her family in Korea all her life. When WWII comes to an end, Yoko and her family are in terrible danger as Koreans rise up to take control of their homeland. They are forced to flee their home, and their harrowing journey to safety is the backbone of this remarkable survival story.

 

Walk Two Moons, Sharon Creech.  As Salamanca Tree Hiddle travels cross-country with her eccentric grandparents, she tells the story of Phoebe Winterbottom, who received mysterious messages, who met a "potential lunatic", and whose mother disappeared. Beneath Phoebe's story is Salamanca's own story--that of a 13-year-old girl whose only wish is to be reunited with her missing mother.

 

When Shlemiel Went to Warsaw,  Issac Bashevia Singer.  Eight stories, some based on traditional Jewish tales, are told by one of the great storytellers.

 

Winter Camp,  Kirkpatrick Hill.  The sequel to Toughboy and Sister, the orphaned children are living with an elderly Athabascan Indian.  In late fall she has them all flown to camp in a bush plane to teach the children how to trap and deal with the Alaskan winter.  When an old miner is seriously injured and Natasha sets off for help, the children find their spirits pushed to the limits.  

 

Zia, Scott O'Dell.  This is the story of what happened to the Indian woman, Karana, in the sequel to The Island of the Blue Dolphins.

 

Zlata's Diary, Ziata Filipovic.  This compelling firsthand account of the war in Sarajevo is told through the eyes of a young Croatian girl.

 

The Wanderer, Sharon Creech.  Sophie is the only girl on the The Wanderer.  Traveling with her family across the Atlantic toward England, Sophie’s real adventures allow her a personal journey into a forgotten past to which she never expected to travel.

 

The Westing Game, Ellen Raskin.  This is a puzzling mystery of 16 players who are all suspects in the curious murder of millionaire Sam Westing--and heirs to his fabulous fortune.
