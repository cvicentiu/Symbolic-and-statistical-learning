Syllabus

Required Texts:

Because I am assigning so many young adult works for this class (approximately 13) I am not going to assign a textbook. However, I will put on reserve in the library several texts, and I will occasionally ask that those of you are education majors read different chapters from those texts.

Objectives:

I want the students to come to an understanding and an appreciation of who young adults are and what their literature is. Future middle school and high school teachers should know how adolescents differ from adults, and how young adult literature differs from adult literature. I want the students to improve their reading skills, their critical thinking skills, their communication skills, and their analysis skills. I also want all of the student to leave the class with a bulging portfolio of ideas, summaries, lists, lesson plans, exams, essay questions, projects, and strategies to incorporate young adult literature in their own classrooms.

All of the students will have an opportunity to participate in a "teaching experience."  This teaching experience will consist of one of two types:  (a) a detailed explication of a YA text, (2) "bit teaching" of one YA text.  The students will participate in the teaching experience in groups of three or four.   Each group must chose one text to “teach” to the class.  But central to their week of teaching are:  (1) What makes this piece of literature great?  (2) How can we use this literature to help others become better citizens, and (3) What service project have we done (or what will we do) as a group to demonstrate that we can make a difference in another person’s life?   Click here for some possible ideas for service projects.

Format:

I will use a combination of lectures, class discussions, group work and presentations, journals, and individual projects. The focus of the semester will be upon creating or adding to resource materials that we will be able to use in our individual classrooms. We will look carefully at the books we read from various positions (psychoanalytic, social science, psychosocial) that come from various criticisms: reader response, feminism, new criticism, new historicism, post modernism, structuralism. The students will notice early that the focus of the class will be on gender issues: gender inequity, gender individuation, gender discourse, gender types.

Evaluation:

There will be no exams. The students will be graded on attendance and classroom participation (100 points), group projects and service (200 points), assigned reading (100 percent) and additional reading (200 points). I encourage you to keep a journal of class discussions and readings.  If you would like me to read your journal, journal entries will be collected every other week. We may begin our discussion of a book with your journal entries. Any student who has four to six absences will be penalized one letter grade.  Any student who accrues six to eight absences will be penalized two letter grades.  Any student who accrues more than eight absences will be asked to withdraw from the class.  Students who read an additional 25 YA titles will receive 100 points; 20 titles equals 80 points; and 15 titles equals 60 points.  Students who read five of the suggested "critical essays" on reserve in the library and submit 2 - 3 page criticisms of the essays will receive 20 points for each essay.  Click here for a list of the criticisms on reserve in the library.    The students must submit a "journal" of their assigned reading and additional readings and writings.  Click here for class project ideas and grading rubric.
