Books Reviews by Virginia Tice

Virginia a student in Oakland, Calif.

Elske
by Cynthia Voight

If Cynthia Voigt books appeal to you, then you absolutely must read Elske. This exquisite novel features Elske, the main character, and her many adventure. The story is of her bravely running from a certain death in the Volkaric (Wolfer) Territory, where everyone is a slave, to freedom in Trastad, a neighboring land. Not only is Elske brave and intelligent, but she also has a sort of charm that both attracts and repels many people. Cynthia Voigt spends a great deal of time developing the character of Elske, which makes the novel more enjoyable to read.

One of Elske's many wonderful qualities is her courage. At the start of the book, she dared fates worse than death and ran from her homeland. Once in the new land, Trastad, she became a faithful servant, who again showed her bravery by defending her mistress against crazy drunken men. "Elske stepped between Idelle and the young captain. 'Quietly now,' she advised her trembling mistress. 'Your fear will make them the more cruel.' Idelle raised her hands to her face and whimpered. Elske screamed. But when Elske screamed, it was the war cry of the Volkaric that came out of her mouth, a howling like the voice of a wolf. The other Adeliers stood wordless." After that, she was given careful respect by all of the other citizens of Trastad.

Elske was not only a fascinating person for her courage, however. She was very intelligent. This was demonstrated in her ability to speak Volkaric, her native tongue, and the language of Trastad. This skill was very rare for anyone not a trader of some sort, so she was put to work as a spy against the foreign merchants. "The serving at table, she discovered, was one of Var Jerrol's uses for her; for some of his guests were merchants from the south. These merchants were cautious when Var Jerrol sat with them, but if he was called away, they would speak unguardedly. Afterwards, the Var called Elske into his chamber and asked her to recite what the merchants had said."

Elske's bravery and intelligence were the driving forces behind her personal growth in her new land. Her homeland was a very cruel, barbaric place that taught that ignorance and cruelty toward others was okay.

Throughout the book, you see Elske's gradually mature into a much kinder and more knowledgeable person. Her courage allowed her to pursue a new life and to accept the changes she went through. Her intelligence also contributed to her maturity. Her quick mind allowed her to adapt smoothly into her new environment and made her a valuable person. This meant that she would have a much larger chance to succeed.

I am sure you will agree when I recommend this book for an enjoyable read. Elske's subtly vibrant personality, intelligence, courage and personal growth join together to make a fantastic book.

Daja's Book

by Tamora Pierce

If you are ever sitting inside on a dreary, rainy day and have nothing to do, pull Daja's Book by Tamora Pierce out and rest it upon your lap. The third book in a quartet, it makes a wonderful addition to a fantasy collection. Daja's book describes in interesting detail the main character, Daja Kisubo. She is, at the beginning of the book, brave but clearly has no self-esteem. Through her own adventures and her three good friends' adventures, she overcomes her inner turmoil.

This book tells in extensive detail of her life as a trangshi Trader (outcast traveler) and how her isolation as an outcast prevents her from conversing with other Traders. Being the trangshi that she is, her self-confidence suffers from not being allowed to speak with her own kind. "'I am trangshi, remember?' Daja asked patiently. 'By Trader law I don't exist. If I don't exist, then she can't talk to me or hear me."'

Daja proves over and over that although young, she does not lack in bravery. She ignores most of the ridicule that is thrown at her because of her status as an outcast, and when she does listen, she responds calmly and quietly. Her bravery is still apparent however, and as much as she may act quiet, she still makes her thoughts known. "They must want this very badly, to make an offer to a trangshi. 'Tenth Caravan Idaram must bargain with me directly. Me. Talk to Daja Kisubo the trangshi, or there will be no talk at all."

In her characterization of Daja, Pierce at first describes a brave girl with no self-esteem who had been repeatedly demeaned by her fellow Traders. As the book progresses, she grows in self-confidence by being brave enough to face the visiting Traders and speak up for herself. By the end of the book, she becomes a heroine who is no longer afraid to talk to her kind. I really enjoyed this book, and I think that if you were to read Daja's story, you would come to admire her bravery and ability to overcome the obstacles in her path. Daja's Book, by Tamora Pierce, really is a book to look forward to reading.

Go Ask Alice

by Anonymous

Go Ask Alice is a nonfiction book about a girl that does drugs. The author of the book made himself or herself anonymous, which made it seem even more interesting in the beginning. The book spoke of a very insecure 16-year old, and her life with drugs. Go Ask Alice was believable because it was a collection of published diary entries edited only slightly from its original teenager slang.

Insecure would be an understatement when talking about the main character. She is crowded with peer-pressure and feels like she is forced to do whatever her peers say. "Oh, to be stoned, to have someone tie me off, and give me a shot of anything." As far as one can tell from the book, "Alice" receives next to no guidance from her relatives, many of which are completely oblivious to the whole incident. You can almost hear her despair.

The key change that takes place with this character is a mental one. After several failed attempts to stop her use of drugs, she concludes that she has been lying to herself. What she realizes is that from the very beginning she has been addicted, and never will be able to go back to the way she was, as hard as she might try. From her drug-twisted perspective, she feels that all that she has to look forward to is death.

I don't think that this book could seem more believable in the fact that we are told from the very beginning that everything is true. As you are reading it, however, it is sometimes hard to imagine that the things that the author goes through actually were real. The diary entry format stresses just how much she goes through every day because of the drugs.

This depressing book would only be fit for a PEP class because of its terrifying content. Go Ask Alice could do a very good job of scaring every student in the class from doing the awful things that the author did. Unless you are curious about her horrifying experiences, I suggest that you not read this book.

The Mirror of Merlin

by T.A. Barron

Once again, I am recommending a fantasy book. This one, The Mirror of Merlin, however, has some qualities that other books left out. None of the books I have read recently speak about such a wise leading character. However, even though he is incredibly intelligent, he still acts like a teenager, with a teenager's anxiety.

The anxiety he faces is based on prophecies given that he will be the greatest wizard of all time. Now, I hope you would understand how much of an impact that would make on his life. He reacts to this problem just as a 20th century teenager would to a problem they find important, he is very anxious and nervous about the future. "'Move.' No response. 'Lift yourself.' Still no response. I released a growl. Already I had wasted the entire morning trying to coax it to move independently. So what if shadow-working was a skill reserved only for the eldest wizards-true mages? I never was much good at waiting."

Merlin's intelligence is what keeps his teenager anxiety from completely overpowering him. He is smart enough to see the possibility that the prophecies might be true, but he is also not so snooty that he is convinced he is perfect. As you might imagine, he is able to see things even at 14 that other people cannot, but he doesn't abuse that power. His intelligence at the beginning of the book grows into wisdom at the end.

This change occurs during a scene in the middle of the book, when he comes across the mirror that the book is named for. This scene plays a very large part in the maturing of his intelligence and the resolution of some of his anxiety. "Find your true self, he had said. Yes, and with it, your true image. Then you shall tap into the greater good, the higher power that breathes life into things." Even though he is almost afraid of this change, he recognizes it as such and grows with his experiences.

Merlin's experiences change him for the better. He not only grows in wisdom, but with that growth he also becomes much surer of the future. He begins believing that he decides what he does, and not a prophecy. "Amidst the swelling cacophony, I heard another voice. Only you can find the way â€¦ Only you." I utterly recommend this fantasy book for a pleasurable read during those rare days when you have nothing at all to do.

A Sort of Homecoming

by Robert Cremins

A Sort of Homecoming by Robert Cremins did not turn out to be as good as I expected. It received great reviews from the Times in London, and The Observer, but my review is not as optimistic. Basically, this guy, Tomás Iremonger (who despises "Tomás" and prefers to be called "Tom") has an idea of what he considers to be the perfect person. This perfect person is what he thinks he becomes. He never outright says that he thinks he is perfect, but his idea of "The New Iremonger" is how it is expressed. "The New Iremonger" has a vision, and the book is almost based around this vision, but the vision is never explained. The reader never is told what exactly Iremonger wants to do.

So Tomás Iremonger, now just "Iremonger" waltzes around in A Sort of Homecoming, spending money he inherited from his grandfather, getting drunk and doing drugs, without a single thought of the consequences. Sure, Cremins's book did seem very realistic, I believed for a while that only Tomás Iremonger could have written this story, but there were some key things missing.

Now I'm sure that there are people born without a thought to any of the principles that keep us in line, but the one & only thing that kept me from believing that this story was true was that he would never snap out of his dream life. He had so much pride in himself, in his wonderfulness, that it made him a character barely able to stand. And when you can hardly bear to read about the main character of the book, it's going to bring it down some. I'm sure that it will appeal so some people, but for my personal taste, it did not. It wasn't an awful book, it kept my attention, but I prefer not to read about a man with a swollen ego.

The Bad Beginning
By Lemony Snicket

There are some people who are very stubborn when they read new books. I am one of these. When I read the back of The Bad Beginning, (the first book in the "Series of Unfortunate Events") by Lemony Snicket, I thought, "Sure, he can say that, but there must be at least one good thing that happens!" Unfortunately, I should have believed what I read. Not only was this book one of the more depressing I've ever read, but the author had a writing style that I abhorred.

Every page or so, some little comment was included that made me, as the reader, feel very inferior. One example: " . . . The money is an incentive-the word 'incentive' here means 'an offered reward to persuade you to do something you don't want to do'-to read long, dull, and difficult books." Every chapter (there are 13 of them) had at least 5 of those little "definitions" inserted.

The only reason I can think of that would make the author include those "helpful additions" was because the lettering was so large it seemed as though it was meant for people who were just beginning to read. The plotline was so terrible, however, with so little skill and such a morbid imagination, that I cannot see a parent giving this book to their kid if they were in their right mind.

The Odyssey

by Homer

The Odyssey, famous for being analyzed in high school English classes across the country, is a surprisingly wonderful read. The adventures that Odysseus goes through are so amazingly beautiful, detailed, and historically accurate that they awe any reader who comes across them. Written around 3,000 years ago, this tale lives on because of its pure magnificence.

The story begins with learning that Odysseus has been trapped by the goddess Calypso, who wants to marry him.

Odysseus is an incredibly loyal husband, and for the many years he has been detained by Calypso, he's never given up hope of returning home. We then learn that the gods on Olympus want him to return home and continue the journey he started when he left home for the battle at Troy. This begins a whole new set of adventures begun with the tale he tells to the Phaecians (Book VI). A background in Homer's preceding book, The Iliad, may help you to better understand this book. With or without its "pre-quel," the Odyssey is a marvelously fascinating read. F

Rescuing Jeffrey
by Richard Galli

If you want to read an emotionally distressing and serious book, here's one for you. It's called Rescuing Jeffrey, and it's the story of a teenager who dives into a pool and becomes paralyzed from the neck down. This true story is told by the father, a man who is still dealing with the difficulties that his son's medical condition brings.

There are sections of the book that could pass as amusing, but the point isn't to make light of the issue. Richard Galli, I believe, is trying to show the reader a million and a half reasons to be careful, and is showing these through heart-felt and painful renderings of what his wife, son and he himself went through. There are points in the book where you can empathize with the author because he has expressed so much anguish in such an articulate way.

He is a very talented writer, and while his book doesn't have fanciful or elaborate dialogue, the way that he brings up each new topic draws the reader in until you feel as if it was your best friend going through each and every dilemma. If you want a casual, amusing read, Rescuing Jeffrey is not the type you're looking for. But if you want a book that will leave you thinking - really, truly thinking - about how precious life and its people are, read this one.

The Tangle Box

by Terry Brooks

The Tangle Box by Terry Brooks, is one in a series called "A Magic Kingdom of Landover." It is definitely not the first, because there are frequent references to characters, scenes and plots from other time periods that do not occur in the book.

Even though I wish I could have read the three other books beforehand (the first being Magic Kingdom for Sale-Sold!), The Tangle Box was so well written I managed to overlook the details I did not understand. The missing pieces, if you could call them that, were not very important, and mostly explained some of the relationships that seemed to have been built already.

I have always been a fan of Terry Brooks, and this book does not lack the ingenious and descriptive qualities that are always found inside the pages he writes. There are concepts that very few other people would have dreamed of, let alone written.

The basis of the book is about the land of Landover, and everything that happens there. One of the main characters is a man named Ben Holiday, the High Lord of Landover and a human, who (in a previous book) found himself in the wonderful world of Landover with absolutely no clue on how to reign. Luckily, he was a lawyer in his world, so since his arrival he has ruled over the land that he can control (the fairy mists not included) with a kind and just hand.

The Tangle Box begins with a troublemaker and conjurer named Horris Kew, who has been living the good life in Ben's world (Horris Kew was expelled from Landover) by a wicked scheme to cheat people out of their money. Horris Kew, in a desperate state, uses his only possession, the Tangle Box, to help him escape an angry mob and ends up back in Landover to cause more chaos. Once you begin to understand the plot, everything starts to fit in. Surprisingly, there is very little of the book that you can guess beforehand, which is good because it is a let-down when you can figure out the ending on your own. This is a wonderful book and if you have the time, I recommend reading the books Magic Kingdom for Sale-Sold!, The Black Unicorn and Wizard at Large before you begin this one.

The Magicians of Caprona

by Diana Wynne-Jones

The Magicians of Caprona, by Diana Wynne-Jones, is a wonderful and comical book about magic, and the havoc it can cause. As the book proclaims, "Tonio is the only person in the famous Montana household who wasn't born with an instinct for creating spells." This is true. Instead of being able to write intricate and helpful charms, he can communicate with cats, and try to bring peace between two rival families.

Casa Montana and Petrocchi have been fighting and competing for longer than anyone can remember. An unknown and very dangerous threat plagues the city of Caprona, and the two families blame each other for all of the problems that arise. To find out who really is causing all of the dilemmas, all of the magicians of Caprona, from Casa Montana and Casa Petrocchi, need to team up and work together. The question is, do they have enough time? Four stars for this outstanding fantasy book. Even someone who's read only non-fiction can understand it. (HarperTrophy 2001)

The Wings of Merlin

by T.A. Barron

Truly, The Wings of Merlin is a work of art. Written by T.A. Barron, it is a wonderful combination of imagination, facts, and pure talent. While some of the characters and occurrences might be better explained in one of the first four books of the five-book series, I find the last to be wonderful anyway. Even if you haven't read the other books, this one is amazing. The Wings of Merlin is a slightly romantic fantasy, wound around two characters, Merlin and his doe friend, Hallia. The book has a nerve-wracking battle between the ultimate examples of good and evil (both with Rhita Gawr and within Merlin himself) and a tear-jerking finish. To all who are mild fantasy fans, high recommendations for this one! (Philomel Books, 2000)
