  People / In Memoriam / Dr. Robert R. Korfhage / Remarks by Christine L. Borgman  
     
     
 

Robert R. Korfhage, A Personal Remembrance from the 1970s

Christine L. Borgman, Professor & Presidential Chair in Information Studies
Graduate School of Education & Information Studies
University of California, Los Angeles
Los Angeles, CA 90095

To appear in the Journal of the American Society for Information Science

November 27, 1998

Bob Korfhage and I first met in Dallas in 1975 at a local chapter meeting of the American Society for Information Science. He was a professor of computer science at Southern Methodist University, studying information retrieval. I was a newly-minted MLS from the University of Pittsburgh who came to Texas to automate the Dallas Public Library. We were kindred spirits, both having arrived at information retrieval via the field of mathematics, but more importantly, through our shared belief that the fields of computer science and library and information science had much to learn from each other. These were heretical ideas in those days of mainframes and punched cards. We were building our own online catalog for DPL, programming it in-house in assembler language. Dialog was only 3 years old, having started with 3 databases, and OCLC had only a toe-hold outside of Ohio. Meanwhile, Bob and I were proselytizing about a future of personal computers, networked information systems, sophisticated models of organization and retrieval, and "user friendly" interfaces, with public libraries playing a major role. We recruited all willing participants to our vision, whether through local meetings or through publication.

Re-reading a paper that we published in the 1978 ASIS proceedings [1], we were remarkably prescient about the challenges that lay ahead. A short paragraph sets the tone:

We believe that the library should take an active role in the provision of information to the citizens of its community, and that to do so requires some understanding of the impact and potential of the new computer and communications technology based on microprocessors. We suggest five major areas of concern over this technological change: cultural, technical, financial, legal, and political.

We went on to identify problems of equity, usability, intellectual property, interoperability, the commoditization of information, and other urgent matters of the late 1990s -- which we were predicting for the mid-1980s. We concluded by stating that "the technical problems involved are solvable; solution of the financial, legal, and political problems is more difficult and requires careful thought and handling." At a time when technology for technology's sake was the sole driver of progress, Bob was a rare voice in the technical wilderness. He recognized that we were constructing a technical world with social consequences, and called for a far broader, multidisciplinary approach that would address these challenges in context. In those days, he was the scholar and I was the library practitioner he had recruited to the cause. We published a similar paper extending these concerns into the field of education [2], as well.

Not satisfied with his accomplishments with ASIS, education, and librarians, he went on to rejuvenate the then-moribund ACM Special Interest Group on Information Retrieval. ACM SIGIR had held only one prior meeting, some years earlier. He took on the role of general chair, appointed me program chair (my first experience at organizing anything beyond local ASIS chapter meetings), and we planned the Second International Conference on Information Storage and Retrieval in Dallas, in 1979. We scheduled it adjacent to the annual meeting of Texas ASIS, of which I was chair at the time. We had only 13 papers for the proceedings, but these included a keynote by Gerard Salton, and contributions by David Penniman, Vijay Raghavan, Bruce Croft, and Emily Fayen. The SIGIR and ASIS meetings were bridged by a panel session entitled "Information implications into the eighties." This meeting was a momentous accomplishment in several respects. It revived the SIGIR meetings, which have been held annually ever since, and now draw hundreds of attendees from many countries. It was an early partnership between ACM and ASIS, exposing each membership to the other, and drawing larger audiences for each meeting in the process. Most importantly, the meeting set information technology challenges in a broader social and policy context. Again, Bob's preface to the proceedings was prescient [3]:

The theme of this conference - Information Implications into the Eighties - has brought forth papers on a wide variety of topics, but all point to the inescapable conclusion of an explosion of information management problems and opportunities. Movement is occurring in many directions - toward large networks and simultaneously toward personal systems. The Eighties will bring challenges to long-held ideas such as how the modern office does its paperwork (or whether it should use paper at all). These and other problems will provide exciting opportunities for real progress.

Bob encouraged me to pursue my doctorate, by involving me in these activities, by inviting me to guest lecture his courses at SMU, and by cajoling in his charming way. I left Dallas in 1980 for Stanford to do so, with many lessons from him in hand. We stayed in contact, though never wrote any more papers together. When he left SMU to become head of the Information Science Department at Pittsburgh, from whence I had come to Dallas, I proudly wrote a reference letter for him. Our careers had converged, once again. Bob was probably the first senior computer scientist to take a post in an information science program, providing him yet more opportunities for cross fertilization. At Pitt he continued his work on user interfaces, visualization, and information retrieval, being among the first technical scholars to explore the relationship between retrieval and presentation, and between navigation and representation. His new textbook, which deservedly has won major awards, will influence a new generation of students.

I am but one of many lucky people in this field whom Bob Korfhage mentored along the way. He was a boundary spanner, visionary, and fine colleague. He was also a good friend for more 20 years. We should all endeavor to leave as rich a legacy for future generations as he has.
