Vannevar Bush
URL: http://www.ibiblio.org/pioneers/bush.html
Vannevar Bush's influential 1945 essay "As We May ThinK" described the foundation of the internet in theoretical terms: Bush foresaw that machines could be developed to store and retrieve documents upon user command, and to link multiple documents by association. The processes, user behaviors, and outcomes he described anticipate those carried out today on the Web and in the digital library.

URL: http://www.ibiblio.org/pioneers/licklider.html

Business Unusual: How 'Event-Awareness' May Breathe Life into the Catalog by Lagoze, Carl (2000)
This paper proposes changes in the use of the catalog and the model upon which it rests.

The first section describes why these changes are necessary if the library is to transition effectively into the digital age, including:
- the disruptive context caused by technological change;
- the costs associated with the catalog; the changing nature of information, how it is delivered, and who takes responsibility for organizing and describing it; and
- dimensions in which metadata varies from the catalog record, e.g., specialization, decentralization, and democratization.

The second section describes one dimension of a new data model - event-awareness - and show it must be an important component of a new cataloging model. This section focuses on the following issues:
- the move way from relatively fixed physical artifacts to generally fluid digital objects;
- the difficulty of establishing integrity, trust and authenticity in the networked environment; and the decentralization and specialization of resource description and problems of mapping among these descriptive vocabularies.

The third section provides the outline of an event model and how it might be used.

Digital Libraries: The Art and Science of Digital Bibliography by Tennant, Roy (1998)
This article suggests that the Internet should not be catalogued, as some people think it should. Rather the author suggests that librarians should be reviewing, selecting and annotating web resources to create bibliographies on particular subjects. This would enable people looking for information on a subject to find it easily. Searchers would also know that these resources are reliable and recommended. Several digital bibliography projects are mentioned including the Resource Organization and Discovery in Subject-based Service (ROADS) project located at the University of Bath.

Digital Libraries by Arms, William Y. (2000)
Digital Libraries, by William Y. Arms, is a great book for those beginning to learn about digital libraries. It gives a thorough look at some of the issues associated with digital libraries, and attempts to define the basic terminology associated with the field. The work also touches on many of the well-known names in digital libraries, and integrates a nice historical aspect, as well.

The book begins with a definition on digital libraries, saying that they are “a managed collection of information, with associated services, where the information is stored in digital formats and accessible over a network” (Arms 2). He goes on to give reasons for digital libraries—why they should exist (i.e. they “bring the library to the user”) and then gives a basic statement that all people in the field of digital libraries should remember: work with the user of the equipment. Design everything so that your user is comfortable. Digital libraries are for the users!

Later in the book Arms attempts to define several digital library terms, such as “collections,” “data and metadata,” and various others. Descriptions and historical aspects are also given for items such as the Marc record and the Z39.50 standard.

Electronic publishing is an area touched on in this book, with a discussion on the background/history of it, as well as economic and legal issues associated with it—the cost and payment of electronic publishing, subscription rates versus one-time use fees, the aims of the publishers versus the goals of the authors, the institutions and organizations involved with electronic publishing, as well as copyright, liability, privacy and software patents.

Research opportunities in digital libraries are also addressed. Arms gives a historical look at the research, as well as how it is funded and areas that are popular for digital libraries. There is also a brief look at the preservation of materials, and how it is important that certain documents are scanned to preserve them and enable them to be shared on a network. One could corrolate this to the Truman State Library…..
These are just some of the topics addressed by this book. It is definitely a must-read for those interested in digital libraries.
Last Reviewed: 2002-04-25
 

Practical Digital Libraries: Books, Bytes, and Bucks by Lesk, Michael (1997)
Despite the word "practical" in the title, this book is not a how-to manual for building a digital library. The author, a computer scientist who has worked with the Commission on Preservation and Access on digital preservation issues, provides background information from a variety of fields (e.g., information retrieval, computer science, economics) that relates to the development of digital libraries. A range of topics are covered, including manipulation of materials in different formats (images, multimedia, etc.), organization, preservation, economics, and intellectual property. According to the preface, the book is aimed at both librarians and computer scientists, but readers without some knowledge of computer science and information retrieval will find some sections difficult to understand. Recommended for library science and information systems collections and for libraries planning to develop large digital collections.

Source of Book Review: Crum, Janet. “Practical Digital Libraries: Books, Bytes, and Bucks” Library Journal 122(16) (October 1, 1997):134.
